Resilience

2026-03-30 The capacity of a system to absorb disturbance and reorganize while retaining essential function — distinct from stability, which measures resistance to change.
Defines resilience, ecological resilience

Resilience is the capacity of a system to absorb disturbance and reorganize while retaining its essential function, structure, and identity. C. S. Holling introduced the concept in 1973 to distinguish it from stability — a distinction that matters politically as much as ecologically.

Stability is the tendency to remain near a single equilibrium. A stable system resists perturbation and returns to its original state. A monoculture crop field is stable in this sense: it maintains high yield under normal conditions and resists small disruptions through pesticide application and irrigation. But it collapses under large disturbance — drought, novel pest, soil depletion — because it lacks the internal diversity to reorganize. Stability and fragility go together.

Resilience is different. A resilient system may fluctuate far from any single equilibrium. It may look messy, inefficient, or contradictory. A diverse old-growth forest contains dead wood, competing species, mycorrhizal networks linking different tree genera, understory layers that appear unproductive. It does not optimize for any single output. But when fire sweeps through, the forest reorganizes: fire-adapted species germinate, the mycorrhizal network redistributes nutrients, and the system persists in a new configuration that retains the essential relationships. The forest survives because it was never optimized for the conditions it was in — it sustained enough internal variety to respond to conditions it had not yet encountered.

Holling and Gunderson’s later work on panarchy (2002) extends this to coupled human-natural systems. They identify an adaptive cycle — growth, conservation, release, reorganization — operating at multiple nested scales. Resilience is highest during the reorganization phase, when the system is open, diverse, and uncommitted to a single trajectory. It is lowest during the conservation phase, when the system has accumulated structure, locked in specific relationships, and become efficient but brittle.

The political application is direct. Social systems optimized for a single form of coordination — whether market efficiency, bureaucratic procedure, or consensus-based assembly — exhibit high stability and low resilience. They function well under the conditions they were designed for and shatter when those conditions change. Systems that sustain multiple, non-aligned forms of coordination — some formal, some relational, some opaque to each other — have the adaptive variety that resilience requires. They survive what stable systems cannot.

This is not an argument for disorder. Resilience is not the absence of structure; it is the presence of enough structural diversity that no single disruption can eliminate all adaptive pathways simultaneously.
