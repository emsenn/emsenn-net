Climates of Duluth, Minnesota and Marquette, Michigan: Past, Present, and Modeled Futures Abstract

Duluth (MN) and Marquette (MI) sit on opposite sides of Lake Superior’s western basin and are often lumped together as “cold, snowy Lake Superior cities.” Observations and regional climate modeling show that they share many broad features—humid-continental climates, strong lake influence, and large seasonal temperature ranges—but differ systematically in mean temperature, snow regime, heavy-rain risk, and the pace and character of recent climate change. Using station climatologies from the Great Lakes Integrated Sciences and Assessments (GLISA), National Weather Service records, and regional climate projections, this paper compares:

for both cities. Duluth is cooler overall, with more days below freezing, slightly more liquid precipitation, and a strong emerging signal of heavier warm-season rainfall. Marquette is somewhat milder but much snowier, sitting in a Lake Superior snowbelt where lake-effect processes dominate winter. Both locations have already warmed substantially, with Duluth showing ~3.9°F of annual warming since 1951 and Marquette ~2.1°F. Under plausible high-emissions futures, both are projected to warm by several additional degrees Fahrenheit by mid-century and 6–11°F by 2100, with shorter winters, longer growing seasons, more heavy precipitation, and complex, non‑monotonic changes in lake-effect snow.

  1. Setting and Data 1.1 Geographic and climatic context

Duluth, Minnesota sits on the western tip of Lake Superior, at the foot of a steep ~250–300 m escarpment that rises rapidly inland. The official weather station (Duluth International Airport) is ~7 miles inland at ~435 m elevation.

Marquette, Michigan lies on the south-central shore of Lake Superior in Michigan’s Upper Peninsula. Its station is closer to lake level (~200 m) and embedded in a classic south-shore snowbelt.

Both cities have humid continental (Dfb) climates: long, snowy winters; short, warm summers; and large seasonal temperature swings, strongly modulated by Lake Superior’s huge thermal mass.

1.2 Data sources and definitions

Observed climate

Local station climatologies (1951–2024, 1991–2020 normals) from GLISA for Duluth and Marquette provide:

1991–2020 mean temperature, precipitation, and extremes.

Snowfall:

Marquette: NOAA / NCEI and derivative summaries report average annual snowfall around 149 inches (~3.8 m), making it one of the snowiest U.S. cities; some sources using different periods give near 200 inches.

Duluth: NWS and related analyses report ~86–90 inches seasonal snowfall (1981–2010 and 1991–2020 normals).

Key takeaways:

Temperature gradient: Marquette’s station is at lower elevation and slightly further south, producing warmer annual averages and fewer days with subfreezing temperatures. Duluth is among the coldest U.S. cities outside alpine and high-plains environments.

Snowfall gradient: Marquette’s position in the core Lake Superior snowbelt means roughly double Duluth’s average snowfall—around 12 feet versus 7–7.5 feet per season—depending on the specific period and location within each metro.

Hydrology: Despite Marquette’s greater snow, annual liquid precipitation is slightly higher in Duluth, which also sees more days with ≥1.25" of rain, a marker of flood-prone heavy events.

2.2 Seasonal structures Winters

Marquette

January mean temperature ~18.5°F.

Long, cloudy, and dominated by persistent lake-effect snow due to relatively ice‑free Lake Superior, with storms distributed through the entire winter.

Snow depths commonly exceed 10 inches; seasonal totals near 150" with high interannual variability (200"+ winters not rare).

Duluth

Extremely long snow cover season from late November to April; ~100 days per year where maximum temperatures do not exceed freezing and ~38 nights ≤0°F.

Average annual snowfall in the 86–90" range, with strong upslope events during easterly flow; record seasons (e.g., 2022–23) exceed 140".

Contrast: Duluth’s winters are colder and more persistently subfreezing, but Marquette’s winters are snowier, because the dominant lake-effect fetch for cold westerly and northwesterly flows favors the south shore.

Summers

Both cities exhibit cool summers for their latitude, with July–August means in the mid‑60s°F and rarely more than a couple of 90°F days per year, thanks to cool lake breezes.

Duluth has earned the nickname “The Air-Conditioned City” for its lake-cooled summers.

Contrast: Marquette is slightly warmer in summer on average, but both remain relatively cool compared to inland Midwestern locations, particularly in daytime highs; differences are modest relative to winter divergence.

  1. Historical Climate Change (1951–Present) 3.1 Regional Great Lakes signals

Across the eight U.S. Great Lakes states, annual average temperatures have risen ~2.3°F (1.3°C) since 1951. Winters have warmed faster than other seasons, and the frost‑free season has lengthened by roughly 1–2 weeks over the 20th century. Climate models tuned for the region project:

+3–6°F regional warming by ~2050 (across low to high emissions).

+6–11°F by 2100, with northern areas—including northern Minnesota and Michigan’s U.P.—on the high end of this range.

The Great Lakes themselves have changed dramatically:

Lake Superior summer surface temperatures increased by about 4.5°F (2.5°C) from 1979–2006, faster than nearby air temperature warming, largely because of declining winter ice cover.

Basin‑wide Great Lakes ice cover has decreased roughly 25% in extent since 1973, and lakes now remain ice‑free 8–46 fewer days per year compared to the early 1970s.

These changes strongly modulate Duluth and Marquette’s local climates, especially winter snow and lake-effect regimes.

3.2 Marquette: observed changes

From GLISA’s station analysis for Marquette (1951–2024):

Temperature trends

Annual mean temperature +2.1°F.

Winter +3.2°F, fall +2.7°F; spring and summer warm more modestly (+0.4°F and +1.0°F).

Precipitation trends

Annual total precipitation essentially unchanged (0.0" change), with a small shift in seasonal distribution:

Slight increases in fall (+0.5", +5%), slight decreases in spring and summer (on the order of ‑4%).

Heavy precipitation (≥1"–1.25" events) shows evidence of modest increases regionally, though the Marquette station’s trend is weaker than some inland Midwestern locations.

Other regional analyses for the Upper Peninsula (e.g., Munising) show the 2000s to be the warmest decade on record, with recent decades ~2.7°F warmer than the 20th‑century average.

Interpretation: Marquette has warmed substantially, particularly in winter and fall, but its annual liquid-precipitation totals have remained roughly stable. At the same time, decreasing ice on Lake Superior and warmer lake water have likely shifted the character of snowfall toward later-starting but more intense lake-effect events early in winter.

3.3 Duluth: observed changes

GLISA’s Duluth station record shows a stronger warming signal:

Temperature trends (1951–2024)

Annual mean temperature +3.9°F (2.1°C).

Winter +5.4°F, summer +3.2°F, spring/fall +3.4–3.5°F.

Precipitation trends

Annual precipitation +1.4" (+4.8%).

Strong increases in winter (+15.5%) and fall (+19.7%), modest increase in spring (+5.8%), and a decrease in summer (−9.3%).

The City of Duluth’s sustainability office reports that from 1950–2015 the city experienced:

+1.8°F increase in annual average temperature,

+11.1% increase in annual precipitation,

~37% increase in the number of heavy precipitation events.

Heavy snow and rain statistics also show a recent uptick in big events:

Minnesota climate records note that several of Duluth’s largest February snowfalls have occurred since 2000, and that 2022–23 set a new all-time seasonal snowfall record (≈140 inches) at the airport station.

Interpretation: Duluth has warmed faster than Marquette, especially in winter, and has become wetter overall, with more heavy rain and snow events and some seasonal redistribution: wetter cool seasons and somewhat drier summers.

3.4 Historical comparison

Summarizing:

Warming magnitude: Duluth’s annual warming (~3.9°F) roughly doubles Marquette’s (~2.1°F), especially in winter.

Precipitation change:

Marquette: essentially flat in annual liquid precipitation, with subtle shifts among seasons.

Duluth: clear upward trend in annual precipitation, driven by wetter winter and fall and more heavy events.

Snow:

Regionally, heavy February and shoulder-season snow events appear to be increasing in northern Minnesota, consistent with warmer air holding more moisture even in winter.

In Marquette’s snowbelt, the signal is partly masked by large natural variability, but case studies and regional modeling suggest more intense but possibly less persistent snow in the long run.

  1. Modeled Futures 4.1 Regional projections for the Great Lakes and Upper Midwest

Across the Great Lakes, GLISA and related assessments project:

Mid‑century (2040–2059):

Annual average air temperatures +3–6°F above late‑20th‑century baselines, with northern Minnesota and the U.P. on the high end.

End-of-century (2080–2099):

Under high emissions, annual temperatures +6–11°F region‑wide.

Independent Michigan-focused analysis finds that under high emissions, statewide annual temperatures could be up to ~15–16°F warmer by 2100, with 2.7–7°F increases already by mid‑century under intermediate scenarios.

Other consistent projections include:

Fewer very cold days: region‑wide decreases in days below 20°F and especially below 0°F.

Longer frost‑free season: an additional 1–2 months by 2100 under high emissions, on top of the ~1–2 week extension already observed.

Precipitation:

Slight increases in annual precipitation, but with strong seasonal and spatial variability.

General trend toward more heavy precipitation days (≥1" per day) even where annual totals change modestly.

4.2 Lake Superior, ice, and lake-effect snow

Dynamic downscaling studies for the Great Lakes (RegCM4 with embedded lake model) show that:

Great Lakes ice cover is projected to “dramatically decline.” By late century, winter ice may be confined to shallow northern shorelines, with most of Lake Superior remaining open for much of winter.

Cold‑season precipitation is projected to increase, especially as rain and mixed precipitation.

For lake-effect snow:

Mid‑21st century:

Warmer air + more open water + higher atmospheric moisture = more intense lake-effect precipitation, often still falling as snow in cold-season events.

Around Lake Superior, heavy lake-effect snowstorms may become more frequent by mid-century.

Late 21st century:

Continued warming pushes more cold-season precipitation over freezing, shifting events from snow to rain.

Heavy lake-effect snowfall extremes become less common by late century across the basin, even though total cold-season precipitation increases.

This produces a nonlinear trajectory for places like Marquette (core snowbelt) and a somewhat muted but similar pattern for Duluth.

4.3 Duluth-specific projections

The Duluth climate vulnerability and adaptation assessment gives a city-level picture under high-emissions futures:

By 2100:

Annual average temperature +4 to +11°F.

Days above 95°F increase from near zero to about 23 days per year.

Days below freezing decrease by ~50 days per year.

Cooling-degree demand (air conditioning energy) increases by over 500%.

By mid‑century (2040–2070):

Summer conditions in Duluth become similar to those currently found several hundred miles to the south, an evocative “city moving ~145 feet south per day” analogy commonly used in outreach.

Combined with regional projections, plausible futures for Duluth include:

Fewer but still significant periods of deep cold; rare −30°F events become very rare.

Hot days (≥90°F and especially ≥95°F) become much more common, though still fewer than in inland Midwestern cities.

Increased extreme rainfall and rain‑on‑snow events, raising risks of flash floods and infrastructure damage (as already seen in the June 2012 flood with >10 inches of rain in 24 hours).

Snowfall:

In the next few decades, winters may remain quite snowy (or even somewhat snowier) due to enhanced moisture and lake‑effect potential.

Toward late century, more winter storms fall as rain or mixed precipitation, and seasonal snow totals likely decline despite occasional blockbuster storms.

4.4 Marquette / south-shore projections

Marquette itself has fewer direct downscaled projections published in public-facing documents, but nearby Alger County and Pictured Rocks National Lakeshore—just east along the same south shore snowbelt—have been studied in detail:

Temperatures near Pictured Rocks are projected to rise by at least 5°F and possibly up to ~14°F by late century, depending on emissions scenario and model.

Michigan-wide assessments suggest:

+2.7–7.0°F by mid-century (2040–2059) under intermediate scenarios.

Up to +8.8°F by mid-century under high emissions and up to ~15.8°F by 2100 under the most pessimistic scenario.

State climate summaries and federal assessments note:

Michigan as a whole has already warmed 2–3°F over the last century, with more frequent heavy downpours and earlier lake-ice breakup.

For Marquette specifically, plausible futures under high emissions include:

Strong winter warming, particularly in minimum temperatures; more winter days oscillating around the freezing point.

More rain in the cold season, and more mixed precipitation events.

Longer frost‑free seasons (1–2 additional months by 2100).

Complex snow trajectories:

In the near- to mid‑term, lake-effect mechanisms plus warmer lake water likely boost heavy snow potential, with big storms (feet of snow in a few days) perhaps even more frequent.

By late century, persistent mid‑winter above‑freezing periods and rain-on-snow events contribute to reduced seasonal snowpack, more frequent mid‑winter thaws, and greater freeze–thaw stress on infrastructure.

  1. Comparative Future Climates

Bringing these threads together:

5.1 Temperature regimes

Baseline difference: Marquette is ~3.5°F warmer annually than Duluth today, largely because of elevation and slightly more southerly, lake‑moderated position.

Future warming:

Both cities likely see mid‑century warming of roughly 3–6°F and end‑of‑century warming of 6–11°F under high emissions, with northern locations (including Duluth and Marquette) on the upper end.

Duluth’s city‑specific planning documents explicitly frame +4–11°F by 2100, while Marquette’s region (Alger County / U.P.) is assigned projections in a similar or slightly higher range depending on model ensemble.

Implication: The temperature gap between the cities narrows but does not vanish. Duluth remains colder on average, but the “brutal winter” identity softens toward something more like today’s climates hundreds of kilometers to the south.

5.2 Frost-free season and extremes

Both cities gain 4–8 weeks of frost‑free season by 2100, in line with regional projections.

Extremely cold nights (≤−20°F) become much rarer, especially in Duluth, which currently sees more of them.

In contrast, hot days (≥90°F and ≥95°F) become common enough in Duluth that the city must plan for heatwaves and air-conditioning demands that historically were negligible.

Marquette, starting from a slightly warmer baseline and already having a couple of 90°F days per year, also sees a strong increase in hot days and warm nights, though probably fewer extremely hot days than Duluth because of its more exposed lake frontage.

5.3 Precipitation and hydrologic risks

Annual totals: both remain in the ~30–35" band under most projections, with modest increases; but seasonal distribution shifts.

Heavy precipitation:

Duluth already shows a strong recent increase in heavy rainfall events, and projections for the Midwest call for continued intensification.

Marquette and Michigan more broadly see increased frequency of heavy downpours (spring rainfall +10–30% in coming decades, heavy rain events roughly doubled over the 20th century).

Floods and erosion:

Duluth faces high flood risk due to steep topography, numerous steep streams, and aging stormwater infrastructure, as dramatically illustrated by the 2012 “mega-rain” event.

Marquette is less topographically extreme but faces shoreline erosion, storm-driven lake-level fluctuations, and rain‑on‑snow flood potential along tributaries.

5.4 Snow and winter character

Short term / mid‑century (~2040–2059):

Declining ice cover and warmer lake water enhance the lake-effect engine, especially for south‑shore snowbelts like Marquette and (to a lesser degree) Duluth.

Models suggest increases in total cold-season precipitation and heavy lake-effect storms, even as mean winter temperatures warm.

Duluth’s winters: still cold and snowy, but with more frequent mixed events and heavy wet snow (e.g. the “Blue Blizzard” type storms).

Marquette’s winters: likely to remain among the snowiest in the Lower 48, potentially featuring more frequent extreme snowfall events in the coming decades.

Late century (~2080–2099):

Continued warming pulls more storm days above freezing, and the precipitation phase shifts from snow to rain for a growing share of winter storms.

Even in traditional snowbelts, model ensembles indicate declining seasonal snow totals and shorter continuous snowpack seasons by late century, especially if emissions remain high.

Contrast:

In Duluth, the trajectory likely moves from “very cold and snowy” → “less cold but still snow-rich, with frequent wet storms” → “milder, with more winter rain and less total snow but higher flood risks.”

In Marquette, the path is “extremely snowy” → “even more extreme lake-effect bursts atop warmer winters” → “eventually less reliable snowpack, but remaining snowier than most of the Midwest.”

  1. Socio-ecological Implications and the “Climate Haven” Narrative

Both cities have been discussed in narratives about “climate havens” because of their abundant freshwater, distance from sea-level rise, and historically cool climates. Duluth in particular has been marketed—and critiqued—as a candidate “climate-proof” destination.

The climate projections above complicate that story:

Less brutal cold improves winter livability and reduces heating demand, but:

Introduces new heat‑stress risks, especially for vulnerable populations and buildings not designed for air conditioning.

More heavy rain and rain‑on‑snow events increase flood and landslide risks, with Duluth’s steep terrain and Marquette’s shoreline both vulnerable.

Changing snow regimes:

On one hand, less severe sustained cold may lengthen construction seasons and reduce some winter hazards.

On the other, heavier wet snows and mid‑winter thaws stress infrastructure, forests, and transportation systems. The Duluth region’s 2022 “Blue Blizzard” and forest damage is a foretaste of such compound extremes.

In ecological terms:

Forests: Upslope conifer forests around both cities face migration pressures, pest and disease shifts, and increased drought and fire risk in a warmer, more variable climate.

Lakes and water quality: Warmer Lake Superior and reduced ice cover increase the risk of harmful algal blooms, nearshore warming, and ecological regime shifts, even if the lake remains relatively cold compared to others.

The broad implication is that neither Duluth nor Marquette is “climate-proof.” They may fare better than many hotter, more drought‑prone regions on some metrics, but their climates are changing rapidly, particularly in winter structure and hydrologic extremes.

  1. Synthesis: Similarities and Differences 7.1 Key similarities

Strong Lake Superior control: Both climates are moderated by the lake, with cool summers, delayed springs, and extended fall mildness.

Humid continental regime: Large seasonal temperature ranges, significant snowfall, and precipitation distributed fairly evenly through the year.

Observed warming: Both have already experienced multi‑degree warming, particularly in winter and at night; both show shifts toward longer growing seasons and fewer cold extremes.

Future trajectory: Both are expected to:

Warm by several more degrees.

See more heavy precipitation events.

Experience shorter winters and longer frost‑free periods.

Undergo complex shifts in snow and ice regimes tied to Lake Superior.

7.2 Key differences

Mean state

Marquette is warmer annual‑mean and particularly in winter, but significantly snowier.

Duluth is colder with longer periods below freezing, but has slightly higher annual liquid precipitation and more heavy rain days.

Historical change

Duluth has warmed faster, especially in winter, and shows a clearer increase in annual precipitation and heavy precipitation events.

Marquette has warmed less, with minimal change in annual precipitation but likely shifts in timing and snow/rain partitioning.

Hazard profile

Duluth: greater flash‑flood and landslide risk, emerging heat‑wave vulnerability, and intense, heavy wet snow events.

Marquette: shoreline erosion and infrastructure stress from extreme snowfall, rain‑on‑snow events, and heavy lake-effect storms.

Snow futures

Both will eventually see less reliable seasonal snowpack, but Marquette will almost certainly remain the snowier of the two, likely retaining a snow-based winter identity longer, even as winters warm.

  1. Conclusion

Comparing Duluth and Marquette across historic, current, and modeled future climates shows a pair of cities deeply shaped by the same water body yet diverging in important ways:

Duluth has already transformed more dramatically, warming faster and becoming wetter with more heavy rain and snow events.

Marquette remains slightly milder yet far snowier, with climate change subtly reshaping its snow‑dominated winters rather than obviously eroding them—for now.

Going forward, both are on track for warmer, shorter winters, more volatile precipitation, and complex changes in lake-effect processes driven by a warming, increasingly ice‑free Lake Superior. Their shared and contrasting experiences will continue to provide a kind of natural experiment in how latitude, topography, and lake exposure modulate the same regional climate signal.
