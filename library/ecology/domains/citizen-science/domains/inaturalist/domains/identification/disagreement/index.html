Disagreement and uncertainty

2025-12-30 by emsenn

Disagreement is normal in biodiversity data. It should be documented, not suppressed.

Practice guidance

  • Use higher-level taxa when evidence is insufficient.
  • Add a comment explaining uncertainty or alternate diagnoses.
  • Avoid “forcing” a species-level ID to reach Research Grade.

Why disagreement is informative

  • Signals that the evidence does not resolve diagnostic traits.
  • Creates a visible audit trail for future reviewers.
  • Helps data users filter or down-rank uncertain records.

What goes wrong if you do this poorly

Misidentifications skew range maps and can mask population decline or expansion. Disagreement, when documented, provides a clear signal of uncertainty to data users.

Sources

  • Kosmala et al. (2016) on data quality and verification: https://doi.org/10.3389/fevo.2016.00114
  • Wiggins & Crowston (2011) on data quality practices: https://doi.org/10.1007/s11280-011-0140-3

Leading ID vs. community ID

  • Use leading ID as a signal of current consensus, not certainty.
  • Use community ID to summarize the most supported taxon.
  • When evidence is limited, prefer a higher-level community ID to avoid false precision.

Constructive disagreement examples

  • “The wing venation isn’t visible, so I’m not confident at species. Suggesting genus.”
  • “This looks like Species B, but the leaf arrangement suggests Genus C. Leaving at genus.”
