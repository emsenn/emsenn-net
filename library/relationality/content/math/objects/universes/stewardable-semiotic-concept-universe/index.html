The Stewardable Semiotic Concept Universe

The Stewardable Semiotic Concept Universe

A Modal–Comonadic Heyting Framework for Stewardable Semantic Concepts

with Annotation Calculus, Constant Identity, Failure Semantics, Provenance, Delta, Subtraction, Interface Algebra, Fibration, Sheaf Semantics, and Coherence Theorems

Author: emsenn
Date: December 2025

Abstract

We extend the Semiotic Universe—a modal–comonadic Heyting structure equipped with definable operators from a typed λ-calculus—into a full Stewardable Semiotic Concept Universe. This extended universe supports the creation, annotation, revision, comparison, restriction, and evaluation of semantic concepts while preserving all structural invariants of the underlying Heyting–modal–comonadic semantics.

On top of the base Semiotic Universe, we introduce:

  1. a formal annotation calculus for concept-level surface structure,
  2. a groupoid of constant identity for concept names,
  3. a layer of failure semantics and partiality compatible with the Heyting structure,
  4. a provenance semantics tracking the intensional history of concept fragments,
  5. a quantitative evaluation semantics for fragments, deltas, and stewardship actions,
  6. a notion of semantic delta at annotation, fragment, and closure levels,
  7. a notion of semantic subtraction defined Heyting-algebraically and fragmentwise,
  8. a monoidal category of stewardship operators,
  9. a stewardship interface algebra compiling steward actions into operators,
  10. a 2-category of partial structures,
  11. a monoidal action of stewardship operators on partial structures,
  12. a Grothendieck fibration of fragments with provenance,
  13. sheaf semantics for fragmentwise interpretation and evaluation, and
  14. coherence theorems establishing compatibility of all these structures with the closure operator of the Semiotic Universe.

All constructions remain internal to the mathematics of the Semiotic Universe. The resulting structure, denoted

USSC, \mathcal{U}^{\mathrm{SSC}},

is a stewardable semiotic concept universe: a mathematically robust setting within which concepts can be created, revised, compared, subtracted, evaluated, and traced under stewardship actions.

1. Preliminaries

We recall only the structures required from the base Semiotic Universe.

1.1 Heyting–Modal–Comonadic Ambient

Let (H,≤)(H,\leq) be a complete Heyting algebra with operations
∧,∨,⇒,⊥,⊤\wedge,\vee,\Rightarrow,\bot,\top.

A modal closure operator j:H→Hj:H\to H is extensive, monotone, idempotent, and join-continuous.

A Heyting–comonadic trace G:H→HG:H\to H is simultaneously:

  • a comonad on the thin category induced by (H,≤)(H,\le), and
  • a complete Heyting algebra endomorphism preserving all Heyting structure and join suprema.

We assume:

  • Modality–trace interaction: j(G(a))≤G(j(a))j(G(a))\le G(j(a)) for all a∈Ha\in H;
  • Stability: a∈Hst  ⟺  G(a)∈Hsta\in H^{\mathrm{st}} \iff G(a)\in H^{\mathrm{st}}, where Hst:={a∈H:j(a)=a}. H^{\mathrm{st}}:=\{a\in H : j(a)=a\}.

1.2 Definable Operators

We assume a typed λ-calculus with base type PP (“propositional concept”).
Definable operators Opdef\mathsf{Op}^{\mathrm{def}} are closed λ-terms of type Pn→PP^n\to P modulo definitional equality.

Each f∈Opdeff \in \mathsf{Op}^{\mathrm{def}} has an interpretation

⟦f⟧:Hn→H \llbracket f\rrbracket : H^n \to H

satisfying:

  • monotonicity and directed join-continuity in each argument,
  • Heyting compatibility,
  • modal homomorphism: j(⟦f⟧(a⃗))=⟦f⟧(j(a⃗)), j(\llbracket f\rrbracket(\vec a))=\llbracket f\rrbracket(j(\vec a)),
  • trace compatibility: G(⟦f⟧(a⃗))=⟦f⟧(G(a⃗)), G(\llbracket f\rrbracket(\vec a))=\llbracket f\rrbracket(G(\vec a)),
  • fragment preservation, and
  • hereditary extensionality (fragmentwise equality propagates under all closure operations).

1.3 Fragments and Global Closure

A fragment is a finitely generated modal–temporal Heyting subalgebra F⊆HF\subseteq H, closed under ∧,∨,⇒,⊥,⊤,j,G\wedge,\vee,\Rightarrow,\bot,\top,j,G.

A partial semiotic concept structure is a pair

X=(H_X,\Op_X)

with HX⊆HH_X\subseteq H and \Op_X\subseteq \mathsf{Op}^{\mathrm{def}}.

We have three closure operators on the complete lattice

\mathcal{U} := \{(H_X,\Op_X)\mid H_X\subseteq H,\ \Op_X\subseteq \mathsf{Op}^{\mathrm{def}}\}:
  • Ssem\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{sem}} (semantic closure),
  • Ssyn\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{syn}} (syntactic closure with finitary justification),
  • Sfus\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{fus}} (fusion closure).

Their composite

S:=Sfus∘Ssyn∘Ssem \mathcal{S} := \mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{fus}} \circ \mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{syn}} \circ \mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{sem}}

is a closure operator on U\mathcal{U}.

The Semiotic Universe is the least fixed point of S\mathcal{S}, denoted

\mathcal{U}_{\mathrm{semiotic}}=(H_{\mathrm{sem}},\Op_{\mathrm{sem}}).

2. The Lattice of Partial Concept Structures

2.1 Definition

\mathcal{U} = \{ (H_X, \Op_X) \mid H_X\subseteq H,\ \Op_X\subseteq \mathsf{Op}^{\mathrm{def}} \}.

We order partial structures pointwise:

(H_X,\Op_X) \le (H_Y,\Op_Y) \quad\text{iff}\quad H_X\subseteq H_Y\ \text{and}\ \Op_X\subseteq \Op_Y.

2.2 Completeness

Proposition 2.1.
(U,≤)(\mathcal{U},\le) is a complete lattice with:

\bigwedge_i (H_i,\Op_i) = \Big(\bigcap_i H_i,\ \bigcap_i \Op_i\Big),

\bigvee_i (H_i,\Op_i) = \Big(\bigcup_i H_i,\ \bigcup_i \Op_i\Big).

Proof. Set-theoretic intersection and union preserve subset inclusion into HH and Opdef\mathsf{Op}^{\mathrm{def}}. ∎

3. Syntax and Typing of Annotation Terms

We define a formal language for surface annotations on concept constants.

3.1 Syntax

Let Const\mathsf{Const} be constants of type PP, one for each concept.

Definition 3.1 (Annotation Terms).

t::=c∣k(t1,…,tn), t ::= c \mid k(t_1,\dots,t_n),

where c∈Constc\in\mathsf{Const} and k∈Opdefk\in \mathsf{Op}^{\mathrm{def}}.

Finite annotation sets are subsets of a set AnnTerm\mathsf{AnnTerm} of annotation terms.

3.2 Typing Rules

Typing context: Γ(c)=P\Gamma(c)=P for constants.

Rules:

Γ⊢c:PΓ⊢ti:PΓ⊢k(t1,…,tn):P. \frac{}{\Gamma\vdash c:P} \qquad \frac{\Gamma\vdash t_i:P}{\Gamma\vdash k(t_1,\dots,t_n):P}.

Theorem 3.2 (Type Soundness).
If Γ⊢t:P\Gamma\vdash t:P then ⟦t⟧\llbracket t\rrbracket is defined in HH.

Proof. Immediate from interpretation of definable operators in the Heyting algebra HH. ∎

3.3 Canonical Forms

Annotation terms may be regarded as written in λ-normal form, with operator symbols as canonical heads. We treat the syntactic category of annotation terms modulo definitional equality.

4. Groupoid of Constant Concept Identity

We formalize the identity of concept constants across revisions and encodings.

4.1 Primitive Identity

We introduce a symmetric relation c≈c′c\approx c' on Const\mathsf{Const}.

4.2 Groupoid Structure

Definition 4.1.
Let G\mathcal{G} be the groupoid with:

  • objects: c∈Constc\in\mathsf{Const};
  • morphisms: (c,c′)(c,c') whenever c≈c′c\approx c';
  • identities (c,c)(c,c);
  • inverses (c′,c)(c',c);
  • composition (c,c′)∘(c′,c′′)=(c,c′′)(c,c')\circ(c',c'')=(c,c'').

Theorem 4.2.
G\mathcal{G} is a small groupoid.

Proof. Reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity of ≈\approx provide identities, inverses, and associativity of composition. ∎

4.3 Transport on Annotation Terms

If c≈c′c\approx c', define t[c′/c]t[c'/c] by replacing each occurrence of cc with c′c' in tt.

Lemma 4.3.
Typing is preserved under transport: if Γ⊢t:P\Gamma\vdash t:P then Γ⊢t[c′/c]:P\Gamma\vdash t[c'/c]:P.

Proof. Replacement preserves the type PP; proof by structural induction on tt. ∎

4.4 Fragmentwise Coherence

Theorem 4.4.
If c≈c′c\approx c', then ⟦t⟧=⟦t[c′/c]⟧\llbracket t\rrbracket = \llbracket t[c'/c]\rrbracket in any fragment FF containing the denotations of both constants.

Proof. Fragment evaluation is extensional and respects definable operators; constants identified by ≈\approx are interpreted as the same element of HH. ∎

5. Failure Semantics and Partiality

We enrich the universe with a minimal notion of failure that is compatible with the Heyting algebra and closure operators.

5.1 Failure Elements and Flags

We designate the Heyting bottom ⊥∈H\bot\in H as the semantic failure value ⊥fail\bot_{\mathrm{fail}}. Conceptually:

  • ⊥fail\bot_{\mathrm{fail}} may represent inconsistent or malformed content,
  • but algebraically it is just ⊥\bot; the additional failure information is carried separately.

Definition 5.1 (Failure-annotated partial structure).
A partial structure with failures is a triple

X = (H_X,\Op_X,F_X)

where:

  • HX⊆HH_X\subseteq H,
  • \Op_X\subseteq \mathsf{Op}^{\mathrm{def}},
  • F_X\subseteq H_X\cup\Op_X is a set of failure-marked elements (semantic or operator-level).

Failure arises from invalid annotations, impossible fragment formation, or malformed operator application.

5.2 Failure Fragment

Definition 5.2 (Failure fragment).
The failure fragment Ffail\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{fail}} is the least fragment containing ⊥fail\bot_{\mathrm{fail}}. Since fragments are closed under all Heyting operations, modality, and trace, we have:

Ffail={⊥fail}. \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{fail}} = \{\bot_{\mathrm{fail}}\}.

Lemma 5.3 (Failure propagation in fragments).
If any generator of a fragment FF is ⊥fail\bot_{\mathrm{fail}}, then F=FfailF = \mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{fail}}.

Proof. For any a∈Fa\in F, closure under ∧\wedge yields a∧⊥fail=⊥faila\wedge\bot_{\mathrm{fail}} = \bot_{\mathrm{fail}}, so all generated content collapses to ⊥fail\bot_{\mathrm{fail}}. ∎

5.3 Failure under Operators and Closure

We extend semantics with the following convention:

  • If any operator application ⟦f⟧(a⃗)\llbracket f\rrbracket(\vec a) is undefined or ill-typed, we interpret it as ⊥fail\bot_{\mathrm{fail}} and mark the corresponding term or operator in FXF_X.

Definition 5.4 (Failure propagation under semantic closure).
Semantic closure Ssem\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{sem}} on a failure-annotated structure (H_X,\Op_X,F_X) satisfies:

  1. If any application of f\in\Op_X to elements of HXH_X yields ⊥fail\bot_{\mathrm{fail}}, then ⊥fail∈HSsem(X)\bot_{\mathrm{fail}}\in H_{\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{sem}}(X)} and Ffail\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{fail}} is included.
  2. Any newly introduced failure is recorded in FSsem(X)F_{\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{sem}}(X)}.

Theorem 5.5 (Monotonicity of failure).
If X≤YX\le Y and XX contains a failure-marked element, then:

S(X)≤S(Y)and⊥fail∈HS(Y). \mathcal{S}(X) \le \mathcal{S}(Y) \quad\text{and}\quad \bot_{\mathrm{fail}} \in H_{\mathcal{S}(Y)}.

Proof. Closure is inflationary and monotone; failure is represented by ⊥fail\bot_{\mathrm{fail}}, which is preserved under extension of carrier sets. ∎

In what follows we suppress FXF_X in notation when not essential, but all closure and operator definitions are implicitly extended to track failures.

6. Annotation Operator and Concept Fragments

We move from syntactic annotations to semantic fragments.

6.1 Surface Annotation Sets

A surface annotation for a concept constant k∈Constk\in\mathsf{Const} is a finite set

Ann(k)⊆AnnTerm. \mathrm{Ann}(k)\subseteq \mathsf{AnnTerm}.

To each partial structure XX we associate an auxiliary mapping

AnnX:Const→P(AnnTerm). \mathrm{Ann}_X : \mathsf{Const}\to \mathcal{P}(\mathsf{AnnTerm}).

6.2 Annotation Operator

Definition 6.1 (Annotation operator).
The annotation operator for a concept constant kk is the endomap

Ak:U→U,Ak(X):=X, \mathcal{A}_k : \mathcal{U}\to\mathcal{U},\qquad \mathcal{A}_k(X):=X,

which leaves the underlying partial structure unchanged and updates only the external annotation map AnnX(k)\mathrm{Ann}_X(k).

All semantic consequences of annotations are mediated through fragment generation.

6.3 Interpretation and Fragment Generation

Definition 6.2 (Interpretation set).
Given XX and kk, define:

Tk,X:={⟦t⟧:t∈AnnX(k)}⊆H. T_{k,X} := \{\llbracket t\rrbracket : t\in \mathrm{Ann}_X(k)\}\subseteq H.

Definition 6.3 (Concept fragment).

Fk,X:=the least fragment containing Tk,X. F_{k,X} := \text{the least fragment containing } T_{k,X}.

Lemma 6.4 (Monotonicity of fragments in annotations).
If AnnX(k)⊆AnnX′(k)\mathrm{Ann}_X(k)\subseteq \mathrm{Ann}'_X(k), then Fk,X⊆Fk,X′F_{k,X}\subseteq F'_{k,X}.

Proof. The generating set of Fk,XF_{k,X} is included in that of Fk,X′F'_{k,X}; fragments are minimal modal–temporal subalgebras containing their generators. ∎

Failure semantics from the previous section apply to fragment generation: if any ⟦t⟧=⊥fail\llbracket t\rrbracket = \bot_{\mathrm{fail}}, then Fk,X=FfailF_{k,X}=\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{fail}}.

7. Provenance Semantics for Concept Fragments

We now equip fragments with intensional histories.

7.1 Provenance Categories

For each fragment FF, we wish to record how its elements arose from annotations and semantic operations.

Definition 7.1 (Provenance histories).
A provenance history for an element a∈Fa\in F is a finite sequence of construction steps, each step being one of:

  • introduction as ⟦t⟧\llbracket t\rrbracket for some annotation term tt,
  • formation by a Heyting operation, modality jj, or trace GG,
  • application of a definable operator ⟦f⟧\llbracket f\rrbracket,
  • inclusion under fragment extension.

Two histories are comparable if one refines the other by adding or collapsing intermediate steps.

Definition 7.2 (Provenance category of a fragment).
For each fragment FF, let P(F)P(F) be the small category whose:

  • objects are provenance histories of elements of FF,
  • morphisms are refinement relations between histories (viewed as arrows from coarser to finer descriptions).

Composition is concatenation of refinements.

7.2 Provenance Functor on Fragment-Indexed Structures

Recall the total category E\mathcal{E} of fragment-indexed structures (defined later in Section 14). We anticipate it now.

Definition 7.3 (Provenance functor).
Define a functor:

P:E→Cat \mathcal{P} : \mathcal{E} \to \mathbf{Cat}

by:

P(F,X):=P(F), \mathcal{P}(F,X) := P(F),

P((F,X)→(F′,X′)):=the functor extending histories along F⊆F′. \mathcal{P}\big((F,X)\to(F',X')\big) := \text{the functor extending histories along }F\subseteq F'.

Lemma 7.4 (Functoriality of provenance).
P\mathcal{P} is a functor: it preserves identities and composition.

Proof. Identity inclusions induce identity refinements of histories; composition of fragment inclusions corresponds to composition of refinement functors. ∎

7.3 Provenance-Lifting of Stewardship Operators

Each stewardship operator E:U→UE:\mathcal{U}\to\mathcal{U} acts on fragments; we require that it lift to provenance.

Definition 7.5 (Lifted provenance action).
For a fragment FF and its image E(F)E(F), define a functor:

EF♯:P(F)→P(E(F)) E^\sharp_F : P(F) \to P(E(F))

sending each history to the history extended by a tagged step “application of EE”.

Lemma 7.6 (Provenance stability under operators).
The assignment F↦EF♯F\mapsto E^\sharp_F is natural in FF; for any inclusion F⊆F′F\subseteq F', the square

P(F)→EF♯P(E(F))↓↓P(F′)→EF′♯P(E(F′)) \begin{array}{ccc} P(F) & \xrightarrow{E^\sharp_F} & P(E(F)) \\ \downarrow & & \downarrow \\ P(F') & \xrightarrow{E^\sharp_{F'}} & P(E(F')) \end{array}

commutes up to canonical isomorphism of refinement.

Proof. Both routes extend histories by inclusion and then record the application of EE; the order of these two operations is coherent. ∎

Provenance semantics will later be shown to be preserved by closure and compatible with the fragment fibration (Section 18).

8. Semantic Contribution via Global Closure

We now compute the semantic “footprint” of a concept in the Semiotic Universe, enriched with failure and provenance.

8.1 Concept-Level Semantic Contribution

Definition 8.1 (Semantic contribution of a concept).
For a concept constant kk in a partial structure XX, set:

SemX(k):=S(Fk,X), \mathrm{Sem}_X(k) := \mathcal{S}(F_{k,X}),

where S\mathcal{S} is the global semiotic closure and Fk,XF_{k,X} the concept fragment generated from annotations.

This closure implicitly:

  • propagates failures,
  • extends provenance histories, and
  • ensures coherence with the base Semiotic Universe.

Theorem 8.2 (Boundedness by the Semiotic Universe).

SemX(k)≤Usemiotic. \mathrm{Sem}_X(k) \le \mathcal{U}_{\mathrm{semiotic}}.

Proof. Usemiotic\mathcal{U}_{\mathrm{semiotic}} is the least fixed point of S\mathcal{S}. The closure of any partial structure lies below that least fixed point in U\mathcal{U}. ∎

8.2 Closure and Provenance

Theorem 8.3 (Provenance stability under closure).
For any fragment FF, the provenance of S(F)\mathcal{S}(F) is the colimit of the provenances of the intermediate fragments used in closure:

P(S(F))≅colim⁡{P(F′)∣F⊆F′⊆Hsem and F′ appears in the closure chain}. \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{S}(F)) \cong \operatorname{colim}\{\mathcal{P}(F') \mid F\subseteq F'\subseteq H_{\mathrm{sem}}\ \text{and}\ F'\ \text{appears in the closure chain}\}.

Sketch. Closure steps form a directed system of fragments; provenance functor P\mathcal{P} sends this system to a diagram in Cat\mathbf{Cat}. Closure’s monotonicity and uniformity ensure that histories glue uniquely along inclusions. ∎

9. Revision and Local Persistence

We study how semantic contributions evolve under annotation revision.

9.1 Monotone Revision

Theorem 9.1 (Monotonicity in annotations).
If AnnX(k)⊆AnnX′(k)\mathrm{Ann}_X(k)\subseteq \mathrm{Ann}'_X(k) then

SemX(k)≤SemX′(k). \mathrm{Sem}_X(k)\le \mathrm{Sem}'_X(k).

Proof. By Lemma 6.4, Fk,X⊆Fk,X′F_{k,X}\subseteq F'_{k,X}. Monotonicity of S\mathcal{S} yields S(Fk,X)≤S(Fk,X′)\mathcal{S}(F_{k,X})\le \mathcal{S}(F'_{k,X}). ∎

9.2 Local Persistence

Definition 9.2 (Locally persistent annotation).
An annotation term t∈AnnX(k)t\in\mathrm{Ann}_X(k) is locally persistent if

⟦t⟧∈Fk,Xbut⟦t⟧∉HSemX(k). \llbracket t\rrbracket\in F_{k,X} \quad\text{but}\quad \llbracket t\rrbracket\notin H_{\mathrm{Sem}_X(k)}.

In words: the annotation contributes inside the local fragment but is not promoted by global closure.

Proposition 9.3 (Local containment).
Locally persistent terms have no effect outside Fk,XF_{k,X}: their denotations do not propagate beyond the concept’s fragment under closure.

Proof. By definition of local persistence, ⟦t⟧\llbracket t\rrbracket is not in the semantic carrier of SemX(k)\mathrm{Sem}_X(k), so closure does not promote it beyond its local fragment. ∎

10. Evaluation Semantics

We add a quantitative evaluation layer over the universe, without imposing any normative semantics.

10.1 Evaluation Monoid and Valuation

Definition 10.1 (Evaluation monoid).
Let (M,≤M,+,0)(M,\le_M,+,0) be a commutative ordered monoid. Intuitively, MM measures size, complexity, cost, or semantic weight.

Definition 10.2 (Semantic valuation).
A valuation is a monotone map

μ:H→M \mu : H \to M

satisfying:

  1. μ(⊥fail)=0\mu(\bot_{\mathrm{fail}}) = 0,
  2. μ(a∨b)≤Mμ(a)+μ(b)\mu(a\vee b) \le_M \mu(a)+\mu(b),
  3. μ(j(a))≥Mμ(a)\mu(j(a)) \ge_M \mu(a),
  4. μ(G(a))≥Mμ(a)\mu(G(a)) \ge_M \mu(a).

We do not require full additivity; subadditivity at joins suffices.

10.2 Fragment and Concept Valuations

Definition 10.3 (Fragment valuation).
For a finitely generated fragment FF with generators gens(F)\mathrm{gens}(F),

μ(F):=∑a∈gens(F)μ(a). \mu(F) := \sum_{a\in\mathrm{gens}(F)} \mu(a).

Definition 10.4 (Concept valuation).
For SemX(k)\mathrm{Sem}_X(k), define:

μ(k;X):=μ(HSemX(k)):=sup⁡{μ(F)∣F⊆HSemX(k) fragment}. \mu(k;X) := \mu\big(H_{\mathrm{Sem}_X(k)}\big) := \sup\{\mu(F)\mid F\subseteq H_{\mathrm{Sem}_X(k)}\ \text{fragment}\}.

10.3 Delta Magnitude

Definition 10.5 (Delta magnitude).
For concept constants k,k′k,k', define:

μ(Δsem(k,k′)):=μ(Δfrag(Fk,X,Fk′,X)), \mu\big(\Delta_{\mathrm{sem}}(k,k')\big) := \mu\big(\Delta_{\mathrm{frag}}(F_{k,X},F_{k',X})\big),

where fragment and semantic delta are defined in Section 16.

10.4 Evaluation and Closure

Theorem 10.6 (Monotonicity of evaluation).
If X≤YX\le Y, then

μ(HX)≤Mμ(HY). \mu(H_X)\le_M \mu(H_Y).

Proof. HX⊆HYH_X\subseteq H_Y and μ\mu is monotone. ∎

Theorem 10.7 (Evaluation compatibility with closure).
For any partial structure XX,

μ(HS(X))=sup⁡{μ(F)∣F⊆HS(X) fragment}. \mu(H_{\mathcal{S}(X)}) = \sup\{\mu(F)\mid F\subseteq H_{\mathcal{S}(X)}\ \text{fragment}\}.

Proof. By definition of μ(HS(X))\mu(H_{\mathcal{S}(X)}) and the fact that fragments generate the closure carrier. ∎

This evaluation layer will be used to quantify the impact of stewardship operators and concept revisions.

11. Restriction

We formalize syntactic and semantic restriction to remove concepts.

11.1 Syntactic Restriction

Definition 11.1 (Restriction by a concept constant).
For a constant k∈Constk\in\mathsf{Const} in a partial structure X=(H_X,\Op_X), define:

X\setminus k := (H_X\setminus\{\text{denotation of }k\},\ \Op_X\setminus\{f\in\Op_X : f\ \text{mentions }k\}).

Proposition 11.2 (Monotonicity of restriction).
If X≤YX\le Y, then X∖k≤Y∖kX\setminus k \le Y\setminus k.

Proof. Removing the same constant and operators from larger carriers or operator sets preserves inclusion. ∎

11.2 Restricted Closure

Definition 11.3 (Restricted closure).
The restricted closure of XX at kk is:

S(X∖k). \mathcal{S}(X\setminus k).

Proposition 11.4 (Idempotence of restricted closure).

S(S(X∖k)∖k)=S(X∖k). \mathcal{S}(\mathcal{S}(X\setminus k)\setminus k) = \mathcal{S}(X\setminus k).

Proof. Once kk is removed, repeated restriction has no further effect; S\mathcal{S} is idempotent on fixed points. ∎

We compare restriction with semantic subtraction in Section 17.

12. Stewardship Operator Algebra

We now formalize stewardship operators as algebraic and categorical objects acting on partial structures.

12.1 Monoidal Category of Stewardship Operators

Definition 12.1 (Stewardship operators).
A stewardship operator is a monotone map

E:U→U E: \mathcal{U} \to \mathcal{U}

compatible with:

  • fragments (preserves fragment structure of carriers),
  • closure (respects S\mathcal{S} up to inequality),
  • failure semantics (does not unmark failures),
  • provenance (admits a lifting E♯E^\sharp),
  • evaluation (does not decrease μ\mu).

Let Stew\mathbf{Stew} be the category with:

  • a single object ∙\bullet,
  • morphisms ∙→∙\bullet\to\bullet given by such operators EE.

Composition is function composition, E⊗F:=E∘FE\otimes F := E\circ F, and identity is id\mathrm{id}.

Theorem 12.2.
Stew\mathbf{Stew} is a strict monoidal category.

Proof. Function composition is associative; identity is strict; tensor is composition. ∎

12.2 2-Category of Partial Structures

Definition 12.3 (2-category of partial structures).
Define a 2-category Part\mathbf{Part} where:

  • 0-cells: partial structures X∈UX\in\mathcal{U},
  • 1-cells: monotone maps f:X→Yf:X\to Y in U\mathcal{U},
  • 2-cells: refinements f⇒gf\Rightarrow g if f(X)≤g(X)f(X)\le g(X) pointwise.

Theorem 12.4.
Part\mathbf{Part} is a strict 2-category.

Proof. Composition of monotone maps is monotone; identities exist; refinement as inequality is reflexive and transitive; vertical and horizontal composition of inequalities is valid. Standard for posets enriched in posets. ∎

12.3 Monoidal Action on Partial Structures

Definition 12.5 (Monoidal action).
A monoidal action of Stew\mathbf{Stew} on Part\mathbf{Part} is a functor:

Stew×Part→Part,(E,X)↦E(X), \mathbf{Stew} \times \mathbf{Part} \to \mathbf{Part}, \quad (E,X) \mapsto E(X),

compatible with the monoidal structure.

Theorem 12.6 (Action coherence).
The stewardship operators define a strict monoidal action on Part\mathbf{Part}.

Proof.

  • Identity: id(X)=X\mathrm{id}(X)=X.
  • Composition: (E∘F)(X)=E(F(X))(E\circ F)(X)=E(F(X)).
  • Functoriality: If X≤YX\le Y, then E(X)≤E(Y)E(X)\le E(Y) since EE is monotone.
  • 2-cells: If E⇒E′E\Rightarrow E' (pointwise inequality), then for all XX, E(X)≤E′(X)E(X)\le E'(X).

These properties satisfy the axioms of a strict action of a monoidal category on a 2-category. ∎

13. Stewardship Interface Algebra

We now define a typed action language for stewards and its compilation into stewardship operators.

13.1 Interface Actions

Definition 13.1 (Interface actions).
Let Act\mathsf{Act} be the set of syntactic stewardship actions generated by:

  • Annotate(k,A)\mathsf{Annotate}(k, A), where k∈Constk\in\mathsf{Const} and A⊆AnnTermA\subseteq\mathsf{AnnTerm} finite;
  • Compare(k,k′)\mathsf{Compare}(k,k') for constants k,k′k,k';
  • Subtract(k,Ctx)\mathsf{Subtract}(k,\mathrm{Ctx}), where Ctx\mathrm{Ctx} is a partial structure;
  • RefineFrag(F,φ)\mathsf{RefineFrag}(F, \varphi), where FF is a fragment and φ\varphi is a refinement directive;
  • Evaluate(F)\mathsf{Evaluate}(F) for fragments;
  • finite compositions and combinations thereof.

These are not yet operators; they are an external syntax.

13.2 Action Typing

We endow actions with simple types indicating their domain and codomain shape:

  • Annotate:Const×P(AnnTerm)→Stew\mathsf{Annotate}: \mathsf{Const} \times \mathcal{P}(\mathsf{AnnTerm}) \to \mathbf{Stew},
  • Compare:Const×Const→Stew\mathsf{Compare}: \mathsf{Const} \times \mathsf{Const} \to \mathbf{Stew},
  • Subtract:Const×U→Stew\mathsf{Subtract}: \mathsf{Const}\times\mathcal{U}\to \mathbf{Stew},
  • RefineFrag:F×Φ→Stew\mathsf{RefineFrag}: \mathcal{F}\times\Phi \to \mathbf{Stew}, where Φ\Phi is a space of refinement policies,
  • Evaluate:F→M\mathsf{Evaluate}: \mathcal{F} \to M, purely observational.

13.3 Compilation to Stewardship Operators

Definition 13.2 (Interface compilation).
Define a map:

⟦−⟧iface:Act→Stew \llbracket - \rrbracket_{\mathrm{iface}} : \mathsf{Act} \to \mathbf{Stew}

by structural recursion:

  • ⟦Annotate(k,A)⟧iface:=Ek,Aann\llbracket \mathsf{Annotate}(k,A)\rrbracket_{\mathrm{iface}} := E^{\mathrm{ann}}_{k,A},
    where Ek,AannE^{\mathrm{ann}}_{k,A} updates AnnX(k)\mathrm{Ann}_X(k) to include AA and re-closes Fk,XF_{k,X}.
  • ⟦Compare(k,k′)⟧iface:=Ek,k′Δ\llbracket \mathsf{Compare}(k,k')\rrbracket_{\mathrm{iface}} := E^{\Delta}_{k,k'},
    where Ek,k′ΔE^{\Delta}_{k,k'} computes Δsem(k,k′)\Delta_{\mathrm{sem}}(k,k') and may extend the structure with comparison artifacts.
  • ⟦Subtract(k,Ctx)⟧iface:=Ek,Ctx⊖\llbracket \mathsf{Subtract}(k,\mathrm{Ctx})\rrbracket_{\mathrm{iface}} := E^{\ominus}_{k,\mathrm{Ctx}},
    where Ek,Ctx⊖E^{\ominus}_{k,\mathrm{Ctx}} performs semantic subtraction (Section 17).
  • ⟦RefineFrag(F,φ)⟧iface:=EF,φref\llbracket \mathsf{RefineFrag}(F,\varphi)\rrbracket_{\mathrm{iface}} := E^{\mathrm{ref}}_{F,\varphi},
    where EF,φrefE^{\mathrm{ref}}_{F,\varphi} manipulates fragments consistent with φ\varphi.
  • Composition of actions maps to composition of operators: ⟦a2∘a1⟧iface:=⟦a2⟧iface∘⟦a1⟧iface. \llbracket a_2\circ a_1\rrbracket_{\mathrm{iface}} := \llbracket a_2\rrbracket_{\mathrm{iface}}\circ \llbracket a_1\rrbracket_{\mathrm{iface}}.

Lemma 13.3 (Well-typedness of compilation).
For any action a∈Acta\in\mathsf{Act},

⟦a⟧iface∈Stew. \llbracket a \rrbracket_{\mathrm{iface}} \in \mathbf{Stew}.

Proof. Each primitive compilation clause yields a monotone endomap on U\mathcal{U} that respects fragments, closure, failures, provenance, and evaluation. Composition of such maps preserves these properties. ∎

13.4 Interface–Operator Compatibility

Theorem 13.4 (Compatibility of interface and stewardship algebra).
Let a∈Acta\in\mathsf{Act} and E:=⟦a⟧ifaceE:=\llbracket a\rrbracket_{\mathrm{iface}}. Then for all partial structures XX:

  1. E(X)E(X) is fragment-preserving,
  2. EE admits a provenance lifting E♯E^\sharp,
  3. failures in XX remain failures (they are not “healed” silently),
  4. μ(HE(X))≥Mμ(HX)\mu(H_{E(X)}) \ge_M \mu(H_X).

Sketch. Follows from the construction of each primitive operator and the monotone, fragmentwise, and evaluation-preserving properties of closure and definable operators. ∎

14. Fragment Fibration

We formalize locality of fragments and their relationship to partial structures.

14.1 Base Category of Fragments

Let F\mathcal{F} be the poset-category of fragments under inclusion:

  • objects: fragments F⊆HF\subseteq H,
  • morphisms: unique arrow F→F′F\to F' iff F⊆F′F\subseteq F'.

14.2 Total Category of Fragment-Indexed Structures

Definition 14.1 (Total category E\mathcal{E}).
Define a category E\mathcal{E} with:

  • objects: pairs (F,X)(F,X) where FF is a fragment and X=(H_X,\Op_X) is a partial structure with HX⊆FH_X\subseteq F;
  • morphisms: (F,X)→(F′,X′)(F,X)\to(F',X') whenever F⊆F′F\subseteq F' and X≤X′X\le X'.

Define projection:

π:E→F,π(F,X)=F. \pi:\mathcal{E}\to \mathcal{F},\quad \pi(F,X)=F.

Theorem 14.2 (Grothendieck fibration).
π:E→F\pi:\mathcal{E}\to \mathcal{F} is a Grothendieck fibration.

Proof Sketch.
Given inclusion u:F⊆F′u:F\subseteq F' and an object (F,X)(F,X), define the cartesian lift as (F′,X)(F',X) with the evident inclusion. For any morphism (G,Z)→(F′,Y′)(G,Z)\to(F',Y') over uu, factor uniquely through this lift by the partial structure inclusion X≤Y′X\le Y' induced by Z≤Y′Z\le Y'. Standard for posets. ∎

Provenance semantics from Section 7 fit over this fibration through the functor P\mathcal{P}.

15. Sheaf Semantics for Fragmentwise Interpretation and Evaluation

We interpret fragments as local semantic and evaluative views.

15.1 Presheaf of Semantic Carriers

Definition 15.1 (Semantic presheaf).
Define a presheaf:

H:Fop→Set \mathcal{H}: \mathcal{F}^{op} \to \mathbf{Set}

by:

H(F):=F, \mathcal{H}(F) := F,

H(F⊆F′):=(inclusion F′↪F)∗ \mathcal{H}(F\subseteq F') := (\text{inclusion }F'\hookrightarrow F)^\ast

given by restriction.

15.2 Sheaf Condition

Definition 15.2 (Covering family).
A family {Fi}i∈I\{F_i\}_{i\in I} covers FF if

F=⋁iFi, F = \bigvee_i F_i,

their fragmentwise join (least fragment containing ⋃iFi\bigcup_i F_i).

Theorem 15.3 (Sheaf condition).
If elements ai∈Fia_i\in F_i satisfy ai=aja_i=a_j in every intersection Fi∩FjF_i\cap F_j, then there exists a unique a∈Fa\in F with a∣Fi=aia|_{F_i}=a_i for all ii.

Proof Sketch. Fragments are sub-Heyting algebras; intersections inherit Heyting structure. The join fragment is generated by the union of generators, and Heyting operations are functional, so a coherent family glues uniquely. ∎

15.3 Closure and Sheaf Structure

Theorem 15.4 (Closure preserves sheaf semantics).
If {Fi}\{F_i\} covers FF, then

S(F)=⋁iS(Fi) \mathcal{S}(F) = \bigvee_i \mathcal{S}(F_i)

inside the sheaf.

Proof Sketch. S\mathcal{S} is built from monotone inflationary operators acting uniformly on generators. Given that the union of generator sets of FiF_i generates FF, closure of the union coincides with the join of the closures. Fragmentwise extensionality guarantees uniqueness. ∎

15.4 Evaluation as a Sheaf

The valuation μ\mu induces a functor:

M:Fop→Pos \mathcal{M}:\mathcal{F}^{op}\to \mathbf{Pos}

sending FF to the poset of valuations restricted to FF. The sheaf condition for H\mathcal{H} lifts to M\mathcal{M} by monotonicity of μ\mu.

16. Semantic Delta

We define annotation, fragment, and semantic deltas for concepts.

16.1 Annotation Delta

For finite annotation sets A,B⊆AnnTermA,B\subseteq \mathsf{AnnTerm}:

Δann(A,B):=(A∖B)∪(B∖A). \Delta_{\mathrm{ann}}(A,B) := (A\setminus B) \cup (B\setminus A).

16.2 Fragment Delta

For fragments F,G⊆HF,G\subseteq H:

Δfrag(F,G):=least fragment containing (F∪G)∖(F∩G). \Delta_{\mathrm{frag}}(F,G) := \text{least fragment containing }(F\cup G)\setminus(F\cap G).

Lemma 16.1.
Δfrag(F,G)\Delta_{\mathrm{frag}}(F,G) is a fragment.

Proof. The generating set (F∪G)∖(F∩G)(F\cup G)\setminus(F\cap G) is finite. Closing under Heyting operations, modality, and trace yields a fragment. ∎

16.3 Semantic Delta for Concepts

Let k,k′∈Constk,k'\in\mathsf{Const} be concept constants with fragments Fk,XF_{k,X}, Fk′,XF_{k',X}.

Definition 16.2 (Semantic delta).

Δsem(k,k′):=S(Δfrag(Fk,X,Fk′,X)). \Delta_{\mathrm{sem}}(k,k') := \mathcal{S}\bigl(\Delta_{\mathrm{frag}}(F_{k,X},F_{k',X})\bigr).

Theorem 16.3 (Monotonicity of semantic delta).
Suppose annotations extend:

AnnX(k)⊆AnnX′(k),AnnX(k′)⊆AnnX′(k′), \mathrm{Ann}_X(k) \subseteq \mathrm{Ann}'_X(k),\quad \mathrm{Ann}_X(k') \subseteq \mathrm{Ann}'_X(k'),

so that corresponding fragments satisfy

Fk,X⊆Fk,X′,Fk′,X⊆Fk′,X′. F_{k,X} \subseteq F'_{k,X},\quad F_{k',X} \subseteq F'_{k',X}.

Then

Δsem(k,k′)  ≤  Δsem(k,k′)′:=S(Δfrag(Fk,X′,Fk′,X′)). \Delta_{\mathrm{sem}}(k,k') \;\le\; \Delta_{\mathrm{sem}}(k,k')' := \mathcal{S}\bigl(\Delta_{\mathrm{frag}}(F'_{k,X},F'_{k',X})\bigr).

Proof Sketch. Enlarged annotations enlarge fragments (Lemma 6.4). Enlarged fragments yield enlarged fragment deltas. Monotonicity of S\mathcal{S} gives the result. ∎

Evaluation μ\mu applied to Δsem\Delta_{\mathrm{sem}} yields a quantitative divergence measure (Definition 10.5).

17. Semantic Subtraction

Semantic subtraction removes the semantic support of one fragment (or concept) from another, using Heyting structure.

17.1 Heyting Subtraction of Elements

Given a,b∈Ha,b\in H, define:

a⊖b:=a∧(b⇒⊥). a\ominus b := a \wedge (b \Rightarrow \bot).

This uses the Heyting pseudo-complement b⇒⊥b\Rightarrow\bot as a “negation” of bb.

Lemma 17.1.
For all a,b∈Ha,b\in H:

  1. a⊖b≤aa\ominus b \le a.
  2. If c∧b≤⊥c\wedge b\le \bot, then c≤a⊖bc\le a\ominus b.

Proof.
(1) Trivial from meet.
(2) c∧b≤⊥c\wedge b\le \bot implies c≤b⇒⊥c\le b\Rightarrow\bot, hence c≤a∧(b⇒⊥)=a⊖bc\le a\wedge(b\Rightarrow\bot) = a\ominus b. ∎

17.2 Fragment Support and Fragment Subtraction

Definition 17.2 (Fragment support).
For fragment FF, define its semantic support:

s(F):=⋁x∈Fx. s(F) := \bigvee_{x\in F} x.

Definition 17.3 (Fragment subtraction).
Given fragments F,GF,G, define:

F⊖G:=the least fragment containing { a⊖s(G):a∈F }. F\ominus G := \text{the least fragment containing } \{\, a\ominus s(G) : a\in F\,\}.

Lemma 17.4.
F⊖GF\ominus G is a fragment.

Proof. The set {a⊖s(G)∣a∈F}\{a\ominus s(G)\mid a\in F\} is finite if FF is finitely generated. Closure under the fragment operations yields a fragment. ∎

17.3 Semantic Subtraction for Concepts

Let kk be a concept constant with fragment Fk,XF_{k,X}.

Definition 17.5 (Semantic subtraction of a concept).
Define:

X \ominus k := \left( H_X',\,\Op_X \right)

where

HX′:=least fragment containing { a⊖s(Fk,X):a∈HX }. H_X' := \text{least fragment containing } \{\, a\ominus s(F_{k,X}) : a\in H_X \,\}.

Theorem 17.6 (Monotonicity of subtraction).
If X≤YX\le Y, then

X⊖k  ≤  Y⊖k. X\ominus k \;\le\; Y\ominus k.

Proof.
HX⊆HYH_X \subseteq H_Y implies {a⊖s(Fk,X)∣a∈HX}⊆{b⊖s(Fk,Y)∣b∈HY}\{a\ominus s(F_{k,X})\mid a\in H_X\}\subseteq\{b\ominus s(F_{k,Y})\mid b\in H_Y\}. Since Fk,X⊆Fk,YF_{k,X}\subseteq F_{k,Y}, we have s(Fk,X)≤s(Fk,Y)s(F_{k,X})\le s(F_{k,Y}), so b⊖s(Fk,Y)≤b⊖s(Fk,X)b\ominus s(F_{k,Y}) \le b\ominus s(F_{k,X}). Closure of generators preserves inclusion. Operators are unchanged. ∎

17.4 Relation to Restriction

Theorem 17.7 (Subtraction versus restriction).
For all XX and kk,

S(X⊖k)  ≤  S(X∖k). \mathcal{S}(X\ominus k) \;\le\; \mathcal{S}(X\setminus k).

Proof Sketch.
X⊖kX\ominus k removes the semantic support associated to kk from each element of HXH_X, while X∖kX\setminus k fully removes the syntactic constant and all expressions mentioning it. Closure from X∖kX\setminus k must eliminate at least as much semantic influence as X⊖kX\ominus k. ∎

18. Unified Coherence Theorems

We now show that the extended system—syntax, identity, failures, annotations, fragments, provenance, evaluation, closure, stewardship operators, interface actions, delta, subtraction, and fragment fibration—coheres into a single mathematical object.

18.1 Identity Coherence

Theorem 18.1 (Identity stability).
If c≈c′c\approx c', then for all stewardship operators E∈StewE\in\mathbf{Stew},

Sem(E(c))=Sem(E(c′)). \mathrm{Sem}(E(c)) = \mathrm{Sem}(E(c')).

Proof Sketch.
Annotations transport via constant identity (Section 4). Fragments for cc and c′c' coincide; delta is trivial; subtraction behaves identically; closure of isomorphic fragments yields the same semantic contribution and provenance histories. ∎

18.2 Sheaf Coherence of Stewardship Actions

Theorem 18.2 (Sheaf coherence of operators).
Stewardship operators preserve the fragment fibration and sheaf semantics:
for all E∈StewE\in\mathbf{Stew} and all morphisms (F,X)→(F′,X′)(F,X)\to(F',X') in E\mathcal{E},

E(X)∣F=E(X′)∣FwheneverX≤X′. E(X)|_F = E(X')|_F \quad\text{whenever}\quad X\le X'.

Proof Sketch.
Monotonicity of EE ensures E(X)≤E(X′)E(X)\le E(X'). Since EE preserves fragments and closure commutes with restriction up to inequality, the restriction of EE to FF agrees whether we act before or after extension to F′F'. Extensionality in fragments yields equality. ∎

18.3 Provenance and Evaluation Coherence

Theorem 18.3 (Provenance coherence).
For any stewardship operator EE and fragment inclusion F⊆F′F\subseteq F', the square

P(F)→EF♯P(E(F))↓↓P(F′)→EF′♯P(E(F′)) \begin{array}{ccc} P(F) & \xrightarrow{E^\sharp_F} & P(E(F)) \\ \downarrow & & \downarrow \\ P(F') & \xrightarrow{E^\sharp_{F'}} & P(E(F')) \end{array}

commutes up to canonical refinement equivalence.

Proof. Follows from the definition of E♯E^\sharp and functoriality of P\mathcal{P} (Lemma 7.6). ∎

Theorem 18.4 (Evaluation coherence).
For any stewardship operator EE,

μ(HX)≤Mμ(HE(X))andμ(HS(E(X)))≥Mμ(HS(X)). \mu(H_X) \le_M \mu(H_{E(X)}) \quad\text{and}\quad \mu(H_{\mathcal{S}(E(X))}) \ge_M \mu(H_{\mathcal{S}(X)}).

Proof. By definition, EE is evaluation non-decreasing; closure preserves or increases valuation by Theorem 10.7. ∎

18.4 Monoidal-2-Category Coherence

Theorem 18.5 (Global coherence).
The monoidal category Stew\mathbf{Stew} acts coherently on the 2-category Part\mathbf{Part}:

E(F(X))=(E⊗F)(X) E(F(X)) = (E\otimes F)(X)

up to a unique 2-cell, and this action preserves:

  • the fragment fibration π:E→F\pi:\mathcal{E}\to\mathcal{F},
  • provenance semantics P\mathcal{P},
  • sheaf semantics H\mathcal{H},
  • evaluation semantics M\mathcal{M},
  • and failure semantics.

Sketch.
Composition and monotonicity of stewardship operators guarantee functoriality. Refinements of operators supply 2-cells. Closure S\mathcal{S} commutes suitably with restriction and fragment inclusion. Provenance and evaluation coherence follow from Theorems 18.3 and 18.4. ∎

18.5 Stability Under Closure

Theorem 18.6 (Universe stability).
For any finite composition of stewardship operators En∘⋯∘E1E_n\circ\cdots\circ E_1 and any X≤UsemioticX\le \mathcal{U}_{\mathrm{semiotic}},

S(En(⋯E1(X)⋯ ))≤Usemiotic. \mathcal{S}(E_n(\cdots E_1(X)\cdots)) \le \mathcal{U}_{\mathrm{semiotic}}.

Proof. Usemiotic\mathcal{U}_{\mathrm{semiotic}} is the least fixed point of S\mathcal{S}. Each EiE_i is an endomap on U\mathcal{U} preserving the base ambient. Monotonicity implies that repeated application of S\mathcal{S} after any composition lands at or below Usemiotic\mathcal{U}_{\mathrm{semiotic}}. ∎

19. The Stewardable Semiotic Concept Universe

We assemble all components into one object.

Definition 19.1 (Stewardable Semiotic Concept Universe).
A Stewardable Semiotic Concept Universe is the structure

USSC:=(H, j, G, Opdef, Const, AnnTerm, G, U, S, F, E→πF, P, H, μ, Stew, Part, Δann,Δfrag,Δsem, ⊖) \mathcal{U}^{\mathrm{SSC}} := \big( H,\ j,\ G,\ \mathsf{Op}^{\mathrm{def}},\ \mathsf{Const},\ \mathsf{AnnTerm},\ \mathcal{G},\ \mathcal{U},\ \mathcal{S},\ \mathcal{F},\ \mathcal{E}\xrightarrow{\pi}\mathcal{F},\ \mathcal{P},\ \mathcal{H},\ \mu,\ \mathbf{Stew},\ \mathbf{Part},\ \Delta_{\mathrm{ann}},\Delta_{\mathrm{frag}},\Delta_{\mathrm{sem}},\ \ominus \big)

equipped with:

  • a complete Heyting–modal–comonadic ambient (H,j,G)(H,j,G),
  • a family of definable operators Opdef\mathsf{Op}^{\mathrm{def}} satisfying fragment preservation and hereditary extensionality,
  • a set of concept constants Const\mathsf{Const} and annotation terms AnnTerm\mathsf{AnnTerm},
  • a groupoid of constant identity G\mathcal{G},
  • a complete lattice of partial structures U\mathcal{U} and closure S\mathcal{S},
  • a poset of fragments F\mathcal{F} and fragment-indexed total category E\mathcal{E},
  • a provenance functor P:E→Cat\mathcal{P}:\mathcal{E}\to\mathbf{Cat},
  • a semantic presheaf H:Fop→Set\mathcal{H}:\mathcal{F}^{op}\to\mathbf{Set} with sheaf semantics,
  • an evaluation monoid MM and valuation μ:H→M\mu:H\to M,
  • a monoidal category of stewardship operators Stew\mathbf{Stew} acting on Part\mathbf{Part},
  • delta and subtraction operations Δann,Δfrag,Δsem,⊖\Delta_{\mathrm{ann}},\Delta_{\mathrm{frag}},\Delta_{\mathrm{sem}},\ominus,
  • and compatibility conditions as proved in Section 18.

Theorem 19.2 (Soundness and completeness of USSC\mathcal{U}^{\mathrm{SSC}}).
USSC\mathcal{U}^{\mathrm{SSC}} is a well-defined, coherent mathematical universe supporting:

  • creation, annotation, revision, and deletion (restriction/subtraction) of semantic concepts,
  • fragmentwise and global semantic evaluation,
  • intensional provenance of concept fragments and closure steps,
  • a typed interface algebra for steward actions compiled to stewardship operators,
  • semantic difference and semantic removal,
  • and stable interaction with the underlying Semiotic Universe.

Proof. Each component is defined using structures already present or admissible in the Semiotic Universe; all interactions are verified by the coherence theorems of Section 18; closure stability guarantees universe-level consistency. ∎

20. Conclusion

We have constructed a Stewardable Semiotic Concept Universe as an extension of the Semiotic Universe with:

  • a formal annotation calculus as syntactic substrate for concept-level knowledge;
  • a groupoid of constants providing identity across revisions and encodings;
  • a layer of failure semantics that localizes and records inconsistencies;
  • provenance categories and a fibration that track the intensional history of fragments;
  • a quantitative evaluation semantics for fragments, deltas, and stewardship actions;
  • semantic delta and subtraction to compare and locally remove conceptual footprints;
  • a monoidal category of stewardship operators equipped with an explicit interface algebra;
  • a 2-category of partial structures plus a Grothendieck fibration of fragments;
  • sheaf semantics for local-to-global coherence; and
  • unified coherence theorems ensuring stability of the whole construction under closure and operator action.

The resulting structure, USSC\mathcal{U}^{\mathrm{SSC}}, is a stewardable semiotic universe of concepts: a setting in which concept-level artifacts can be created, revised, compared, subtracted, evaluated, and traced—internally, mathematically, and in a way that is compatible with external stewardship interfaces.
