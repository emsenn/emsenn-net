On Relationality

by emsenn

On Relationality

A trace of the reductive tension of nothing

Introduction

The question “Why does anything exist at all?” smuggles in two opposing recognitions: Relating (to ask) and Not Relating (to deny). These recognitions cannot both vanish, so the trace below simply records the acts forced by resolving that tension. Each subsequent section earns a new act by showing how it necessarily follows from the previous one.

Style Guide

In text, Relational Terms are written in Strong Emphasized Title Case when they are being defined, and Emphasize Title Case when they are being referenced, with a parentheticized MonospacePascalCase representation of how they will be used in our relational expression format. For example, “A Relational Term (RelationalTerm) is symbol used to represent a Recognition.” After being defined in prose, the term must be given concretely in a code block.

Every code block in this document follows a single convention: each non-empty line is either a binding Atom = Tree or a standalone Tree. Atoms are bare identifiers (letters, digits, dashes, underscores). Trees are S-expressions built from atoms and parentheses.

program  := 1*line

tree     := atom / "(" 1*tree-or-atom ")"

tree-or-atom := tree / atom

bare-atom := 1*(letter / digit / "-" / "_" / ".")
quoted-atom := "\"" *char "\""

Part I — Algebra of Relational Structure

This text presents a mathematical development of Relationality (Relationality) beginning with Relationlessness and exploring the mathematical space that unfolds. These mathematics are separated into:

  • Algebra of Relational Structure
  • Calculus of Relational Language
  • Dynamics of Relational Geometry
  • Mechanics of Relational Physics

Expressing Relationality

In this text, Relationality is represented with a “domain-specific language,” fully documented in the appendix “Relational Expressions Syntax,” but in plain terms, () hold symbols that refer to different things we’ll explore in this text, with the first symbol daying what happens, and any that might follow being what it happens to, i.e. (meet cloud sun)."

The goal of this section is to derive the core algebraic data of relationality from Relationlessness and Reflex and obtain a Relational Carrier of recognitions.

Relationlessness

Relationlessness (Relationlessness) is the void of relation. No distinctions exist yet, so reflexive activity has no content and yields no recognition.

Relationlessness

Even “having no relation” is itself a relation: it is the recognition of total absence. We write this baseline relation simply as Relationlessness, and it is the canvas on which every later distinction is painted.

Reflex

Reflex (Reflex) denotes the primitive act that draws a distinction out of Relationlessness. Each invocation produces or refines recognitions in the emergent carrier.

Reflex

The first application of Reflex to Relationlessness produces the relation of “not Relationlessness” versus “Relationlessness”; every other act of Reflex iterates that same process, reusing or partitioning the relations already in play.

Differentiation

The first invocation of Reflex draws a boundary inside Relationlessness. We record this as the Differentiate operator: it does not presuppose pre-existing entities; it simply names the marked/unmarked sides created by Reflex.

(fn Differentiate (relation-1 relation-2)
  (Reflex relation-1 relation-2))

Every further invocation of Reflex is an iteration of Differentiate, refining or reusing distinctions that already exist. In particular, (Differentiate Relationlessness relation) is simply Reflex drawing the first possible boundary: relation versus no relation. Once that initial boundary exists, Reflex can act again using either side as input; each subsequent Differentiation therefore nests or reuses earlier recognitions. This recursive reuse is what lets recognitions compare one another and ultimately organizes them into an ordered structure.

First Relation

The complementary recognition created alongside Relationlessness is the first “something.” We denote it by Relation to emphasize that even the minimal mark produced by Reflex is already a relation.

(fn Relation ()
  (Differentiate Relationlessness Relationlessness))

Because Differentiate always returns recognitions, its outputs can immediately become inputs for further differentiations. No separate iteration operator is required: Reflex simply keeps acting on the recognitions it just produced, which is why the reflexive order never stops unfolding.

Includes

Two recognitions can now be compared. We say Includes(a,b) when the act that produced a could just as well have produced b—the recursive reuse noted above. This is taken as a primitive preorder arising directly from repeated Differentiation.

Excludes

Excludes(a,b) captures incompatibility: no chain of Differentiations can produce both recognitions simultaneously. Whenever a is drawn, b must lie outside that boundary (and vice versa). Like Includes, this predicate is part of the reflexive order generated by the very first boundary.

Reflexive Order

The closure of Differentiation under Includes/Excludes yields the reflexive order that underlies the rest of the algebra. Every recognition both includes itself and excludes its negation; chains of inclusion compose transitively, and exclusion propagates backward along them. This order is the “carrier” on which further structure—meets, joins, implication, and the iterative maps appearing later—will be built.

Recognition Combinators

Repeated Differentiation allows recognitions to be combined inside the same boundary. We introduce two basic combinators:

  • Together(a,b) — the recognition carved by differentiating within both a and b. Think of it as the shared refinement where both acts hold.
  • Either(a,b) — the recognition carved by differentiating within at least one of a or b.
(fn Together (a b)
  (Differentiate a b))

(fn Either (a b)
  (Differentiate b a))

These are just two complementary ways Reflex can reuse the recognitions it already produced: one placing the mark with respect to a first, then b, and the other in the opposite order. Because the reflexive order is closed, both expressions land back in R.

Meet and Join

With Together and Either in hand we can define the usual order-theoretic combinators without appealing to new primitives:

  • Meet(a,b) is simply Together(a,b).
  • Join(a,b) is simply Either(a,b).
(fn Meet (a b)
  (Together a b))

(fn Join (a b)
  (Either a b))

These definitions ensure that every meet/join expression is literally an iterated differentiation of recognitions already in R.

Negation

Negation arises by differentiating a recognition against the baseline void. To negate a we simply run Differentiate with Relationlessness and a, which returns the “unmarked” side relative to a.

(fn Negate (a)
  (Differentiate Relationlessness a))

This definition matches the intuition that negation is “what a leaves outside the mark,” and again stays entirely within the same reflexive process.

Implication

Implication expresses the idea “given a, we can refine to obtain b.” With Meet already modeled by Together, we define implication as the recognition that, when combined with a, sits inside b.

(fn Implication (a b)
  (Differentiate a b))

Thus (Implication a b) is nothing more than the refinement you’d have to draw after marking a in order to land in b.

Quantified Recognition

Quantification is nothing more than repeatedly combining recognitions via Join/Meet. To keep the definition concrete we start with binary aggregators (larger families are obtained by nesting, since these operations are associative).

(fn Exists2 (a b)
  (Join a b))

(fn ForAll2 (a b)
  (Meet a b))

Higher-arity existentials/universals are just iterated applications of Exists2/ForAll2. The λ-calculus developed later relies on this property to encode genuine binders using only the operations we have earned so far.

Order

Order (Order) compares recognitions directly through the reflexive preorder built from Differentiation. We read x ≤ y exactly when x is included in y (i.e., x arises by differentiating inside y). Formally, Order is simply the Includes predicate.

(fn Order (x y)
  (Includes x y))

Extremes

Every differentiation reuses the two primitive recognitions born from the Relationlessness boundary. We therefore take

  • Bottom to be Relationlessness, the recognition contained in every other;
  • Top to be Relation, the recognition that contains every other.
(fn Bottom ()
  Relationlessness)

(fn Top ()
  (Relation))
