Relating

2026-03-06 The ongoing activity that holds things together. Not a state but a process — what must keep happening for something to persist.
Defines Relating

Relating

A conversation is not a thing that exists and then, separately, involves people talking. A conversation is the talking. If the turns stop being taken and understood, the conversation doesn’t pause — it ends. Relating is this kind of activity: the ongoing work that holds things together.

In relational dynamics, relating is the core process. It is not a state but an activity — something that must keep happening for the thing it sustains to persist. The stone on the desk exists through the ongoing relating of gravity, friction, and surface. The school club exists through the ongoing relating of meetings called, rooms opened, members showing up. When the relating stops, the thing it held together comes apart.

This is a stronger claim than saying “things are connected.” Connection implies entities that exist first and relate second. Relating reverses this: the entities are constituted by the relational activity. There is no stone-on-desk without the gravity-friction-surface relation. There is no club without the meeting-room-member relation. What exists, exists through relating.

Formal expression

In the Lean formalization, relating is not itself formalized as a single operation — it is what the entire RecognitionField structure models. The partial order (Including), the lattice operations (Togethering, Eithering), and the implication (Inducing) are all aspects of relational activity. Relating is the activity; the Heyting algebra is its structure.

In the Interactive Semioverse, relating corresponds to interaction: the evaluation of interaction terms against the semantic field. An interaction term that is evaluated produces semantic content (a generator in the Heyting algebra). The temporal trace operator G records the history of relational activity — what has happened, preserved across time.
