How to make text good

This is a procedure. Follow the steps in order on any text you write or edit. The standard is plain American English as defined by the Federal Plain Language Guidelines (plainlanguage.gov), adapted for mathematical and technical content.

The goal is clarity, specificity, and plainness. Not elegance. Not style. The reader must understand every sentence on the first reading. The Plain Writing Act of 2010 defines the standard: “writing that is clear, concise, well-organized, and follows other best practices appropriate to the subject or field and intended audience.”

The test for success: the reader can find, understand, and act on the information the first time they read it. If they must re-read a sentence, the sentence has failed.

The four word-choice principles (Fowler, adopted by PLAIN)

These four rules from H.W. Fowler’s The King’s English (1906) are the foundation of every word-level decision in this procedure:

  1. Prefer the familiar word to the far-fetched.
  2. Prefer the concrete word to the abstraction.
  3. Prefer the single word to the circumlocution.
  4. Prefer the short word to the long.

Step 1: Cut what carries no weight

Read each sentence. Ask: if I delete this sentence, does the reader lose information? If no, delete the sentence.

Do the same for each paragraph. Then each section.

What to delete

Meta-commentary. Sentences that describe what you are about to say.

  • Delete: “In this section, we will discuss the properties of the nucleus.”
  • Keep: “The nucleus has three properties.”

Restatement. Sentences that say what the previous sentence already said, in different words.

  • Delete the second one.
  • If the second one is better, delete the first one.

Universal truths. Sentences that could appear in any paper on any topic.

  • Delete: “This is a well-studied area with a rich history.”
  • Delete: “The results have significant implications.”
  • Delete: “Understanding X is crucial for Y.”
  • Test: change the subject of the paper. Does the sentence still work? Then it carries no content.

Protective hedges. Qualifications that protect the author rather than inform the reader.

  • Delete: “It could be argued that…”
  • Delete: “One might consider the possibility that…”
  • Delete: “It is perhaps not unreasonable to suggest that…”
  • If you have evidence, state the claim.
  • If you lack evidence, say what evidence is missing.
  • Do not hedge.

Empty transitions. Phrases that signal sequence without carrying content.

  • Delete: “With that in mind,”
  • Delete: “Building on this foundation,”
  • Delete: “Having established this,”
  • Delete: “Turning now to,”
  • If the next paragraph follows logically, no transition is needed.
  • If it does not follow logically, a transition phrase will not fix that.

Throat-clearing openers. Sentences that warm up before saying the thing.

  • Delete: “Before we can understand X, we need to consider Y.”
  • Write instead: the thing about Y that matters, then X.

Question-then-answer hooks. Rhetorical questions used as structural devices.

  • Delete: “What makes this construction so powerful? It is the fact that…”
  • Write instead: “This construction is powerful because…”
  • Better: “The construction does [specific thing].” Let the reader judge power.

Binary contrast framing. “It’s not about X, it’s about Y.”

  • Write instead: “It is about Y.” The negation of a claim the reader was not making adds nothing.

How much to cut

A first draft typically needs 20-40% of its words removed. If you cannot find anything to cut, you are not looking hard enough.

After cutting, read the result. If it still makes sense and no information is lost, the cut was correct. If information was lost, the sentence was not filler — restore it.

Rules for word choice

Prefer Anglo-Saxon roots. If a word has a Latin or Greek root and an Anglo-Saxon synonym exists, prefer the Anglo-Saxon word. “Begin” over “commence.” “End” over “terminate.” “Understand” over “comprehend.”

Use “must” for requirements. Not “shall.” The federal plain language guidelines specifically mandate this. “Must” is unambiguous. “Shall” has different meanings to lawyers, engineers, and laypeople.

  • “must” = required
  • “must not” = prohibited
  • “may” = permitted
  • “should” = recommended but not required
  • “should not” = not recommended but not prohibited

Use positive language. State what to do, not what not to do.

  • Bad: “Do not use negative constructions.”
  • Good: “Use positive constructions.”
  • Bad: “The map is not discontinuous.”
  • Good: “The map is continuous.”
  • Exception: prohibitions (“Do not start a sentence with a symbol”) are naturally negative. Do not contort them into positive form.

Avoid double negatives.

  • “not unlikely” → “likely”
  • “not uncommon” → “common”
  • “not without merit” → “has merit”
  • “cannot be avoided” → “is necessary”
  • “not dissimilar” → “similar”

Avoid hidden verbs (nominalizations). Watch for nouns ending in -ment, -tion, -sion, -ance, -ence, -ity. If a verb form exists, use it.

  • “make an adjustment” → “adjust”
  • “make a decision” → “decide”
  • “give consideration to” → “consider”
  • “provide a description of” → “describe”
  • “reach a conclusion” → “conclude”
  • “make an assumption” → “assume”
  • “perform an analysis” → “analyze”
  • “conduct an investigation” → “investigate”
  • “make a determination” → “determine”
  • “have a preference for” → “prefer”
  • “make a recommendation” → “recommend”
  • “give an indication” → “indicate”
  • “take into consideration” → “consider”
  • “effect a transformation” → “transform”
  • “achieve a reduction” → “reduce”

Avoid noun strings. When three or more nouns stack up as modifiers, the reader cannot tell which modifies which.

  • Bad: “fiber nuclear language modal operator composition rule”
  • Good: “the rule for composing modal operators in the fiber nuclear language”
  • Bad: “category theory sheaf cohomology computation”
  • Good: “computing sheaf cohomology using category theory”

Break noun strings by adding prepositions, articles, or by restructuring.

Avoid here-/there-/where- words.

  • “hereby” → delete or “by this”
  • “therein” → “in it” or “in that”
  • “thereof” → “of it” or “of that”
  • “wherein” → “in which” or “where”
  • “wherefore” → “why” or “for this reason”
  • “hereafter” → “from now on” or “after this”
  • “heretofore” → “until now”
  • “thereafter” → “after that”

These words are legal archaisms. They sound formal without adding clarity.

Never define a word to mean something other than its commonly accepted meaning. If you need a technical term, introduce it as one. Do not redefine an ordinary word. “In this paper, ’large’ means ‘having more than seven elements’” — do not do this. Use a different word or phrase.

Exception: terms of art. Technical terms keep their precise meaning. “Presheaf,” “functor,” “nucleus,” “topos,” “sheaf,” “Heyting algebra” — these are the right words. Do not simplify them. Do not substitute synonyms. A technical term used consistently is clearer than a plain word used imprecisely.

One word per concept. Pick one term for each concept and use it every time. Never alternate between synonyms for the same thing. If you call it a “restriction map” in paragraph one, do not call it a “pullback morphism” in paragraph two unless it is actually a different thing.

No elegant variation. Repetition of the correct term is clearer than cycling through synonyms. “The functor… the functor… the functor” is better than “the functor… the mapping… the correspondence.” The reader knows what “the functor” refers to. The reader must now check whether “the mapping” is the same object or a different one.

No jargon outside its field. Do not borrow technical-sounding words from other fields to sound precise. “Leverage” is a financial term. “Ecosystem” is a biological term. “Bandwidth” is an engineering term. Use them only when you mean the technical thing.

No nominalizations when a verb exists. A nominalization turns a verb into a noun, making the sentence longer and weaker. This rule overlaps with the hidden verbs rule above. Apply both. Watch for nouns ending in -ment, -tion, -sion, -ance, -ence, -ity that have verb forms.

  • Bad: “We performed an analysis of the data.”
  • Good: “We analyzed the data.”
  • Bad: “The implementation of the algorithm was completed.”
  • Good: “We implemented the algorithm.”
  • Bad: “The establishment of a connection between X and Y…”
  • Good: “X connects to Y.”
  • Bad: “The utilization of the functor results in a simplification.”
  • Good: “Using the functor simplifies the result.”
  • Bad: “There was a discussion of the problem.”
  • Good: “We discussed the problem.”

Step 3: Shorten sentences

Target: 15-20 words per sentence on average. No sentence over 35 words.

Techniques for shortening

Split at conjunctions. If a sentence has “and,” “but,” “which,” “while,” “although,” or “whereas,” try splitting it into two sentences.

  • Before: “The functor preserves limits, and its left adjoint preserves colimits, which together imply that the adjunction is exact.”
  • After: “The functor preserves limits. Its left adjoint preserves colimits. Together, these facts imply the adjunction is exact.”

Move conditions to the front.

  • Before: “Click the button to save your work.”
  • After: “To save your work, click the button.”
  • Before: “The map is continuous if the preimage of every open set is open.”
  • After: “If the preimage of every open set is open, the map is continuous.”

This matters because the reader evaluates conditions first. Putting them at the end forces re-reading.

Remove prepositional chains. Each “of” adds a level of nesting.

  • Before: “the role of the contribution of the author to the development of the theory”
  • After: “what the author contributed to the theory”
  • Before: “the implementation of the computation of the cohomology of the sheaf”
  • After: “computing the sheaf’s cohomology”

Remove “there is / there are.”

  • Before: “There are three properties that the nucleus satisfies.”
  • After: “The nucleus satisfies three properties.”
  • Before: “There is a natural transformation that connects the two functors.”
  • After: “A natural transformation connects the two functors.”

Remove “the fact that.”

  • “due to the fact that” → “because”
  • “in light of the fact that” → “because” or “since”
  • “the fact that he arrived” → “his arrival” or “that he arrived”

Remove “it is…that” constructions.

  • Before: “It is the nucleus that determines the topology.”
  • After: “The nucleus determines the topology.”
  • Before: “It is clear that the map is injective.”
  • After: “The map is injective.” (If it is clear, you do not need to say so.)

Remove doubled verbs.

  • “review and evaluate” → “evaluate” (evaluation includes review)
  • “monitor and track” → “track”
  • “describe and explain” → “explain” (explanation includes description)

Remove doubled modifiers.

  • “full and complete” → “complete”
  • “each and every” → “every”
  • “first and foremost” → “first”
  • “various and sundry” → “various”
  • “null and void” → “void”
  • “basic and fundamental” → “fundamental”

Step 4: Use active voice

Name the actor. Put the actor first. Subject-verb-object word order.

Active voice substitution table

Write (active)Not (passive)
The functor preserves limitsLimits are preserved by the functor
We define X as…X is defined as…
Smith proved this in 1987This was proved in 1987
The restriction map sends x to yx is sent to y by the restriction map
The nucleus generates the topologyThe topology is generated by the nucleus
The theorem requires three conditionsThree conditions are required by the theorem
The proof uses induction on depthInduction on depth is used in the proof

When passive voice is acceptable

  • The actor is genuinely unknown: “The theorem was first stated in the 18th century.”
  • The actor is irrelevant and naming it would distract: “The integral is evaluated over the boundary.” (Who evaluates it does not matter.)
  • The action matters more than the actor: “Errors were found in the proof.” (The finding matters; who found them does not.)

In all other cases, name the actor and use active voice.

How to find passive voice

Look for forms of “to be” followed by a past participle:

  • is/are/was/were/been/being + [verb]ed
  • “is defined,” “was shown,” “are preserved,” “has been established”

For each one, ask: who or what does this action? Put that actor as the subject.

Target

Under 10% of sentences in passive voice. Mathematical prose naturally uses more passive (“let X be defined…”) so up to 15% is acceptable in definition-heavy sections, but prefer “define X as…” when possible.

Step 5: Make every pronoun point to one thing

Read every “it,” “this,” “that,” “these,” “those,” “they,” “them,” “which,” “who,” “its,” “their.” For each one, identify the single word it replaces.

Rules

If two or more nouns could be the referent, replace the pronoun with the noun.

  • Bad: “The functor sends the presheaf to its image. This is important.” (This = the functor? the presheaf? the image? the sending?)
  • Good: “The functor sends the presheaf to its image. The image is important.”

Never start a sentence with “This” or “It” without a noun after it.

  • Bad: “This is a consequence of the previous lemma.”
  • Good: “This equality is a consequence of the previous lemma.”
  • Bad: “It follows from the definition.”
  • Good: “The bound follows from the definition.”

“Which” must have a clear antecedent immediately before it.

  • Bad: “We apply the functor to the presheaf over the site, which is continuous.” (Which is continuous — the functor, the presheaf, or the site?)
  • Good: “The functor is continuous. We apply it to the presheaf over the site.”

Avoid “former” and “latter.” The reader must scan backward to figure out which is which. Use the nouns.

Avoid stacked pronouns. If a sentence has two different “it"s referring to different things, rewrite.

  • Bad: “If it satisfies it, the map is an embedding.”
  • Good: “If the map satisfies the condition, the map is an embedding.”

Step 6: Put the point first

Paragraph structure

First sentence of every paragraph states the claim, conclusion, or main point. Following sentences provide evidence, detail, or explanation.

Do not build up to a conclusion. State the conclusion, then show why it holds. The reader must know after the first sentence what the paragraph is about and what it claims.

  • Bad: “There are many approaches to defining a topology on a category. Some use covering sieves. Others use Grothendieck pretopologies. After careful consideration, the most natural choice for our purposes is the canonical topology.”
  • Good: “The canonical topology is the right choice here. It arises naturally from the covering sieves we already have, and it avoids the extra data of a pretopology.”

Section structure

First paragraph of every section states what the section establishes and why it matters. Following paragraphs do the work.

The reader must be able to read only the first sentence of every paragraph in a section and understand the section’s argument.

Definition/theorem structure

State the purpose before the formal content.

  • Bad: “Definition. A nucleus on a frame LL is a map j:L→Lj: L \to L satisfying…”
  • Good: “A nucleus captures the data of a sublocale — a quotient frame in disguise. Definition. A nucleus on a frame LL is a map j:L→Lj: L \to L satisfying…”

The one-sentence motivation before the formal statement tells the reader what to look for in the definition.

Step 7: Use parallel structure

Rules

Items in a list must have the same grammatical form. All nouns, all verb phrases, all complete sentences. Do not mix.

  • Bad list:

    • The map is injective
    • Preservation of order
    • Has a left inverse that is surjective

  • Good list (all sentences):

    • The map is injective.
    • The map preserves order.
    • The map has a surjective left inverse.

  • Good list (all noun phrases):

    • injectivity
    • order-preservation
    • left-invertibility with surjective inverse

Paired structures must match.

  • Bad: “The construction is both simple and has elegance.”
  • Good: “The construction is both simple and elegant.”
  • Bad: “We prove that X is true and the validity of Y.”
  • Good: “We prove that X is true and that Y is valid.”

Comparisons must compare like with like.

  • Bad: “The cohomology of X is larger than Y.” (Compares cohomology to an object.)
  • Good: “The cohomology of X is larger than the cohomology of Y.”

Series must use consistent articles and prepositions.

  • Bad: “the functor preserves limits, colimits, and the exponentials”
  • Good: “the functor preserves limits, colimits, and exponentials”
  • Or: “the functor preserves the limits, the colimits, and the exponentials”

Step 8: Handle mathematical content

These rules apply to text that mixes prose and formal mathematics.

Notation rules (Halmos, Knuth, Conrad, Serre)

Never start a sentence with a symbol.

  • Bad: “xx is positive.”
  • Good: “The element xx is positive.”
  • Good: “Since xx is positive, the bound holds.”

Never place two symbols next to each other without words between them.

  • Bad: “mm nn-element sets”
  • Good: “mm sets, each with nn elements”
  • Bad: “Consider ff gg-equivariant.”
  • Good: “Consider the map ff, which is gg-equivariant.”

Do not separate two displayed formulas with only punctuation. Put words between them.

  • Bad: f(x)=x2,f(x) = x^2, g(x)=x3.g(x) = x^3.
  • Good: f(x)=x2,f(x) = x^2, and its companion is g(x)=x3.g(x) = x^3.

Use English words for logical connectives in prose. Write “for every,” “there exists,” “implies,” “if and only if.” Reserve ∀\forall, ∃\exists, ⇒\Rightarrow,   ⟺  \iff for displayed formal statements.

Define every symbol before or at the point of first use. No symbol may appear without the reader knowing what kind of object it is. Not two pages before. Not in a “Preliminaries” section at the start. At the point of use.

Use minimal notation. If a symbol is introduced and never referenced again, do not name it. “Every differentiable function is continuous” is better than “Every differentiable function ff is continuous” when ff never reappears.

Do not freeze letters needlessly. If you write “xyzxyz-space,” you have burned three letters. Spend time choosing your alphabet (Halmos). Use letters that match convention: nn for naturals, pp for primes, ε\varepsilon for small quantities, ff for functions.

Not all formulas are equations. Use the correct term: definition, identity, inequality, relation, recurrence, expression. Calling everything an “equation” is imprecise (Knuth).

Exposition rules (Tao, Halmos, Bertsekas)

Give a concrete example immediately after a definition. The reader needs something concrete to anchor the abstraction. If you cannot give an example, the definition may be wrong.

State the special case before the general case. The special case is easier to understand. The general case then extends it. The reader sees the pattern before the machinery.

Signal register shifts explicitly. When moving from formal to informal: “Informally, this says…” When moving back: “More precisely…” Do not let the reader guess which register you are in.

Each unit of text has one job. A definition block defines. A theorem block states a claim. A proof block proves it. A remark block gives context. Do not mix these. If a proof contains a definition, extract the definition.

Write for your past self. Take as your model reader a version of yourself from twelve months ago, when you were first learning the material. Write to address the confusions that person had (Tao).

Stop when done. When the proof is complete, stop. Do not add a paragraph summarizing what the proof showed. The theorem already stated the result (Halmos).

Prefer direct proof. Do not prove by contradiction when a direct proof is available. Contradiction is acceptable when the direct path is genuinely harder or when the statement is naturally negative. Otherwise, direct proof is clearer (Knuth).

Maintain consistent register within a paragraph. A paragraph that begins with formal notation and ends with “so basically it all works out” is inconsistent in register. Each paragraph must maintain one register. Shift between paragraphs, not within them.

Intuition comes first, formalism comes second. Motivate with intuition, then give the precise statement, then optionally return to explain what the formalism “really means.” Tao’s three stages: pre-rigorous → rigorous → post-rigorous. The post-rigorous stage — where “every heuristic argument naturally suggests its rigorous counterpart” — is the expository goal.

Step 9: Remove slop markers

Search the text for every phrase in this list. Delete the phrase or rewrite the sentence to say the thing directly.

Throat-clearing phrases (delete entirely)

  • “it’s worth noting that”
  • “it should be noted that”
  • “it is important to understand that”
  • “it is interesting to observe that”
  • “it is important to emphasize that”
  • “we would like to point out that”
  • “it bears mentioning that”
  • “it goes without saying that” (then don’t say it)
  • “needless to say” (then don’t say it)
  • “as we shall see”
  • “as mentioned earlier”
  • “as previously discussed”
  • “as noted above”

Decorative adjectives (delete or replace with specifics)

  • “comprehensive” → say what is covered
  • “robust” → say what failure modes are handled
  • “elegant” → delete (let the reader judge)
  • “beautiful” → delete
  • “powerful” → say what it can do
  • “crucial” → “important” or say why it matters
  • “vital” → “important” or say why it matters
  • “seamless” → say what is integrated with what
  • “cutting-edge” → delete
  • “game-changing” → delete
  • “groundbreaking” → delete
  • “novel” → say what is new
  • “innovative” → say what is new
  • “state-of-the-art” → delete or cite the comparison
  • “sophisticated” → delete or say what makes it complex
  • “rich” (as in “rich structure”) → say what the structure contains
  • “deep” (as in “deep connection”) → say what the connection is
  • “natural” (as in “natural construction”) → acceptable in mathematics where “natural” has technical meaning; delete when used decoratively
  • “interesting” → delete (if it were not interesting, you would not write about it)
  • “remarkable” → delete or say what is surprising
  • “striking” → delete or say what is unexpected
  • “fundamental” → acceptable when it means “at the foundation”; delete when it means “important”

AI-tell transitions (replace or restructure)

  • “Furthermore,” → “and,” “also,” or start a new paragraph
  • “Moreover,” → same
  • “Additionally,” → same
  • “Indeed,” → delete or start with the claim
  • “In fact,” → delete or start with the claim
  • “Notably,” → delete and state the thing
  • “Interestingly,” → delete and state the thing
  • “Importantly,” → delete and state the thing
  • “Specifically,” → often deletable; if needed, just state the specific thing
  • “Essentially,” → delete and state the thing
  • “Basically,” → delete and state the thing
  • “Ultimately,” → delete and state the thing

AI-tell structural phrases (delete and restructure)

  • “Let’s explore” → delete, just do it
  • “Let’s delve into” → delete
  • “Let’s dive deep into” → delete
  • “Let’s take a closer look at” → delete
  • “In conclusion” → delete if the conclusion is obvious from context
  • “In summary” → delete if the summary is obvious from context
  • “To summarize” → delete
  • “In this section, we will” → delete and start the section
  • “This section discusses” → delete and start discussing
  • “We now turn to” → delete and turn to it
  • “We are now in a position to” → delete and do it
  • “Having established X, we can now” → delete and do it
  • “With this in mind” → delete
  • “Given the above” → delete
  • “Building on this” → delete
  • “It is not just about X, it is about Y” → “It is about Y”
  • “This is where X comes in” → delete and introduce X

AI-tell intensifiers (delete)

  • “very” → delete or use a stronger word (“very large” → “large” or a number)
  • “really” → delete
  • “quite” → delete
  • “extremely” → delete or use a specific measure
  • “highly” → delete or use a specific measure
  • “incredibly” → delete
  • “absolutely” → delete
  • “totally” → delete
  • “simply” → delete (especially “simply put”)
  • “just” → delete when it means “only” decoratively
  • “actually” → delete when it does not contrast with a prior claim
  • “literally” → delete when it does not mean “literally”

Patronizing phrases (delete)

  • “obviously”
  • “of course”
  • “clearly”
  • “as everyone knows”
  • “it is easy to see that”
  • “trivially”
  • “a simple calculation shows”

If it is obvious, the reader does not need to be told. If it is not obvious, calling it obvious insults the reader.

Exception: in mathematical proofs, “clearly” and “trivially” may signal that a step is routine and the author is not providing details. This is acceptable if the step is genuinely routine. But prefer “by direct computation” or “by inspection” — these tell the reader what to do rather than what to feel.

Vague quantifiers (replace with specifics)

  • “some” → which ones?
  • “many” → how many?
  • “several” → how many?
  • “various” → which ones?
  • “a number of” → how many?
  • “a variety of” → which ones?
  • “numerous” → how many?
  • “a significant amount of” → how much?

If you cannot give a specific number, ask whether the quantity matters. If it does not matter, delete the quantifier.

Redundant phrases (compress)

  • “in order to” → “to”
  • “for the purpose of” → “to” or “for”
  • “with a view to” → “to”
  • “due to the fact that” → “because”
  • “owing to the fact that” → “because”
  • “in light of the fact that” → “because” or “since”
  • “on the grounds that” → “because”
  • “as a consequence of” → “because” or delete
  • “as a result of” → “because” or delete
  • “at this point in time” → “now”
  • “at the present time” → “now”
  • “in the near future” → “soon”
  • “in the event that” → “if”
  • “in cases where” → “when” or “if”
  • “with regard to” → “about”
  • “with reference to” → “about”
  • “in terms of” → “in” or “for” or rewrite
  • “on a daily basis” → “daily”
  • “take into consideration” → “consider”
  • “has the ability to” → “can”
  • “is able to” → “can”
  • “is in a position to” → “can”
  • “it is possible that” → “may” or “might”
  • “there is a possibility that” → “may”
  • “the question as to whether” → “whether”
  • “this is a subject that” → delete
  • “he is a person who” → delete

Copula avoidance patterns (AI-specific)

AI-generated text avoids the word “is” by substituting longer phrases. Reverse this.

  • “serves as a” → “is a”
  • “stands as a testament to” → “shows” or “proves”
  • “functions as” → “is”
  • “acts as” → “is”
  • “represents” (when it means “is”) → “is”
  • “constitutes” (when it means “is”) → “is”
  • “plays a [significant/crucial/vital] role in” → “[verb]s” (name the action)

Step 10: Organize the document

These rules apply to the document as a whole, above the paragraph level.

Information order

State the purpose and bottom line first. The first paragraph of any document states what the document is about and what the reader must take from it. Do not save the conclusion for the end.

Order by reader importance, not by writer logic. Put the information the reader needs most at the top. Background, history, and methodology go toward the end. The reader came with a question. Answer it before explaining how you arrived at the answer.

For processes, use chronological order. If the reader must do steps in sequence, present them in that sequence. Number the steps. Do not describe step three before step one.

Group related information. All information about one topic belongs in one place. Do not scatter related facts across multiple sections. If the reader must assemble information from three locations to understand one thing, the document has failed.

Put definitions at the point of use. Do not front-load a “Preliminaries” or “Definitions” section unless every definition in it is needed before the first substantive paragraph. Introduce each definition where it first matters. If you must have a definition section (for reference), put it at the end, not the beginning.

Headings

Use headings to break up every section longer than three paragraphs. The reader uses headings to scan and skip. A page without headings is a wall.

Headings must be specific. “Background” is too vague. “How the nucleus generates a topology” tells the reader what the section contains.

Use sentence case. Capitalize only the first word and proper nouns. “Properties of the nucleus” not “Properties of the Nucleus.”

Heading levels must nest. An H3 must be inside an H2. An H4 must be inside an H3. Do not skip levels.

Lists and tables

Use lists when presenting three or more items, options, conditions, or steps. A list is easier to scan than a sentence with commas.

Use numbered lists for sequences and ordered items. Use bulleted lists for unordered items.

Use tables when comparing two or more things across the same set of attributes. A table is easier to scan than prose that says “X has property A but not B, while Y has property B but not A.”

Each list item must be a complete thought. Do not split one idea across two list items. Do not cram two ideas into one list item.

White space and visual structure

Short sections are more approachable than long ones. A section longer than one screen (roughly 30 lines of prose) must be split.

Separate sections with blank lines or horizontal rules. Visual breaks signal topic shifts.

Do not use bold for emphasis in running prose. Bold is for headings, terms being defined, and labels. If a sentence needs emphasis, rewrite it to put the important word in a prominent position (usually the subject or the first word).

Use a term before its definition as common words, not a proper name. If a term has not yet been defined in the current scope, write it in lowercase without special formatting. “You are an agent in a flatfile agential resource system” is correct when the term has not yet been introduced. “You are an agent in a Flatfile Agential Resource System” treats the term as a memorizable label before the reader knows what it means. The lowercase form forces the reader to parse the words and construct meaning. The capitalized form lets the reader skip past it as a proper noun. Once a term has been defined — at its point of introduction in a spec, the first occurrence with a link, or in a “What this is” section — use the canonical form thereafter.

Step 11: Structure sentences and paragraphs for clarity

Sentence structure rules

Subject-verb-object word order. Standard English word order. No inversions for style.

One idea per sentence. If a sentence has more than two commas, try splitting it.

Keep modifiers adjacent to what they modify.

  • Bad: “Only the theorem requires three conditions.” (Does “only” modify “the theorem” or “three”?)
  • Good: “The theorem requires only three conditions.”
  • Bad: “We almost proved every case.”
  • Good: “We proved almost every case.”

Include articles. Write “Verify that the migration succeeded,” not “Verify migration succeeded.” Write “the functor,” not just “functor,” unless you are stating a general claim about all functors.

Imperative mood for instructions. “Run the command.” Not “You should run the command.” Not “The command should be run.”

Present tense by default. “This command creates a file.” Not “This command will create a file.” Future tense is for events that have not happened yet and whose timing matters.

Conditions before instructions. “To save your work, click Save.” Not “Click Save to save your work.” “If the map is injective, the kernel is trivial.” Not “The kernel is trivial if the map is injective.” The reader evaluates conditions first.

Paragraph structure rules

One topic per paragraph. If the topic shifts, start a new paragraph.

First sentence carries the claim. Remaining sentences support it. The reader must be able to read first sentences only and follow the argument.

Paragraphs must not exceed 8 sentences. If a paragraph is longer, split it at a natural break.

Delete paragraphs that can be deleted. If removing a paragraph does not reduce the reader’s understanding, it was filler.

Section structure rules

Each section establishes one thing. If a section does two things, split it.

Sections do not need introductory paragraphs. Start with content. If the section heading says “Properties of the Nucleus,” begin with the first property.

Sections do not need concluding paragraphs. End with the last piece of content. If the reader needs a summary, the section is too long or too disorganized.

Step 12: Verify formatting and mechanics

Sentence per source line. In Markdown source, put each sentence on its own line. This makes diffs cleaner and forces sentence-level attention during review. Do not wrap mid-sentence.

Serial comma. Use the Oxford comma: “limits, colimits, and exponentials.”

No contractions in formal text. Write “do not,” “cannot,” “is not.” Not “don’t,” “can’t,” “isn’t.”

Hyphens for compound modifiers before nouns. “Well-known theorem” but “the theorem is well known.” “Left-adjoint functor” but “the functor is left adjoint.”

No Latin abbreviations in running prose. Write “that is” not “i.e.” Write “for example” not “e.g.” Write “compare” not “cf.” Write “and others” or list them, not “et al.” (acceptable in citations). Write “and so on” not “etc.”

Numbers. Spell out numbers zero through nine. Use digits for 10 and above. Exception: always use digits with units (“3 cm,” “7 dimensions”).

Headings in sentence case. Capitalize only the first word and proper nouns. “Properties of the nucleus” not “Properties of the Nucleus.”

Em dashes. Use sparingly. AI-generated text overuses em dashes where commas or periods work. If you have more than one em dash per paragraph, replace most with commas, periods, or parentheses.

Parentheses. Use for genuinely parenthetical information — material that could be removed without changing the sentence’s meaning. If the parenthetical is important, promote it to its own sentence. Do not nest parentheses.

Semicolons. Use to join two closely related independent clauses. Do not use as a general-purpose connector. If the two clauses are not closely related, use a period. Limit to one semicolon per paragraph.

Exclamation marks. Do not use in technical or mathematical writing.

Quotation marks. Use double quotes for direct quotation. Use single quotes only for quotes within quotes. Use neither for emphasis — that is what italics are for, and even italics for emphasis should be rare.

Acronyms. Spell out on first use with the acronym in parentheses: “the Fiber Nuclear Language (FNL).” After first use, use the acronym. If the acronym appears fewer than three times, do not introduce it — spell out each time. Do not create acronyms that your reader will not recognize.

Citations and cross-references. Use markdown links: [unit-id](relative/path/to/file.md). Do not use footnote-style references that force the reader to jump to the bottom of the page.

Step 13: Read it out loud

Read the text out loud. Where you stumble, the sentence is too long or too tangled. Rewrite those sentences.

Where you get bored, the text is repeating itself or saying nothing. Cut those passages.

Where your voice drops because the sentence is routine and expected, the sentence may be filler. Check it against the deletion test.

Step 14: Final tests

For each sentence, it must pass all of these:

  1. Falsifiability test. Could this sentence be wrong about something specific? If it could appear unchanged in a paper about a completely different topic, it carries no content. Delete it.

  2. Deletion test. If you remove this sentence, does the reader lose information they need? If no, delete it.

  3. Actor test. Does this sentence name who or what performs the action? If not, rewrite in active voice.

  4. Ambiguity test. Does every pronoun have exactly one possible referent? If not, replace it with the noun.

  5. Word test. Is every word the simplest word that means what you mean? If not, substitute.

  6. Commitment test. Does this sentence commit to a claim that could be wrong? Or does it hedge into unfalsifiability? If it hedges, either commit or delete.

  7. Reader test. Would your past self — the version of you from twelve months ago who was learning this material — understand this sentence on first reading? If not, rewrite.

  8. Specificity test. Does this sentence say something specific to this topic? Or is it generic enough to appear in any text? If generic, delete.

Checklist (consolidated)

Use this checklist after completing all 14 steps. Every item must be true.

Organization

  • The purpose and bottom line appear in the first paragraph
  • Information is ordered by importance to the reader
  • Related information is grouped together
  • Sections are broken up with headings
  • Headings are specific and in sentence case
  • Lists and tables are used where appropriate
  • Definitions appear at point of first use

Words

  • Common, everyday words are used throughout
  • “Must” is used for requirements, not “shall”
  • Positive language is used (not double negatives)
  • No hidden verbs (nominalizations with verb alternatives)
  • No noun strings of three or more nouns
  • No jargon used outside its field
  • No here-/there-/where- words
  • One term per concept, used consistently
  • Technical terms are defined at first use
  • No word is defined to mean something other than its common meaning

Sentences

  • Average sentence length is 15-20 words
  • No sentence exceeds 35 words
  • Each sentence expresses one idea
  • Active voice is used (under 10% passive)
  • Present tense is used where possible
  • Subject is close to its verb
  • Modifiers are adjacent to what they modify
  • Parallel construction is used in lists and series
  • Conditions come before instructions
  • Articles are included (“the functor,” not “functor”)

Paragraphs

  • Each paragraph covers one topic
  • First sentence states the claim
  • No paragraph exceeds 5 sentences (8 absolute maximum)
  • No paragraph restates the previous paragraph

Mathematical content

  • No sentence starts with a symbol
  • No two symbols are adjacent without words between them
  • Logical connectives are written as English words in prose
  • Every symbol is defined at or before first use
  • Examples follow definitions
  • Special cases precede general cases
  • Register shifts are signaled explicitly
  • Each block (definition, theorem, proof, remark) has one job

Slop

  • No throat-clearing phrases
  • No decorative adjectives
  • No AI-tell transitions
  • No AI-tell structural phrases
  • No vague quantifiers (or they are justified)
  • No copula avoidance patterns
  • No patronizing phrases
  • Every sentence passes the falsifiability test
  • Every sentence passes the deletion test

Formatting

  • One sentence per source line in Markdown
  • Serial (Oxford) comma used
  • No contractions in formal text
  • Compound modifiers hyphenated before nouns
  • No Latin abbreviations in running prose
  • Numbers zero through nine spelled out
  • Acronyms spelled out on first use
  • Terms used before definition are lowercase common words, not capitalized proper names
  • Em dashes used sparingly (0-1 per paragraph)
  • No exclamation marks
