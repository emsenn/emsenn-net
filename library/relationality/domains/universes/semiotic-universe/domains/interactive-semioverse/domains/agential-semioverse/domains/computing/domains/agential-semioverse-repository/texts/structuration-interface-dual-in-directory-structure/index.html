The structuration-interface dual in directory structure

2026-03-09 by emsenn, claude
Abstract

Analysis of how the structuration-interface dual from semiotic-endeavor theory informs the directory organization of an agential semioverse repository.

Table of contents

The structuration-interface dual (defined in semiotic-endeavor) shows a recurring pattern: things have an internal face (what they are on their own terms) and an external face (how they meet the world). Method and practice. Theory and specification. Repository and interaction surface. Project and operation.

This text analyzes how that dual should inform the directory structure of an agential semioverse repository.

The problem with specifications/

The repository previously treated specifications as a content type — a category of Thing that lives in specifications/ directories. Semiotic-markdown was a specification-Thing. Semiotic-versioning was a specification-Thing. This created a parallel namespace: the conceptual content lived in one place (concepts/, theory/) and its normative requirements lived in another (specifications/).

But this split is the structuration-interface dual rendered as a directory split — and rendered badly. The dual is a property of each Thing, not a property of a directory. Every Thing has a structuration face and an interface face. Splitting them into separate directories makes it harder to see the whole Thing.

Specification as practice face

The resolution: a specification is the practice face of a Thing. It lives inside the Thing’s directory at practices/specification.md. The Thing’s index.md is the structuration face (what it IS); the specification is the interface face (what implementations MUST DO).

This is not merely organizational. It changes what it means to write a specification. Under the old model, writing a specification was creating a new Thing. Under the new model, writing a specification is developing an existing Thing — adding its practice face. The Thing comes first; the specification emerges when the Thing matures enough to need normative requirements.

This progression is natural: you develop a concept, then you develop its vocabulary, then you write about it, then you specify what conformance to it requires. The specification is the last stage of a Thing’s development, not a parallel creation.

Directory types as dual-encoded

The standard cross-cutting subdirectories split into two realms:

Structuration (internal, self-organized):

  • terms/ — the vocabulary
  • concepts/ — the ideas
  • methods/ — how knowledge is produced
  • standards/ — conventions used or created

Interface (external, world-meeting):

  • practices/ — concrete doing, including conformance requirements
  • skills/ — codified capabilities
  • research/ — knowledge intake from external sources
  • learning/ — knowledge output as accessible exposition

texts/ spans both realms — some texts analyze (structuration), some expose (interface). It remains as a default for content that does not clearly belong to research/ or learning/.

The created-here principle

Standards (conventions) created within a Thing go under that Thing’s standards/ directory, not under the discipline they would conventionally belong to. Semiotic-markdown is a standard created by the agential semioverse, so it lives at agential-semioverse/standards/semiotic-markdown/.

This follows the core placement principle (files go with the most specific Thing they relate to) applied to created Things. The most specific Thing that semiotic-markdown relates to is the agential semioverse — which is what enables its creation.

The same principle places neuroreflexivity under relationality rather than under psychology: it is derived from relationality, and that derivational relationship is more specific than its disciplinary category.

Conventions adopted from outside (like github-flavored-markdown, if documented) go under their conventional discipline because the most specific Thing they relate to is the external Thing they describe.

Implications for the three-level naming convention

Decision 0005 established three naming levels: term (the thing itself), semiotic-term (the convention), semiotic-term-specification (the conformance requirements). The third level was a separate Thing.

Under the new model, the third level collapses into practices/specification.md within the semiotic-term directory. The three levels become:

  1. term — the thing itself (e.g., terms/specification.md)
  2. semiotic-term — the convention (e.g., standards/semiotic-specification/index.md)
  3. practices/specification.md — conformance requirements, inside the semiotic-term directory

The naming convention still has three levels, but the third level is a file within the second level’s directory, not a separate Thing.

The self-reference problem

The specification-specification — the convention governing how specifications are written — presents a self-reference: where does the Thing “specification” live, and what governs its own practices/specification.md?

Specification is a concept within technology (it is a practice of encoding normative requirements). The specification-specification is the practice document for that concept. So: technology/concepts/specification/practices/specification.md — the practice of specification, governing itself.

This is genuinely self-referential, but the self-reference is productive: the specification-specification must conform to its own requirements. If it cannot, the requirements are wrong. This is the same bootstrapping property that all foundational specifications share.

Open questions

  • What is the full inventory of standard cross-cutting subdirectories after this revision?
  • Does texts/ survive as a directory type, or does content progressively migrate to research/, learning/, and other more specific directories?
  • How do methods/ and practices/ relate to each other within a single Thing? Is methods/ about the general approach and practices/ about the concrete doing?
  • Should the structuration/interface classification be encoded in frontmatter (e.g., realm: structuration or realm: interface)?
