Footprint

2025-12-31
Defines Footprint, footprints, semiotic footprint

The semiotic footprint of a handle τ\tau in state XX is the closed partial state obtained by applying the composite closure operator S\mathcal{S} to the handle fragment: FootX(τ)=SX(Fτ,X)\mathrm{Foot}_X(\tau) = \mathcal{S}_X(F_{\tau,X}). The footprint formalizes Peirce’s pragmatic maxim: the meaning of a handle is the totality of its conceivable practical effects — the full semantic, syntactic, and fusion closure of everything generated by the handle’s interactions.

The footprint is always bounded by the core semioverse state X*: for any X <= X* and any tau, Foot_X(tau) <= X*. This boundedness ensures that no single handle’s interactions can produce semantic material beyond what the semioverse’s least fixed point contains. The footprint is also monotone in the interaction surface: more interactions produce a larger or equal footprint.

Footprints are the objects compared by the delta operation (Delta_foot(tau, tau’; X) = S_X(Delta_frag(F_{tau,X}, F_{tau’,X}))) and diminished by subtraction. They can be quantified by an evaluation valuation mu(tau; X) = mu(Foot_X(tau)), allowing quantitative comparison of how much semantic material different handles contribute to the semioverse.
