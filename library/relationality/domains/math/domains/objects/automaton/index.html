2026-03-20 An Automaton is a quintuple (Q, Σ, δ, q₀, α) — a state set Q, input alphabet Σ, transition structure δ encoding how inputs drive state changes, an initial state q₀, and an acceptance condition α that classifies runs as accepting or rejecting. The defining structure: an automaton recognizes (classifies) behaviors rather than producing outputs. Every run — a path through Q driven by an input sequence — either satisfies α (accepted) or does not (rejected); the automaton's meaning is its recognized language L(A). An automaton is not a process (which produces outputs, not acceptance decisions) and not a derivation (which applies licensed inference rules, not input-driven transitions).

Automaton

Formal definition

An Automaton is a quintuple A=(Q,Σ,δ,q0,α)\mathcal{A} = (Q, \Sigma, \delta, q_0, \alpha):

A=(Q:States,  Σ:Alphabet,  δ:Transition,  q0∈Q,  α:Acceptance)\mathcal{A} = (Q : \mathrm{States},\; \Sigma : \mathrm{Alphabet},\; \delta : \mathrm{Transition},\; q_0 \in Q,\; \alpha : \mathrm{Acceptance})

where:

  • QQ is the state set — the carrier of the automaton; for finite automata QQ is finite; for infinite-state automata QQ may be countable or uncountable
  • Σ\Sigma is the input alphabet — the finite set of symbols the automaton reads; inputs arrive one symbol at a time from w∈Σ∗w \in \Sigma^* (finite words) or w∈Σωw \in \Sigma^\omega (infinite words)
  • δ\delta is the transition structure — for a deterministic automaton, δ:Q×Σ→Q\delta : Q \times \Sigma \to Q is a total function; for a nondeterministic automaton, δ:Q×Σ→P(Q)\delta : Q \times \Sigma \to \mathcal{P}(Q) maps each state-symbol pair to a set of possible successors; for a weighted automaton, δ:Q×Σ×Q→K\delta : Q \times \Sigma \times Q \to K assigns weights in a semiring KK
  • q0∈Qq_0 \in Q is the initial state — where every run begins
  • α\alpha is the acceptance condition — for finite automata over finite words, α=F⊆Q\alpha = F \subseteq Q is a set of final states: a run is accepting iff it ends in FF; for automata on infinite words, α\alpha is a condition on the states visited infinitely often (Büchi, Rabin, Muller, parity)

A run of A\mathcal{A} on w=a1⋯an∈Σ∗w = a_1 \cdots a_n \in \Sigma^* is a sequence ρ=q0q1⋯qn\rho = q_0 q_1 \cdots q_n where δ(qi−1,ai)=qi\delta(q_{i-1}, a_i) = q_i (deterministic) or qi∈δ(qi−1,ai)q_i \in \delta(q_{i-1}, a_i) (nondeterministic) for each ii. The recognized language:

L(A)={w∈Σ∗∣there exists an accepting run of A on w}L(\mathcal{A}) = \{w \in \Sigma^* \mid \text{there exists an accepting run of } \mathcal{A} \text{ on } w\}

Two invariants. A\mathcal{A} is an automaton iff:

  1. Input-driven transitions: A\mathcal{A} only transitions upon receiving an input symbol. The transition structure δ\delta is defined on state-symbol pairs; the automaton does not evolve autonomously. This distinguishes automata from dynamical systems (which evolve without external input) and from processes equipped with output (which both transition and produce). The input-driven character means the automaton’s behavior is always a response to an externally supplied sequence.

  2. Run-defined acceptance: the acceptance condition α\alpha is a property of runs (paths through QQ driven by inputs), not a property of individual states or transitions in isolation. For finite-word automata, acceptance is determined by the run’s terminus; for infinite-word automata, by the set of states visited infinitely often. This dependence on runs makes automata recognizers of behavioral patterns, not transducers of values.

Kleene: regular languages and finite automata

Stephen Kleene (Representation of Events in Nerve Nets and Finite Automata, 1956) proved the fundamental theorem connecting automata-theoretic and algebraic descriptions:

Kleene’s theorem: L⊆Σ∗L \subseteq \Sigma^* is recognized by a finite automaton iff LL is regular — expressible by a regular expression built from: ∅\emptyset, {ε}\{\varepsilon\}, {a}\{a\} for a∈Σa \in \Sigma; union R1∪R2R_1 \cup R_2; concatenation R1⋅R2R_1 \cdot R_2; Kleene star R∗R^* (zero or more concatenations from RR).

The proof:

  • Automaton ⇒\Rightarrow regular expression: eliminate states one by one, computing the language through each surviving state pair
  • Regular expression ⇒\Rightarrow automaton: union yields a combined NFA; concatenation sequences two NFAs; Kleene star adds an ε\varepsilon-loop to the initial state

Kleene’s theorem establishes that regular expressions and finite automata are two syntactic presentations of the same class of languages — the regular languages. This is the first major closure-and-characterization result in formal language theory.

Rabin and Scott: nondeterminism and the powerset construction

Michael O. Rabin and Dana Scott (Finite Automata and Their Decision Problems, 1959) introduced nondeterministic finite automata (NFAs) — allowing multiple transitions from a single state on the same input and ε\varepsilon-transitions — and proved their equivalence with deterministic finite automata.

The powerset construction: from any NFA (Q,Σ,δ,q0,F)(Q, \Sigma, \delta, q_0, F), construct a DFA (P(Q),Σ,δ^,{q0},F^)(\mathcal{P}(Q), \Sigma, \hat\delta, \{q_0\}, \hat F) where:

  • States are subsets S⊆QS \subseteq Q — tracking the set of NFA states reachable by any run on the input seen so far
  • δ^(S,a)=⋃q∈Sδ(q,a)\hat\delta(S, a) = \bigcup_{q \in S} \delta(q, a)
  • F^={S⊆Q∣S∩F≠∅}\hat F = \{S \subseteq Q \mid S \cap F \neq \emptyset\}

Rabin-Scott theorem: L(ANFA)=L(ADFA)L(\mathcal{A}_\mathrm{NFA}) = L(\mathcal{A}_\mathrm{DFA}). The powerset construction converts every NFA to an equivalent DFA, at the cost of at most 2∣Q∣2^{|Q|} states. Determinism and nondeterminism are equi-expressive for finite automata — unlike for pushdown automata, where nondeterminism strictly increases expressive power. Rabin and Scott also proved closure properties (union, intersection, complement, reversal, homomorphism) and decidability of emptiness and equivalence.

Myhill-Nerode: the minimal automaton

The Nerode relation (Nerode 1958): x∼Lyx \sim_L y iff for all z∈Σ∗z \in \Sigma^*: xz∈L⇔yz∈Lxz \in L \Leftrightarrow yz \in L. Two strings are equivalent iff indistinguishable by any future continuation.

Myhill-Nerode theorem: LL is regular iff ∼L\sim_L has finitely many equivalence classes. The number of classes equals the number of states in the minimal DFA for LL.

The minimal DFA: states are ∼L\sim_L-classes [ ⁣[x] ⁣][\![x]\!]; initial state is [ ⁣[ε] ⁣][\![\varepsilon]\!]; transition δ^([ ⁣[x] ⁣],a)=[ ⁣[xa] ⁣]\hat\delta([\![x]\!], a) = [\![xa]\!]; accepting states {[ ⁣[x] ⁣]∣x∈L}\{[\![x]\!] \mid x \in L\}. The minimal DFA is unique up to isomorphism.

Hopcroft (1971) gave an O(nlog⁡n)O(n \log n) algorithm for minimizing a DFA with nn states (optimal). The minimal automaton is the canonical state space for a language: its states are exactly the observational equivalence classes of strings (Nerode equivalence = bisimulation for finite automata).

Büchi: automata on infinite words

J. Richard Büchi (On a Decision Method in Restricted Second Order Arithmetic, 1960) extended finite automata to infinite words w∈Σωw \in \Sigma^\omega, motivated by the decidability of the monadic second-order theory of one successor (S1S).

A Büchi automaton has the same structure as a finite automaton but with acceptance: an infinite run ρ=q0q1q2⋯\rho = q_0 q_1 q_2 \cdots is Büchi-accepting iff Inf(ρ)∩F≠∅\mathrm{Inf}(\rho) \cap F \neq \emptyset — the run visits some state in FF infinitely often.

Büchi’s theorem: the ω\omega-languages recognized by nondeterministic Büchi automata are exactly the ω\omega-regular languages — finite unions of U⋅VωU \cdot V^\omega where U,VU, V are regular over Σ\Sigma.

Unlike finite automata, nondeterministic Büchi automata are strictly more expressive than deterministic Büchi automata. Stronger deterministic conditions restore full expressiveness: Muller automata (accepting runs whose Inf(ρ)\mathrm{Inf}(\rho) belongs to a specified family), Rabin automata (pairwise conditions on infinitely and finitely visited sets), and parity automata (the minimum infinitely-visited priority is even).

Büchi automata are the primary tool for model checking (Clarke, Emerson, Sistla 1986): a reactive system is modeled as a Büchi automaton; an LTL specification is translated to a Büchi automaton for its negation; the system satisfies the specification iff the intersection automaton accepts no infinite run.

Open questions

  • Whether the Myhill-Nerode minimization theorem is the automata-theoretic instance of a general coalgebraic minimization, and whether every final coalgebra arises as such a quotient of behavioral equivalence.
  • Whether Büchi acceptance — “visits FF infinitely often” — has a formulation in tower dynamics: whether the ω\omega-recurrence condition corresponds to recurrence at periodic tower levels, making ω\omega-regular languages the behaviors that repeat at bounded tower depth.
  • Whether there is a sheaf-theoretic characterization of automaton minimization — whether the Nerode equivalence is a congruence in the topos of presheaves over the history site, and whether the minimal automaton is the colimit of a covering diagram.
  • Whether the class of behaviors recognized by automata in the final coalgebra of the behavior functor F(X)=O×XIF(X) = O \times X^I exactly corresponds to the ω\omega-regular languages, or whether the final coalgebra sees behaviors requiring automata on higher ordinals.
