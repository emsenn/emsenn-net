What this is

Weather is the condition of the atmosphere at a particular place and time — the answer to “what is it doing outside right now?” The World Meteorological Organization (Guide to Climatological Practices, WMO-No. 100, 1992) defines weather as “the state of the atmosphere at a specific time, as determined by the simultaneous occurrence of several meteorological elements.” This definition is deliberately short-term: weather is what happens over hours to days; climate is the 30-year statistical envelope of weather.

The nuclear reading makes this temporal distinction precise. A weather state W∈WtW \in W_t is a single fiber element at a specific history tt. Climate is not a fiber element but the long-time measure — the statistical density of Wt∗W^*_t elements over a 30-year sequence of histories, as standardized by the WMO. Climate is the coarse shape of the fixed fiber over time; weather is the particular fiber element at a particular history.

The shadow class structure adds three dimensions invisible in the WMO definition: the assimilation status (has this state been reconciled with all available observations?), the forecast status (does the NWP dynamics commit to this state persisting forward?), and the gap (how much further nuclear processing is required before the state enters Wt∗W^*_t?).

Atmospheric state space and the primitive equations

The primitive equations (Richardson 1922, Weather Prediction by Numerical Process, Cambridge; Charney, Fjörtoft, von Neumann 1950, Tellus 2, 237–254) are the conservation laws governing large-scale atmospheric dynamics. They encode four physical principles:

DuDt=−∇pρ−2Ω×u−∇Φ+F(momentum)\frac{D\mathbf{u}}{Dt} = -\frac{\nabla p}{\rho} - 2\mathbf{\Omega} \times \mathbf{u} - \nabla \Phi + \mathbf{F} \quad \text{(momentum)}∂ρ∂t+∇⋅(ρu)=0(mass)\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{u}) = 0 \quad \text{(mass)}DθDt=Qcp(thermodynamic energy)\frac{D\theta}{Dt} = \frac{Q}{c_p} \quad \text{(thermodynamic energy)}∂p∂z=−ρg(hydrostatic approximation)\frac{\partial p}{\partial z} = -\rho g \quad \text{(hydrostatic approximation)}

These four equations jointly define the NWP forward operator: given the full atmospheric state W∈WtW \in W_t, integration of these equations produces Wt′∈Wt′W_{t'} \in W_{t'} at the next history t′=t⋆gen(s)t' = t \star \mathrm{gen}(s), via the restriction map H(t′→t)(Wt′)=WH(t' \to t)(W_{t'}) = W.

The state space. The full atmospheric state at history tt over the global locale ℓglobal\ell_{\mathrm{global}} is the tuple (u(x,t),p(x,t),ρ(x,t),θ(x,t))(\mathbf{u}(x,t), p(x,t), \rho(x,t), \theta(x,t)) for all spatial coordinates x∈ℓx \in \ell. The Lorenz attractor as the fixed fiber’s geometry. Despite the $10^8-dimensional full phase space, the atmospheric dynamics are attracted to a strange attractor with Hausdorff dimension approximately 27.1 (Lorenz 1969, *Bulletin of the American Meteorological Society* 50, 345–352 — estimate for the real atmosphere). The fixed fiber W^_t consists of those atmospheric propositions that are simultaneously backward-consistent and forward-stable — those compatible with the attractor's geometry. States that venture far from the attractor fail the transfer-stability condition: the NWP dynamics carry them back toward the attractor, so \Delta^W_t(W) > Wforstatesfarfrom for states far from W^_t$.

Quasigeostrophic theory and potential vorticity

Quasigeostrophic (QG) theory (Charney 1948, Journal of Meteorology 5, 289–321) is the reduced description of synoptic-scale atmospheric dynamics. At synoptic scale (1000–5000 km), the Rossby number Ro=U/(fL)≪1\mathrm{Ro} = U/(fL) \ll 1 — the Coriolis force dominates inertia — and the flow is nearly geostrophic: nearly balanced between pressure gradient and Coriolis force. QG theory exploits this balance to reduce the full $10^8$-dimensional primitive equations to a single conserved scalar field: the quasigeostrophic potential vorticity (QGPV).

QGPV conservation. The fundamental QG equation is conservation of QGPV following the geostrophic flow:

DQDt=0\frac{D Q}{Dt} = 0

where QQ is the QGPV, combining relative vorticity ∇2ψ\nabla^2 \psi (curvature of the flow, from the stream function ψ\psi), planetary vorticity f0f_0 (Coriolis parameter), and stretching vorticity (f02/N2)(∂2ψ/∂z2)(f_0^2/N^2)(\partial^2 \psi / \partial z^2) (vertical stratification). The stream function ψ\psi — from which the geostrophic wind is recovered as ug=−∂ψ/∂yu_g = -\partial\psi/\partial y, vg=∂ψ/∂xv_g = \partial\psi/\partial x — is the single scalar potential that determines the full synoptic flow.

QGPV conservation as transfer-stability. In nuclear terms: the QGPV field Q(x,t)Q(x,t) is a fiber element Q∈WtQ \in W_t. Conservation DQ/Dt=0DQ/Dt = 0 is the statement that QQ is transfer-stable under the QG approximation of the NWP forward operator: ΔtW(Q)=Q\Delta^W_t(Q) = Q — the NWP forward operator does not move QQ from its value along geostrophic trajectories. The QGPV is an element of Fix(ΔtW)\mathrm{Fix}(\Delta^W_t) — it sits in the transfer-fixed sublocale of WtW_t.

PV thinking. The practitioner technique of potential vorticity thinking (Hoskins, McIntyre, Robertson 1985, Quarterly Journal of the Royal Meteorological Society 111, 877–946) diagnoses atmospheric development by tracking QGPV anomalies. A positive upper-level QGPV anomaly (jet-stream trough) induces cyclonic circulation below; a negative surface thermal anomaly induces anticyclonic flow above. Their mutual interaction drives baroclinic instability — the growth of mid-latitude weather systems from initially small perturbations. In nuclear terms: the upper and surface QGPV anomalies are elements of two sub-fibers of WtW_t (upper-troposphere locale and surface locale); baroclinic growth is the process of their joint fiber element moving toward Wt∗W^*_t through mutual nuclear amplification.

Chaos, Lyapunov exponents, and the predictability horizon

Lorenz (1963) (Journal of the Atmospheric Sciences 20, 130–141) demonstrated that deterministic atmospheric convection is chaotic: small differences in initial conditions grow exponentially. The formal content: the atmospheric dynamical system has a positive largest Lyapunov exponent λ1>0\lambda_1 > 0, the maximum rate of exponential divergence of nearby trajectories in atmospheric phase space. For the real atmosphere, λ1≈0.35\lambda_1 \approx 0.35 day−1^{-1} (Lorenz 1969), giving a Lyapunov time of TL=1/λ1≈2.9T_L = 1/\lambda_1 \approx 2.9 days for the fastest-growing scales. Accounting for error cascade from small to large scales, practical predictability ends at approximately 10–14 days (ECMWF deterministic forecast skill verification 2019).

The Lorenz attractor. The Lorenz (1963) three-equation system — a model of Rayleigh-Bénard convection — exhibits a strange attractor: a bounded, aperiodic, fractal subset of phase space with Hausdorff dimension approximately 2.06 (Tucker 2002, Foundations of Computational Mathematics 2, 53–117 — the first rigorous proof of the Lorenz attractor’s existence). The attractor has two wings (the “butterfly”); trajectories spiral around one wing for several cycles before switching to the other. The two wings correspond to two quasi-stationary circulation regimes; wing-switching is the dynamical analog of regime transitions in the real atmosphere.

Lorenz sensitivity as nuclear non-adjointness. The sensitive dependence on initial conditions is the operational symptom. The structural cause is the non-adjointness of the data assimilation operator and the NWP forward operator: because σtW⊣̸ΔtW\sigma^W_t \not\dashv \Delta^W_t, there is a genuine gap between what the observational record commits to (the saturation fixed-fiber, assimilated states) and what the dynamics commit to (the transfer fixed-fiber, forecast-stable states). A weather state can be in one without being in the other — and closing the gap requires nuclear processing that cannot be shortened. If the two were adjoint, no such gap would exist: every observed state would be forecast-stable, and every forecast-stable state would be consistent with every observation. This is the nuclear expression of the impossibility of perfect weather prediction.

Data assimilation and the observational topology

4D-Var data assimilation (Lorenc 1986, Quarterly Journal of the Royal Meteorological Society 112, 1177–1194) is the operational implementation of the saturation nucleus: it finds the element of Wt∗W^*_t nearest (in the background error covariance metric) to the prior forecast. The 4D-Var cost function:

J(W0)=∥W0−Wb∥B−12+∑k=0N∥H(Mk(W0))−yk∥R−12J(W_0) = \|W_0 - W_b\|^2_{B^{-1}} + \sum_{k=0}^{N} \|H(\mathcal{M}^k(W_0)) - y_k\|^2_{R^{-1}}

where WbW_b is the background (prior forecast, an element of TrShadowtW\mathrm{TrShadow}^W_t), yky_k are observations (elements of SatShadowt+kW\mathrm{SatShadow}^W_{t+k}), HH is the observation operator, Mk\mathcal{M}^k is the NWP model integrated kk steps forward, and BB, RR are background and observation error covariance matrices. Minimization of JJ moves W0W_0 from TrShadow\mathrm{TrShadow} toward Wt∗W^*_t — pulling the prior forecast into consistency with the observations by applying both nuclear operators simultaneously.

The Ensemble Kalman Filter (Evensen 1994, Journal of Geophysical Research 99, 10143–10162) is the stochastic alternative: instead of solving the 4D-Var optimization, it maintains an ensemble of NN atmospheric states {Wt(i)}i=1N\{W^{(i)}_t\}_{i=1}^N, each a fiber element in WtW_t, and updates each member toward observations using the sample covariance of the ensemble spread. The ensemble mean Wˉt=(1/N)∑iWt(i)\bar{W}_t = (1/N)\sum_i W^{(i)}_t is the analyzed state; the ensemble spread Var({Wt(i)})\mathrm{Var}(\{W^{(i)}_t\}) is the empirical estimate of the gap magnitude.

The observational topology. The Grothendieck topology JobsJ_{\mathrm{obs}} on the history site encodes the observational network structure: a covering family {t′→t}t′∈S\{t' \to t\}_{t' \in S} in Jobs(t)J_{\mathrm{obs}}(t) is a set of observation histories sufficient to globally constrain the atmospheric state at tt. The WMO Global Observing System — surface stations, radiosonde networks, satellite data streams, aircraft reports — jointly define the operational covering families. A gap in the observing system (Southern Ocean, central Africa, deep Arctic) corresponds to a hole in the covering sieve: the atmospheric state in that region is constrained only by the prior NWP forecast, not by direct observational coverage.

Climate as fixed-fiber measure; climate change as topology change

Climate is not a weather state but a statistical property of the fiber sequence. The WMO standard (Technical Regulations, WMO-No. 49, Volume I, 2018): climate normals are computed over the 30-year WMO Standard Normal period (most recently 1991–2020). In nuclear terms:

Q‾ℓ=1n∑i=1nQti(Wti∗(ℓ))\overline{Q}_\ell = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} Q_{t_i}(W^*_{t_i}(\ell))

where (t1,…,tn)(t_1, \ldots, t_n) is the 30-year sequence of analysis histories (n≈10957n \approx 10957 days) and Qti(Wti∗(ℓ))Q_{t_i}(W^*_{t_i}(\ell)) is the quality value (temperature, precipitation, wind, etc.) of the analyzed state at locale ℓ\ell at history tit_i. The climate normal Q‾ℓ\overline{Q}_\ell is the empirical mean of quality values of fixed-fiber elements across the 30-year sequence. It is NOT a fiber element; it is a measure on the sequence of fixed-fiber elements.

Climate change as topology change. A shift in climate regime — the process by which new global mean temperatures, altered precipitation distributions, and changed extreme-event frequencies become established — is formally a change to the Grothendieck topology J→J′J \to J' on the history site. The new topology J′J' includes the altered radiative forcing (elevated CO2_2 concentrations, changed albedo) as part of the standard covering data for global atmospheric state witnessing. Under J′J':

  • Former Wt∗JW^{*J}_t elements (pre-industrial climate normals) move to SatShadowtJ′\mathrm{SatShadow}^{J'}_t: they remain historically recognized (the observational archive has not changed) but are no longer forward-committed by the altered dynamics
  • The new W∗J′W^{*J'} elements are higher-mean-temperature states that are now both observation-consistent and NWP-forward-stable under the new radiative forcing
  • The WMO updates its Climate Normal period every decade precisely to track this topology shift: the 1991–2020 normals reflect Wt∗J′W^{*J'}_t rather than Wt∗JW^{*J}_t for the pre-industrial topology

This is the exact formal analog of overruling a Precedent: the climate norm was a Wt∗W^*_t element (doubly settled) under the old topology; the new topology renders it backward-recognized but no longer forward-committed.

Etymology and meteorological grounding

Weather derives from Old English weder (wind, air, sky, storm), from Proto-Germanic *wedra- (cognate with Old Norse veðr, German Wetter, Gothic weidwo), from Proto-Indo-European *ue-dh-ro- from *ue- (to blow) — the same root as Latin ventus (wind). The earliest recorded use is in Beowulf (~750 CE). Weather is etymologically the force that arrives on the wind — the atmospheric condition that comes from elsewhere, uncontrolled, and acts.

The scientific history of weather prediction as a formal enterprise:

Vilhelm Bjerknes (1904, Meteorologische Zeitschrift 21, 1–7): framed weather prediction as a problem in mathematical physics — exact knowledge of the atmospheric state at time t0t_0 plus physical laws determines the state at all future times. The first formal statement of the NWP forward operator.

Lewis Fry Richardson (1922, Weather Prediction by Numerical Process, Cambridge University Press): implemented the first numerical weather forecast — a 6-hour forecast over central Europe for 20 May 1910, computed by hand over six weeks in 1922. The computation was too slow for operational use (Richardson estimated 64,000 human computers would be needed to run forecasts in real time) but established the method.

Jule Charney (1950, Tellus 2, 237–254): led the first successful NWP computation on ENIAC at the Institute for Advanced Study — 24-hour forecasts of 500 hPa geopotential height, using QG theory to filter acoustic and gravity waves that would otherwise require prohibitively small timesteps. The first analyzed weather states in Wt∗W^*_t produced computationally.

Edward Lorenz (1963, Journal of the Atmospheric Sciences 20, 130–141): demonstrated the theoretical limit to NWP. Lorenz discovered chaos by accident: re-running a weather simulation with initial conditions rounded to 3 decimal places (instead of the stored 6), he found the trajectory diverged completely within a simulated month. This established the predictability horizon as a structural feature of atmospheric dynamics, not a remediable data problem.

ECMWF (European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts, founded 1975): the operational institution that extended the predictability horizon from 3 days (1975) to approximately 10 days (2020) through improved models, data assimilation, and observational coverage — demonstrating that the gap between TrShadow\mathrm{TrShadow} and Wt∗W^*_t is closable by better nuclear processing, up to the hard Lyapunov limit.

Proposition 4 (Forecast degradation and the predictability horizon). Let W∈Wt0∗W \in W^*_{t_0} be an analyzed weather state. The kk-step forecast W^t0+k\hat{W}_{t_0+k} is produced by kk applications of the NWP forward operator from WW. For small kk, W^t0+k∈TrShadowt0+kW\hat{W}_{t_0+k} \in \mathrm{TrShadow}^W_{t_0+k}. where λ1\lambda_1 is the largest Lyapunov exponent, Δt\Delta t is the model timestep, ϵ0\epsilon_0 is the initial condition uncertainty, and ϵobs\epsilon_{\mathrm{obs}} is the observational uncertainty at verification.

Proof. Forecast error grows exponentially: ϵ(k)≈ϵ0exp⁡(λ1kΔt)\epsilon(k) \approx \epsilon_0 \exp(\lambda_1 k \Delta t) by definition of λ1\lambda_1. The forecast is transfer-stable (W^t0+k∈TrShadow\hat{W}_{t_0+k} \in \mathrm{TrShadow}) while ϵ(k)<ϵobs\epsilon(k) < \epsilon_{\mathrm{obs}} — while the NWP commitment is tighter than observational uncertainty. A Weather is a triple (W, t, ell) — an atmospheric state W in the atmospheric state fiber W_t, an analysis history t at which the state is evaluated, and a locale ell over which W is sampled — where the shadow class of W classifies the epistemic status of the atmospheric condition: W in W*_t (analyzed: doubly settled — assimilated against the full observational record by the RelationalHistoryFiberSaturatingNucleus and forward-committed by the RelationalHistoryFiberTransferringNucleus acting as the NWP forward operator), W in SatShadow_t (observed-unforecast: backward-recognized against observations but not forward-committed by the primitive-equation dynamics), W in TrShadow_t (forecast-unverified: forward-stable under NWP integration but not yet backward-recognized against incoming observations), W in FreeShadow_t (raw: neither assimilated nor forecast-stable; initial model output or unprocessed sensor data).
