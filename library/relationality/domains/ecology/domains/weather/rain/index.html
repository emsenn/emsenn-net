What this is

Rain is the weather phenomenon every inhabited world knows. What is not obvious is that the moment rain becomes settled — recognized by the past, committed in the future, no longer movable by either nucleus — is the moment its contribution to the atmospheric spectral mass drops to zero. Until then, the precipitation proposition is a mode carrying positive eigenvalue: it contributes to the total unsettledness of the atmosphere, which has both a thermodynamic temperature and a Hawking temperature derived from it.

The odd thing is the duality between these temperatures. As an atmosphere approaches settled rain, its spectral mass decreases. The thermodynamic temperature — measuring how much energy is stored in unsettled modes — decreases with it. But the Hawking temperature — measuring the rate at which trapped modes tunnel out of the spectral interior — increases. The approach to rain is simultaneously a cooling of the mode system and a heating of the spectral horizon.

At fixed-point rain (λrt=0\lambda_{r_t} = 0, rt∈Ht∗r_t \in H_t^*), the thermodynamic temperature contribution from precipitation is zero and the spectral horizon temperature has reached its maximum for that mode. The precipitation mode has fully evaporated from the unsettled spectrum.

The two spectral tiers

The spectral content of the atmosphere divides into two independent tiers (RelationalHistoryFiberSpectralStratification):

The lower tier (history combinatorics): the depth growth series ZT(z)=1/μI(z)Z_T(z) = 1/\mu_I(z), determined entirely by the step set Σ\Sigma and commutation relation II before any fiber algebra has been imposed. For the two-generator commuting site (T=N2T = \mathbb{N}^2), there are exactly n+1n+1 histories at depth nn and ZT(z)=1/(1−z)2Z_T(z) = 1/(1-z)^2. The upper tier (fiber eigenvalues): the settlement spectrum at each history tt, the set {λa:a∈Ht}\{\lambda_a : a \in H_t\}. This is determined by the nuclear quartet (Ht,σt,Δt)(H_t, \sigma_t, \Delta_t) at tt and carries no information about the count or arrangement of histories. The calibration defect as atmospheric temperature

From RelationalHistoryFiberSpectralThermodynamicsTemperatureDualityGoldenConjecture (conjecture, status: stub), the spectral mass Mt(δ)M_t(\delta) supports two natural temperature interpretations:

Tthermo∝Mt(δ),TH:=12 Mt(δ).T_{\mathrm{thermo}} \propto M_t(\delta), \qquad T_H := \frac{1}{2\, M_t(\delta)}.

The thermodynamic temperature TthermoT_\mathrm{thermo} measures the energy stored in unsettled modes — how much total nuclear travel remains across all atmospheric propositions. A storm in development has large Mt(δ)M_t(\delta): many propositions are far from Ht∗H_t^*, the fiber is hot. A stable, settled atmosphere has small Mt(δ)M_t(\delta): most propositions are already calibrated.

The Hawking temperature TH=1/(2Mt(δ))T_H = 1/(2M_t(\delta)) measures the effective temperature of the radiation emitted when trapped modes tunnel out of the spectral interior — the rate at which the settling process releases unsettled spectral energy. A large unsettled atmosphere (large MtM_t) has a cold spectral horizon: the settling is slow and cold. A nearly-settled atmosphere (small MtM_t) has a hot spectral horizon: the few remaining unsettled modes are being released at high effective temperature.

At the fixed point Mt(δ)=0M_t(\delta) = 0: Tthermo=0T_\mathrm{thermo} = 0 (all modes settled, no stored energy), TH=∞T_H = \infty (the spectral horizon is infinitely hot, but there is nothing left to evaporate). Rain reaching RelationalHistoryFixedFiber is the precipitation proposition arriving at exactly this point — its eigenvalue contribution drops to zero, the precipitation mode has evaporated from the spectrum.

The two temperatures are not in contradiction because they describe different aspects: TthermoT_\mathrm{thermo} is the temperature of the mode system itself; THT_H is the temperature of the emitted settling radiation. This is the same distinction as in black hole thermodynamics — a massive black hole is internally energetic but emits cold Hawking radiation; a nearly-evaporated black hole has little mass-energy left but emits hot Hawking radiation.

The mode algebra and compound weather conditions
