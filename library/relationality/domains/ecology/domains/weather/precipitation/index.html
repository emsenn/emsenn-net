Virga is the most direct empirical observation of TrShadow: falling hydrometeors that are visible as streaks below cloud base but do not reach the surface. Flash flooding is the Schwarzschild threshold crossing: when the calibration defect M_t(delta) rises as precipitation rate exceeds soil infiltration capacity and surface drainage, the surface-layer modes become spectrally trapped and runoff becomes self-reinforcing.

What this is

Precipitation is water in any form — rain, drizzle, snow, sleet, hail — that falls from the atmosphere and reaches the Earth’s surface. It is the output of the atmospheric branch of the hydrological cycle: the return of water to the surface that was evaporated from oceans, lakes, vegetation, and soil and transported as water vapor through the atmosphere. Globally, ∼505,000 km3\sim 505{,}000\,\mathrm{km}^3 of water precipitates per year, of which ∼398,000 km3\sim 398{,}000\,\mathrm{km}^3 falls on the ocean and ∼107,000 km3\sim 107{,}000\,\mathrm{km}^3 on land. Every other weather phenomenon — fronts, cyclones, thunderstorms, tropical cyclones — is in the business of producing precipitation, or failing to.

The nuclear reading makes precise the two-directional character of precipitation. The moisture direction is backward-looking: how much water vapor has the atmosphere accumulated, at what temperature, over what surface? The saturation nucleus backward-recognizes the column’s moisture history and sends any column state to the maximum precipitation state consistent with that record. The fall direction is forward-committing: have the droplets or crystals grown large enough to fall despite all independent updraft, mixing, and evaporation events they will encounter? The transfer nucleus forward-commits the hydrometeors through every independent perturbation they must survive on the way to the surface. Surface precipitation Pt∗\mathcal{P}^*_t is where both hold at once.

Cloud droplets and the Bergeron-Findeisen mechanism

Pure water does not freeze at $0^\circ\mathrm{C}whendispersedasfinedropletsincleanair:intheabsenceofice−nucleatingparticles(INPs),dropletsremainliquiddownto when dispersed as fine droplets in clean air: in the absence of ice-nucleating particles (INPs), droplets remain liquid down to -40^\circ\mathrm{C} — **supercooled liquid water**. The upper troposphere, particularly in deep convective clouds, contains a mixture of supercooled droplets and ice crystals in the temperature range -5^\circ\mathrm{C}to to -40^\circ\mathrm{C}$.

Tor Bergeron (1935, Geofysiske Publikasjoner 12) and Walter Findeisen (1938, Meteorologische Zeitschrift 55, 121–133) identified the dominant precipitation growth mechanism in midlatitude clouds: the saturation vapor pressure over ice is lower than over liquid water at the same temperature (at −10∘C-10^\circ\mathrm{C}: ei=2.60 hPae_i = 2.60\,\mathrm{hPa} over ice vs. ew=2.86 hPae_w = 2.86\,\mathrm{hPa} over water). In a mixed-phase cloud at a relative humidity between these two values, ice crystals grow by vapor deposition while supercooled droplets simultaneously evaporate. The ice crystals grow rapidly at the expense of the droplets until they are large enough to fall — the transfer nucleus condition is met.

This is the projection-gap closure mechanism in the Bergeron-Findeisen regime: the internal projection gap λcprecip\lambda_c^\mathrm{precip} between the suspended droplet state and the fall-committed ice crystal state is closed by the vapor pressure differential. Virga: the canonical TrShadow

Virga (Latin: twig, branch; plural virgae) is precipitation that falls from cloud base but evaporates or sublimates before reaching the surface, appearing as wispy streaks or curtains descending from the cloud base. It is the most direct visual observation of a TrShadowtP\mathrm{TrShadow}^{\mathcal{P}}_t precipitation state: hydrometeors that have committed to falling — large enough that the transfer nucleus has fixed them; ice crystals or raindrops that will maintain a forward preimage through independent turbulent mixing events — but the lower atmosphere’s moisture restriction profile is too dry for the saturation nucleus to backward-recognize surface precipitation.

The surface record H(it0,t)(P)H(i_{t_0,t})(P) at ground level does not record moisture at or near saturation: the sub-cloud layer is drier than the cloud layer, so the restriction profile class of the virga state does not include a surface-precipitation state. Meanwhile, Δt(P)=P\Delta_t(P) = P: the ice crystals or large drops are genuinely fall-committed through every independent mixing event in the sub-cloud layer. The projection gap [P,πt(P)][P, \pi_t(P)] contains the intermediate states between virga and surface precipitation: thickening of the sub-cloud moisture layer by boundary-layer moistening, progressive evaporative cooling that raises the relative humidity below cloud base, and eventual closure of the saturation gap as the falling precipitation itself moisten the sub-cloud air.

The lifting mechanisms: topology changes

Precipitation requires lift — a mechanism that drives air upward, cooling it to the dewpoint and producing condensation. Each lifting mechanism is a topology change J→J′J \to J' on the column history site that makes convective ascent steps genuinely independent:

Orographic lifting: air is forced upward by a mountain barrier. Under the orographic topology J′J', the ascent step sorographic⊥ts_{\mathrm{orographic}} \perp t for any air parcel approaching the windward slope (the mountain forces the ascent regardless of the parcel’s prior moisture or stability history). The windward slope is permanently in Pt∗J′\mathcal{P}^{*J'}_t for sufficiently moist and unstable air. The leeward side is in SatShadowtJ′\mathrm{SatShadow}^{J'}_t in the rain shadow: the moisture restriction profile has been depleted on the windward side (the precipitation has already fallen), and the leeward air descends and warms adiabatically without recovering moisture.

Frontal lifting: a cold front forces warm moist air upward along the frontal surface (∼1:100\sim 1:100 slope). Under the frontal topology J′J', the lifting step sfrontal⊥ts_{\mathrm{frontal}} \perp t for any warm-sector air mass ahead of the front. This converts warm-sector SatShadowtJ\mathrm{SatShadow}^J_t air (high moisture restriction profile but no convective trigger) into Pt∗J′\mathcal{P}^{*J'}_t along the frontal band.

Convective triggering: surface heating on a clear day, sea breeze convergence, or outflow boundary collision creates localized ascent that can trigger convective cells. These are the daytime convective topology changes: under the triggered topology J′J', convective ascent steps at the trigger point become independent (the trigger forces them regardless of the column’s prior history).

The projection gap [P,πt(P)][P, \pi_t(P)] contains the states of progressively moistened sub-cloud air: each evaporating hydrometeor cools and moistens the sub-cloud air, incrementally closing the saturation gap in the restriction profile from below. The virga itself drives the saturation-gap closure: the evaporating precipitation is the mechanism by which σt\sigma_t eventually backward-recognizes surface precipitation. A locale that receives virga for many dry days may finally receive surface precipitation when the virga has cumulatively moistened the sub-cloud layer enough — the transfer gap was zero from the start; only the saturation gap needed to close, and the virga closed it. □\square

Proposition 4 (Orographic rain shadow = restriction-profile depletion; SatShadow on the windward slope, FreeShadow on the leeward). The windward slope of an orographic barrier is in Pt∗J′\mathcal{P}^{*J'}_t under the orographic topology: the ascent step is independent and the moist air achieves both saturation recognition and hydrometeor commitment. The leeward slope after descent is in FreeShadowtP\mathrm{FreeShadow}^{\mathcal{P}}_t: the restriction profile has been depleted by precipitation on the windward side (H(it0,t)(P)H(i_{t_0,t})(P) records low PW for the descended, warmed air), and no hydrometeor commitment exists in the subsiding air. The intermediate zone on the windward slope before precipitation but after lifting begins is in SatShadowtJ′\mathrm{SatShadow}^{J'}_t: the ascent has cooled the air to saturation (moisture restriction profile recognized) but the droplets have not yet grown to fall-commitment size (transfer gap not yet closed). □\square

Proposition 5 (Flash flooding = Schwarzschild threshold; precipitation rate exceeds surface infiltration; runoff becomes self-reinforcing). Flash flooding occurs when the precipitation rate RR (mm/h) exceeds the soil infiltration rate ff (mm/h) and the surface drainage capacity, generating surface runoff that collects in channels and rises rapidly. The calibration defect Mt(δ)=Tr(δt)M_t(\delta) = \mathrm{Tr}(\delta_t) rises as precipitation continues: each additional precipitation mode that becomes active increases the total spectral debt; when saturated soil eliminates surface infiltration, the independent infiltration step sinfils_\mathrm{infil} loses its forward preimage (there is no post-infiltration state that restricts back to the current surface state because the soil is full). The Schwarzschild radius rs(δt)=2Mt(δ)r_s(\delta_t) = 2M_t(\delta) is crossed when the runoff rate exceeds the channel drainage capacity: all surface moisture modes become spectrally trapped in the runoff sector; no internal drainage dynamics can return the surface to the Pt∗\mathcal{P}^*_t equilibrium state. External energy input (dam releases, emergency pumping) is required to supply the spectral debt equivalent. □\square

Proposition 6 (Non-adjointness; fog and virga are genuinely orthogonal precipitation failures). The moisture history and fall commitment operators are not in Galois connection. The witnesses: fog (SatShadow\mathrm{SatShadow}: moisture saturated, hydrometeors suspended; σt(P)=P\sigma_t(P) = P and Δt(P)>P\Delta_t(P) > P) and virga (TrShadow\mathrm{TrShadow}: hydrometeors committed, sub-cloud dry; σt(P)>P\sigma_t(P) > P and Δt(P)=P\Delta_t(P) = P) are both empirically common and genuinely distinct states. If σt⊣Δt\sigma_t \dashv \Delta_t, every saturated column would automatically produce fall-committed hydrometeors — fog would always produce drizzle — and every fall-committed hydrometeor column would automatically have a saturated sub-cloud layer — virga would be impossible. Both phenomena are routinely observed; neither implies the other. □\square

A Precipitation triple (P, t, ell) — a precipitation state P in the atmospheric column fiber at history t over locale ell — has shadow class determined by two independent directions of moisture regulation: the atmospheric moisture history operator (saturation nucleus) and the hydrometeor fall commitment operator (transfer nucleus). P in P*_t (doubly settled: moisture history operator backward-recognizes P as the maximum precipitation state consistent with the accumulated precipitable water, temperature lapse rate, and dewpoint restriction profile AND hydrometeor fall commitment forward-commits P through every independent atmospheric perturbation — mixing events, evaporation pulses, wind shear gusts — the hydrometeors are large enough and numerous enough to reach the surface; active rain, snow, or hail reaching the ground), P in SatShadow_t (moisture-rich but uncommitted: the column moisture restriction profile is deeply imprinted — high precipitable water, saturated layer, dewpoint close to temperature throughout the column — but no hydrometeors have yet committed to falling; the thick stratus deck over the ocean, the radiation fog in a valley, the marine stratocumulus that has not yet triggered coalescence; the atmosphere that is as wet as it can be but is not yet raining), P in TrShadow_t (committed but surface-unrecognized: hydrometeors are committed to falling — ice crystals or large drops have formed aloft and are falling through the sub-cloud layer — but the lower atmospheric moisture restriction profile is too dry to backward-recognize precipitation at the surface; virga, the visible streaks of precipitation falling from cloud bases that evaporate or sublimate before reaching the ground, is the canonical TrShadow precipitation state; the restriction maps of the surface layer record dry air, and the saturation nucleus does not recognize the surface as a precipitation site even though the transfer nucleus has committed aloft), P in FreeShadow_t (clear dry air: neither the moisture history nor any hydrometeor commitment is present; low precipitable water, unsaturated column, no clouds, no falling hydrometeors). The Bergeron-Findeisen process (Bergeron 1935, Geofysiske Publikasjoner 12; Findeisen 1938, Meteorologische Zeitschrift 55, 121-133) — ice crystals growing at the expense of supercooled water droplets because the saturation vapor pressure over ice is lower than over liquid water — is the microphysical mechanism by which the transfer nucleus closes the projection gap: ice crystal growth by vapor deposition in mixed-phase clouds is the hydrometeor growth process that moves P from SatShadow toward the fixed fiber P*_t. The collision-coalescence process (Langmuir 1948, Science 108, 264-268) is the warm-rain analog: large collector drops grow by sweeping up smaller droplets, committing the precipitation through every independent collision event. Virga is the most direct empirical observation of TrShadow: falling hydrometeors that are visible as streaks below cloud base but do not reach the surface. Flash flooding is the Schwarzschild threshold crossing: when the calibration defect M_t(delta) rises as precipitation rate exceeds soil infiltration capacity and surface drainage, the surface-layer modes become spectrally trapped and runoff becomes self-reinforcing.
