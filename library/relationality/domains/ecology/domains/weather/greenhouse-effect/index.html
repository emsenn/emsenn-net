Greenhouse Effect

Formal definition

A GreenhouseRadiation triple (F,t,ℓ)(F, t, \ell):

(F∈Ft,  t∈T,  ℓ∈Locale)(F \in \mathcal{F}_t,\; t \in T,\; \ell \in \mathrm{Locale})

where:

  • Ft\mathcal{F}_t is the radiative flux fiber at optical-depth history tt — the complete Heyting algebra of radiative flux state propositions at tt; elements of Ft\mathcal{F}_t are statements about the spectral irradiance field I(ν,z)I(\nu, z) at wavelength ν\nu and altitude zz, the longwave and shortwave irradiance profiles F↓(z)F_{\downarrow}(z) and F↑(z)F_{\uparrow}(z), the net flux Fnet(z)F_\mathrm{net}(z), and the derived radiative quantities (optical depth profile τ(ν,z)\tau(\nu, z), brightness temperature Tb(ν)T_b(\nu), outgoing longwave radiation at the top of atmosphere) at locale ℓ\ell at history tt
  • t∈Tt \in T is the optical-depth history — the position in the history monoid; each step s∈Σs \in \Sigma advances the column through one absorbing layer (one pressure-altitude increment from the top of atmosphere downward); the composition s⋆ts \star t adds one more layer of gas below the current top-of-column level; the independence structure s⊥ts \perp t captures the fact that different spectral wavelength bands absorb independently of each other in the first approximation — adding optical depth in the CO2_2 15 μ\mum band does not change the optical depth in the H2_2O 6.3 μ\mum band or the atmospheric window
  • ℓ\ell is the locale — the geographical column; the locale’s surface type, temperature, and emissivity determine the boundary condition at the surface (the lower-boundary constraint on the optical-depth history)

The greenhouse nuclear pair. The fiber Ft\mathcal{F}_t at optical-depth history tt carries the two canonical nuclei. Let Σ\Sigma be the step monoid for the column site (T,J)(T, J). Each step s∈Σs \in \Sigma represents one spectral absorption event at wavelength νs\nu_s: a unit increment of optical depth in one spectral band. The commutation condition s⊥ts \perp t means the absorption event at wavelength νs\nu_s is genuinely independent of the current optical-depth history tt — absorbing in one spectral channel does not alter the absorption state in any other channel (the independent-band approximation that underlies radiative transfer in the atmosphere).

A flux state at or above the ERL for all bands is transfer-stable: every additional atmospheric layer below it has a preimage that restricts back.

The stratospheric cooling fingerprint is the diagnostic signature of the internal holograph: when tropospheric greenhouse forcing increases (more CO2_2), the tropospheric bulk T\mathcal{T} heats (the ERL rises, the lower ERL now emits from a higher, colder level), but the stratosphere above the tropopause P\mathcal{P} cools (it loses its radiation to space more efficiently because the tropopause OLR decreases). This simultaneous tropospheric warming and stratospheric cooling — the clearest observational fingerprint of greenhouse forcing as opposed to solar forcing (which would warm both) — is the internal holograph splitting: the bulk and boundary evolve in opposite directions under the projection πtrop\pi_\mathrm{trop}.

What this is

The greenhouse effect is the mechanism by which the atmosphere traps thermal radiation from the surface and re-emits it at a colder temperature, maintaining the surface at a temperature well above what the planet would have if it radiated directly to space. It is not a malfunction but the default state of any atmosphere containing polyatomic molecules: the same mechanism that makes Earth habitable (average surface temperature 288 K vs. a bare-rock effective temperature of 255 K) also makes Venus an extreme case (735 K vs. a bare-rock effective temperature of 227 K).

The physics is straightforward: the surface absorbs incoming shortwave solar radiation and re-emits as longwave (thermal infrared, roughly 4–100 μ\mum). Greenhouse gases in the atmosphere absorb this outgoing longwave and re-emit it in all directions. The upward half of that re-emission reaches space; the downward half returns to the surface (back-radiation). The effect is that the surface must be warmer than the effective radiating level to maintain the energy balance — the planet radiates the same total energy to space as it absorbs from the sun, but does so from the effective radiating level (altitude ∼\sim 5–9 km, temperature ∼\sim 255 K) rather than from the surface (temperature ∼\sim 288 K). The greenhouse effect is the 33 K difference.

The nuclear reading makes precise the two-directional character of radiative regulation. The optical-depth direction is backward-looking: how much absorption has accumulated in every spectral channel through every prior atmospheric layer? This is the restriction profile — the spectral optical depth τ(ν)\tau(\nu) at each wavelength. The ERL-commitment direction is forward-committing: for every independent spectral channel, does the current flux state have a preimage in the adjacent layer? This is the ERL condition — whether the flux state is already committed to outgoing longwave at the level where each spectral band becomes optically thin.

The restriction profile: spectral optical depth

The spectral optical depth profile τ(ν,z1→z2)\tau(\nu, z_1 \to z_2) is the restriction-profile quantity of the saturation nucleus. The Beer-Lambert law gives the fraction of radiation at frequency ν\nu transmitted through a path from z1z_1 to z2z_2:

T(ν,z1→z2)  =  exp⁡ ⁣(−τ(ν,z1→z2))\mathcal{T}(\nu, z_1 \to z_2) \;=\; \exp\!\bigl(-\tau(\nu, z_1 \to z_2)\bigr)

At each wavelength ν\nu, the atmosphere has an effective radiating level zERL(ν)z_\mathrm{ERL}(\nu) defined by τ(ν,zERL→∞)=1\tau(\nu, z_\mathrm{ERL} \to \infty) = 1: the level where the atmosphere becomes optically thin to space. The brightness temperature at the top of atmosphere:

Tb(ν)  =  T ⁣(zERL(ν))T_b(\nu) \;=\; T\!\bigl(z_\mathrm{ERL}(\nu)\bigr)

is the temperature at the effective radiating level for that wavelength. The total OLR integrates the Planck function at the ERL temperature over all frequencies.

When greenhouse gas concentration increases, zERL(ν)z_\mathrm{ERL}(\nu) rises (the atmosphere becomes optically thick at lower optical depths — higher altitudes). The temperature at the new, higher ERL is colder (following the lapse rate). So OLR decreases, creating a radiative imbalance — the instantaneous forcing. This is the projection gap [F,πt(F)][F, \pi_t(F)]: the current flux state FF (with updated optical depth history but old temperature profile) is not in Ft∗\mathcal{F}^*_t (the ERL is not yet at the right temperature to balance incoming solar).

Logarithmic forcing and the saturation nucleus ceiling

The forcing formula derived by Myhre, Highwood, Shine, and Stordal (1998, Geophysical Research Letters 25, 2715–2718):

ΔF  =  5.35⋅ln⁡ ⁣(CC0)  W m−2\Delta F \;=\; 5.35 \cdot \ln\!\left(\frac{C}{C_0}\right) \;\mathrm{W\,m^{-2}}

applies to CO2_2 in the range C∈[180,1000] ppmC \in [180, 1000]\,\mathrm{ppm}. For a doubling (C=2C0C = 2C_0): ΔF=5.35ln⁡(2)≈3.7 W m−2\Delta F = 5.35 \ln(2) \approx 3.7\,\mathrm{W\,m^{-2}}.

The logarithmic form is the saturation nucleus signature. The central CO2_2 absorption band near 15 μ\mum is already optically thick at pre-industrial concentrations: τ(15 μm)≫1\tau(15\,\mu\mathrm{m}) \gg 1 through the entire troposphere. Adding more CO2_2 cannot add more absorption at 15 μ\mum — the restriction profile at the band center is saturated in the sense of the saturation nucleus: σt(Fcenter)=Fcenter\sigma_t(F_\mathrm{center}) = F_\mathrm{center} at the band center, because the profile-class maximum is already achieved. Only the spectral wings of the 15 μ\mum band (at wavelengths slightly away from the center, where τ<1\tau < 1) are in SatShadow\mathrm{SatShadow} — their restriction profile has room to grow. Each doubling of CO2_2 adds the same finite increment of wing-channel absorption. This produces the logarithm: ΔF∝ln⁡(C/C0)\Delta F \propto \ln(C/C_0), not linearly proportional, because each marginal CO2_2 doubling saturates one more increment of the spectral wing, but the band center is already fixed.

This is the saturation nucleus’s marginal-content behavior: once the restriction profile of a spectral channel is saturated (σt\sigma_t fixes it), no further forcing is added by increasing that absorber. Growth continues only in the not-yet-saturated channels. The logarithm counts how many saturation events have occurred across the spectral wing profile.

Nuclear reading

Proposition 1 (Planck function = saturation nucleus ceiling; LTE = saturation fixed point). The local thermodynamic equilibrium (LTE) condition — that matter emits the Planck function B(ν,T)B(\nu, T) at its local temperature — is the condition σt(F)=F\sigma_t(F) = F for the spectral flux state FF at the layer. LTE holds when the layer has thermalized with the radiation field: every possible higher flux state in the restriction-profile class would require a hotter local temperature than the layer actually has. At altitudes below ∼70\sim 70 km (the homosphere), LTE is satisfied and the saturation nucleus is at its fixed point at every layer. Above ∼70\sim 70 km (the thermosphere), LTE fails: collisional de-excitation is rarer than radiative de-excitation, the populations of excited molecular states depart from the Boltzmann distribution, and σt(F)>F\sigma_t(F) > F — the saturation nucleus has a non-trivial gap: the radiative state is below its restriction-profile maximum because the population cannot equilibrate fast enough. □\square

Proposition 2 (ERL = transfer fixed point; atmospheric window = canonical TrShadow). A spectral flux state at wavelength ν\nu is in Fix(Δt)\mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) iff the altitude zz satisfies τ(ν,z→∞)≤1\tau(\nu, z \to \infty) \leq 1 — i.e., the altitude is at or above the effective radiating level for ν\nu. Below the ERL (τ(ν,z→∞)>1\tau(\nu, z \to \infty) > 1), the flux state is not transfer-stable: for the independent channel consisting of absorption at ν\nu, adding one more layer of absorber below zz changes the restriction to the surface, so the current state at zz cannot be found as the image of a state at the adjacent layer. Above the ERL (τ(ν,z→∞)<1\tau(\nu, z \to \infty) < 1), the flux state is transfer-stable: every independent spectral step from below has a preimage that restricts up to the current state — the atmosphere is optically thin here and surface radiation passes through. The atmospheric window at 8–12 μ\mum satisfies τ<1\tau < 1 through the entire troposphere: it is in Fix(Δt)\mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) throughout — transfer-stable from the surface to space. But the window has no optical depth history (σt(F)>F\sigma_t(F) > F because there is no accumulated absorption to back-recognize the surface emission as the maximum). Window flux is TrShadowtF\mathrm{TrShadow}^{\mathcal{F}}_t: ERL-committed, not optically thick. □\square

Proposition 3 (Radiative forcing = projection-gap size; CO2 doubling as SatShadow perturbation). When CO2_2 concentration doubles, the optical-depth history is updated: τ(ν,z)\tau(\nu, z) increases throughout the 15 μ\mum band (and wings). The saturation nucleus σt\sigma_t is now fixed at the new, higher optical depth: Fnew∈Fix(σtnew)F_\mathrm{new} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t^{\mathrm{new}}) — the absorption record is fully committed to the doubled CO2_2. But the temperature profile has not changed: the ERL at 15 μ\mum has risen by ∼160\sim 160 m (using a 6.5 K/km lapse rate: a new ERL at 1 K colder temperature radiates ∼1.9\sim 1.9 W/m² less). The transfer nucleus is not yet fixed: the new, higher ERL level does not yet have the right temperature to make FF transfer-stable in every spectral channel — there is no state in the adjacent (warmer, deeper) layer that restricts back to the new ERL-level flux. The projection gap [F,πt(F)][F, \pi_t(F)] has size λF\lambda_F corresponding to ΔF≈3.7 W m−2\Delta F \approx 3.7\,\mathrm{W\,m^{-2}} of instantaneous forcing — the forcing that must be overcome before the planet reaches a new radiative equilibrium. The warming required to close the gap: δT=ΔF/λclimate≈3.7/0.8≈3–5 K\delta T = \Delta F / \lambda_\mathrm{climate} \approx 3.7 / 0.8 \approx 3\text{--}5\,\mathrm{K} for a climate sensitivity parameter λclimate≈0.8 W m−2 K−1\lambda_\mathrm{climate} \approx 0.8\,\mathrm{W\,m^{-2}\,K^{-1}} (including feedbacks). □\square

Proposition 4 (Runaway greenhouse = Schwarzschild threshold; Venus as the crossed case). The RelationalHistoryFiberSpectralSchwarzschildCriticalEigenvalueHorizonOperator gives the Schwarzschild threshold rs(δt)=2Mt(δ)r_s(\delta_t) = 2M_t(\delta) — the value of the calibration defect at which no equilibrium projection exists. In the greenhouse context: the runaway greenhouse threshold is the Simpson-Komabayashi-Ingersoll limit, approximately 310 W/m² for a water-vapor-dominated atmosphere. Above this limit, increasing surface temperature increases water vapor (positive feedback), which increases absorption, which reduces OLR below the incoming solar flux, making the imbalance larger, not smaller. The calibration defect Mt(δ)M_t(\delta) grows without bound: no radiative equilibrium state exists in the fiber. At or above the threshold, Ft∗=∅\mathcal{F}^*_t = \emptyset: the doubly-settled set is empty. Venus has crossed this threshold: incoming solar (absorbed, after clouds reflect most) ≈160 W/m2\approx 160\,\mathrm{W/m^2}, but Venus’s massive CO2_2 atmosphere creates an optical depth so large that the ERL is at the top of the cloud deck, far from any equilibrium temperature that would satisfy both the saturation and transfer conditions simultaneously. The surface at 735 K emits 16,600 W/m² at the Stefan-Boltzmann law, all of which is re-absorbed by the 90 bar atmosphere before it can escape. Venus is in FreeShadowtF\mathrm{FreeShadow}^{\mathcal{F}}_t: neither the optical-depth history admits a maximum consistent state (the atmosphere is opaque at all wavelengths) nor is ERL commitment achievable (there is no spectral window through which a stable ERL can be established). □\square

Proposition 5 (Stratospheric cooling fingerprint = internal holograph diagnostic; troposphere-stratosphere split). The internal holograph (T,P,πtrop)(\mathcal{T}, \mathcal{P}, \pi_\mathrm{trop}) predicts the greenhouse fingerprint: when tropospheric CO2_2 increases, the tropopause-level OLR decreases (the internal projection gap λFtrop\lambda_F^\mathrm{trop} grows), so the stratosphere, which is heated by solar UV absorption in ozone and cooled by CO2_2 emission, finds its CO2_2 cooling channel enhanced: more CO2_2 means more emission from the stratosphere (which is in LTE but not optically thick), so the stratosphere cools. Troposphere warms, stratosphere cools — the two sides of the internal holograph boundary move in opposite directions. This is the primary observational fingerprint distinguishing greenhouse forcing from solar forcing: solar increases would warm both troposphere and stratosphere (more energy input); greenhouse increases warm the troposphere and cool the stratosphere (the ERL shifts, not the total input). Balloon radiosonde records and satellite MSU (Microwave Sounding Unit) data since 1979 show this split: tropospheric warming of +0.13 K/decade+0.13\,\mathrm{K/decade} and lower stratospheric cooling of −0.5 K/decade-0.5\,\mathrm{K/decade} (Santer et al. 2013, Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences). □\square

Proposition 6 (Non-adjointness; optically thin and optically thick are orthogonal shadow classes). The optical-depth history operator and the ERL commitment operator are not in Galois connection. The witnesses: the CO2_2-forced SatShadow state (high optical depth, fully saturated absorption record, but ERL not yet established at the new equilibrium temperature — the instantaneous post-forcing state after CO2_2 doubling) and the window-channel TrShadow state (ERL-committed from surface to space, but no optical depth history). If σt⊣Δt\sigma_t \dashv \Delta_t, every optically thick atmosphere would automatically produce a committed ERL — radiative forcing would be instantaneously self-resolved, and climate sensitivity would be zero. If Δt⊣σt\Delta_t \dashv \sigma_t, every ERL-committed state would automatically accumulate optical depth — the atmospheric window would not exist. Both witnesses are observed at every moment: the atmosphere contains CO2_2 (SatShadow in the band), water vapor absorption lines (SatShadow in those bands), and the atmospheric window (TrShadow). Non-adjointness is why climate sensitivity is positive and finite: the optical-depth forcing and the ERL response are genuinely independent operations that must both be resolved to reach equilibrium. □\square
