It’s not that COVID isn’t still here. It’s that most of us have trained ourselves to behave like it isn’t, and built our worlds around the enforcement of that behavior.

We’re in a moment where the virological risk—of repeat infection, of permanent immune disruption, of cumulative neurological damage—is still climbing. Transmission is constant. The virus mutates fast, stacks risk fast. There is no herd immunity horizon. No safety-in-numbers. Long COVID is still under-researched, still disabling people with one infection, and more reliably with each reinfection. The scientific consensus is not subtle: SARS-CoV-2 is airborne, cumulative, and actively destructive to our capacity to live, relate, and organize. This is happening.

And yet: packed conferences. Shared microphones. Benefit shows in basements. “Outdoor” events that mean twenty people under a tent, shoulder-to-shoulder. Posts in Signal, Instagram stories, TikToks from movement spaces—distro events, mutual aid meetings, affinity group hangouts—where nobody masks, and the only air mitigation is the breeze. This isn’t critique from a distance. This is what’s happening among us. We’ve all seen it. We’ve all participated in it.

This didn’t happen because we were lied to. It happened because the system was built to metabolize disruption into continuity. That includes this one. The feedback model is simple: detect instability, adapt outputs, reinforce stabilization. Narrative isn’t a story. It’s a loop-closer. It processes deviation from baseline into something manageable, legible, routable. Early COVID management did this via policy: flatten the curve, essential workers, masking as community care. But the deeper stabilization happened behaviorally: test before the show, isolate if you can, don’t go if you’re symptomatic, and if you do—well, just don’t tell.

Eventually, that processing burden shifted to individuals entirely. And we took it on. Not just as compromise, but as care. As participation.

What we’re seeing now is not state failure—it’s distributed, social stabilization of a permanent mass disabling event. Most people have stopped masking not because they’re unaware of the risks, but because the risk is now understood as background noise. Something acknowledged once and then maintained through narrative habits.

These narratives aren’t delusions. They’re entropy management techniques. They give form to an otherwise intolerable condition. “We test before big events.” “I just had it, so I’ve probably got antibodies.” “It’s outside, so it’s low risk.” “Everyone here is careful.” None of these are objectively safe. Some are barely even defensible. But they function. They reduce cognitive and social friction. They allow participation without pause. That’s all the system needs.

This is cybernetic settlerism functioning without a central directive. It routes incoherence into routable behavior. And it doesn’t need belief. It just needs enough people to move in sync, producing the affective and narrative signals that support throughput.

That’s how non-masking became default. Not through enforcement, but through stabilization. There’s no need to justify it anymore—it just is. The coherence is self-maintaining. The stories don’t have to convince anyone. They just have to get repeated.

Masking, in this context, is not just non-participation. It’s failed metabolization. It doesn’t support narrative closure. It creates entropy without offering an interpretation.

And so it feels reckless. Or selfish. Or paranoid. Even when it is none of those things. Even when it is simply a person breathing through a filter because they understand what’s in the air.

This is what we mean by entropic sabotage. Not that the act is violent or deliberate. But that it fails to resolve. It’s not routable. It doesn’t affirm the loop. It doesn’t help the system maintain coherence.

And in a system like this, failing to support coherence is the threat.

Even among anarchists. Even among abolitionists. Even among people who organize against the state and against capital. Because the logic of recursive feedback doesn’t belong to the state—it belongs to behavior. And behavior that becomes narratively stabilizing will reproduce power whether or not it believes in it.

We’re in a moment where we’ve trained ourselves to see unmasked faces in these spaces as trust signals. “This is a safe room.” “We’re real with each other.” “We’re not going to punish anyone for doing what they need to do.” And so the presence of a mask—especially one not explained, not signaled as immunocompromised or caregiving or temporary—disrupts that.

It doesn’t threaten safety. It threatens rhythm.

And rhythm is the real god here.

If a behavior can’t sync, can’t scale, can’t circulate—then it is treated like disruption. And that disruption is interpreted as harm.

Even when it’s just a mask.
Even when the real harm is in the air.
