The Illusion of Clarity in a Confused World

This concept explores the idea that modern society, through various ideological apparatuses, maintains a state of confusion to keep individuals passive and complacent.
Defines Ideological State Apparatus, Spectacle, Althusser's Trap

Hey.
You.
Yeah—you, reading this in a browser tab behind an Excel sheet or a Figma prototype or a Jira ticket.
Scrolling to figure out what the hell is going on in the world.
Curious. Concerned. Maybe even a little hopeful that with enough information, something might finally make sense.

With a healthy awareness of the irony, I’m going to try and explain something to you.
This place—this internet, this job, this mind you’ve been given to think with—it’s built to keep you confused.
That confusion? That’s not a glitch in the system.
That is the system.

You’re part of a class positioned with just enough proximity to the ruling class to believe you might influence it, and just enough proximity to the working class to convince yourself you’re not part of the problem.
You have a “voice,” but no microphone.
You have “access,” but no leverage.
And above all, you have curiosity—a thousand tabs of it, endlessly refreshing.

But here’s the thing.
Curiosity without clarity is captivity.

I. Confusion is a Class Relation

In Marxist terms, ideology isn’t just lies we’re told—it’s the total environment of thought that reproduces the material conditions of power. The dominant ideology of an era is the one that keeps the dominant class in power. So when you feel overwhelmed, over-informed, under-activated? That’s ideology doing its job.

Confusion, here, is not a personal failure or a foggy moment. It’s a social relation, a deliberate structure. One that keeps the bourgeois middle stratum—the white-collar “knowledge worker,” the “content consumer,” the manager, the anxious grad student—with just enough information to stay anxious, and just enough impotence to stay quiet.

You’re not meant to be clear.
You’re meant to feel like you should be.

II. Spectacle as Pacification

Guy Debord, writing about the spectacle, called this perfectly: we don’t just consume media—we consume representations of life in place of life. In spectacle society, reality is deferred and replaced. You know that something is wrong, but what you see is a thousand representations of what could be right. A better political take. A new framework. A fresh start—after one more scroll.

This has a function. It keeps you watching instead of acting. It keeps you informed instead of organized. It keeps you decoding meaning instead of creating it.

And if you’re bourgeois-adjacent, you’re especially vulnerable. You’ve been taught that understanding is a moral good. That reading enough books or following the right accounts is a kind of praxis.

Spoiler: it isn’t.

III. Althusser’s Trap

Louis Althusser’s concept of the Ideological State Apparatus (ISA) is where things start to get scalpel-sharp. The state doesn’t just rule by force (through Repressive State Apparatuses like the police or the military). It rules by ideology, through schools, media, religion, and—yes—the internet. These apparatuses produce subjects who misrecognize themselves as free individuals.

What’s that look like today?

You feel overwhelmed, alienated, like you’re doing your best to “understand” the world.
You feel like you are the problem—disorganized, too online, not radical enough.
You keep reading. You keep scrolling.
You remain still.

The brilliance of modern ISAs is they’ve turned confusion into a lifestyle. You are subjected through constant stimulation, and trained to mistake interpretation for intervention.

IV. The Anarchist Intervention: Let Go of Their Meaning

If you’re bourgeois-adjacent, you’re especially vulnerable.
You’ve been taught that understanding is a moral good. That if you read enough books, follow the right thinkers, nail the right takes—you’ll be one of the good ones. That clarity is something you earn through intellectual consumption.

And if you’re marginalized? You’ve been taught that your role is to provide clarity. To translate pain into legibility. To perform knowing in a way that affirms the worldview of the confused-but-curious middle class. The job description is always the same: make yourself understandable to power without ever threatening it.

But here’s the deeper twist:
Most of what you’re calling “understanding” is confusion with better branding.
What you’re calling “clarity” is just colonial legibility—the kind that demands your life be organized, labeled, and flattened into something a university or a LinkedIn post can comprehend.

To escape confusion, you can’t just “act.” You have to disbelieve.
You have to learn to stop trusting the symbols you were trained to read.
The map is not the territory.
The meaning was planted.

V. Kill the Spectacle Before It Speaks

Here’s where Debord loops back in. The spectacle isn’t just flashy images—it’s meaning that arrives pre-packaged. The meme that tells you how to feel. The infographic that explains the world. The headline that sparks outrage before you’ve even read the piece.

And the more you consume meaning that was made for you, the less capable you are of making your own.

If you want to stop being confused, the answer is not more clarity.
It’s fewer borrowed meanings.
It’s sitting with the discomfort of not knowing until you start to notice again. To feel meaning form in real time—through conversation, contradiction, care. Not consumption.

Clarity isn’t a light switch. It’s a fire you have to build with other people.
Out of real things. Not symbols.
