The Neogrammarian axiom — die Ausnahmslosigkeit der Lautgesetze, sound laws operate without exception (Osthoff and Brugmann 1878, Morphologische Untersuchungen) — is the claim that sound change maps are natural transformations: they commute with restriction maps H(i_{t₀,t}) and are therefore path-independent. w in L*_t (doubly settled: sound change history backward-recognizes w as the maximum form consistent with the accumulated phonological restriction profile AND paradigmatic integration forward-commits w through every independent morphological context — a regular form, the zero-projection-gap ideal; Modern English 'helped', Latin 'amavi', Sanskrit 'bharati'), w in SatShadow_t (phonologically committed, paradigmatically broken: the restriction maps have imprinted the suppletive or irregular phonological history, but the form does not forward-commit through all independent morphological contexts; Latin fero/tuli/latum deriving from three distinct PIE roots each with its own committed restriction profile; English go/went; the strong verb forms 'holp', 'clave', 'wrought' at the moment before analogical leveling replaces them), w in TrShadow_t (paradigmatically integrated but phonologically unrecognized: the analogically created form is paradigmatically committed through all independent morphological contexts but the sound change restriction history has not yet backward-recognized it; English 'cows' replacing 'kine', 'helped' replacing 'holp', 'worked' replacing 'wrought' — forms that satisfy paradigmatic forward-commitment before the restriction-profile class has stabilized around them), w in FreeShadow_t (opaque fossil: neither the phonological history recognizes the form nor any paradigmatic context sustains it; isolated relics, petrified idioms, the Latin ablative absolute as a morphological context that no longer generates forward preimages for most speakers). Sound change as natural transformation: the Neogrammarian principle states that for every sound law s, the restriction map H(i_{s,t}) commutes with the lexical projection — the sound-changed form at history s-star-t restricts back to exactly the predicted form at t, with no lexeme-by-lexeme exceptions. Verner's Law (1875, Zeitschrift fur vergleichende Sprachforschung 23, 97-130) resolves apparent Grimm exceptions by expanding the restriction profile: the 'exceptions' had a different PIE accentual position in their restriction map, making them regular under a more fine-grained topology. The Comparative Method is the algorithm for computing restriction maps H(i_{t₀,t}): given daughter languages as post-change states, reconstruct the proto-language restriction profile by finding the minimum common ancestor form *w such that H(i_{s_i,t})(*w) = w_i for each daughter. The star notation for reconstructed proto-forms (*pater, *wlkwos) is the restriction-map notation: *w is the unique pre-image of the observed daughter forms under the sound-change restriction maps.

Sound Change

Formal definition

A SoundChange triple (w,t,L)(w, t, L):

(w∈Lt,  t∈T,  L∈Language)(w \in \mathcal{L}_t,\; t \in T,\; L \in \mathrm{Language})

where:

  • Lt\mathcal{L}_t is the language fiber at history tt — the complete Heyting algebra of lexical form propositions at tt; elements of Lt\mathcal{L}_t are statements about the phonological form, morphological category, inflectional paradigm, and semantic value of lexemes in language LL at history tt; the fiber inherits its Heyting algebra structure from HtH_t
  • t∈Tt \in T is the analysis history — the position in the history monoid at which the language state is evaluated; each step s∈Σs \in \Sigma advances the language through one sound change event, one analogical reformation, one borrowing episode, or one paradigmatic leveling; the datable sound changes of a language family (Grimm’s Law, Verner’s Law, the Great Vowel Shift) are specific step atoms in Σ\Sigma
  • LL is the language — the speech community and its lexicon; the language determines the phoneme inventory, morphological type (fusional, agglutinative, isolating), and the set of inflectional contexts that each lexeme must appear in

The linguistic nuclear pair. The fiber HtH_t at history tt carries the two canonical nuclei, each corresponding to one of the two independent pressures that determine whether a lexical form is stable. Let Σ\Sigma be the step monoid for the language site (T,J)(T, J). Each step s∈Σs \in \Sigma represents one linguistic context event — one sound change application, one morphological deployment in a new paradigm cell, one speaker-to-speaker transmission. The commutation condition s⊥ts \perp t means context ss is genuinely independent of the current history tt: a sound change applying in unstressed syllables is independent of which root the word has; an analogical reformation of the accusative plural is independent of whether the nominative singular has already been leveled. The one-step extension is,t:t→s⋆ti_{s,t} : t \to s \star t is the history extended by one linguistic event; the restriction map H(is,t):Hs⋆t→HtH(i_{s,t}) : H_{s \star t} \to H_t sends a post-change lexical form back to its pre-change state.

where H(it0,t):Lt→Lt0H(i_{t_0,t}) : \mathcal{L}_t \to \mathcal{L}_{t_0} projects the current lexical form back to the language state at prior history t0t_0. The restriction profile quantity entering σt\sigma_t is the phonological history of the lexeme — the sequence of sound changes its phonological form has undergone, recorded in the restriction maps H(it0,t)(w)H(i_{t_0,t})(w). Every sound change that has applied to ww is recorded: H(it0,t)(w)H(i_{t_0,t})(w) is the form ww had at history t0t_0 — the pre-Grimm form, the pre-Verner form, the pre-Great-Vowel-Shift form. The Neogrammarian axiom as naturality. Osthoff and Brugmann’s 1878 manifesto claimed: die Ausnahmslosigkeit der Lautgesetze — sound laws operate without exception. In the nuclear reading, this is the claim that every sound change ss is a natural transformation of presheaves on TT: the sound change map ϕs:Lt→Ls⋆t\phi_s : \mathcal{L}_t \to \mathcal{L}_{s \star t} must commute with all restriction maps H(it0,t)H(i_{t_0,t}). A sound law that fails naturality would claim: the sound change applies to some tokens of a phoneme but not others based on the lexical identity — sound change is path-dependent, tracking which specific word is changing rather than which phonological environment it occurs in. The Neogrammarians denied this: sound change is phonetically conditioned (depends only on the phonological environment, not the lexical item) and therefore natural. The restriction map H(is,t)(w)H(i_{s,t})(w) is the unique pre-image of a post-change form — there are no lexeme-by-lexeme exceptions.

Linguistically: ww is paradigmatically integrated iff for every independent inflectional slot and derivational context the language provides, there exists a form that deploys ww in that context and returns to ww when the context is removed. Δt(w)\Delta_t(w) is the minimum paradigmatically integrated form above ww: the smallest lexical form that survives every independent morphological context and is at least as specified as ww.

The suppletive verb fero / tuli / latum fails paradigmatic integration: given the stem tuli, there is no forward preimage through the present-tense context step spres⊥ts_{\mathrm{pres}} \perp t — there is no present-tense form that restricts back to tuli. The language switches to a different suppletive stem (fero) for the present. The context spress_{\mathrm{pres}} is genuinely independent of the phonological history of tuli (the present tense is required regardless of what the perfect stem is), so the failure of the forward preimage is a genuine failure of Fix(Δt)\mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) membership, not a topology artifact.

The internal holograph: the inflectional paradigm. Each lexeme carries an internal holographic structure (F,P,πpar)(\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{P}, \pi_{\mathrm{par}}):

  • P\mathcal{P} = the paradigm boundary — the full inflected paradigm; all person/number/tense/mood/voice forms; the complete set of contexts the lexeme must be deployable in

The internal holograph is complete iff F=P\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{P} — when the stem specification IS the paradigm, with no unpredictable forms. Regular verbs in a fully agglutinative language (Turkish, Finnish, Swahili) have complete internal holographs: the stem plus the systematic affixation rules determines every form without exception. Suppletive paradigms have incomplete internal holographs: πpar\pi_{\mathrm{par}} must switch stems, and the internal projection gap λfpar=∣[f,πpar(f)]∣−1\lambda_f^{\mathrm{par}} = |[f, \pi_{\mathrm{par}}(f)]| - 1 is nonzero for the defective or suppletive principal parts.

What this is

Sound change is the systematic, gradual shift in the phonological forms of words across speaker generations — the mechanism by which Latin pater becomes Italian padre, French père, Spanish padre, Portuguese pai, Romanian tată. The paired discipline analogical leveling is the mechanism that regularizes paradigmatically irregular forms by replacing them with forms built on the productive pattern — by which Old English holp becomes helped, Latin honor (from earlier honos) stabilizes to honor, Greek aorist irregularities are leveled in the Koine. Together they constitute the two independent pressures on every lexical form in every language at every moment: the phonological history pulling it toward the outcome its sound-change restriction profile demands, and the paradigmatic pressure pulling it toward the regular inflectional pattern the language supports.

The core insight of the nuclear reading: the Neogrammarian debate of the 1870s is the argument about whether σt\sigma_t is well-defined as a function, and the response to the Neogrammarians (analogy, borrowing, dialect mixing) is the argument that Δt\Delta_t is not always natural. The Neogrammarians (Leskien, Brugmann, Osthoff, Verner, Paul) argued that sound change is exceptionless — a claim that σt\sigma_t acts as a natural transformation on the lexical presheaf, with no lexeme-specific exceptions. Their critics (Schmidt, Whitney, later Schuchardt) argued that the boundaries between sound changes are fuzzy, that dialect borrowing introduces exceptions, that individual words resist change — a claim that σt\sigma_t sometimes fails naturality. The nuclear reading shows both are right at different levels: σt\sigma_t is natural on the phonological stratum (the Neogrammarians were right that sound change is phonetically conditioned), but the Grothendieck topology JJ is not arboreal (Schmidt was right that languages influence each other through dialect contact, changing which steps are independent).

Grimm’s Law, Verner’s Law, and the restriction profile

Grimm’s Law (Jacob Grimm 1822, Deutsche Grammatik, 2nd ed.) is the most celebrated natural transformation in linguistics. It states that Proto-Indo-European stop consonants underwent systematic shifts in Proto-Germanic:

PIE  ∗p,∗t,∗k  →  PGmc  ∗f,∗θ,∗x  (voiceless stops→voiceless fricatives)\mathrm{PIE}\; *p, *t, *k \;\to\; \mathrm{PGmc}\; *f, *\theta, *x \;\quad (\text{voiceless stops} \to \text{voiceless fricatives})

PIE  ∗b,∗d,∗g  →  PGmc  ∗p,∗t,∗k  (voiced stops→voiceless stops)\mathrm{PIE}\; *b, *d, *g \;\to\; \mathrm{PGmc}\; *p, *t, *k \;\quad (\text{voiced stops} \to \text{voiceless stops})

PIE  ∗bh,∗dh,∗gh  →  PGmc  ∗b,∗d,∗g  (voiced aspirates→voiced stops)\mathrm{PIE}\; *b^h, *d^h, *g^h \;\to\; \mathrm{PGmc}\; *b, *d, *g \;\quad (\text{voiced aspirates} \to \text{voiced stops})

This is a natural transformation ϕGrimm:LPIE⇒LPGmc\phi_{\mathrm{Grimm}} : \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PIE}} \Rightarrow \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PGmc}}. The naturality condition is the Neogrammarian axiom: for every subsequent phonological context step s⊥tPGmcs \perp t_{\mathrm{PGmc}}, the diagram commutes — applying Grimm’s Law and then restricting to any sub-context gives the same result as restricting first and then applying. There are no exceptions in Sanskrit pitar, Greek pater, Gothic fadar, English father — the PIE *p maps to PGmc *f everywhere it occurs.

Verner’s Law (Karl Verner 1875, Zeitschrift für vergleichende Sprachforschung 23, 97–130): Grimm’s Law appeared to have exceptions — some PIE voiceless stops produced voiced fricatives in PGmc rather than voiceless. Verner showed that the exceptions were governed by the PIE accent position: when the PIE accent fell on the syllable following the consonant, Grimm’s fricatives became voiced. The restriction map H(it0,tPIE)H(i_{t_0,t_{\mathrm{PIE}}}) must include the accentual position: H(it0,tPIE)(∗patar)H(i_{t_0,t_{\mathrm{PIE}}})(*\mathrm{patar}) has accent on the second syllable, while H(it0,tPIE)(∗paˊter)H(i_{t_0,t_{\mathrm{PIE}}})(*\mathrm{p\acute{a}ter}) has accent on the first. The topology of the PIE history site must be fine enough to record this accentual distinction for the restriction maps to make Verner’s Law natural.

Verner’s resolution is the paradigmatic statement of the Neogrammarian method: apparent exceptions to a sound law are not failures of naturality — they are evidence that the restriction profile is not fine enough. Refine the topology (include the accentual position in the history site), and naturality is restored. Sound laws have no exceptions; what appear to be exceptions are evidence of additional conditioning factors that must be included in the restriction map.

The Comparative Method as restriction-map computation

The Comparative Method (developed across the 19th century by Bopp, Rask, Grimm, Schleicher; systematized by Brugmann and Delbrück in the Grundriss der vergleichenden Grammatik, 1886–1900) is the algorithm for computing restriction maps H(it0,t)H(i_{t_0,t}) from observed daughter language data. Given daughter languages L1,…,LnL_1, \ldots, L_n with attested forms w1,…,wnw_1, \ldots, w_n at their respective histories t1,…,tnt_1, \ldots, t_n, the Comparative Method reconstructs the proto-language form ∗w0*w_0 at ancestral history t0t_0 such that:

H(it0,ti)(∗w0)=wifor all i=1,…,nH(i_{t_0, t_i})(*w_0) = w_i \quad \text{for all } i = 1, \ldots, n

The star (*) notation — *pater, *wlkwos, *septm — IS the restriction-map notation: ∗w*w is the unique pre-image in the language fiber at the ancestral history that maps to all the attested daughter forms under the respective sound-change restriction maps. The reconstructed form is the element of Lt0\mathcal{L}_{t_0} whose restriction-profile class intersects all observed daughter forms.

The Indo-European laryngeal hypothesis (Saussure 1879, Mémoire sur le système primitif des voyelles dans les langues indo-européennes) exemplifies the power of restriction-map reasoning: Ferdinand de Saussure, at age 21, predicted the existence of unknown phonemes in Proto-Indo-European — now called laryngeals, conventionally written H1,H2,H3H_1, H_2, H_3 — from the distribution of vowel length and quality in the attested daughter languages. The laryngeals were later confirmed by the decipherment of Hittite (Hrozný 1915), which preserved them as phonetically realized consonants. Saussure had inferred the existence of elements in LtPIE\mathcal{L}_{t_\mathrm{PIE}} that must exist for the restriction maps to the attested daughters to be natural transformations.

Grimm’s Law as topology change; dialect continua as non-arboreal topology

The wave theory (Schmidt 1872, Die Verwandtschaftsverhältnisse der indogermanischen Sprachen) challenged Schleicher’s family-tree (Stammbaum) model: rather than clean branching splits, language change propagates as waves across a dialect continuum, with each change having its own isogloss — the geographic boundary between the forms that have undergone the change and the forms that have not. In nuclear terms: the wave theory is the claim that the Grothendieck topology JJ on the language history site is not arboreal — the independence structure of dialect steps is not a tree. Two dialects that appear to have branched independently may share covering families from areal contact, making their respective history steps s1⊥Js2s_1 \perp^J s_2 false — they are not genuinely independent.

The High German consonant shift (the sound change that distinguishes Standard German and Yiddish from Low German, Dutch, and English) illustrates this: the shift progressed as a wave from south to north, with partial application in the Rhineland and Rhine Franconian dialects. The Grothendieck topology of the Germanic dialect continuum records which dialect-steps are independent: the shift applied fully in Alemannic and Bavarian (complete topology change), partially in Rhine Franconian (partial topology change, covering families shared with the northern dialects), and not at all in Saxon and Low Franconian (topology unchanged). The High German / Low German boundary — the Uerdinger line, the Maken/Machen line, the Appel/Apfel line — are the boundaries of partial topology change.

Paradigmatic integration and the three principal parts

The principal parts of a Latin verb — the four forms (amō, amāre, amāvī, amātum) from which all other forms can be derived — are exactly the minimal generating set for the internal holograph (F,P,πpar)(\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{P}, \pi_{\mathrm{par}}). A fully regular Latin verb has a complete internal holograph: given the four principal parts, πpar\pi_{\mathrm{par}} determines all 120+ inflected forms (six tenses × three moods × two voices × six persons/numbers, plus participles, infinitives, gerunds, supine). The four principal parts are the minimal boundary specification for the paradigm interior.

An irregular verb breaks the internal holograph in specific ways:

Suppletive paradigms: ferō (I carry, present stem fer-) / tulī (I carried, perfect stem tul- from PIE *tle-) / lātum (carried, supine from PIE *tla-). The three stems derive from three different PIE roots with completely disjoint restriction profiles. The internal projection gap λfpar\lambda_f^{\mathrm{par}} is maximal: the perfect stem tul- has no forward preimage through the present-tense context step (the present ferō is not obtainable by regular derivation from tul-). Three distinct restriction-profile chains converge on what modern speakers treat as a single paradigm.

Defective paradigms: Latin ōdī (“I hate”), meminī (“I remember”), coepī (“I began”) — perfect forms with present meaning, lacking a genuine present tense. The forward-preimage failure is absolute: there is no present-tense step spress_{\mathrm{pres}} that has a forward preimage for these forms. Δt(w)>w\Delta_t(w) > w because the minimum paradigmatically integrated form above odi\mathrm{odi} would require inventing a present stem that the Latin language does not supply. The defective verb is the paradigm with a permanent projection gap that the language’s morphological resources cannot close.

Analogy and the TrShadow trajectory

Analogical leveling is the process by which speakers create new forms on the model of the most productive pattern in the paradigm. In nuclear terms, analogical creation is the movement of a form from the SatShadow or FreeShadow sector toward Lt∗\mathcal{L}^*_t by producing a form that has a forward preimage through the previously missing context step — at the cost of abandoning the restriction profile of the phonologically committed predecessor.

Under JJ, the ablaut steps sablaut⊥ts_{\mathrm{ablaut}} \perp t are in the independent step set — they are genuine morphological contexts. The phoneme as a quality

The phoneme is a quality — a natural transformation:

ϕphon:Xlexeme⇒Vphoneme\phi_{\mathrm{phon}} : X_{\mathrm{lexeme}} \Rightarrow V_{\mathrm{phoneme}}

assigning to each lexeme type at history tt its phonological form in the phoneme inventory Vphoneme(t)V_{\mathrm{phoneme}}(t). The naturality condition for ϕphon\phi_{\mathrm{phon}}: the phonological form of a lexeme must commute with history restriction — the phoneme assignment must be path-independent of which sequence of sound changes produced the current form. This is exactly what Grimm’s Law asserts for the Germanic consonant system: the phoneme assignment of fish (PIE *peysk-, PGmc *fiskaz) is path-independent — it doesn’t matter which specific speakers carried the p → f change; the restriction map H(itPIE,tPGmc)H(i_{t_\mathrm{PIE}, t_\mathrm{PGmc}}) commutes with the phoneme assignment. A sound change that fails naturality would mean: the same phoneme has a different descendant in different words — which is exactly what the Neogrammarians denied.

The phoneme inventory as the value presheaf Vphoneme(t)V_{\mathrm{phoneme}}(t): the presheaf assigns to each history tt the phoneme inventory of the language at that stage. Sound mergers (two phonemes collapsing to one, as in the cot-caught merger in American English) are restriction maps that reduce the fiber: ∣Vphoneme(t2)∣<∣Vphoneme(t1)∣|V_{\mathrm{phoneme}}(t_2)| < |V_{\mathrm{phoneme}}(t_1)| for t1<t2t_1 < t_2. Nuclear reading

A SoundChange triple (w, t, L) — a lexical form w in the language fiber at history t over language L — has shadow class determined by two independent directions of linguistic stabilization: the phonological history operator (saturation nucleus) and the paradigmatic integration operator (transfer nucleus). The Neogrammarian axiom — die Ausnahmslosigkeit der Lautgesetze, sound laws operate without exception (Osthoff and Brugmann 1878, Morphologische Untersuchungen) — is the claim that sound change maps are natural transformations: they commute with restriction maps H(i_{t₀,t}) and are therefore path-independent. w in L*_t (doubly settled: sound change history backward-recognizes w as the maximum form consistent with the accumulated phonological restriction profile AND paradigmatic integration forward-commits w through every independent morphological context — a regular form, the zero-projection-gap ideal; Modern English 'helped', Latin 'amavi', Sanskrit 'bharati'), w in SatShadow_t (phonologically committed, paradigmatically broken: the restriction maps have imprinted the suppletive or irregular phonological history, but the form does not forward-commit through all independent morphological contexts; Latin fero/tuli/latum deriving from three distinct PIE roots each with its own committed restriction profile; English go/went; the strong verb forms 'holp', 'clave', 'wrought' at the moment before analogical leveling replaces them), w in TrShadow_t (paradigmatically integrated but phonologically unrecognized: the analogically created form is paradigmatically committed through all independent morphological contexts but the sound change restriction history has not yet backward-recognized it; English 'cows' replacing 'kine', 'helped' replacing 'holp', 'worked' replacing 'wrought' — forms that satisfy paradigmatic forward-commitment before the restriction-profile class has stabilized around them), w in FreeShadow_t (opaque fossil: neither the phonological history recognizes the form nor any paradigmatic context sustains it; isolated relics, petrified idioms, the Latin ablative absolute as a morphological context that no longer generates forward preimages for most speakers). Sound change as natural transformation: the Neogrammarian principle states that for every sound law s, the restriction map H(i_{s,t}) commutes with the lexical projection — the sound-changed form at history s-star-t restricts back to exactly the predicted form at t, with no lexeme-by-lexeme exceptions. Verner's Law (1875, Zeitschrift fur vergleichende Sprachforschung 23, 97-130) resolves apparent Grimm exceptions by expanding the restriction profile: the 'exceptions' had a different PIE accentual position in their restriction map, making them regular under a more fine-grained topology. The Comparative Method is the algorithm for computing restriction maps H(i_{t₀,t}): given daughter languages as post-change states, reconstruct the proto-language restriction profile by finding the minimum common ancestor form *w such that H(i_{s_i,t})(*w) = w_i for each daughter. The star notation for reconstructed proto-forms (*pater, *wlkwos) is the restriction-map notation: *w is the unique pre-image of the observed daughter forms under the sound-change restriction maps.
