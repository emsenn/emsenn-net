2025-12-26 A collection of objects, morphisms between them, and a composition operation satisfying associativity and identity laws — the basic organizational unit of abstract mathematics.

A category C consists of a collection of objects, a collection of morphisms between objects, and a composition operation satisfying two laws.

For every pair of objects A and B, there is a collection Hom(A, B) of morphisms from A to B. For every composable pair f:A→Bf : A \to B and g:B→Cg : B \to C, there is a composite g∘f:A→Cg \circ f : A \to C. For every object A, there is an identity morphism idA:A→A\mathrm{id}_A : A \to A. These satisfy:

  • Associativity: h∘(g∘f)=(h∘g)∘fh \circ (g \circ f) = (h \circ g) \circ f for all composable triples.
  • Unit laws: f∘idA=ff \circ \mathrm{id}_A = f and idB∘f=f\mathrm{id}_B \circ f = f for every f:A→Bf : A \to B.

That is the entire definition. Objects, morphisms, composition, two laws. Everything else in category theory is built from this.

A category studies objects through their morphisms rather than through their internal structure. Two objects that are isomorphic — connected by invertible morphisms in both directions — are interchangeable for all categorical purposes. What an object IS is what it does, measured by its morphisms to and from other objects.

Standard examples: Set (sets and functions), Grp (groups and homomorphisms), Top (topological spaces and continuous maps), Vect (vector spaces and linear maps). Each captures a mathematical domain by specifying what its objects are and what structure-preserving maps between them look like.

A partial order is a category where there is at most one morphism between any two objects. The morphism from a to b exists when a≤ba \leq b. Composition is transitivity. Identity morphisms are reflexivity. This is called a thin category — the simplest nontrivial example, and the bridge between order theory and category theory.
