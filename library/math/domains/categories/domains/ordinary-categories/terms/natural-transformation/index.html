2025-12-26 A morphism between functors — a coherent family of morphisms that commutes with every arrow in the source category.

A natural transformation α:F⇒G\alpha : F \Rightarrow G between two functors F,G:C→DF, G : C \to D assigns to each object c in C a morphism αc:F(c)→G(c)\alpha_c : F(c) \to G(c) in D, such that for every morphism f:c→c′f : c \to c' in C, the following square commutes:

G(f)∘αc=αc′∘F(f)G(f) \circ \alpha_c = \alpha_{c'} \circ F(f)

The commutativity condition is the substance of “natural.” It says that α\alpha does not depend on arbitrary choices. Transforming an object and then mapping it forward gives the same result as mapping it forward and then transforming it. The components αc\alpha_c are coherent across the entire category — they fit together into a single, globally consistent mapping from F to G.

A natural transformation is to functors what a functor is to categories and what a morphism is to objects. Each level maps between structures at the level below, and each level preserves the relevant structure. Morphisms preserve nothing on their own — they are the arrows. Functors preserve composition and identity. Natural transformations preserve the naturality square — the coherence between how two functors interact with morphisms.

Natural transformations are the morphisms in the functor category [C,D][C, D]. The objects of [C,D][C, D] are functors from C to D. The morphisms are natural transformations between them. Composition of natural transformations is defined componentwise: (β∘α)c=βc∘αc(\beta \circ \alpha)_c = \beta_c \circ \alpha_c.

A natural transformation is a natural isomorphism if every component αc\alpha_c is an isomorphism. Natural isomorphisms express that two constructions are “the same” in the strongest sense available — same result at every object, coherently across the entire category. Two functors related by a natural isomorphism are interchangeable for all categorical purposes.

Mac Lane said: “I didn’t invent categories to study functors; I invented them to study natural transformations.” The naturality square is the reason category theory exists. Without it, there is no way to say that a construction is “canonical” or “choice-free” — natural transformations are the formal tool for making that claim precise.
