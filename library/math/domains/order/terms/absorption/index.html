2026-03-30 The lattice law tying meet and join together: a ∧ (a ∨ b) = a and a ∨ (a ∧ b) = a. Combining with something larger and then taking the smaller recovers the original.

The absorption laws are the two identities that tie meet and join together in a lattice:

a∧(a∨b)=aa∨(a∧b)=aa \wedge (a \vee b) = a \qquad a \vee (a \wedge b) = a

The first says: joining aa with bb gives something at least as large as aa, and meeting that with aa recovers aa. The second says: meeting aa with bb gives something at most as large as aa, and joining that with aa recovers aa.

Absorption is what makes a lattice a lattice rather than just a set with two unrelated operations. Without absorption, meet and join could behave independently. Absorption forces them to interact: each operation partially undoes the other.

A lattice can be defined equivalently as a set with two binary operations satisfying commutativity, associativity, idempotence, and absorption. The partial order is then recoverable: a≤ba \leq b iff a∧b=aa \wedge b = a.
