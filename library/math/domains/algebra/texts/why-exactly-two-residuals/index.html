Why Exactly Two Residuals?

2026-03-31
Abstract

A product has two argument positions. Each position generates one residual. This is why predicates decompose into extending and restricting — and why there is no third mode.

When you multiply two numbers, the product has two inputs: a×ba \times b. If you know the product is at most cc and you know one of the inputs, you can ask what the other input must have been.

“Given aa and that a×b≤ca \times b \leq c, what can bb be?” This is division: b≤c/ab \leq c / a.

“Given bb and that a×b≤ca \times b \leq c, what can aa be?” This is also division: a≤c/ba \leq c / b.

For numbers, these are the same operation (division is commutative). But for non-commutative operations — like function composition, or word concatenation, or predicate-argument combination — the two questions have different answers. “What goes on the left?” and “what goes on the right?” are genuinely different questions.

These two questions generate two residuals:

a⋅b≤ciffb≤a\ciffa≤c/ba \cdot b \leq c \quad\text{iff}\quad b \leq a \backslash c \quad\text{iff}\quad a \leq c / b

The left residual a\ca \backslash c: “what must go on the right of aa to stay below cc?” The right residual c/bc / b: “what must go on the left of bb to stay below cc?”

There are exactly two because the product a⋅ba \cdot b has exactly two argument positions: left and right. A ternary operation would have three residuals. A unary operation would have one (or zero). But a binary operation — which is what combination IS — has two.

Lambek (1958) proved this for predicate-argument combination in natural language. A verb takes a subject and an object. These are its two argument positions. The two residuals are the two ways the verb can interact with its arguments: it can extend forward to consume the object (right residual // ), or an argument can restrict backward to satisfy the verb’s requirements (left residual \\backslash).

Lawvere (1969) found the same structure in logic: existential quantification ∃\exists (extends a predicate outward) and universal quantification ∀\forall (restricts a predicate inward) are the two residuals of substitution. Two, because substitution has two sides: “substituting into a predicate” and “substituting from a context.”

When the product is commutative (a⋅b=b⋅aa \cdot b = b \cdot a), the two residuals collapse into one: a\c=c/aa \backslash c = c / a. But even then, the adjunction has two SIDES — the product side (combining, generating, extending) and the implication side (testing, constraining, restricting). The two modes of interaction persist even when they share a single formal operation.

This is not a classification we impose. It is a consequence of the fact that combination takes two inputs. Two inputs, two residuals, two modes. Extending and restricting.
