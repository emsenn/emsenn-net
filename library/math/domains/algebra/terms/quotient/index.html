2026-03-31 The structure obtained by identifying elements that are equivalent under some relation. Collapsing distinctions that the relation says do not matter.

A quotient of a structure identifies elements that are equivalent under some relation, collapsing the distinctions the relation says do not matter.

Given a set SS and an equivalence relation ∼\sim on SS, the quotient S/∼S / {\sim} is the set of equivalence classes: each class groups the elements that are equivalent to each other.

The integers modulo 3: 0∼3∼6∼…0 \sim 3 \sim 6 \sim \ldots, 1∼4∼7∼…1 \sim 4 \sim 7 \sim \ldots, 2∼5∼8∼…2 \sim 5 \sim 8 \sim \ldots. The quotient Z/3\mathbb{Z}/3 has three elements: the classes [0][0], [1][1], [2][2]. Addition and multiplication pass through to the quotient, making it a ring.

For algebraic structures (groups, rings, lattices), a quotient must respect the operations: the equivalence relation must be a congruence, meaning equivalent inputs give equivalent outputs. The quotient then inherits the operations of the original.

Quotients connect to free algebras: every algebra on a set of generators is a quotient of the free algebra on those generators. The free algebra imposes no extra relations; a quotient imposes some. The four-valued bilattice is the free bounded distributive bilattice on one generator (Fitting 2002). Any other bounded distributive bilattice generated by one element is a quotient of it — obtained by imposing additional equations.
