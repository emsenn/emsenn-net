paracosm

2026-03-05

A paracosm is a detailed, internally structured imaginary world, typically developed over an extended period through sustained imaginative engagement. The term originates in developmental psychology, introduced by Robert Silvey and Stephen MacKeith in the 1970s to describe the elaborate fantasy worlds constructed by children, and later elaborated by David Cohen and MacKeith in Creating Imaginary Worlds (1991). Their research emphasized the process of paracosmic creation—the interlinked, iteratively developing properties of the imagined domain—rather than the specific content or scale of any particular world.

In media studies and literary theory, the term has come to denote any sustained imaginative construction that exhibits internal coherence, developmental continuity, and sufficient structural depth to support multiple narrative realizations. Authors such as J.R.R. Tolkien, Ursula K. Le Guin, and Stephen King are frequently cited as paracosmic creators, though the concept extends beyond fiction to encompass speculative philosophical systems, game worlds, and collaborative imaginative projects.

A paracosm is distinct from a secondary world in emphasis: the paracosm foregrounds the phenomenology and psychology of world-creation (how the creator relates to and develops the imagined domain), while the secondary world foregrounds the literary and structural properties of the resulting artifact. A single creative project may be both. The emsean paracosm exemplifies this overlap.

The formal treatment of paracosms as categorical structures appears in the Reproducible Paracosm framework, which models paracosms as sketches admitting modal endofunctors and defines conditions under which a paracosm remains invariant across retellings or re-enactments.
