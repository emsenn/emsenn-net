Combinatorial Scent Mereology

2026-03-03 by emsenn
Abstract

Odor percepts as mereological composites of olfactory receptor activation subsets, situated within a hyperbolic manifold.

<p>Combinatorial Scent Mereology (CSM) is a framework for modeling olfactory perception. Its central claim: odor percepts aren&rsquo;t point-like features in a Euclidean space — they&rsquo;re <em>mereological wholes</em> composed from overlapping subsets of olfactory receptor activations, situated within a manifold of negative curvature.</p> <p>The framework connects three bodies of work: the neuroscience of olfactory receptor (OR) binding, the mathematics of hyperbolic geometry, and classical <a href="../../philosophy/terms/mereology.md" class="link-internal">mereology</a>. What emerges is a model that explains several puzzling features of smell — categorical boundaries, non-metric similarity, and the existence of &ldquo;fantasy odors&rdquo; that no molecule produces — while opening a computational approach to synthetic scent design.</p> <h2 id="the-problem-with-euclidean-odor-space">The problem with Euclidean odor-space</h2> <p>Most models of olfactory perception attempt to place odors in a Euclidean feature space: each odorant is a point, and perceptual similarity corresponds to Euclidean distance. This approach struggles with three well-documented phenomena.</p> <p><strong>Non-metric similarity judgments.</strong> Human odor similarity ratings violate the triangle inequality. For percepts <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span>, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>C</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">C</annotation></semantics></math></span>, psychophysical data regularly show patterns where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> is similar to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span> is similar to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>C</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">C</annotation></semantics></math></span>, but <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> is dissimilar to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>C</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">C</annotation></semantics></math></span> — a relationship that can&rsquo;t hold in Euclidean space but arises naturally in negatively curved geometries (Keller et al. 2017; Haddad et al. 2010).</p> <p><strong>Category discontinuities.</strong> Small changes in molecular structure — a shift in chain length, a branching position — can produce disproportionately large perceptual changes (Rossiter 1996). This categorical behavior suggests the underlying geometry has sharp boundaries, not the smooth gradients of a Euclidean manifold.</p> <p><strong>Sparse manifold occupation.</strong> Computational work shows that only a small fraction of the theoretical OR activation space is occupied by known molecules, natural or synthetic (Sanchez-Lengeling et al. 2019). The space is mostly empty — but that emptiness isn&rsquo;t perceptually empty.</p> <h2 id="hyperbolic-odor-manifolds">Hyperbolic odor-manifolds</h2> <h3 id="or-tuning-graphs-are-tree-like">OR tuning graphs are tree-like</h3> <p>Mammalian olfactory receptors don&rsquo;t bind a single molecule each. They display broad, overlapping ligand-binding profiles organized in branching similarity hierarchies (Mainland et al. 2014; Haddad et al. 2008). Receptor <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>R</mi><mn>1</mn></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">R_1</annotation></semantics></math></span> might bind molecules <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">{</mo><mi>a</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>b</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>c</mi><mo stretchy="false">}</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\{a, b, c\}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, receptor <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>R</mi><mn>2</mn></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">R_2</annotation></semantics></math></span> might bind <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo stretchy="false">{</mo><mi>b</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>c</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>d</mi><mo stretchy="false">}</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\{b, c, d\}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, and these overlapping profiles form tree-like structures when represented as a graph.</p> <p>Tree-like metric graphs embed quasi-isometrically in hyperbolic space — this is a consequence of Mikhail Gromov&rsquo;s work on hyperbolic groups (Gromov 1987). The key property: in a hyperbolic space <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">H</mi><mi>n</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{H}^n</annotation></semantics></math></span>, the volume of a ball grows exponentially with its radius. This accommodates the branching, hierarchical structure of OR binding far more naturally than a flat Euclidean space, where volume grows polynomially.</p> <p><strong>Proposition.</strong> The OR similarity graph <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>G</mi><mrow><mi>O</mi><mi>R</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">G_{OR}</annotation></semantics></math></span> admits a low-distortion embedding into a hyperbolic space <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">H</mi><mi>n</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{H}^n</annotation></semantics></math></span> for small <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span>.</p> <p>This means distances in the OR binding landscape grow exponentially from any reference point — which is consistent with the empirical finding that odor similarity judgments are non-metric.</p> <h3 id="evidence-from-psychophysics">Evidence from psychophysics</h3> <p>Andreas Keller and colleagues showed that odor similarity judgments exhibit triangle inequality violations: for percepts <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span>, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>C</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">C</annotation></semantics></math></span>, data often show</p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>d</mi><mi>H</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>A</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>C</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>≰</mo><msub><mi>d</mi><mi>H</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>A</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>B</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>+</mo><msub><mi>d</mi><mi>H</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>B</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>C</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d_H(A, C) \not\leq d_H(A, B) + d_H(B, C)</annotation></semantics></math></span><p>where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>d</mi><mi>H</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d_H</annotation></semantics></math></span> is the distance function (Keller et al. 2017). Hyperbolic embeddings achieve lower distortion than Euclidean ones when reconstructing olfactory perceptual maps (Haddad et al. 2010). Rafi Haddad and colleagues&rsquo; work on global features of odor similarity provides further evidence that the metric structure of odor-space is negatively curved.</p> <h2 id="mereological-structure-of-percepts">Mereological structure of percepts</h2> <h3 id="activation-subsets-as-parts">Activation subsets as parts</h3> <p>Let <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>O</mi><mi>R</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">OR</annotation></semantics></math></span> be the set of olfactory receptors. An odorant induces an activation subset <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi><mo>⊆</mo><mi>O</mi><mi>R</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A \subseteq OR</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Odor percepts are functions</p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mo>:</mo><mi mathvariant="script">P</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>O</mi><mi>R</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">Ω</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">P: \mathcal{P}(OR) \to \Omega</annotation></semantics></math></span><p>from the power set of receptors to perceptual space <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">Ω</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\Omega</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The key claim of CSM:</p> <blockquote> <p>Percepts are mereological composites of activation subsets — not atomic features associated with individual molecules.</p> </blockquote> <p>This means the relevant unit of analysis isn&rsquo;t the molecule but the <em>activation pattern</em>. Two structurally different molecules that produce the same activation subset smell the same. Two applications of the same molecule that produce different activation subsets (because of concentration, adaptation, or context) smell different.</p> <h3 id="three-mereological-operations">Three mereological operations</h3> <p>CSM defines three operations on activation subsets:</p> <p><strong>Fusion</strong> (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>⊔</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\sqcup</annotation></semantics></math></span>): combining activation subsets. When odorants are blended, their activation subsets fuse. This models accords — the perfumer&rsquo;s technique of blending molecules to produce a percept that none of the components produces individually. Formally: <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo>⊔</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><mo>=</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo>∪</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>2</mn></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A_1 \sqcup A_2 = A_1 \cup A_2</annotation></semantics></math></span> at the receptor level, but <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo>⊔</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">P(A_1 \sqcup A_2)</annotation></semantics></math></span> need not equal any simple combination of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">P(A_1)</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">P(A_2)</annotation></semantics></math></span> in perceptual space, because the mapping <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">P</annotation></semantics></math></span> is nonlinear.</p> <p><strong>Fission</strong> (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>⊓</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\sqcap</annotation></semantics></math></span>): extracting shared activation. The intersection of two activation patterns picks out the receptors they have in common — the &ldquo;common notes&rdquo; between two odorants. Formally: <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo>⊓</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><mo>=</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo>∩</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>2</mn></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A_1 \sqcap A_2 = A_1 \cap A_2</annotation></semantics></math></span>.</p> <p><strong>Deletion</strong> (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">¬</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\neg</annotation></semantics></math></span>): inhibitory removal of receptor contributors. Some receptor activations suppress others through lateral inhibition in the olfactory bulb. Deletion models this: <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi><mo>∖</mo><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A \setminus B</annotation></semantics></math></span> removes the contribution of subset <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span> from the percept generated by <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>.</p> <p>These operations give the space of activation subsets a mereological algebra. Percepts aren&rsquo;t just &ldquo;present&rdquo; or &ldquo;absent&rdquo; — they&rsquo;re composed, decomposed, and modified through operations that have direct neural and chemical correlates.</p> <h3 id="similarity-as-hyperbolic-geodesics">Similarity as hyperbolic geodesics</h3> <p>Within this framework, perceptual similarity between two percepts corresponds to the hyperbolic geodesic distance between their activation patterns:</p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><mi>d</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>P</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>A</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>P</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>B</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>≈</mo><msub><mi>d</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">H</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>A</mi><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>B</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">d(P(A), P(B)) \approx d_{\mathbb{H}}(A, B)</annotation></semantics></math></span><p>Similarity isn&rsquo;t a Hamming distance over OR activations. It&rsquo;s the geodesic in a negatively curved space, which means:</p> <ul> <li>Nearby percepts in the same branch of the hierarchy can be very similar</li> <li>Percepts in different branches are exponentially distant, even if they share some activated receptors</li> <li>Small changes in activation can cross categorical boundaries, producing the discontinuities observed in psychophysics</li> </ul> <h2 id="fantasy-odors-and-non-realizable-percepts">Fantasy odors and non-realizable percepts</h2> <h3 id="the-emptiness-of-activation-space">The emptiness of activation space</h3> <p>Define two sets:</p> <ul> <li><span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>V</mi><mrow><mi>r</mi><mi>e</mi><mi>a</mi><mi>l</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">V_{real}</annotation></semantics></math></span>: activation vectors that are realizable — meaning some stable molecule exists whose binding profile produces that pattern</li> <li><span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>V</mi><mrow><mi>p</mi><mi>o</mi><mi>s</mi><mi>s</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">V_{poss}</annotation></semantics></math></span>: theoretically possible activation vectors — all subsets of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>O</mi><mi>R</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">OR</annotation></semantics></math></span> that could in principle be activated simultaneously</li> </ul> <p>Computational and empirical work shows that <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><msub><mi>V</mi><mrow><mi>r</mi><mi>e</mi><mi>a</mi><mi>l</mi></mrow></msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><mo>≪</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><msub><mi>V</mi><mrow><mi>p</mi><mi>o</mi><mi>s</mi><mi>s</mi></mrow></msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">|V_{real}| \ll |V_{poss}|</annotation></semantics></math></span> (Sanchez-Lengeling et al. 2019). The activation space is vast; chemistry fills only a small corner of it. The regions between occupied clusters are &ldquo;mereological holes&rdquo; — combinations of receptor activations that no molecule produces.</p> <h3 id="accords-as-projections-onto-empty-regions">Accords as projections onto empty regions</h3> <p>These empty regions aren&rsquo;t perceptually empty. When no single molecule activates the right subset, a <em>blend</em> of molecules can approximate it through mereological fusion:</p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>A</mi><mrow><mi>f</mi><mi>a</mi><mi>n</mi><mi>t</mi><mi>a</mi><mi>s</mi><mi>y</mi></mrow></msub><mo>≈</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mo>⊔</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><mo>⊔</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mn>3</mn></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A_{fantasy} \approx A_1 \sqcup A_2 \sqcup A_3</annotation></semantics></math></span><p>This is exactly what perfumers do when they construct accords. Examples of molecules and accords that occupy low-density regions of the hyperbolic manifold:</p> <ul> <li><strong>Hedione</strong> — an &ldquo;airy, transparent floral&rdquo; quality that doesn&rsquo;t correspond to any natural flower. Its activation pattern falls in a sparsely occupied region of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">H</mi><mi>n</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbb{H}^n</annotation></semantics></math></span>.</li> <li><strong>Iso E Super</strong> — produces a &ldquo;velvety,&rdquo; almost anti-scent effect. It activates a receptor pattern that natural organic chemistry rarely produces.</li> <li><strong>Calone</strong> — a &ldquo;sea-like&rdquo; quality with no natural analog. Marine environments produce complex olfactory signatures, but Calone&rsquo;s specific activation profile has no single molecular origin in nature.</li> </ul> <p>These aren&rsquo;t approximations of natural smells. They&rsquo;re <em>genuine occupants</em> of regions of perceptual space that chemistry left empty. The framework predicts their existence: a vast hyperbolic manifold with sparse chemical coverage must contain perceptible but unrealized coordinates.</p> <h3 id="algorithmic-scent-design">Algorithmic scent design</h3> <p>Given a target point <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>T</mi><mo>∈</mo><msup><mi mathvariant="double-struck">H</mi><mi>n</mi></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">T \in \mathbb{H}^n</annotation></semantics></math></span> in an unoccupied region, accord design becomes an optimization problem: find real activation vectors <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>A</mi><mi>i</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A_i</annotation></semantics></math></span> (producible by available molecules) that minimize</p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><munder><mo>∑</mo><mi>i</mi></munder><msub><mi>d</mi><mi mathvariant="double-struck">H</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>A</mi><mi>i</mi></msub><mo separator="true">,</mo><mi>T</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\sum_i d_{\mathbb{H}}(A_i, T)</annotation></semantics></math></span><p>subject to chemical feasibility constraints. This connects CSM to computational methods:</p> <ul> <li><strong>Poincaré embeddings</strong> (Nickel &amp; Kiela 2017) can represent the receptor space in a hyperbolic model</li> <li>A <strong>mereological graph calculus</strong> with nodes as receptor subsets, edges as mereological relations (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>⊔</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\sqcup</annotation></semantics></math></span>, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mo>⊓</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\sqcap</annotation></semantics></math></span>, <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">¬</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\neg</annotation></semantics></math></span>), and weights as hyperbolic distances provides the computational substrate</li> <li><strong>Accord optimization</strong> over this graph, constrained by molecular availability, yields candidate blends for approaching any target coordinate</li> </ul> <h2 id="implications">Implications</h2> <h3 id="for-neuroscience">For neuroscience</h3> <p>CSM predicts that non-metric perceptual transitions, odor illusions, and categorical boundaries emerge from the negative curvature of the receptor-binding manifold rather than from higher-level cortical processing. If the geometry is hyperbolic at the receptor level, the nonlinearities are structural, not learned.</p> <h3 id="for-perfumery">For perfumery</h3> <p>The framework predicts where &ldquo;fantasy&rdquo; regions lie in the manifold and how accords can approach them. It explains why accords feel more &ldquo;conceptual&rdquo; than single molecules: they&rsquo;re literally occupying regions of perceptual space that no single molecule can reach.</p> <h3 id="for-geometry">For geometry</h3> <p>The framework suggests that the geometry of sensory spaces may be negatively curved more generally — wherever receptor systems have branching, hierarchical binding profiles, Gromov&rsquo;s theorem implies hyperbolic structure. Vision and audition have more regular receptor organization; olfaction&rsquo;s combinatorial, overlapping binding landscape may make it the clearest biological case of hyperbolic perceptual geometry.</p> <h2 id="references">References</h2> <ul> <li>Gromov, M.
