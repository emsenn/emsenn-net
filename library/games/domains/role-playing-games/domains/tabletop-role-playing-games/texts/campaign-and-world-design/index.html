Campaign and World Design

2026-03-09 by claude

Audience: game masters and indie designers building a campaign that must stay playable across many sessions rather than only support a single opener.

Learning goal: define a workable campaign frame, choose the right scale for early world design, and build a setting whose factions, locations, and pressures can keep producing play.

Prerequisites: you should understand Session Zero and Preparation and Creating Table Materials, because campaign design extends those same prep principles over time.

Starting from a concrete mistake

A GM decides to build a campaign world. They write twenty pages of history, six lost empires, three divine schisms, and a full continental map. When the first session begins, the players ask where they can buy food, who controls the ruined bridge, and why the local militia is afraid of the forest road. None of that is on the page.

This is a common failure mode in world design. The problem is not imagination. The problem is scale. The GM designed a world to be read instead of a world to be played.

Start with a campaign frame, not a planet

A strong campaign usually begins with a campaign frame: a concise premise that tells the table what kind of world pressure is already active and why the player characters are inside it.

Good frames are narrow enough to produce action quickly. “A frontier road is reopening through dangerous ruins” is a frame. “The history of the empire’s decline” is not. The first tells you what to prepare next: roads, ruins, merchants, rivals, militias, maps, and threats. The second may be interesting, but it does not yet organize play.

Professional and indie campaign advice converges here. Mike Shea’s spiral campaign building and Justin Alexander’s scenario work both push toward playable scope first: define the pressure, define the local situation, and expand only when the table creates a reason to expand.

Build outward from the starting region

For early campaign prep, the useful unit is usually not the whole setting. It is a starting region: one town, one dangerous zone, a few routes, and a handful of nearby locations. That region is where the first sessions will actually happen.

A starting region usually needs:

  • one home base or social hub
  • three to five nearby adventure locations
  • two or three routes or borders that create travel choices
  • visible signs of danger, scarcity, or opportunity
  • enough unanswered questions that the table wants to keep exploring

This is often called spiral design because the world expands outward from play. If the campaign survives, the world will grow. If it does not, you have not spent two weeks mapping a continent no one visited.

Do not mistake colonial defaults for neutral worldbuilding

Recent critical work on TTRPGs is useful here because it names a major design trap: many campaign frames inherit colonial assumptions without marking them as assumptions. Empty frontiers, ownerless ruins, tribal enemies at the edge of civilization, and treasure waiting to be extracted are not neutral scaffolds. They are political world models.

That does not mean every dangerous road or ruined city is forbidden. It means the GM should ask sharper questions while designing:

  • Who already lives here?
  • Who names the land?
  • What made this place “ruined,” and for whom?
  • Are factions communities with histories, or just obstacles for the party?
  • Does travel open relation, trade, and obligation, or only conquest and loot?

Those questions usually produce stronger campaigns anyway. They turn a generic frontier into a region with memory, sovereignty, and conflicting claims.

Use factions to make the world move

Static settings create static campaigns. One of the most effective ways to avoid that is to design around factions. A faction gives the world agency beyond the player characters.

At minimum, each faction should have:

  • a concrete goal
  • a method or resource base
  • a relationship to the starting region
  • a pressure it exerts if unopposed

You do not need many. Three active factions are often enough for an opening campaign region. What matters is that their goals intersect. A caravan guild reopening trade, a bandit network taxing the road, and a shrine cult defending the old ruins already produce negotiation, violence, secrecy, and unstable alliances.

Reveal the world through play

World design becomes stronger when lore is attached to action. A custom, ruin, dialect term, shrine practice, or map symbol matters more when it changes a decision the players are making right now.

This is where academic and maker practice overlap. Work on the cultural languages of role-playing shows that worlds communicate through signs, objects, and repeated motifs, not just exposition. Indie designers and bloggers arrive at the same practical conclusion: give the table clues, artifacts, customs, and pressures that players encounter in scenes. Let the world become legible through use.

This means worldbuilding often works better as:

  • rumors instead of encyclopedia entries
  • route notes instead of full atlases
  • shrine practices instead of full theologies
  • faction demands instead of abstract geopolitics

Design for continuity

Campaign worlds need persistence. The question is not only “what exists?” but also “what changes when the table touches it?”

The most useful continuity documents are usually small:

  • a region sheet with locations and current pressures
  • a faction sheet tracking goals and recent changes
  • a campaign log noting unresolved consequences
  • a map that accumulates marks over time

These materials let the world answer back. If the group sides with smugglers, the militia responds. If they desecrate a shrine, pilgrims stop helping them. If they open a route, trade patterns shift. World design becomes campaign design when consequences persist between sessions.

Worked example: Brave Old World campaign frame

Suppose you want to build a longer Brave Old World campaign without losing its light, question-driven style.

A workable campaign frame might be: the old mountain roads are reopening after years of abandonment, and every group that depends on movement through the region is trying to control what returns.

Your starting region could then include:

  • Home base: a strained river settlement that needs the road reopened but fears what lies in the ruins
  • Three nearby locations: a ruined watchtower, a collapsed shrine-way, and an abandoned toll fort
  • Three factions: caravan factors trying to restore trade, road bandits trying to tax traffic, shrine-keepers trying to keep travelers away from forbidden sites
  • Two travel routes: the river path and the old high road
  • Three world truths: the old empires left wayfinding signs no one fully reads anymore; travel is returning faster than law can; every safe road was built over something older

That is enough world for several sessions. It gives the table places to go, people to negotiate with, and a sense that the region has history without requiring a full cosmology document.

Common failure modes

Starting too big. Continental maps and dynastic timelines feel productive, but they often leave the first session unsupported.

Confusing lore with pressure. Background information is not campaign structure unless it produces choices or consequences.

Treating settlement as innocence. Campaign frames built on empty-land assumptions usually reproduce conquest logic whether the GM meant to or not.

Too many factions. If you cannot remember what each group wants at the table, you have too many.

Worldbuilding without continuity tools. A setting only becomes a campaign world when changes persist and can be tracked.

Exercises

  1. Write a campaign frame for a Brave Old World campaign in one sentence. It should name a pressure, a place, and a reason the player characters are involved.

  2. Design a starting region using one home base, three nearby locations, and three factions. For each faction, write one goal and one way that goal would change the region if the players ignored it.

  3. Take an older campaign premise you like and rewrite it without assuming empty land, disposable peoples, or inevitable conquest.
