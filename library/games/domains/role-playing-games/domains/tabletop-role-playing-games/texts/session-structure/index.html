Audience: groups preparing to run their first session.

Learning goal: identify the typical phases of a tabletop RPG session and explain what each phase accomplishes.

Prerequisites: you should understand what distinguishes tabletop RPGs from other RPG formats (Tabletop RPG Overview).

Starting from a concrete example

A group meets for a three-hour Brave Old World session. The narrator opens by saying: “Last time, you fled the ruined village with the merchant. The bandits did not follow, but you lost your supplies. You are now camped by a stream about an hour south. It is dawn. What do you do?” The players spend a moment recalling what happened. One player reviews their character notes. Then the adventurers begin making plans for the day, and play begins.

Two and a half hours later, the narrator says: “The cave entrance is ahead. You can hear something moving inside. That feels like a good place to pause - we will pick up here next time.” The group spends ten minutes talking about the session: what surprised them, what they want to explore next, and whether the tone is working.

This session had a shape - opening, play, closing - even though nobody announced the transitions. Understanding that shape helps groups manage their time and energy.

The phases of a session

Most TTRPG sessions move through recognizable phases. Not every session includes all of them, and experienced groups often move through them fluidly, but naming them helps new groups plan their time.

Gathering and check-in (5-10 minutes). Players arrive, settle in, and reconnect socially. Some groups use this time for a brief out-of-character check-in: how is everyone feeling, any real-world distractions, any safety concerns to flag. This phase is easily skipped under time pressure, but it sets the social tone for everything that follows.

Recap (5-10 minutes). The game master or a player summarizes where the fiction left off. Recaps serve two purposes: they refresh shared memory, especially with weeks between sessions, and they establish a common starting point so everyone enters play with the same understanding of the situation. Some GMs ask players to recap, which reveals what they found memorable and what was forgotten.

Active play (the bulk of the session). The scene-choice-consequence loop takes over. The GM frames scenes, players make decisions, and the mechanics resolve uncertainty. Within active play, the pacing shifts naturally - intense combat or social confrontation alternates with quieter exploration or planning. Good session pacing varies the intensity rather than sustaining maximum tension throughout.

Closing and debrief (5-15 minutes). The GM finds a natural stopping point - a cliffhanger, a resolution, or a transition between locations. After play ends, the group steps out of the fiction and reflects. Some groups use structured tools like Stars and Wishes. Others simply talk. The debrief gives the GM information for future preparation, gives players a chance to process what happened, and marks the transition back out of play.

Worked example: structuring a Brave Old World session

A group has three hours. The narrator plans:

  • Check-in (5 min): “How is everyone? Any topics we should be careful with tonight?”
  • Recap (5 min): “Last session, Mira found inscriptions in the ruins. Kael was injured in the bandit attack. You are camped by the stream.”
  • Active play (2.5 hours): The narrator has prepared two scenes - crossing through a forest with a navigation challenge, and arriving at a settlement where the merchants are suspicious of outsiders. Each scene presents a situation that demands choices. If the adventurers resolve both scenes faster than expected, the narrator can improvise a third (a stranger approaches the camp at night) or let the players drive exploration.
  • Closing (10 min): The narrator ends at a decision point: “The settlement elder says there is a path through the mountains, but the last group that tried it did not return. Do you take it?” Then out of character: “Stars and wishes - what worked tonight?”

The preparation is light. The narrator has situations, not scripts. This matches Brave Old World’s design philosophy: the narrator frames, the players decide, and the dice resolve.

Pacing and energy management

A common mistake in early sessions is spending too long in the opening phases (an hour of shopping, planning, and debating before anything risky happens) or running through the closing too quickly (the GM calls time abruptly and players disperse without debriefing). Both patterns degrade the experience.

The active play phase also benefits from pacing awareness. Sustained high-intensity play (combat, arguments, chases) is exhausting. Sustained low-intensity play (travel, inventory management, exposition) is dull. Alternating between the two - an intense encounter followed by a quieter scene where players can process and plan - gives the session a rhythm that keeps players engaged without burning them out.

Exercises

  1. You have a two-hour session with four players. Sketch a time plan: how many minutes for each phase? How many scenes do you expect to fit into active play?

  2. A session is running long, and the group is in the middle of a tense scene with no clear stopping point. Describe two ways the GM could bring the session to a satisfying pause without rushing the fiction.
