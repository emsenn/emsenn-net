Tabletop RPG Overview

Audience: players who understand what an RPG is and want to learn what makes tabletop play specifically different.

Learning goal: describe how tabletop RPGs differ from digital RPGs, identify the role of human adjudication, and explain why the medium of play matters.

Prerequisites: you should be able to define a role-playing game and name the core roles (RPG Overview).

Starting from a concrete example

A player says: “I want to convince the guard to let us through by telling him we are healers sent by the governor.” In a video game RPG, this might mean selecting a dialogue option labeled “Persuade” and watching the game check a stat. In a tabletop RPG, it means saying those words out loud - at the table, to the game master or to the group - and having a conversation about what happens next. The facilitator might ask: “What does your character say exactly? Do you show the governor’s seal, or are you bluffing?” The response changes the situation before any dice are rolled.

This difference - that play happens through conversation and human judgment rather than programmed options - is the defining feature of tabletop RPGs.

What makes tabletop play distinctive

Tabletop RPGs share the RPG core (character portrayal, shared fiction, open-ended action) but add three medium-specific features:

Real-time social interaction. Players communicate through spoken conversation, reading each other’s tone, body language, and timing. This creates an intimacy that asynchronous or computer-mediated play does not replicate. A facilitator can gauge whether players are engaged or confused and adjust on the fly. Players can riff off each other’s ideas in real time, building scenes collaboratively through dialogue.

Human adjudication. No automated system enforces the rules. A GM, rotating facilitator, or the group collectively interprets rule text, resolves ambiguity, and decides when mechanics intervene and when the fiction is sufficient. This makes tabletop RPGs flexible - the group can accommodate actions the fiction supports even if no rule covers them - but also dependent on judgment, fairness, and social awareness.

Physical or virtual artifacts as anchors. Dice, character sheets, maps, tokens, rulebooks, and virtual tabletops are not the game - the conversation is the game - but they anchor the shared fiction in something tangible. Rolling physical dice creates a shared moment of suspense. A map gives everyone a common reference point whether it is drawn on paper or shared on-screen. These artifacts serve the humans; they do not run the game.

Distributed authority is still tabletop play

Many new players imagine that a TTRPG always needs one central GM who owns the world. That model is common, but it is not the only tabletop form. Some games rotate facilitation. Others distribute world authority across the whole table. What makes them tabletop RPGs is not one sovereign host. It is that people, rather than software, keep the game state intelligible.

This matters because authority design changes the feel of play. A traditional GM-led dungeon game, a remote VTT campaign, and a no dice, no masters game can all be tabletop RPGs while giving players very different relationships to narration, consequence, and responsibility.

Worked example: comparing the same moment across media

Consider the Brave Old World scenario: an adventurer wants to lift a wagon wheel off a pinned merchant while bandits approach. In the tabletop version:

  1. The player describes the action in their own words.
  2. The narrator judges this is risky - it takes strength and time, and the bandits are close.
  3. The player rolls 2d6, gets an 8: success with a cost.
  4. The narrator improvises a consequence: “You free the merchant, but drop your pack. The bandits are on you before you can grab it.”

Now imagine the same scenario in a digital RPG. The game might present a button: “[Strength Check] Lift the wheel.” If the stat check passes, a cutscene plays showing success. The player had no opportunity to describe how they lift it, no chance to negotiate the terms of success, and no moment of improvised consequence.

A remote TTRPG group using a virtual tabletop sits between those poles. The map and tokens may be digital, but the group still talks through the action, a human still adjudicates risk, and the outcome still depends on interpretation rather than pre-authored code.

The tabletop version is slower, more dependent on the group’s creativity, and less visually polished. The digital version is faster, more consistent, and unable to accommodate actions the designers did not anticipate. Neither is better. They are different media for the same core activity.

The medium is also social and political

Because tabletop play depends on humans and shared artifacts, the medium also carries social and political assumptions more openly than software often does. A map can imply that land is empty. A character option can imply that a people is naturally violent. A facilitation model can centralize or distribute authority.

This does not make TTRPGs uniquely flawed. It makes them unusually visible. The medium shows its assumptions in public conversation, table procedure, and authored materials. That visibility is one reason tabletop RPGs repay close design criticism.

Exercises

  1. Think of a moment in a video game RPG where you wished you could do something the game did not offer as an option. Describe how a tabletop RPG group might handle that same moment through conversation and human adjudication.

  2. In Brave Old World, the narrator resolves uncertain actions with a 2d6 roll. What would be lost if a computer made this roll silently and simply reported the outcome? What does the physical act of rolling contribute to the social experience?

  3. Name one tabletop artifact you use often: a map, a sheet, a token layout, a handout. What assumption about play does it make visible?
