Creating Table Materials

Audience: game masters and designers who want to prepare usable tabletop materials without over-preparing the session.

Learning goal: identify the main materials worth creating before play, explain why each exists, and distinguish prep that supports player choice from prep that scripts outcomes.

Prerequisites: you should understand session zero and preparation, because table materials only make sense once the group has already aligned on tone, safety, and system.

Starting from a concrete example

A narrator is preparing the first full session of Brave Old World. They do not write a story with a beginning, middle, and ending. Instead, they prepare a small packet of materials:

  • a one-page scenario about a ruined watchtower and the people converging on it
  • a short list of names, motives, and visual cues for three non-player characters
  • a rough map sketch showing the stream, the tower, and the collapsed road
  • one handout: a stained merchant note mentioning an alternate mountain route
  • a tiny reference sheet reminding everyone of the 2d6 result bands

That packet is enough to run a session because it supports action without dictating it. The players can bargain, scout, fight, flee, misread the note, ignore the tower, or follow the merchant instead. The prep creates a playable situation, not a required sequence.

Prepare situations, not plots

The most important principle in creating tabletop materials is simple: prepare what the players can interact with, not the exact path they must take. A plotted script answers questions the table has not yet asked. A scenario gives the table locations, pressures, clues, and people with goals, then lets play determine the sequence.

This distinction matters because TTRPGs are not novels and not cutscenes. The group needs authored material, but that material should remain responsive to choice. A good prep packet therefore tends to emphasize starting conditions, active factions, visible stakes, and information flow over scene-by-scene scripting.

Materials are never neutral

Recent critical work on tabletop design is useful here because it corrects a common blind spot: prep artifacts are not merely technical. A map, rumor table, faction sheet, ancestry option, or handout always carries assumptions about what kinds of people exist, what land is for, and what kinds of conflict the table should treat as normal.

That means good material design is not only a question of clarity. It is also a question of what the artifact invites.

  • A map can invite route choice, or it can quietly frame a region as empty land waiting for extraction.
  • A faction sheet can stage conflicting communities, or it can flatten a people into an enemy block.
  • A handout can preserve a living culture’s terms carefully, or it can turn them into decorative flavor without accountability.

When you prepare materials, ask not only “can the table use this?” but also “what world does this document normalize?”

The core material set

Most tabletop sessions do not need dozens of pages. They need a few durable artifacts that reduce cognitive load during play.

Scenario sheet. A short sheet naming the central problem, the important locations, the people involved, and what will happen if the players do nothing. This is the GM’s anchor document.

NPC roster. A list of names, motives, and one or two memorable details. The point is not literary depth. The point is recall under time pressure.

Spatial aid. This may be a battle map, a relationship diagram, a route sketch, or a dungeon outline. It exists so everyone can reason from the same geography or structure.

Player-facing handouts. Use handouts when a clue, symbol, list, or diegetic document will matter after first presentation. If players may need to compare, revisit, or share information, make it visible.

Rules reference. A very short reference sheet for procedures that are likely to recur: core resolution, initiative, damage, or safety reminders. This is especially useful for new groups and low-prep one-shots.

What handouts are for

Handouts are not decoration. They are memory tools. A handout is worth making when spoken delivery would be lossy or repetitive: a letter with exact wording, a map with landmarks, a cast list, a coded message, a menu of rumors, or a summary of mission objectives.

The best handouts are compact and usable. They should make players more capable of acting, not bury them under flavor text. If a player can point at the object and say, “This line changes what we do next,” the handout is doing its job.

A lightweight prep model for first sessions

For a first session, a practical target is five artifacts:

  1. One scenario sheet.
  2. One NPC roster.
  3. One spatial aid.
  4. Zero to two handouts.
  5. One rules reference.

That package is small enough to finish and rich enough to run. It also scales well to remote play: the same materials can be uploaded to a virtual tabletop, shared as images, or pasted into a group document.

Cultural and political checks for authored materials

Before you finalize a prep packet, run a short audit:

  • Does any document assume that unknown land is ownerless or that local people exist only as obstacles?
  • Did you borrow names, symbols, or cultural practices from living peoples without a reasoned basis for doing so?
  • Have you turned a whole people into a creature list, encounter table, or faceless faction?
  • If the material draws on a marginalized culture, did that culture shape the design or only decorate it?

These checks do not replace research, consultation, or judgment. They make sure those concerns appear while the material is still small enough to fix.

Worked example: materials for a Brave Old World opener

The narrator wants the party to arrive near a ruined watchtower while two outside forces close in: a merchant caravan looking for a safe pass and a bandit scout trying to secure the same road.

Their prep packet looks like this:

  • Scenario sheet: ruined watchtower, unstable stairs, hidden cellar, merchant urgency, bandit pressure.
  • NPC roster: Lysa the caravan factor (hurried, pragmatic), Eren the scout (watchful, injured hand), Old Harn at the river crossing (talkative, suspicious).
  • Map sketch: stream, crossing, tower hill, collapsed road, tree line.
  • Handout: Lysa’s half-burned route note with two place names and a warning about the mountain pass.
  • Rules card: 10+ clean success, 7-9 success with cost, 6 or less failure with consequence.

Nothing in that packet says how the session must end. It only ensures that when the players choose a direction, the narrator has material ready to support it.

Common failure modes

Overwriting scenes. If the prep reads like a screenplay, it will break the first time players do something unexpected.

Preparing lore without action. A beautiful page of history is not useful if it does not generate choices at the table.

Treating aesthetics as innocence. A map, ancestry list, or rumor table can reproduce colonial or racial defaults even when it looks clean and polished.

Making handouts too dense. If players cannot scan it quickly, they will stop using it.

Skipping reference materials. The GM may remember the rules at home and blank on them during play. A tiny rules sheet is often more useful than another page of setting text.

Exercises

  1. Draft a one-page first-session prep packet for Brave Old World. Include a scenario, three NPCs, one spatial aid, and one handout.

  2. Take a scene you have planned as a sequence of events and rewrite it as a situation with pressures, locations, and clues instead.

  3. Audit one map or handout you already use. What assumptions does it make about land, danger, and the people who live there?
