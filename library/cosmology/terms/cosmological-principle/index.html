Cosmological Principle

2026-03-31
Defines Cosmological Principle, cosmological principle, homogeneity, isotropy

The cosmological principle is the assumption that the universe, viewed on sufficiently large scales, is homogeneous (the same at every location) and isotropic (the same in every direction).

This is not obvious. At small scales the universe is spectacularly lumpy: stars, planets, galaxies, clusters, voids. But above roughly 300 megaparsecs (about a billion light-years), galaxy surveys show the distribution of matter becomes statistically uniform. The cosmic microwave background confirms isotropy to one part in 100,000.

The cosmological principle is what makes cosmology tractable as a science. Without it, the geometry of the universe would vary from place to place in ways that would require knowing the detailed distribution of all matter everywhere. With it, the universe’s geometry reduces to a single function – the scale factor a(t)a(t) – and the Einstein field equations reduce to the Friedmann equations. The entire Lambda-CDM model rests on this assumption.

The principle is sometimes stated as: there is no privileged position in the universe. This echoes Copernicus’s removal of Earth from the center, extended to the claim that no location is special. It is an idealization, not an exact truth, but it is confirmed by observation at the scales where cosmology operates.
