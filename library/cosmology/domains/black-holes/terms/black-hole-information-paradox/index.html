Black Hole Information Paradox

2026-03-31
Defines Black Hole Information Paradox, black hole information paradox, information paradox
Requires
  • hawking-radiation
  • bekenstein-hawking-entropy

The black hole information paradox is the unresolved conflict between quantum mechanics – which requires that information is conserved (unitarity) – and the apparent destruction of information when a black hole evaporates via Hawking radiation.

The paradox in detail: quantum mechanics is unitary, meaning the evolution of a quantum state is reversible in principle. Given the final state, you could in principle reconstruct the initial state. But Hawking radiation appears to be exactly thermal – random noise characterized only by the black hole’s mass, charge, and angular momentum. It carries no information about the specific matter that fell into the black hole. If the black hole evaporates completely, the information about its contents appears to be destroyed, violating unitarity.

Proposed resolutions fall into several categories:

Information escapes in the radiation. The radiation is not exactly thermal; subtle correlations between early and late Hawking quanta encode the information. Recent work on the “Page curve” and “island formula” (building on the holographic principle) supports this view: the entropy of radiation initially increases, then decreases, following the unitary prediction. This is currently the leading approach.

Information is stored in a remnant. The black hole does not fully evaporate but leaves behind a Planck-scale remnant containing the information. This creates its own problems (the remnant would need an enormous number of internal states).

Information is destroyed. Unitarity is violated. This would require modifying quantum mechanics, which most physicists consider a last resort.

Information goes elsewhere. A baby universe or other disconnected region receives the information. Exotic but not excluded.

The paradox is one of the deepest open problems in theoretical physics because it sits at the intersection of general relativity, quantum mechanics, and information theory. Any resolution will constrain the theory of quantum gravity. See also: describing black holes as informational stability optimizers for an information-theoretic treatment.
