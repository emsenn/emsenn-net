Scale Factor

2026-03-31
Defines Scale Factor, scale factor
Requires
  • cosmic-expansion

The scale factor a(t)a(t) is a dimensionless function that describes how distances in the universe grow over time. It is the central dynamical variable of cosmology.

If two galaxies are separated by a distance d0d_0 at a reference time t0t_0, their separation at any other time tt is d(t)=a(t)⋅d0/a(t0)d(t) = a(t) \cdot d_0 / a(t_0). By convention, aa is set to 1 at the present epoch. At earlier times a<1a < 1 (the universe was smaller); at later times a>1a > 1 (it will be larger).

The scale factor encodes the entire expansion history of the universe. Its time derivative gives the expansion rate. The Hubble parameter is defined as H(t)=a˙(t)/a(t)H(t) = \dot{a}(t)/a(t). The Friedmann equations are differential equations for a(t)a(t) that depend on the energy content of the universe.

The redshift of light from a distant source is directly related to the scale factor at the time the light was emitted: 1+z=anow/aemit=1/aemit1 + z = a_{\text{now}} / a_{\text{emit}} = 1 / a_{\text{emit}}, since anow=1a_{\text{now}} = 1. A galaxy observed at redshift z=1z = 1 emitted its light when the universe was half its current size. The cosmic microwave background has redshift z≈1100z \approx 1100, meaning the universe was about 1,100 times smaller when that light was released.
