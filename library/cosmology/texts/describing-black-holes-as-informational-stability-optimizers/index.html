<h1 id="abstract">Abstract</h1> <p>We propose that black holes can be modeled as information-theoretic stability optimizers: physical systems that maximize entropy while minimizing divergence between interior and exterior information distributions. Using the definition of stability reward as a function of coupling between systems (see <em>Information-Theoretic Stability as Reward Function</em>), we reformulate the laws of black-hole thermodynamics as expressions of informational coupling across the horizon. The event horizon is interpreted as a stability boundary where entropy flux across the surface balances the internal rate of informational compression. This perspective unifies the thermodynamic, holographic, and information-geometric descriptions of black holes and suggests a general stability principle underlying gravitational dynamics.</p> <h1 id="1-introduction">1. Introduction</h1> <p>The thermodynamics of black holes reveals a deep correspondence between gravitational dynamics and information theory (Bekenstein 1973; Hawking 1975; Bousso 2002). The entropy–area law, the temperature of Hawking radiation, and the holographic principle all imply that black holes organize information in an optimal fashion. Here we interpret these properties through the lens of <strong>information-theoretic stability</strong> (see <em>Information-Theoretic Stability as Reward Function</em>): stability as a reward function of coupling between systems. The exterior field adapts to the black hole&rsquo;s geometric constraints; the black hole&rsquo;s interior adapts to infalling matter. Each side&rsquo;s stability is a function of its coupling to the other across the horizon.</p> <h1 id="2-information-theoretic-stability">2. Black holes therefore function as <strong>maximum-stability reservoirs</strong> for information flows in the universe.</p> <h1 id="5-information-preservation-and-hawking-radiation">5. Information Preservation and Hawking Radiation</h1> <p>Hawking radiation provides the feedback mechanism restoring global stability when perturbations disturb equilibrium. Each emitted quantum carries away precisely the information necessary to maintain <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi>R</mi><mi>s</mi><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mtext>int</mtext><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msubsup><mo>+</mo><msubsup><mi>R</mi><mi>s</mi><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mtext>ext</mtext><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msubsup><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">R_s^{(\text{int})}+R_s^{(\text{ext})}=0</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Holography and Stability Mapping</h1> <p>The holographic principle (’t Hooft 1993; Susskind 1995) asserts that the informational content of a volume is encoded on its boundary. In stability terms, the boundary mapping <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">Φ</mi><mo>:</mo><mi mathvariant="script">V</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∂</mi><mi mathvariant="script">V</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\Phi:\mathcal{V}\to\partial\mathcal{V}</annotation></semantics></math></span> preserves the stability functional: </p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>R</mi><mi>s</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><mi mathvariant="script">V</mi><mo stretchy="false">]</mo><mo>=</mo><msub><mi>R</mi><mi>s</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∂</mi><mi mathvariant="script">V</mi><mo stretchy="false">]</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex"> R_s[\mathcal{V}] = R_s[\partial\mathcal{V}], </annotation></semantics></math></span><p> analogous to a holographic duality where bulk and boundary share identical divergence dynamics. This recasts AdS/CFT correspondence (Maldacena 1998) as a geometric equality of stability optimization across dimensions.</p> <h1 id="7-cosmological-implications">7. Cosmological Implications</h1> <p>At cosmological scale, black holes act as attractors for informational instability: they absorb high-divergence regions and return the universe toward maximal entropy subject to global stability conservation. The universe thereby evolves toward configurations that minimize global divergence gradients—a universal tendency consistent with Jaynesian entropy maximization (Jaynes 1957) and thermodynamic self-organization (Crooks 1999; Lloyd 2000).</p> <h1 id="8-conclusion">8. Conclusion</h1> <p>Black holes exemplify the principle of information-theoretic stability optimization. They maintain minimal divergence between interior and exterior informational states, converting local instability into global equilibrium through entropy exchange at the horizon. This view unifies gravitational thermodynamics, quantum information, and statistical inference within a single variational framework, suggesting that the dynamics of spacetime itself instantiate the general stability principle of information.</p> <h1 id="references">References</h1> <ul> <li>Bekenstein, J.
