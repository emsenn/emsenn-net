Harm Reduction

2026-03-03 A public health approach that reduces negative consequences of risky behaviors without requiring abstinence, originally built as communal infrastructure against state abandonment.

Harm reduction is a public health approach that aims to reduce the negative consequences of risky behaviors — particularly drug use — without requiring abstinence as a precondition for care. Needle exchanges, naloxone distribution, supervised consumption sites, and safer supply programs all operate on harm reduction principles: meet people where they are, reduce the immediate dangers, and treat survival as the first priority rather than demanding behavioral transformation before offering help.

The approach emerged from grassroots organizing in the 1980s and 1990s, built by communities facing the overlapping crises of drug criminalization and HIV/AIDS. What distinguishes harm reduction from clinical risk management is its origin as collective infrastructure. Needle exchanges were not designed by public health agencies and handed down; they were built by drug users, sex workers, and community organizers in direct resistance to state policies of criminalization and abandonment. Dean Spade documents this history in Mutual Aid (2020), and Leah Lakshmi Piepzna-Samarasinha extends it in Care Work (2018) — both emphasizing that harm reduction programs were shared responses to structural conditions, not individualized coping strategies.

emsenn critiques the misuse of the term in “On white-supremacist covid-eugenicist queers” (2025-09-23). The newsletter emsenn responds to redefines harm reduction as individual optimization — wear your own mask, make your own risk assessment, manage your own exposure. This strips harm reduction of its collective, infrastructural character and turns a practice of communal care into a vocabulary for privatized abandonment. When harm reduction becomes “you do you,” it has been emptied of the political commitment that gave it meaning.
