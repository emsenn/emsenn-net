Free Open-Access Medicine (FOAM)

2026-03-03 The open sharing of medical education resources across web-native formats, emphasizing rapid dissemination and practical clinical utility

Free Open-Access Medicine (FOAM) is the open sharing of medical education resources across web-native formats — blog posts, podcasts, videos, infographics, social media threads, and discussion forums — with emphasis on rapid dissemination and practical utility.

The term was coined by emergency physician Mike Cadogan in 2012, drawing on the culture of open knowledge-sharing that had been developing in emergency medicine and critical care communities since the mid-2000s. FOAM is not a platform or an organization but a movement: anyone who produces or shares openly accessible medical education content participates in FOAM.

FOAM emerged from specific conditions. Emergency medicine and critical care operate under time pressure, manage rapidly evolving evidence, and depend on pattern recognition — features that favor rapid, informal knowledge exchange over the slower cycle of journal publication and guideline revision. Blog posts could distill a new study into clinical takeaways within days of publication. Podcasts could walk through differential diagnoses during a commute. Social media could amplify a clinical pearl from a community hospital to a global audience overnight.

This speed is both FOAM’s strength and its risk. FOAM content is not peer-reviewed in the traditional sense. Quality varies widely — from rigorous evidence synthesis by experienced clinicians to uncritiqued case reports or opinion presented as fact. The same distribution mechanisms that accelerate good information also accelerate errors. A misleading infographic can reach thousands of clinicians before correction arrives.

Safe use of FOAM requires critical appraisal discipline: evaluating each piece of content for source credibility, evidence quality, and applicability to one’s own clinical context. FOAM supplements but does not replace formal medical education, primary literature review, and local protocol adherence.
