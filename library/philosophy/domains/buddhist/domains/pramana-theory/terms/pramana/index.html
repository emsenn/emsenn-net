Pramāṇa

2026-03-05 A source or method of knowledge that produces justified, reliable cognition — the framework within which Indian formal logic operates, embedding inference within epistemology from the start.
Defines Pramāṇa, pramana, valid cognition, means of knowledge
<p>Pramāṇa (Sanskrit: &ldquo;means of valid cognition&rdquo;) is the epistemological concept that grounds Indian logical traditions, including Buddhist logic. A pramāṇa is a source or method of knowledge that produces justified, reliable cognition. The theory of pramāṇas — how many there are, what they deliver, and what their limits are — is the Indian counterpart to Western epistemology, and it provides the framework within which Indian formal logic operates.</p> <p>The Buddhist pramāṇa tradition, developed principally by Dignāga (c. 480–540 CE) and Dharmakīrti (c. 600–660 CE), recognizes two pramāṇas. Perception (<em>pratyakṣa</em>) is direct, non-conceptual awareness of a particular. It grasps the unique particular (<em>svalakṣaṇa</em>) — the thing as it is in its singular, unrepeatable character. For Dignāga, perception is entirely free of conceptual construction; the moment conceptualization enters, it is no longer perception but inference. Inference (<em>anumāna</em>) is knowledge derived from a logical mark (<em>liṅga</em> or <em>hetu</em>). It grasps the general characteristic (<em>sāmānyalakṣaṇa</em>) — properties that recur across instances. Seeing smoke and inferring fire is the paradigm case. Dignāga codified the conditions for valid inference in the three-aspect schema (<em>trairūpya</em>): the reason must be present in the subject, present in positively correlated cases, and absent from negatively correlated cases.</p> <p>This two-fold epistemology is notably more restrictive than its Hindu Nyāya counterpart, which recognizes four pramāṇas (adding testimony and comparison). The Buddhist restriction reflects a deeper philosophical commitment: knowledge must be grounded in causal contact with reality (perception) or in reliable inferential connections (inference). Testimony is reducible to inference (one infers the truth of what a reliable speaker asserts), and comparison is reducible to perception and inference.</p> <p>The significance of pramāṇa theory for logic is that it embeds inference within epistemology from the start. Where Western logic traditionally separates the study of valid inference (logic) from the study of knowledge (epistemology), the Indian pramāṇa tradition treats inference as one kind of knowledge-producing activity among others. Validity is not a purely formal property of argument structures but an epistemic achievement — the successful production of knowledge from evidence.</p>
