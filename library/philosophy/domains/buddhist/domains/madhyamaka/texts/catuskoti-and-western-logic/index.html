The Catuṣkoṭi and Western Logic

2026-03-29
Abstract

Four ways Western logical traditions have tried to make sense of Nāgārjuna's tetralemma — many-valued, paraconsistent, pragmatic, and Heyting-algebraic readings — and what each captures and misses.

<p>The relationship between the catuṣkoṭi and Western logical frameworks is contested, and the contestation reveals as much about the assumptions of Western logic as about the catuṣkoṭi itself.</p> <p><strong>Many-valued logic reading.</strong> The catuṣkoṭi can be modeled as a four-valued logic with truth values {T, F, B, N} (true, false, both, neither), paralleling Belnap&rsquo;s four-valued logic for reasoning with incomplete and inconsistent information. This reading captures the structure — four exhaustive, mutually exclusive positions — but misses the therapeutic function. In Belnap&rsquo;s system, the four values describe an agent&rsquo;s epistemic state (what they&rsquo;ve been told); in Nāgārjuna&rsquo;s deployment, the four corners are exhausted and rejected, not occupied.</p> <p><strong>Paraconsistent reading.</strong> The &ldquo;both&rdquo; corner (true and false simultaneously) is a dialetheia — a true contradiction — making the catuṣkoṭi an anticipation of dialetheism. Graham Priest has argued this position explicitly, reading Nāgārjuna as a dialetheist who accepts that some contradictions are genuine features of reality. This reading takes the &ldquo;both&rdquo; corner seriously but may over-commit Nāgārjuna to a metaphysical position he would reject. Nāgārjuna&rsquo;s point is that <em>all four corners fail</em>, including &ldquo;both.&rdquo; If the &ldquo;both&rdquo; corner were a stable truth value, Nāgārjuna&rsquo;s method would stop there rather than proceeding to exhaust it.</p> <p><strong>Pragmatic reading.</strong> The catuṣkoṭi is not a logic at all but a therapeutic device — a way of systematically exhausting the space of possible positions to release the practitioner from attachment to any of them. On this reading, the four corners are not truth values but traps, and the method&rsquo;s purpose is dissolution of questions rather than answers to them. This reading respects Nāgārjuna&rsquo;s anti-metaphysical intent but risks making the catuṣkoṭi logically empty — a rhetorical gesture rather than a rigorous procedure.</p> <p><strong>Heyting algebra reading.</strong> The &ldquo;neither&rdquo; corner maps onto the intuitionistic gap between assertion and denial — propositions that are neither proved nor refuted, where the law of excluded middle fails. The &ldquo;both&rdquo; corner exceeds what Heyting algebras can represent (they do not admit gluts), but the overall structure of non-bivalent, non-binary predication is closer to intuitionistic than to classical semantics. This reading connects the catuṣkoṭi to the relational universe&rsquo;s formal architecture, which is built on Heyting algebras precisely because meaning is not a settled binary partition.</p> <p>Each reading captures something real and distorts something real. The catuṣkoṭi is not a Western logical system in exotic dress. It operates within a different set of commitments — about what logic is for, about what emptiness reveals, about the relationship between reasoning and liberation — and the Western readings are most useful when they acknowledge what they cannot translate rather than claiming full capture.</p>
