Contingency

2026-03-06 The condition of existing without necessity — something is contingent when it exists but could have been otherwise, produced by specific historical causes that weren't inevitable.
Defines contingency, contingent

Contingency is the condition of existing without necessity — something is contingent when it exists but could have been otherwise. A contingent thing depends on conditions that could have been different. It is not random — a contingent arrangement has specific, traceable causes — but those causes were not inevitable, and different causes would have produced a different arrangement.

The concept operates at multiple levels. Metaphysically, contingency names the difference between things that must exist (if anything must — mathematical truths, logical laws, depending on one’s commitments) and things that happen to exist because of the specific way the world unfolded. The English language is contingent: had history gone differently, the people of the British Isles might speak a different language or no common language at all. The laws of thermodynamics are (arguably) necessary: they hold regardless of historical accident.

Politically, contingency is the most powerful tool critical thought possesses. Most of the arrangements people treat as natural or inevitable — the way schools work, the way prisons operate, the way medicine classifies disease, the way gender organizes social life — are contingent. They exist because of specific historical events, institutional decisions, and power struggles that could have gone differently. To reveal the contingency of an arrangement is to open the possibility that it could be otherwise. Arrangements that present themselves as necessary are harder to question than arrangements recognized as contingent. The distinction between “this is how things are” and “this is how things happen to be, given what happened” is the space in which political imagination operates.

Foucault’s genealogy is built on making contingency visible. A genealogical analysis takes something that presents itself as necessary — the prison, sexuality, the clinical gaze — and traces the specific, identifiable history that produced it. The point is not that the arrangement is bad (though it may be) but that it is not the only possible arrangement, because the causes that produced it were themselves contingent. Ontologization is the inverse operation: the process by which contingent arrangements harden into claims about what necessarily exists, closing the space of political imagination that contingency keeps open.
