Attentiveness

2026-03-05 The first phase of Tronto's care model — the capacity to notice that care is needed, perceiving vulnerability without filtering through abstract principle or self-interest.
Defines Attentiveness
Requires vulnerability

Attentiveness is the first phase of Joan Tronto’s four-phase model of care and the ethical disposition that makes care possible. It names the capacity to notice that care is needed — to perceive the vulnerability or dependency of another without filtering that perception through abstract principle or self-interest. Without attentiveness, the need for care goes unrecognized, and the remaining phases (taking responsibility, performing the care, and assessing responsiveness) never begin.

Attentiveness is not passive awareness. It requires actively overcoming the habits of inattention that dominant social arrangements produce. Tronto argues that privilege operates precisely through structured inattention: those who benefit from existing distributions of care do not see the needs that others must meet. The executive does not see the janitorial staff’s exhaustion. The housed person does not see the unhoused person’s cold. The able-bodied person does not see the barriers the disabled person navigates. This is not callousness but structure — the social world is organized so that certain needs are visible and certain needs are not, and the distribution of visibility tracks the distribution of power. Attentiveness is therefore both a moral and a political achievement: it demands seeing what power has organized you not to see.

In Nel Noddings’s framework, attentiveness appears as the precondition for engrossment — the receptive attending to the other that initiates the caring relation. For Noddings, attentiveness is not a rule to follow but a quality of presence: turning toward the particular other and receiving their reality as they present it, on their own terms rather than through the carer’s interpretive categories. The attentive carer does not ask “what would I need in this situation?” — they ask “what is this person, in this situation, presenting to me?”

This distinguishes care ethics from justice-based frameworks. Where Kantian ethics demands impartial application of universal principles — treating all persons the same regardless of relationship — care ethics demands situated attention to specific persons in specific circumstances. Attentiveness is the capacity that makes this possible: without it, the carer falls back on general rules that may have nothing to do with what the person before them actually needs.
