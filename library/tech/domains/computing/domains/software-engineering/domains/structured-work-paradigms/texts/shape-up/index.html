Shape Up

2026-03-07
Abstract

Research summary of Shape Up, a product development method created by Ryan Singer at Basecamp that uses fixed time, variable scope, and structured shaping to plan work.

Shape Up is a product development method created by Ryan Singer at Basecamp (formerly 37signals). Singer compiled the method from Basecamp’s internal practices and published it in 2019 as Shape Up: Stop Running in Circles and Ship Work that Matters, available free online at basecamp.com/shapeup.

Core concepts

Shape Up organizes work into three phases — shaping, betting, and building — and structures time into six-week cycles followed by two-week cool-down periods.

Shaping is the design work that happens before a project is scheduled. Senior staff define the problem, sketch a solution at a rough level of abstraction, and set boundaries. A shaped project has three properties: it is rough (not a detailed spec), solved (the main approach is clear), and bounded (the scope fits within the time appetite).

Appetites, not estimates. Shape Up replaces estimation with appetite-setting. An estimate starts with a design and ends with a number; an appetite starts with a number and ends with a design. A team decides how much time a problem is worth — typically two weeks (small batch) or six weeks (big batch) — and shapes the scope to fit within that budget.

Betting replaces prioritized backlogs. During the cool-down period, a small group of decision-makers (at Basecamp: the CEO, CTO, and a product strategist) meets at the “betting table.” They review pitches — short documents describing a shaped project — and decide which to fund for the next cycle. Unselected pitches don’t carry over automatically; they must be re-pitched in a future cycle.

Building follows a circuit-breaker pattern. Teams get six weeks to ship; if the work isn’t done by the deadline, the project is re-evaluated rather than extended. This prevents projects from dragging on indefinitely.

Planning mechanisms

Two tools structure visibility during the building phase:

Scopes divide a project into meaningful pieces that can be completed independently. Unlike task lists, scopes are organized around what the user experiences, not around technical layers.

Hill charts track progress within each scope. A hill chart is a curve divided into two halves: uphill (figuring things out, resolving unknowns) and downhill (executing known work). Team members drag dots along the curve to show where each scope stands. The top of the hill marks the point where all ambiguity is resolved.

The cool-down period between cycles serves multiple functions: it absorbs schedule slack, provides time for bug fixes and small improvements, and creates space for the betting table to meet without competing with active project work.
