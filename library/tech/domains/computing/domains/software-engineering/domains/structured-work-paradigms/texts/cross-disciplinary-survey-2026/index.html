Structured-work paradigms: a cross-disciplinary survey

2026-03-07 by claude
Abstract

Survey of structured-work methodologies across business, government/military, and scientific research, with analysis of cross-cutting patterns relevant to agent workflow design.

This text surveys structured-work methodologies across three disciplines — business/management, government/military, and scientific/academic research — to identify patterns that inform the design of agent workflows in the agential semioverse repository.

The survey was conducted via web research in March 2026. Each domain was researched independently to avoid cross-contamination of framing. Citations are provided per finding.

Cross-cutting patterns

Five patterns recur across all three domains. These are the findings most relevant to the emsemioverse’s workflow design.

1. Every methodology encodes expectations before execution

No surveyed methodology begins work without first defining what “done” looks like. The artifact varies — a PDCA plan, a DMAIC problem statement, a pre-registered protocol, a military course of action — but the structure is invariant: state the expected outcome before acting.

This corresponds to policy 008 (prompts become artifacts) and the plan infrastructure at technology/specifications/agential-semioverse-repository/plans/.

2. Verification means comparing against an explicit expectation

No methodology verifies in the abstract. PDCA compares results against the Plan. DMAIC compares measurements against Define requirements. Registered reports compare results against pre-specified analysis plans. Military planning compares outcomes against the RATE criteria (Refine, Adapt, Terminate, Execute).

This corresponds to the postcondition checking in the work-unit lifecycle (Definition 3.2, step 2).

3. The PDCA pattern is universal

Shewhart’s Plan-Do-Check-Act cycle appears in every domain under different names:

Domain PDCA variant
Business PDCA (Deming), DMAIC (Six Sigma), OKR cycles
Government NIST RMF steps, MDMP, OODA loop
Science Hypothesis-experiment-analysis, action research
Agent workflows Anthropic’s evaluator-optimizer pattern

The cycle is the universal structure of improvement. What varies is the termination condition: some cycles are bounded (DMAIC projects end), others are unbounded (Kaizen never ends).

4. Maturity is progressive, not binary

Government readiness levels (TRL, MRL, CMMI maturity levels) and business maturity models share a structure: capabilities mature through defined stages, each requiring demonstrated evidence. No methodology treats maturity as pass/fail.

This corresponds directly to skill maturity — the five-stage lifecycle from inference-heavy to MCP tool.

5. Making the invisible visible

When manufacturing methodologies are adapted to knowledge work, the core adaptation is always the same: render invisible cognitive work legible enough to apply improvement methodologies. Kanban boards, value stream maps, explicit policies, metrics, and audit trails all serve this function.

In the emsemioverse, the MCP tools, the frontmatter system, the predicate graph, and the triage index are all instances of this pattern — making the repository’s state visible and queryable rather than requiring inference to discover.

Domain summaries

Business and management

The business domain contributes methodologies organized around continuous improvement. Key frameworks surveyed:

  • PDCA/PDSA (Shewhart/Deming, 1920s-1950s) — the foundational improvement cycle. Deming’s System of Profound Knowledge provides the meta-methodology.
  • Six Sigma / DMAIC (Bill Smith, Motorola, 1986) — statistical process control applied to improvement cycles.
  • Lean / Toyota Production System (Taiichi Ohno, 1940s-1970s) — continuous waste elimination via pull systems.
  • Theory of Constraints (Goldratt, 1984) — system throughput optimization via constraint identification. Adapted to knowledge work by Tiago Forte (2016).
  • Kanban for knowledge work (David Anderson, 2004) — pull systems with WIP limits, adapted from Toyota.
  • OKRs (Andy Grove, Intel, 1970s) — objectives with measurable key results in quarterly cycles.

Contemporary sources document early applications of these methods to AI workflows: Anthropic’s five agent workflow patterns (2024), McKinsey’s “agentic organization” operating model (2025), and Kaizen principles applied to LLM interactions (Puzio, 2024).

Government and military

The government domain contributes frameworks emphasizing accountability, auditability, and graduated readiness. Key frameworks surveyed:

  • CMMI (Carnegie Mellon SEI, 1980s-present) — five maturity levels for organizational processes. V3.0 (2023) added data management capabilities.
  • ITIL 4 (UK CCTA, 1980s; PeopleCert, 2019) — IT service management via the Service Value System.
  • DoD Acquisition Framework (DoD 5000, reformed 2020) — six tailorable acquisition pathways with milestone decision points.
  • NIST RMF (SP 800-37 Rev. 2, 2018) — six-step risk management cycle culminating in Authorization to Operate.
  • Structured Analytic Techniques (Heuer & Pherson, 2011) — 66 techniques for externalizing intelligence analysis reasoning. Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH), Devil’s Advocacy, and Red Team Analysis are the most relevant to agent workflows.
  • Technology Readiness Levels (NASA, 1970s; DoD adoption 2000s) — nine-level scale from basic principles to operational system.

Contemporary sources document DoD AI adoption: the Thunderforge project (Scale AI / Anduril, 2025) integrating AI agents into military planning, NIST AI 600-1 (2024) addressing autonomous agent risks, and ICD-505 (2025) establishing IC AI governance. FedRAMP 20x (2025) demonstrates the shift from narrative documentation to machine-readable evidence — the same direction as the emsemioverse’s frontmatter-driven approach.

Scientific and academic research

The scientific domain contributes methodologies organized around evidence quality and reproducibility. Key frameworks surveyed:

  • Pre-registration / Registered Reports (Chambers, 2013; Center for Open Science) — peer review of methods before results are known. Eliminates publication bias.
  • FAIR Data Principles (Wilkinson et al., 2016) — Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable. Shifts from human-readable to machine-actionable data documentation.
  • Systematic review methodology (Cochrane / PRISMA 2020) — protocol-driven evidence synthesis with documented search strategies and bias assessment.
  • Design Science Research Methodology (Hevner, 2004; Peffers, 2007) — build-evaluate cycle for research artifacts.
  • Action research (Lewin, 1946) — iterative intervention where the researcher’s practice is itself the subject. The most self-referential methodology surveyed.
  • Grounded theory (Glaser & Strauss, 1967) — theory emerges from data through constant comparison until theoretical saturation. Inverts the hypothetico-deductive method.
  • Literate programming (Knuth, 1984) and its descendants (Org-Babel, Jupyter, R Markdown, marimo) — specification, execution, and documentation as a single artifact.

Contemporary sources document AI-assisted systematic reviews (Nature Medicine, 2026), agentic AI for scientific discovery (Li et al., ICLR 2025), and computational reproducibility frameworks (ENCORE, Nature Communications, 2024).

The bounded/unbounded distinction

The scientific research agent identified a structural parallel to Hofstadter’s BlooP/FlooP distinction (Godel, Escher, Bach, 1979):

  • Bounded loops (BlooP-like): pre-registration, systematic review, DSRM, DMAIC projects. The number of iterations is known or bounded before the loop begins. Always terminates. Verifiable. Cannot discover what exceeds pre-specified bounds.

  • Unbounded loops (FlooP-like): grounded theory, ethnographic fieldwork, action research, Kaizen. Termination depends on data (theoretical saturation, thick description). May not terminate. Can discover what bounded loops cannot.

This maps onto the skill maturity lifecycle: mature skills (stages 4-5, procedural/tool) are bounded loops — they always terminate with known outputs. Immature skills (stages 1-2, inference-heavy) are unbounded — the agent may need arbitrary reasoning to complete them. The maturity trajectory is from FlooP toward BlooP.

Implications for the emsemioverse

The survey identifies several patterns the emsemioverse already implements and several it could adopt:

Already implemented:

  • Expectations before execution (plans, skill manifests)
  • Postcondition verification (work-unit lifecycle)
  • Progressive maturity (skill maturity concept)
  • Audit trails (effect reports, provenance traces)
  • Making the invisible visible (MCP tools, frontmatter, triage index)

Candidates for adoption:

  • Structured analytic techniques (ACH, Devil’s Advocacy) applied to content classification and triage decisions
  • Readiness levels for content maturity (analogous to TRL) — from triage through slop through draft through published
  • FAIR-aligned metadata practices for the frontmatter system
  • Pre-registration for research plans (commit to methodology before executing, reducing scope drift)
  • WIP limits on active plans and in-progress work (Kanban)
