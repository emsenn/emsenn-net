Model

A model is a mathematical structure that makes a set of logical sentences true. It is the bridge between syntax (the formal sentences of a theory) and semantics (what those sentences are about). The axioms of group theory are sentences in a formal language. The integers under addition are a model of those axioms — a concrete mathematical object in which every axiom holds true.

The concept solves a fundamental problem: formal logic manipulates symbols according to rules, but symbols by themselves do not refer to anything. A model gives them reference. The symbol “0” becomes the number zero. The symbol “+” becomes the addition operation. The sentence “∀x (x+0=x)\forall x\, (x + 0 = x)” becomes a claim about actual numbers — and in the model of integers under addition, that claim is true.

Formally, given a first-order theory TT over a signature Σ\Sigma (a list of constant symbols, function symbols, and relation symbols), a model MM consists of: (1) a nonempty set ∣M∣|M| called the domain or universe; (2) for each constant symbol cc, an element cM∈∣M∣c^M \in |M|; (3) for each nn-ary function symbol ff, a function fM:∣M∣n→∣M∣f^M: |M|^n \to |M|; and (4) for each nn-ary relation symbol RR, a subset RM⊆∣M∣nR^M \subseteq |M|^n. Truth is then defined recursively through the satisfaction relation: M,σ⊨φM, \sigma \models \varphi (read “MM satisfies φ\varphi under assignment σ\sigma”), where σ\sigma maps variables to elements of the domain. Atomic formulas are checked against the model’s interpretation of relation symbols; connectives work as expected; and quantifiers range over the domain.

Alfred Tarski formalized this semantic definition of truth in “The Concept of Truth in Formalized Languages” (1933), making precise for the first time what it means for a sentence to be true in a structure. His approach — truth is a relationship between sentences and models, defined by recursion on the structure of formulas — remains the foundation of model theory.

The deepest connection between syntax and semantics is Kurt Gödel’s completeness theorem, proved in 1930 and published in “Die Vollständigkeit der Axiome des logischen Funktionenkalküls.” It states that a theory TT has a model if and only if TT is consistent — that is, TT does not prove a contradiction. This means the purely syntactic notion of consistency (you cannot derive ⊥\bot) coincides exactly with the semantic notion of satisfiability (there exists a world where the theory holds). Proof and truth are two sides of the same coin, at least for first-order logic.

The standard model of Peano arithmetic is (N,0,S,+,×)(\mathbb{N}, 0, S, +, \times) — the natural numbers with zero, successor, addition, and multiplication. The standard model of the theory of real closed fields is (R,0,1,+,×,<)(\mathbb{R}, 0, 1, +, \times, <). But theories typically have many models, not just one. The Löwenheim-Skolem theorem states that any consistent theory with an infinite model has models of every infinite cardinality — a first-order theory cannot pin down the “size” of its models. Abraham Robinson exploited this fact to create non-standard analysis in Non-standard Analysis (1966), constructing models of arithmetic and analysis containing infinitesimal and infinite elements.

In Heyting algebra semantics, a model of TT is a Heyting algebra homomorphism from the Lindenbaum-Tarski algebra of TT into a Heyting algebra HH. Truth values are elements of HH rather than the two-element Boolean algebra, so a formula may receive intermediate truth values between ⊤\top and ⊥\bot, representing partial truth.
