Dimension

2026-03-05

The dimension of a vector space is the number of vectors in any basis — the number of independent directions in the space. The real line R\mathbb{R} is one-dimensional: a single number determines a point. The plane R2\mathbb{R}^2 is two-dimensional: you need two numbers (an xx-coordinate and a yy-coordinate). Ordinary space R3\mathbb{R}^3 is three-dimensional: three numbers are needed. The pattern extends: Rn\mathbb{R}^n has dimension nn, meaning nn independent numbers are needed to specify a point.

Dimension is well-defined because all bases of a given vector space have the same cardinality. This is a nontrivial theorem, not an obvious fact. A priori, you might worry that one basis could have 3 vectors and another could have 4. But this cannot happen: any two bases of the same space are in bijection. (In the finite-dimensional case, this is proved by the Steinitz exchange lemma; the infinite-dimensional case requires the axiom of choice.) Because the basis size is invariant, dimension is an intrinsic property of the space — it does not depend on which basis you happen to choose.

Dimension is the complete invariant for finite-dimensional vector spaces over a given field. Two such spaces are isomorphic — structurally identical as vector spaces — if and only if they have the same dimension. R3\mathbb{R}^3 and the space of real polynomials of degree at most 2 (which has basis {1,x,x2}\{1, x, x^2\}) are both 3-dimensional real vector spaces, and are therefore isomorphic: there exists a bijective linear map between them. This is a remarkable classification result — a single number (the dimension) completely determines the structure. Most algebraic structures are not this simple to classify.

Familiar examples: Rn\mathbb{R}^n has dimension nn. The space of polynomials of degree at most dd has dimension d+1d + 1 (the basis is {1,x,x2,…,xd}\{1, x, x^2, \ldots, x^d\}, which has d+1d + 1 elements). The space of m×nm \times n matrices has dimension mnmn (each of the mnmn entries is an independent coordinate). The space of all polynomials (of any degree) is infinite-dimensional: no finite set of polynomials spans it.

Dimension governs many key facts in linear algebra. The rank-nullity theorem says that for a linear map T:V→WT: V \to W, the dimension of the kernel (nullity) plus the dimension of the image (rank) equals the dimension of VV. A system of mm linear equations in nn unknowns has a nontrivial solution whenever n>mn > m — because the kernel of the corresponding linear map must have positive dimension. The number of eigenvalues of a map on an nn-dimensional space is at most nn. At every turn, dimension is the number that controls what is possible.
