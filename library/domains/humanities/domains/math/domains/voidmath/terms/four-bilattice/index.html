Four-bilattice

2026-04-29 The canonical four-element bilattice FOUR — the smallest non-trivial bilattice, with carrier {t, f, ⊥, ⊤} corresponding to true, false, no evidence, and contradictory. Originally Belnap's four-valued logic; the canonical example of voidmath's bilattice structure.

The four-element bilattice, denoted FOUR\mathbf{FOUR}, is the smallest non-trivial bilattice. Its carrier is

{t,f,⊥,⊤}\{\mathbf{t}, \mathbf{f}, \bot, \top\}

and its truth order, knowledge order, and negation make it a complete bilattice with all the structure voidmath uses.

This bilattice was introduced by Belnap (1977) as a four-valued logic for reasoning under incomplete and contradictory information, and recognized by Ginsberg (1988) and Fitting (1991) as the canonical small example of the bilattice structure later generalized.

¶The two orders

The truth order ≤t\leq_t is generated by:

f≤t⊥≤ttandf≤t⊤≤tt.\mathbf{f} \leq_t \bot \leq_t \mathbf{t} \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbf{f} \leq_t \top \leq_t \mathbf{t}.

It runs from f\mathbf{f} (false) to t\mathbf{t} (true), with ⊥\bot and ⊤\top both at intermediate truth-axis positions — they are neither false nor true, on this axis.

The knowledge order ≤k\leq_k is generated by:

⊥≤kf≤k⊤and⊥≤kt≤k⊤.\bot \leq_k \mathbf{f} \leq_k \top \quad \text{and} \quad \bot \leq_k \mathbf{t} \leq_k \top.

It runs from ⊥\bot (no evidence) to ⊤\top (full evidence in both directions), with t\mathbf{t} and f\mathbf{f} at intermediate knowledge-axis positions — they have full evidence in one direction but none in the other.

The two orders are independent. The four corners are the joint extremes: t\mathbf{t} (high truth, mid knowledge), f\mathbf{f} (low truth, mid knowledge), ⊤\top (mid truth, high knowledge), ⊥\bot (mid truth, low knowledge).

¶As a twist product

FOUR\mathbf{FOUR} is isomorphic to the twist product 2⋈\mathbf{2}^\bowtie of the two-element Boolean lattice 2={0,1}\mathbf{2} = \{0, 1\} with itself:

f=⟨0,1⟩,⊥=⟨0,0⟩,⊤=⟨1,1⟩,t=⟨1,0⟩.\mathbf{f} = \langle 0, 1 \rangle, \quad \bot = \langle 0, 0 \rangle, \quad \top = \langle 1, 1 \rangle, \quad \mathbf{t} = \langle 1, 0 \rangle.

The first component tracks positive evidence (1 = full positive, 0 = none); the second tracks negative evidence. Reading off the four canonical configurations:

Value ⟨a+,a−⟩\langle a_+, a_- \rangle Evidence reading
⊥\bot ⟨0,0⟩\langle 0, 0 \rangle no evidence — told nothing
t\mathbf{t} ⟨1,0⟩\langle 1, 0 \rangle only positive — told true
f\mathbf{f} ⟨0,1⟩\langle 0, 1 \rangle only negative — told false
⊤\top ⟨1,1⟩\langle 1, 1 \rangle both directions — told both

Belnap’s original epistemic interpretation of the four values: told true, told false, told nothing, told both. The bilattice structure makes this interpretation formally precise — the four corners are the four canonical evidence configurations, and the orders capture how evidence accumulates and tilts.

¶Negation and the symmetries

Negation on FOUR\mathbf{FOUR} swaps positive and negative evidence:

∼t=f,∼f=t,∼⊥=⊥,∼⊤=⊤.\sim \mathbf{t} = \mathbf{f}, \quad \sim \mathbf{f} = \mathbf{t}, \quad \sim \bot = \bot, \quad \sim \top = \top.

It fixes the symmetric corners (⊥\bot and ⊤\top, which have equal evidence in both directions) and swaps the asymmetric ones (t\mathbf{t} and f\mathbf{f}).

The full symmetry group of FOUR\mathbf{FOUR} — generated by negation and conflation — is the Klein four-group Z2×Z2\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2. Negation is the truth-axis flip; conflation is the knowledge-axis flip; their composition is the 180° rotation that fixes the diagonal and swaps the off-diagonal corners.

¶Why FOUR is canonical

FOUR\mathbf{FOUR} is the free bounded distributive bilattice on one generator (Fitting 2002) — the universal small example of the bilattice structure. Any four-cornered settlement structure of the form true, false, both, neither embeds into a bilattice that contains FOUR\mathbf{FOUR} as a sub-bilattice, and the four canonical evidence configurations always appear in the embedded copy.

In voidmath specifically, FOUR\mathbf{FOUR} is the smallest bilattice on which a non-trivial predication fiber algebra can be built. For larger or richer reasoning, larger bilattices (twist products of bigger residuated lattices) provide finer evidence levels — but the four-cornered structure is always preserved at the bilattice’s extremes.
