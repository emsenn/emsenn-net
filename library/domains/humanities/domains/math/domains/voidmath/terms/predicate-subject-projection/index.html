Predicate subject-projection

2026-04-29 A morphism class in the predicate category, induced by the diagonal collapse (s, o) ↦ (s, s) on the entity-pair index. Builds a binary predicate q whose value at (s, o) depends only on p's value at (s, s), broadcasting the subject-diagonal content across all object coordinates. One of the four canonical generators of the predicate category.

A predicate subject-projection is a morphism in the predicate category, denoted f:q→pf : q \to p, induced by the collapse of the entity-pair index to the subject diagonal:

(s,o)↦(s,s).(s, o) \mapsto (s, s).

The induced fiber-algebra map f∗:Ap→Aqf^* : A_p \to A_q acts cellwise:

f∗(v)(s,o)=v(s,s).f^*(v)(s, o) = v(s, s).

The resulting predicate qq has values that depend only on pp’s subject-diagonal content: at every cell (s,o)(s, o), qq inherits whatever value pp takes at (s,s)(s, s). The object coordinate oo becomes irrelevant — it doesn’t enter the definition of qq’s value at all. Subject-projection is one of the four canonical generator classes of the predicate category.

¶What subject-projection encodes

Subject-projection is the formal source of unary predicates derived from binary ones. A unary predicate — one that depends only on a single entity — is, in the framework’s binary-base structure, a binary predicate whose value happens to be insensitive to one of its coordinates.

The subject-projection morphism makes this precise: qq’s value at (s,o)(s, o) is determined entirely by pp’s subject-diagonal value at (s,s)(s, s). The framework’s binary-base structure (every cell is (s,p,o)(s, p, o)) does not need to admit unary predicates as primitives; they appear as the subject-projection (or object-projection, or both) of binary predicates with appropriate diagonal content.

Examples:

  • A predicate is-tall (unary, of an individual) appears as the subject-projection of taller-than-self — formally, taller-than applied to the subject diagonal.
  • A predicate is-employed (unary) appears as the subject-projection of employs-self, where the diagonal content of employs records who employs whom only when subject and object coincide.

¶The structural source of “unary predicates”

The four-generator structure of the predicate category exhausts the natural maps under Sym(E)\mathrm{Sym}(E)-equivariance. The two projection generators (subject-projection and object-projection) are what allow apparently-unary predicates to appear within the binary base. Without them, unary predicates would need a separate categorical structure; with them, they arise as morphisms within the binary-base predicate category.

The two projections are duals: subject-projection collapses to the subject diagonal (s,s)(s, s); object-projection collapses to the object diagonal (o,o)(o, o). Whether a derived unary predicate captures the subject or the object of the original binary predicate depends on which projection generates it.

¶Composition with converse

Subject-projection composed with converse yields object-projection: swapping subject and object converts collapse-onto-subject-diagonal into collapse-onto-object-diagonal. Symbolically, fconv∘fsp=fopf_{\mathrm{conv}} \circ f_{\mathrm{sp}} = f_{\mathrm{op}} at the level of generator classes (with appropriate composition signs). This gives the predicate category a clean four-generator structure where each pair of generators interacts predictably with the others.
