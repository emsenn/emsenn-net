Negation

2026-04-29 The unary involution on a bilattice that reverses the truth axis while preserving the knowledge axis. Antitone in truth, monotone in knowledge, and self-inverse. On twist products, negation is the swap of evidence components — it exchanges positive and negative evidence.

A negation ∼\sim on a bilattice is a unary operation satisfying three axioms:

  • Antitone in the truth order: a≤tb⇒∼b≤t∼aa \leq_t b \Rightarrow \sim b \leq_t \sim a.
  • Monotone in the knowledge order: a≤kb⇒∼a≤k∼ba \leq_k b \Rightarrow \sim a \leq_k \sim b.
  • Involutive: ∼∼a=a\sim \sim a = a.

A pre-bilattice equipped with a negation is a bilattice — negation is the structure that distinguishes a full bilattice from the bare-bones two-order pre-bilattice.

¶On twist products: evidence swap

When the bilattice is a twist product L⋈\mathbf{L}^\bowtie — the construction that produces voidmath’s bilattice from a residuated lattice — negation acts by swapping the two coordinates:

∼⟨a+,a−⟩=⟨a−,a+⟩.\sim \langle a_+, a_- \rangle = \langle a_-, a_+ \rangle.

The first component (positive evidence) becomes the second component of the negated value; the second component (negative evidence) becomes the first. The two evidence kinds are exchanged.

This evidence-swap reading makes the three axioms transparent:

  • Antitone in truth: the truth axis raises positive evidence and lowers negative; swapping components reverses both — what was tilting toward affirmation now tilts toward denial.
  • Monotone in knowledge: the knowledge axis raises both components symmetrically; swapping components preserves the joint position — the total amount of evidence is unchanged.
  • Involutive: swapping twice returns the original.

¶On the four-element bilattice

In the four-element bilattice FOUR\mathbf{FOUR}, negation acts by:

Original Negated
⊥=⟨0,0⟩\bot = \langle 0, 0 \rangle ⊥=⟨0,0⟩\bot = \langle 0, 0 \rangle (fixed)
t=⟨1,0⟩\mathbf{t} = \langle 1, 0 \rangle f=⟨0,1⟩\mathbf{f} = \langle 0, 1 \rangle
f=⟨0,1⟩\mathbf{f} = \langle 0, 1 \rangle t=⟨1,0⟩\mathbf{t} = \langle 1, 0 \rangle
⊤=⟨1,1⟩\top = \langle 1, 1 \rangle ⊤=⟨1,1⟩\top = \langle 1, 1 \rangle (fixed)

Negation fixes ⊥\bot and ⊤\top — the two corners with symmetric evidence configurations (no evidence and equal evidence) — and swaps t\mathbf{t} and f\mathbf{f}, which are the asymmetric configurations. This is the structural reason the bilattice is non-Boolean: a Boolean negation would fix neither ⊥\bot nor ⊤\top, and would have a complement structure that the bilattice does not.

¶Negation and conflation

A bilattice may carry a second involution called conflation, which has the dual axiomatic profile (antitone in knowledge, monotone in truth). When both negation and conflation are present, they are typically required to commute: ∼ −=− ∼\sim \, - = -\, \sim. Together they generate the Klein four-group of bilattice symmetries: {id,∼,−,∼∘−}\{\mathrm{id}, \sim, -, \sim \circ -\}, with each element order 2 and the four together giving Z2×Z2\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2.

The Klein four-group structure is the symmetry group of the bilattice viewed as a square with the four corners t,f,⊤,⊥\mathbf{t}, \mathbf{f}, \top, \bot. The corners can be thought of as the vertices of the square; negation is the horizontal reflection (truth axis); conflation is the vertical reflection (knowledge axis); their composition is the 180° rotation.
