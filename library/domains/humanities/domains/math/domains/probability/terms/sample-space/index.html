Sample Space

2026-01-01

The sample space is the set of all possible outcomes of a probabilistic experiment — everything that could happen, listed exhaustively. For a coin flip, the sample space is Ω={heads,tails}\Omega = \{\text{heads}, \text{tails}\}. For rolling a standard die, Ω={1,2,3,4,5,6}\Omega = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}. For measuring the temperature outside right now, Ω\Omega might be the real numbers, or more realistically some interval like [−50,60][-50, 60] degrees Celsius. The sample space is the universe within which probability operates: everything that receives a probability must be expressible in terms of outcomes from Ω\Omega.

Choosing the right sample space is the first and most consequential decision in modeling uncertainty. The sample space determines what questions can be asked. If you model a coin flip with Ω={heads,tails}\Omega = \{\text{heads}, \text{tails}\}, you cannot ask “did the coin land on its edge?” — that outcome is not in the sample space. If you model two dice rolls with Ω={2,3,…,12}\Omega = \{2, 3, \ldots, 12\} (the possible totals), you lose the ability to distinguish whether a 7 came from 3+4 or from 2+5 — information that matters if the dice are not identical. A finer sample space Ω={(i,j):1≤i,j≤6}\Omega = \{(i, j) : 1 \leq i, j \leq 6\}, listing all 36 ordered pairs, preserves this information.

An event is a subset of the sample space: a collection of outcomes to which a probability can be assigned. “Rolling an even number” is the event {2,4,6}\{2, 4, 6\}. “The temperature exceeds 30°C” is the event (30,60](30, 60]. Events combine through set operations: union ("AA or BB"), intersection ("AA and BB"), complement (“not AA”).

For finite or countable sample spaces, every subset is typically treated as an event. But for uncountable sample spaces like R\mathbb{R}, not every subset can be consistently assigned a probability — there exist pathological subsets (non-measurable sets, whose existence depends on the axiom of choice) that break the probability axioms. The solution is to restrict events to a σ-algebra F\mathcal{F}: a collection of subsets of Ω\Omega that is closed under complement and countable union, and that contains Ω\Omega and ∅\emptyset. The standard σ-algebra on R\mathbb{R} is the Borel σ-algebra, generated by all open intervals — it contains every set you would encounter in practice while excluding the pathological ones.

The triple (Ω,F,P)(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P) — sample space, σ-algebra of events, probability measure — is a probability space, the formal foundation for all probabilistic reasoning. Andrey Nikolaevich Kolmogorov established this framework in Grundbegriffe der Wahrscheinlichkeitsrechnung (1933), grounding probability theory in measure theory and giving it the same axiomatic rigor as the rest of mathematics.
