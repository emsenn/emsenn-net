Integral Domain

2026-03-05

An integral domain is a commutative ring where multiplication behaves the way you expect it to: if the product of two things is zero, then at least one of them must be zero. This property — the absence of zero divisors — is so natural that it takes effort to imagine a ring without it. But such rings exist. In Z/6Z\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} (the integers modulo 6), 2×3=6≡0(mod6)2 \times 3 = 6 \equiv 0 \pmod{6}, even though neither 2 nor 3 is zero modulo 6. That makes Z/6Z\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} not an integral domain. The integers Z\mathbb{Z}, by contrast, are the prototypical integral domain: if ab=0ab = 0 in Z\mathbb{Z}, then a=0a = 0 or b=0b = 0, period.

Formally, an integral domain is a commutative ring with 1≠01 \neq 0 and the property that for all a,ba, b in the ring, a⋅b=0a \cdot b = 0 implies a=0a = 0 or b=0b = 0. The condition 1≠01 \neq 0 excludes the trivial zero ring (which has only one element and is a degenerate case).

The absence of zero divisors is equivalent to the cancellation law: if a⋅b=a⋅ca \cdot b = a \cdot c and a≠0a \neq 0, then b=cb = c. You can “cancel” a common nonzero factor from both sides of an equation. In a ring with zero divisors, cancellation fails: in Z/6Z\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}, 2×1=2×42 \times 1 = 2 \times 4 (both are 2 mod 62 \bmod 6), but 1≠41 \neq 4. The ability to cancel is what makes algebraic manipulation in integral domains feel like working with ordinary numbers.

Every field is an integral domain — if ab=0ab = 0 and a≠0a \neq 0, multiply both sides by a−1a^{-1} to get b=0b = 0. But not every integral domain is a field: the integers are an integral domain, but 22 has no multiplicative inverse in Z\mathbb{Z} (there is no integer nn with 2n=12n = 1). Polynomial rings F[x]F[x] over a field FF are integral domains for the same reason: the degree of a product is the sum of the degrees, so a nonzero polynomial times a nonzero polynomial cannot be zero.

Every integral domain can be embedded into a field — its field of fractions — by the same construction that builds the rationals from the integers. You form formal fractions a/ba/b (with b≠0b \neq 0), declare a/b=c/da/b = c/d when ad=bcad = bc, and define addition and multiplication in the usual way. The field of fractions of Z\mathbb{Z} is Q\mathbb{Q}. The field of fractions of F[x]F[x] is the field of rational functions F(x)F(x). This construction guarantees that every integral domain has a natural home inside a field, extending the original ring with just enough new elements to make division (by nonzero elements) always possible.
