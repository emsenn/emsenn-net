Energy

2026-03-04 The capacity to produce change — what enables a thing to act.
Table of contents

  • Energy and Force
  • Connections

Energy is the capacity to act — what enables a thing to produce change.

The word comes from the Greek energeia, which Aristotle used to mean actuality or activity. It literally means “in-work” — en (in) plus ergon (work). When you have energy, you have the capacity to do work. When a thing has energy, it can produce change in itself or the world around it.

¶What It Is

In physics, energy is precisely defined: it is the capacity to do work, where work is force applied across distance. A moving object has kinetic energy — the energy of motion. An object held above the ground has potential energy — the energy of position. A hot object has thermal energy — the capacity to transfer heat. A chemical bond has chemical energy — the capacity to release itself. These are not different substances. They are different measures of a thing’s capacity to produce change.

The crucial insight: energy is not a fluid or substance. You cannot point at energy the way you point at a rock. Energy is always the capacity of something — an object, a field, a system. A battery has chemical energy. A spring compressed has elastic energy. An elevated weight has gravitational energy. Energy belongs to a thing; it describes what that thing can do.

Energy converts between forms but does not disappear. A falling weight has potential energy at the top and kinetic energy at the bottom. Burn wood and chemical energy becomes thermal energy and light energy. This is the first law of thermodynamics: energy is conserved. In a closed system, the total energy before and after a change is the same. The forms change, but the total amount remains constant.

Yet energy also degrades. When you burn fuel, you release energy as heat and work. But the heat disperses into the surrounding environment, spreading to air and ground until it mixes uniformly with the cooler background. This heat energy still exists — the first law still holds — but it is no longer useful. You cannot use it to drive a car or light a bulb. This is the second law of thermodynamics: entropy increases. Usable energy becomes unusable. Energy becomes spread and diluted. The universe tends toward disorder.

This matters for how acts work. An action requires energy. Your muscles need energy to move your arm. A plant needs energy from the sun to grow. A river has energy from the height of the land to flow downhill. But as energy does its work, some of it degrades. You get tired because some of the chemical energy in food becomes muscle work, and some becomes waste heat. The plant captures some light energy and converts it to growth, but most is absorbed and lost. This is not failure or inefficiency in an absolute sense — it is the second law asserting itself. All real processes degrade some energy.

¶Two Views of Energy

There are two very different ways to understand what energy is.

The first view is mechanical and measurable. Energy is a quantity. You measure it in joules or calories. You calculate how much energy is in a system, how much is released, how much remains. This is the physics view, and it has been extraordinarily successful. It powers engineering, thermodynamics, chemistry. Everything in the natural world obeys these laws.

The second view is qualitative and directional. Energy is not a quantity but an orientation, a striving, a going-toward. Aristotle meant this when he coined energeia: it is actuality, the having-become of what was merely potential. For Aristotle, a thing has energy when it is actively exercising its capacity — when the rock, falling, is actually falling, not just sitting on a ledge with the potential to fall. Energy here is not measured; it is the fact of a thing being-in-act.

Bergson’s élan vital (often called “life force,” though that is misleading) captures this sense. The élan is not a substance. It is the principle of creative advance — the drive toward novelty, the tendency toward greater complexity and elaboration. Living things seem to aim at their own continuation and elaboration. They respond to their world, they adapt, they elaborate new forms. This is not mechanism — not just the working-out of a pre-set program. It is genuine creation, the emergence of something new. The élan vital is what drives this. It is not a quantity you measure; it is a directionality, an immanence to life itself.

Whitehead, in Process and Reality, calls this the subjective aim. Every actual occasion (every happening, every act of becoming) has an orientation toward a goal. The occasion is not a passive being-shaped by external forces. It is self-creating, and its self-creation has a direction built into it. The occasion’s energy is its own internal directionality — what Whitehead calls its subjective aim. Energy here is not something the occasion has; it is what the occasion is — its own process of becoming itself.

¶Energy and Force

Energy and force are often confused. They are distinct.

A force is an influence that produces change against resistance. Gravity is a force. It pulls bodies together. Electromagnetism is a force. It holds atoms together and repels magnets. A force acts on a thing; it pushes or pulls.

Energy is the capacity to do work — to produce change. Energy is what a thing has; force is what a thing exerts. A rock on a high ledge has potential energy. The force of gravity acts on it. When the rock falls, the force of gravity does work on it, converting potential energy to kinetic energy. The force is the cause; the energy is the capacity that enables the force to produce change.

You can think of it this way: a thing with energy is ready to act. A moving car has kinetic energy; it is ready to plow into whatever stands in its way. A compressed spring has elastic energy; it is ready to push. A hot cup of coffee has thermal energy; it is ready to warm your hands. Force is applied energy against resistance. When the car presses against a wall, the wall exerts a force back. The energy of the car becomes work done on the wall, or waste heat, or sound. The force is the moment of application. The energy is the capacity that made application possible.

¶Connections

Energy enables acts. An act is an exercise of capacity, and capacity is what energy is. To act is to produce change using the resources available to you — and those resources are your energy.

Energy relates to force. Forces apply energy, converting potential capacity into actual change. Without force, energy is dormant. Without energy, force has nothing to apply.

Energy produces change. Change does not happen without energy. Nothing moves, nothing transforms, nothing becomes unless there is energy to power it.
