Time

2026-04-03 The dimension in which change occurs and events are ordered — what makes before and after meaningful.

Time is the dimension in which change occurs and events are ordered — what makes “before” and “after” meaningful. Without time, there is no change, no events, no history. A thing could have no past and no future. It could only be what it is right now, forever. Augustine captured this in Confessions XI: “What then is time? If no one asks me, I know; if I want to explain it, I do not know.”

Time separates a thing’s state at one moment from its state at another. A patient’s blood pressure at Monday 10am is not the same as their blood pressure at Monday 4pm, because time has passed. A chess game at move 5 is not the same as the game at move 20, because moves have occurred. Time is what makes this difference real. It is not just a label you apply after the fact; it is the structure within which change becomes possible.

¶A-series and B-series

McTaggart (1908) distinguished two ways time is real. The A-series is tensed: past, present, future, ordered around the moving NOW. Today is today. Yesterday it was tomorrow. Tomorrow it will be yesterday. The present moment is privileged — it is where reality happens. The B-series is tenseless: earlier-than, later-than, simultaneous-with. It has no privileged moment. One event stands in a before/after relation to another, full stop. Both describe the same sequence of events; they differ in whether the present is special.

Both are real. A deadline that lies in the future and a deadline that has passed feel different — this is the A-series. They also stand in a definite temporal order — this is the B-series. The difference matters. The A-series captures the lived experience of time’s flow. The B-series captures time’s objective structure. You cannot have one without the other.

¶Duration and clock time

Bergson (Time and Free Will, 1889) made a discovery that splits time itself in two. Real time is duration (durée) — qualitative, continuous, indivisible. It is the flow of becoming. When you experience a melody, you do not hear a sequence of discrete notes; you hear a continuous unfolding where each note colors what comes next. Duration is lived time.

Clock time spatializes duration. It lays time out as a line of points: 10:00, 10:01, 10:02. You can measure it, count it, reverse the direction on paper. But this is an abstraction. The clock treats time like space — as if you can move backward through it, as if moments are independent objects laid out side by side. Real duration is irreversible. Once you have lived a moment, it is gone. You cannot go back. The melody that has sounded cannot be unheard.

Whitehead (Process and Reality, 1929) inverted the usual way we think about this. Time is not a container in which events happen. Time is the creative advance of actual occasions — discrete drops of becoming. Each occasion comes into being, achieves definiteness (what Whitehead calls concrescence — growing together), and then perishes. It becomes data for the next occasion. Time is what happens when occasions arise and give way to new ones. Without events, there is no time. Time is the process of reality coming into being.

¶The arrow of time

Time has a direction. You can unscramble an egg in thought, but never in practice. The second law of thermodynamics — entropy increases — gives time a direction. Prigogine (The End of Certainty, 1997) argued that this irreversibility is not a statistical illusion or a limitation of knowledge. It is fundamental. In systems far from equilibrium, genuine novelty emerges. The universe does not merely rearrange pre-existing possibilities; it generates new ones. Forward in time means genuinely different states. Backward is forbidden.

Consider a court proceeding. It moves forward in real time. Evidence is presented. Witnesses testify. Arguments are made. Once that testimony is given, it is given. The proceeding cannot reverse and unhear what was said. The trial has an arrow. It is moving forward through genuine novelty — each moment opens possibilities that would not have existed without that moment’s having occurred. This is what the thermodynamic arrow means: time is not neutral. It has a direction because reality itself is generative.

¶Cyclical and spiral time

Western philosophy has often treated time as linear — a straight line from past through present to future. But this is not the only way time is real. Many Indigenous traditions understand time as cyclical: seasons return, ceremonies recur, patterns repeat. The Lakota, Haudenosaunee, and Aboriginal Australian peoples organize temporal experience around cycles — the day-night cycle, the seasonal cycle, the generational cycle. Obligations return across generations. The ceremonial year returns. Time is not a line; it is a spiral.

The spiral captures something neither pure linearity nor pure cyclicity captures alone. The year returns to spring — the cycle is real. But the spring that returns is spring in a changed world. The climate has shifted. The seeds you plant are different. Last spring and this spring are both springs, but they are distinct. They recur, but they are not identical. Time is a spiral: cycles are real, but novelty is real too. The pattern returns; the world moves forward.

¶A condition is what time makes possible

A condition is a quality a thing has at a time. “The patient is febrile” is a condition — it is true (or false) at a specific moment, at a specific location. Without time, there would be no conditions, only permanent eternal qualities. But the patient’s fever comes and goes. The chess position changes. The trial moves from one state to another. Time is what allows qualities to be contingent — real at one moment and different at another. Time is what makes history possible.
