Act

2026-03-04 What a thing does that produces change — the realization of capacity, from Aristotle's energeia to Whitehead's actual occasions.

An act is what a thing does that produces change. Pulling a trigger, healing a patient, writing a sentence, deciding to stop — all acts. An act is the manifestation of a thing’s capacity, the realization of its potential to affect the world or itself.

In Aristotle’s Metaphysics (Book IX), an act is energeia — actuality, being-at-work. Aristotle distinguishes this from dunamis, potentiality — the capacity to act. A doctor has the capacity to heal (dunamis); when healing a patient, the doctor is actually healing (energeia). Potentiality is what something can do; actuality is what it is doing. The distinction cuts deeper than tense. Aristotle distinguishes two types of change: kinesis is incomplete motion toward an end — a runner engaged in running has not yet reached the finish. Energeia is activity complete at every moment of its occurrence — the pleasure of seeing, the joy of thinking are fully realized at every instant, not waiting for completion. This separates acts that are means to an end (kinesis) from acts that are their own end (energeia).

The same physical event can be many different acts depending on how you describe it. G. E. M. Anscombe, in Intention (1957), shows this with a single example: moving a finger is pulling a trigger is firing a gun is killing a person. These are not four different events. They are one event under four different descriptions, each true and complete. Acts nest under multiple descriptions simultaneously. Alvin Goldman’s A Theory of Human Action (1970) systematizes this nesting: acts form trees where simpler acts level-generate into more complex ones. Walking to the store is the way you buy groceries. Buying groceries is the way you feed your family. The act-tree shows which acts are basic (what you do without doing anything else first) and which are derived (realized through performing other acts). This composition is not arbitrary — it reflects the real structure of how acts are performed.

For Alfred North Whitehead, in Process and Reality (1929), the picture inverts. There is no persistent substance that does the acting. An actual occasion IS its activity — the process of concrescence where the occasion takes in the world (its predecessor occasions, the contemporary world, eternal possibilities) and creates itself. When the occasion completes its becoming, it perishes as a subject and becomes data (past) for the next occasion. What we call a thing — a rock, a person, a tree — is a society of occasions, a temporal series with a persisting pattern. The rock is not a substance underlying change. The rock is a pattern of occasions, each becoming and perishing, each inheriting and carrying forward the character of its predecessors. For Whitehead, acts (occasions) are more fundamental than things. Things are abstractions from sustained patterns of acts. To ask what a rock is, is to ask what it does — and the answer is the entire process of its self-actualization across time.

Acts compose. They form sequences and hierarchies. An act can be a part of a larger act. The whole structure of human activity — from basic muscular contractions to complex projects spanning years — is built from acts composing into more complex acts. A gesture is part of a greeting. A greeting is part of a social encounter. A social encounter is part of a relationship. Each level is an authentic act, describable in its own terms, yet also a component of something larger.

An act requires energy — the capacity to act. It produces change in the environment or in the thing itself. An act that occurs at a specific time is an event. An act that succeeds reaches its end; an act that fails does not. Whether an act is an expression of a thing’s nature or a violation of it depends on what state the thing is in and what capacities it has.
