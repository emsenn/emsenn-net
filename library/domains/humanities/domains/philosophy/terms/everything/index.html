Everything

2026-03-04 Every thing taken together — the totality without boundary, the universe of reference.

Everything is every thing taken together — the totality without exclusion or boundary.

The concept seems simple until you try to define it precisely. What is the whole? The universe? All that exists? But as soon as you formalize this, paradoxes appear. Mereology — the logic of parts and wholes — offers a framework: everything is the mereological sum of all parts. Transitivity, closure, structure. But mereology requires that you can actually identify all the parts, that parthood has determinate meaning. It works for bounded systems (a machine, an organism) but breaks when applied to the infinite.

Set theory offers another approach: everything is the set of all things. But set theory runs into Russell’s paradox (1901). The set of all sets that do not contain themselves leads to contradiction either way. To avoid this, modern set theory simply refuses to speak of the set of all sets. There is no universal set. The totality is not a set.

Georg Cantor (1872–1918) discovered something more troubling. For any set S, the power set of S (the set of all subsets of S) has a larger cardinality than S. This means there is no largest infinity. For any infinity, there is a larger one. The hierarchy of infinities is itself infinite. Cantor distinguished between transfinite sets (infinite but graspable, calculable) and the absolute infinite (beyond transfinite, ungraspable, the totality itself). The absolute infinite is not a set; it is the condition that makes any set possible.

What this reveals is that totality is not a thing, not an enumerable collection, not a complete object you can reason about the way you reason about particular things. The totality exceeds the logic of sets and wholes.

Alfred North Whitehead (1929) approached this differently. Reality is not a collection of things that exist at a time. Reality is an ongoing process of becoming. Each actual occasion comes into being by gathering the data of the past and adding something new — a determination of how the past is to be felt and transformed. This determination is novel; it is not fully contained in the past. So when a new occasion becomes, the universe advances creatively. Everything is this continuous creative advance. You cannot inventory it at a moment in time because it is always becoming, always opening into new possibilities.

Nāgārjuna (c. 150 CE) took a different path through the problem. Everything is the totality of dependently-originated phenomena. Nothing has intrinsic self-nature. Everything exists only in dependence on other things. A table exists only in dependence on wood, a maker, a concept of “table,” someone to call it a table. Change any of these dependencies, and the table ceases to be. There is no table in itself. Applied to the totality: there is no “all things” as a fixed whole. There is only the infinite mutual dependence of things on each other. The totality is the relational fabric itself.

Donna Haraway (2016) grounds this in actual material relations. Nothing makes itself. Every being is sympoietic — made with, through, and because of other beings. A human is made of bacteria, of dietary elements (animals and plants consumed and transformed), of cultural inheritance (language, knowledge, practices). There is no clear boundary between the human and what is “not the human.” The totality is the continuously generating field of sympoietic relations. Every entity is an entanglement of other entities. You cannot separate the organism from its world without destroying both.

Indigenous relational ontologies express similar insights. The Lakota concept Mitákuye Oyás’iŋ (“all my relatives”) expresses totality not as an enumerable set of things but as a web of kinship. Everything is kin because everything is in relation. A stone is not a non-living thing that happens to be present; it is a relative. Robin Wall Kimmerer (2013) describes the three sisters — corn, beans, and squash — as a relation, not as three separate plants. Each is what it is through its relation to the others. The totality is the web of reciprocal relations, the continuously woven fabric of obligation and gift.

What unites these accounts is that they all refuse to treat totality as a completed inventory or a fixed being. The totality cannot be totalized. It cannot be captured in a complete, static, enumerable form. But it can be grasped relationally, through the structure of dependence that constitutes it. To understand the totality is not to achieve complete knowledge of all things (which is impossible). It is to understand the patterns of relation that make any particular thing meaningful. It is to see how everything is implicated in everything else, how boundaries between things are enacted and perspectival, how new possibilities continuously emerge.

Everything is not a thing or a set of things. It is a relational process. It is open, not closed. It is becoming, not being. It is the continuous web of making-with, the endless creative advance, the infinite mutual dependence that makes any particular thing possible.
