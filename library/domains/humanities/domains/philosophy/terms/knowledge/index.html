Knowledge

2026-03-04 Information that has been warranted through inquiry — understanding tested, structured, and integrated into practice.

Knowledge is information that has been warranted through inquiry — not just accumulated data but understanding that has been tested, structured, and integrated.

Information is a difference that makes a difference; it reduces uncertainty. But information alone is not knowledge. A person can have access to all true information about a domain — every fact, every law, every principle — and yet lack knowledge of how to act, how to judge, how to recognize patterns in new contexts.

Plato (Theaetetus) began the Western tradition with a definition that held for 2,300 years: knowledge is justified true belief. Three parts, all necessary. You must believe it (mental commitment). It must be true (match reality). You must have justification (grounded in reasons). All three together constitute knowledge.

Edmund Gettier (1963) showed this definition is insufficient. He constructed cases where someone has justified true belief but lacks genuine knowledge. The appearance of justification can mask hidden defects in warrant. This reveals a deep structure: you can have perfect information, sound reasoning, and true conclusions and still lack knowledge if the chain of reasoning is defeated.

Michael Polanyi (The Tacit Dimension, 1966) inverted the question. Rather than asking “What makes belief count as knowledge?” he asked “What do we actually know when we know?” The answer: we know more than we can tell. A surgeon recognizes a condition through touch but cannot articulate the criteria. A craftsperson judges quality through feel but cannot reduce it to rules. A native speaker has command of grammar but cannot state the rules. This is tacit knowledge: embodied, practical, implicit. No explicit instruction can fully capture it. It is transmitted through apprenticeship, through shared practice, through the prolonged exposure that allows knowing to be internalized.

John Dewey (Logic: The Theory of Inquiry, 1938) transformed epistemology by treating knowledge not as a mental possession but as the outcome of inquiry. To know is to have gone through a process. Inquiry begins with an indeterminate situation — a problem, a confusion, something that doesn’t fit expected patterns. Inquiry is the process of transforming that indeterminacy into determinacy, resolving the confusion so that action becomes possible. Knowing is the settlement of inquiry, the point where the situation becomes clear enough to act.

Inquiry is not solitary. Charles Sanders Peirce argued that knowledge is what the community of inquiry converges on in the long run. Truth is not what an individual mind certifies; it is what sustained, self-correcting inquiry across a community produces. A belief is true if it would be sustained by inquiry carried far enough and refined enough. This gives knowledge a social structure: it emerges from the clash of perspectives, the testing of ideas against experience and against other minds.

Donna Haraway (1988) emphasized that all knowledge is situated. There is no view from nowhere. Knowledge is always produced from a particular location, with particular interests, particular blindnesses. Indigenous knowledge is relational, reciprocal, place-based — knowledge of how to live well with particular land, particular relatives, particular conditions. This is not inferior to scientific knowledge; it is a different structure of knowing, grounded in different commitments and relationships.

Knowledge differs from information in that it is integrated into practice and community. It is tested against experience. It has been structured through inquiry and debate. It carries the weight of justification — reasons that hold it up. It is durable: it can be transmitted, built on, refined. A fact might be informative but isolated, disconnected. Knowledge is a web of relationships, each element supporting others, the whole structured by what can be acted on.
