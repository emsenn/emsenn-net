Property

2026-04-03 What a thing possesses — an intrinsic feature distinct from an observed quality. A knife is sharp whether or not anyone tests it. Distinguishes categorical properties (what something IS) from dispositional properties (what it CAN DO).

A property is what a thing HAS whether or not anyone observes it — an intrinsic feature rather than an observed quality. A knife is sharp whether or not anyone cuts with it. A diamond is hard whether or not anyone tests its hardness. The property persists independent of observation. Quality can be observer-dependent (a color looks different under different light); a property is intrinsic to the thing itself.

¶Categorical Properties: What Something IS

David Armstrong’s Universals and Scientific Realism (1978) makes a fundamental distinction that clarifies properties. A categorical property is what a thing currently IS — its intrinsic state, observable right now. A knife’s shape is categorical. The blade IS sharp-edged, pointed, curved. A crystal’s structure is categorical: it IS cubic or hexagonal. A person’s height is categorical: they ARE six feet tall. These properties are definite, current, determinate. When you observe a categorical property, you see what the thing is, independent of what it does.

Categorical properties are not merely relational. A crystal’s shape exists whether or not anyone measures it. A stone’s weight exists whether or not anyone lifts it. These are intrinsic to the thing. However, they are realized through the thing’s structure and composition. A diamond is hard because of its atomic lattice. An iron bar is heavy because of its atomic density. The categorical property has a physical ground.

Categorical properties are relatively stable. A stone remains hard across decades unless broken. A person’s genetic code remains constant across their lifetime. But stability is not permanence. Categorical properties can change through external action (an edge dulls, a person ages) or internal processes (rust, decay). The point is that at any moment, the thing HAS this categorical property — this IS its current state.

¶Dispositional Properties: What Something CAN DO

But a property is not merely its current state. George Molnar’s Powers: A Study in Metaphysics (2003) makes a radical claim: many of the most important properties are dispositional — powers, capacities, tendencies to act under certain conditions.

A glass is fragile. This is not a categorical state you can observe in isolation. A glass sitting peacefully on a table shows no visible fragility. But the glass HAS the property of fragility — the power to shatter when struck. This power is real even when unmanifested. A lump of sugar is soluble. Sitting in a dry room, it shows no visible solubility. Drop it in water, and the power manifests — the sugar dissolves. Solubility is a real property, a capacity intrinsic to sugar’s nature, whether or not water is present.

Molnar argues this applies broadly. Sharpness is not merely a geometric property (the blade’s edge angle) but a power-to-cut. Trustworthiness is not a static trait but a power-to-act-reliably under obligation. Courage is not a stable state but a power-to-act-well under danger. A substance’s toxicity is a power-to-harm when ingested. These dispositional properties are as real as categorical ones, but they manifest only under the right conditions.

The distinction matters because it shows that reality is more fundamentally characterized by potentials and powers than by static states. A metal is not merely a certain density and color right now. It is a power-to-conduct-electricity, a power-to-rust, a power-to-ring-when-struck. The dispositional properties are what the thing can DO; the categorical properties are what it IS. Both are real.

¶Primary vs. Secondary Properties: The Locke Distinction

John Locke’s Essay Concerning Human Understanding (1689) Book II, Chapter 8 introduces a different distinction: between properties that are intrinsic to a thing and properties that depend on observation.

A primary property belongs to the thing itself, whether or not anyone observes it. Extension (size, shape), solidity (occupying space), motion, number, divisibility. A block of gold has shape, mass, and spatial extent independent of whether anyone measures it. Remove all observers and these properties remain.

A secondary property exists in relation to observers. Color, taste, smell, sound — these seem to be in the object but depend essentially on how observers perceive the object. Gold appears gold because light reflects from its surface and human eyes perceive that wavelength. The gold itself has no color; it has surface properties (microstructure, reflectance) that cause a sensation of color in us. A piece of food has no taste until someone tastes it; it has chemical properties that cause taste-sensations in the taster.

Locke’s distinction clarifies something real: some properties are determinate independent of observation (mass, extension, solidity), while others are inherently relational, existing only in the interaction between thing and observer. Yet the boundary is less sharp than Locke suggested. Is the shape of a surface “in” the object or dependent on how we measure it? Is solubility intrinsic or relational (depending on what solvents are available)? The useful insight is that understanding any complex thing requires attending to both intrinsic determinations and observer-dependent, relational properties.

¶Eternal Objects: The Process View

The substance tradition (Locke, Armstrong, Molnar) asks: “What ARE properties?” and answers with intrinsic features, whether categorical or dispositional. The process tradition asks: “How do properties become actual?” and inverts the dependency.

Alfred North Whitehead’s Process and Reality (1929) Part III posits that properties are not features things have. Properties are abstract potentials that things realize. Whitehead calls these eternal objects — patterns, determinants, pure possibilities. Sharpness is an eternal object. So is cubic-structure, solubility, redness, the capacity-to-sustain-life. An eternal object is not a Platonic form floating in a realm apart. It is pure potential, awaiting actualization by a concrete thing.

When a thing comes into being, it grasps or prehends certain eternal objects. It ingresses them — actualizes them by embodying them. A person at this moment is an actual occasion that realizes the eternal objects {this-body-shape, this-emotional-tone, this-intention, this-relational-nexus}. The person does not have properties like a substance has clothes. The person consists of the eternal objects she realizes, together with her relations to other things.

On this view, there is no property apart from actualization. The potential-to-be-sharp is not yet a property; it is pure potential. When it ingresses into the steel blade, sharpness becomes a property. The property is the eternal object’s realization in concrete actuality.

¶Property vs. Quality

The distinction between property and quality is subtle but important. Both refer to features of things, but with different emphasis.

Property suggests something intrinsic to the thing, whether or not observed. The emphasis is on what-the-thing-IS, independent of context or observers. A property is a feature the thing possesses.

Quality is the broader term, encompassing anything distinguishable about a thing. A quality can be observer-dependent (color under this light), relational (being-larger-than another object), or observer-independent (mass). The emphasis is on what-is-distinguishable, what-can-be-observed-or-measured.

In some philosophical traditions, the terms are used interchangeably. Aristotle’s poiotes (quality) covers both categorical features (shape) and dispositional capacities (the ability to be shaped). Locke’s primary qualities are properties intrinsic to the thing. But “property” picks out the intrinsic, thing-focused aspect, while “quality” picks out the observable, experiential aspect. A property might be intrinsic but unobservable; a quality might be observable but relational.

¶Properties and Identity

Gottfried Leibniz’s principle of the identity of indiscernibles states: if two things share ALL properties and relations, they ARE the same thing. This means properties are not incidental to identity. What a thing IS (its total constellation of properties and relations) exhausts what makes it the thing it is. Change a property, and you change the thing — or change which thing you are talking about.

This principle has deep consequences. It means there is no mysterious “thing-itself” apart from its properties and relations. The thing just IS the bundle of its properties. A person is not a substance that happens to have courage, intelligence, and affection. The person IS the particular realization of those properties (among many others) in a continuous process. Change the properties fundamentally, and you change the person.

¶Properties in Process

In the process view, properties are not static. They are realized, maintained, transformed through the continuous becoming of things. A person’s courage is not a property she acquired at age seven and keeps forever. Courage is a power realized in concrete situations, manifested through actions and choices. In one context she is courageous; in another, fearful. The dispositional property exists as a continuous potential; the categorical manifestation shifts.

Similarly, a river’s property of flowing is not a single state. It is continuously realized through the movement of water, conditioned by the terrain and rainfall. The property persists as the pattern continues; it dissolves if the river dries up.

This suggests that properties are better understood as eternal objects (potentials) that things continuously realize, rather than as static features things possess. The property has both an intrinsic aspect (what the thing CAN BE or CAN DO) and a relational aspect (how it IS realized in concrete interaction with other things).
