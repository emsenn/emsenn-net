Authenticity

2026-04-02 The claim that a person, practice, or community expresses its true nature — used in Romantic nationalism to distinguish 'real' folk life from corrupted modernity, and in genring to police the boundary between who counts as a genuine member of a people and who does not.

Authenticity is the claim that a person, practice, or community expresses its true nature — that it is what it really is, uncontaminated by external influence, faithful to an original form. The concept has roots in Existentialist philosophy (Heidegger, Sartre) but its political deployment belongs to Romantic nationalism: the claim that each people has an authentic spirit (Volksgeist) expressed in authentic practices, and that these practices must be preserved against the corruptions of modernity.

The political problem with authenticity is that it requires a judge. Someone must decide which practices count as authentic — which songs, which costumes, which rituals express the true spirit of the people — and which are corrupted, borrowed, or modern. This curation is the first step of genring: selecting features, codifying them, and circulating the codified version as the real thing. The Brothers Grimm decided which tales were authentically German. The Pahlavi regime decided which traditions were authentically Iranian. The Trachtenverein movement decided which dress was authentically Bavarian. In each case, the “authentic” product was a recent construction serving present needs — an invention of tradition claiming ancient origins.

Richard Handler (1986) argues that authenticity is a Western cultural category, not a universal standard. The demand that a culture be “authentic” — coherent, bounded, internally consistent, traceable to an origin — reflects Western assumptions about identity that do not map onto all cultural self-understandings. Many peoples organize their identity not around purity and origin but around relationship, adaptation, and ongoing negotiation. The demand for authenticity forecloses these alternatives.

Authenticity functions as a trap for genred peoples. The genre figure (the Noble Indian, the Hearty Bavarian, the Mountain Kurd) is defined as the authentic version of the people. Anyone who matches the genre is recognized as real. Anyone who deviates is experienced as inauthentic — not merely different but wrong, a threat to genre coherence. A Lakota person who does not perform the Noble Indian is not just unrecognized; they are suspected of having betrayed their own nature. The concept of authenticity makes the genre feel like a standard the person has failed to meet, rather than a fiction imposed on them.
