Identity

2026-04-03 What makes a thing THIS thing and not another — what persists through change. Identity is not primitive; it is grounded in properties, relations, causal history, and the continuity of a process of becoming.

Identity is what makes a thing THIS thing and not another — what persists through change. Your body replaces nearly every cell over seven years, yet you are still you. The Ship of Theseus: replace every plank, is it still the same ship? Identity is not trivial. It is not given in advance. Identity must be explained, grounded, constituted.

The Identity of Indiscernibles

Gottfried Leibniz’s principle of the identity of indiscernibles states the classical answer: if two things are absolutely identical in ALL their properties and ALL their relations, then they ARE the same thing. Conversely: if two things differ in ANY property or relation, they are distinct.

This principle has enormous consequences. It means identity is NOT primitive — not a bare fact that just holds or does not hold. Identity is grounded in properties and relations. What makes this person the same person across time is the continuity of their properties (genetic code, bodily organization, psychological continuity) and their relations (to family, to place, to their own past). If another being had your exact properties and relations — every cell identical, every memory identical, every relation identical — that being would not be you in a mysterious further sense. The complete sameness of properties and relations just IS identity.

But Leibniz introduces a subtlety: location matters. Two atoms might be indistinguishable in every universal property (both elementary particles, same mass, same charge, same structure). Yet they differ in location — a haecceity, a “thisness” specific to each. So Leibniz concludes there are no two absolutely indiscernible things in the actual universe; spatial position ensures diversity. But the principle holds: identity reduces to the total constellation of properties and relations. There is no identity-itself, no self-identity, apart from some ground in what the thing IS.

This dissolves the mystery. You are the same person not because some immaterial soul persists, but because there is continuity in your physical organization, your memories, your character, your projects. Change enough of those fundamentally, and you change which person you are talking about — or whether the thing is still a person at all.

Rigid Designation and Essential Properties

Saul Kripke’s Naming and Necessity (1980) challenges the Leibnizian picture from another angle. Kripke argues that proper names are rigid designators — they refer to the same individual in all possible situations, even when the individual’s properties differ.

The name “Aristotle” refers to Aristotle whether he became a philosopher (as he did) or a plumber (as he might have). The name still picks out the same individual. This suggests that identity cannot be fully determined by properties observable at a given moment. The very same observable state might realize different identities depending on the actual causal history of how the thing came to be.

Kripke introduces a distinction: essential properties (properties without which a thing could not be itself) and contingent properties (properties a thing could lack and remain itself). Aristotle had contingent properties: he could have had a beard or not, been in Athens or Rome. But Aristotle could not have arisen from different parents and still be Aristotle. His essential properties include his actual origins. Identity depends on which properties are essential — which ones the thing could not lack and remain itself — and essence is determined by the actual history of the thing’s genesis, not by what observable properties happen to coincide.

This matters because it means identity is not fully capturable by any description of current properties. The identity of Aristotle depends on the actual fact that he arose from Macedonian parents, from the actual sperm and egg, in the actual historical chain. Another person with all of Aristotle’s current properties but arising from different parents would be someone else.

Relative Identity: Identity is Always Identity-In-Respect-Of

Peter Geach’s essay “Identity” (1967) cuts through the philosophical confusion with a simple but profound move: “Identity is not the same for all things.”

We can say: “A is the same ship as B” — the Ship of Theseus, continuously repaired, remains the same ship. We can say: “A is not the same collection-of-original-matter as B” — the planks have been replaced. We can say: “A is the same person as B” — the infant and the adult are the same person. We can say: “A is not the same body as B” — the infant’s body has been entirely replaced.

Geach’s insight: identity is always identity relative to a sortal — a kind or category. There is no bare identity; only identity-as-a-ship, identity-as-a-person, identity-as-a-mass-of-matter. The question “Is this the same thing?” is meaningless until you specify the sortal. “The same what?” becomes essential.

This dissolves the classical problem of the Ship of Theseus. The ship IS the same ship (sortal: ship). The ship IS NOT the same collection of original material (sortal: material object). Both are true because identity is relative to kinds. In one respect continuous, in another respect not. The confusion arises from thinking there is one absolute identity relation that holds or fails to hold. There are many identity relations, each sortal-specific.

Geach’s insight extends deeper: identity is not a pure logical predicate, fixed independently of context. Identity is context-dependent, kind-dependent, relational. A person remains the same person (identity relative to the kind “person”) through cellular replacement because what matters for personal identity is psychological continuity, character, memory, relational roles. An organism remains the same organism (identity relative to “organism”) through growth because what matters is the continuity of the living organization.

Individuation: Identity Through Process

Gilbert Simondon’s Individuation in Light of Notions of Form and Information (1964) rejects the assumption that individuals are primary and questions about change are secondary. The reverse is true: individuation is a process, and the individual emerges through that process.

The standard metaphysical model (hylomorphism — form imposed on matter) misses what individuation actually is. A crystal does not pre-exist as an individual substance and then acquire a crystalline form. The crystal individuates through a process of phase transition. The individual crystal AND the structure of the surrounding medium emerge together through the process. You cannot understand the crystal’s identity by looking at a snapshot at one moment. The identity lies in the continuity of the process through which the crystal came to be and continues to be constituted.

Simondon writes: “The individual is what results from the process of individuation; individuation does not proceed from the individual.” A person individuates through the process of differentiation from her parents, her culture, her material circumstances. The person is not fully formed at birth and then lives out a fixed essence. The person IS the continuous process of individuation — the differentiation from a pre-individual field, the integration with an environment, the maintenance of coherence through continuous becoming.

This dissolves the Ship of Theseus problem from the inside. The ship maintains identity not because some essence persists unchanged, but because the shipwright’s ongoing work, the ship’s operational role in continuous use, the continuity of maritime history and maintained function — these processes constitute the ship’s continuous individuation. A ship identical in every observable respect but created yesterday by accident rather than maintained through years of continuous work and repair is not the same ship, because it lacks the individuation history.

This insight applies universally: identity is the continuity of a process of becoming, not a static property. Interrupt the process, and identity ceases. A river ceases to be a river if the water stops flowing. A person ceases to be that person (in some sense) if consciousness is permanently interrupted. The process is what individuates; the individual is what persists through the process.

Emptiness and Dependent Origination: The Buddhist Account

Nāgārjuna’s Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (c. 150 CE) develops a radical relational account. The core principle is dependent origination (pratītyasamutpāda): “When this is, that is; when this is not, that is not.”

Nothing has intrinsic self-nature (svabhāva, “own-being”). Nothing exists independently. Everything exists only through relations to other things. This is emptiness (śūnyatā) — not the absence of things, but the absence of intrinsic self-nature. A thing is empty of svabhāva while being fully real in its relational dependence.

Crucially: identity and difference are not intrinsic features. A thing’s identity is relational, context-dependent. The same event has different identities relative to different contexts. Consider a seed: relative to its past causes (the plant that produced it), it is an effect. Relative to the plant it will become, it is a cause. Relative to the conditions that must be present (soil, water, sun), it is a dependent-on-conditions. None of these identities is “the true one.” Each is how the seed appears relative to a different relational context.

Nāgārjuna argues that this is not mysticism or relativism. It is ontological clarity about dependence. The seed has no identity-in-itself, independent of all relations and contexts. But this does not make its identity arbitrary. The seed IS precisely the node in a network of dependent origination. Its identity is constituted by its place in that network.

This explains why identity must be relational and processual. There is no absolute standpoint from which to say “this is what makes this thing what it is.” Identity is always relative to a perspective, a context, a relational nexus. The person asking the question (“Who are you?”) is already part of the network that constitutes the thing’s identity. So the identity-answer depends on the relational frame.

Properties, Relations, History, and Process

Identity is multiply grounded. These accounts are not contradictory; they are complementary dimensions of how identity works:

  1. Properties and indiscernibility (Leibniz): An entity is distinguished from every other entity by its unique constellation of properties and relations. Two things cannot be completely identical in all respects.

  2. Essential properties and actual history (Kripke): Among properties and relations, some are essential (constitutive of what the thing is) and some are contingent. Essential properties are grounded in the actual genesis and causal history of the thing.

  3. Sortal relativity (Geach): Identity is never bare. It is always identity-under-a-description, relative to a kind. “Same F but not same G” is perfectly coherent.

  4. Individuation process (Simondon): Identity is the continuity of a process of becoming, not a static property at an instant. The identity lies in how the thing maintains coherence through change.

  5. Relational dependence (Nāgārjuna): Nothing has identity-in-itself, independent of relations and context. Identity is constituted through a thing’s place in networks of dependent origination.

These dimensions work together. A person remains the same person through time because:

  • They retain the properties that distinguish them from others (Leibniz)
  • They maintain essential relations (to their own past, to others who know them) grounded in actual genesis (Kripke)
  • They remain the same person in the sortal sense, though not the same body or brain state (Geach)
  • They maintain continuity of their individuation process — consciousness, memory, intention, relational roles (Simondon)
  • They persist as a node in networks of relation and dependence that constitute their identity (Nāgārjuna)

None of these accounts alone exhausts identity. All together, they show what identity is: not a bare fact, not a mysterious essence, but a relational, processual, historically grounded, contextually-relative phenomenon.

What Identity is Not

Identity is not a primitive fact. There is no identity-in-itself, apart from properties, relations, histories, and processes. This does not make identity unreal or arbitrary. It makes identity intelligible, grounded, explained.

Identity is not a single relation. Geach shows that identity-relations are multiple, sortal-specific, context-dependent.

Identity is not static. Simondon and Buddhist philosophy show that identity is maintained through continuous becoming, not through unchanging essence. Interrupt the process, and identity ceases.

Identity is not intrinsic. Nāgārjuna’s dependent origination and Leibniz’s principle together show that identity is relational and constituted through networks of relation and property.
