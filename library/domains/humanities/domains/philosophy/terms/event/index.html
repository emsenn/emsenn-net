Event

2026-04-03 Something that happens at a specific time — the exemplification of a property by an object at a moment, from Kim's structured triples to Whitehead's actual occasions.

An event is something that happens at a specific time — a change that can be dated and identified. A window breaking, a patient’s fever spiking, a vote being cast, a bell ringing. These are events. Events mark the transition of a thing from one state to another.

Jaegwon Kim gave the clearest structural account: an event is the exemplification of a property by an object at a time. Write it as [object, property, time]. The window (object) broke (property: intact → shattered) at 3pm (time). The patient (object) developed hypertension (property change) on Monday (time). This is clean and compositional — you can build events from independent parts.

But there is a persistent problem with Kim’s account. An object can exemplify the same property at different times. The patient can be diagnosed with hypertension on Tuesday and again, independently, on Friday. Are these one event or two? If we say they are two, we must distinguish them somehow. If we say the property is “being-diagnosed-with-hypertension-on-Tuesday,” then the property becomes specific and gerrymandered, and we have lost the compositional advantage.

Donald Davidson solved this by shifting the criterion: an event is individuated by its causes and effects. Two events are the same if and only if they have the same cause and same effect — regardless of how you describe them. The assassination of the Archduke and the shooting at Sarajevo are the same event because they share causes and effects. “The event that started World War I” may or may not be the same event, depending on your theory of causation. But two descriptions pick out the same event whenever they have identical causes and identical effects. This coarse-grained approach treats many descriptions as referring to a single event. It is also intrinsically causal — you cannot understand an event without understanding its place in a causal order.

For Alfred North Whitehead, an event is not something that happens to a substance — an event is the fundamental unit of reality. Whitehead calls it an “actual occasion.” An actual occasion comes into being, achieves definiteness (what Whitehead calls “concrescence” — growing together, integrating influence from prior occasions), and then perishes. It becomes data for the next actual occasion. A person is not a single persistent substance; a person is a “society” of actual occasions, connected by causal order. A chess game is not one game enduring through moves; it is a causal series of distinct game-occasions, each prehending the previous one. Time is not a line that occasions sit on. Time is the creative advance of actual occasions.

Events exist under descriptions. This means that what counts as one event or many depends on the vantage point you are describing from. The same physical happening — a person’s blood pressure dropping — can be described as “the medication took effect,” “the patient relaxed,” “the crisis passed.” These are different descriptions of what may or may not be the same event, depending on how you individuate events (by Kim’s criterion, or Davidson’s, or Whitehead’s, or another account altogether).

Events and states are fundamentally different. A state is a configuration at a moment. An event is a transition between configurations. A state is a snapshot; an event is the change. A state can persist (the chess game is white-to-move for several minutes while the player thinks). An event cannot persist — it happens and then is done. The breaking of the window is over in an instant. The bell’s ringing lasts for seconds. Once the event is finished, it becomes history. A state is always present (a state exists at some moment). An event happens at a moment or during an interval, but then it is past.

Events structure time. Without events, time is an empty container. McTaggart distinguished the A-series (past, present, future — centered on NOW) from the B-series (before, after, simultaneous — no privileged moment). Events sit naturally in the B-series: the breaking of the window is before the calling of the glazier, regardless of when “now” is. But events matter in the A-series too: an upcoming deadline and a past deadline are different things, even if the B-series treats them symmetrically.

A sequence of events is a narrative. A structured set of events with causal relations is a process. The events of your life constitute your history. The events in a trial are evidence. Events are how we understand what happened, to what, and when.
