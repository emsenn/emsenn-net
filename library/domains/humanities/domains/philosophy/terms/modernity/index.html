Modernity

2026-04-02 The historical condition defined by industrialization, urbanization, secularization, rationalization, and the nation-state — and, crucially, by the claim that these processes constitute progress from tradition. The condition that generates Romantic nationalism as its reaction.

Modernity names the historical condition characterized by industrialization, urbanization, secularization, bureaucratic rationalization, the nation-state, mass media, and the global circulation of capital. More precisely, it names the claim that these processes constitute progress — a directional movement from tradition toward something better, or at least something inevitable.

The claim generates a binary: modern or traditional, progressive or backward, civilized or primitive. This binary is the engine of both colonial domination (Indigenous peoples classified as “pre-modern” and therefore available for displacement) and Romantic nationalism (the reaction that valorizes tradition, folk life, and organic community against industrial alienation). Both poles depend on modernity’s self-understanding as a break with the past. The colonizer says: we have broken through, they have not. The Romantic nationalist says: the break was a catastrophe, and we must recover what was lost.

What both poles erase is the temporal middle — the position of people who are neither frozen in tradition nor fully absorbed into industrial modernity, but inheriting, continuing, and transforming. A Lakota mathematician, a Kurdish dengbej who performs on satellite television, a Mohawk steelworker who carries a Haudenosaunee passport — each occupies this middle position. Modernity’s binary has no category for them. They are classified as either traditional (and therefore vanishing) or modern (and therefore no longer authentically themselves).

James C. Scott (1998) documents what modernity does when it encounters complexity it cannot classify: it simplifies. The modern state produces legibility — replacing diverse, locally adapted practices with standardized, administrable categories. Customary land becomes private property. Vernacular names become surnames. Heterogeneous forests become monoculture plantations. The genring of peoples is the cultural counterpart: diverse, internally contradictory communities are simplified into genre figures the modern state — or the modern audience — can recognize.
