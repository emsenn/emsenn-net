Relation

2026-04-03 A connection between things — from Aristotle's relatives through Russell's defense of relations as primitive to Whitehead's internal relations and Nāgārjuna's dependent origination.

Note on grounding (2026-04-05): The 561 Group’s relationality is rooted in Lakota epistemology, not in the Western tradition surveyed below. See Lakota philosophy and the relationality domain. In Lakota thought (mitákuye oyás’iŋ — “all my relatives”), relating is prior to things: what exists are acts of relating sustained in kinship with land, ancestors, other-than-human beings, and ceremony. The Western tradition below — Aristotle, Russell, Bradley, Whitehead, James, Peirce — is recorded as contrast material, useful for triangulating how European metaphysics arrived at neighbouring but distinct positions. It is not the foundation. The foundation is relational kinship epistemology; the Western survey is how that foundation looks from outside.

A relation is a connection between things. Parent-of, taller-than, inside, before, causes — all relations. A relation is what holds between two or more things. It is not a thing in the same way a rock is a thing. A relation depends on the things it connects. Remove one of the things, and the relation dissolves. But relations are real. “Parent of” is a real connection between a parent and a child. “Taller than” is a real connection between two objects of different heights.

Aristotle called these relatives (pros ti, “said of something in relation to something else”). In Categories Chapter 7, he writes: “A relative is that which is said to be what it is of or than something else.” A parent is a parent OF a child. A master is a master OF a slave. A mountain is taller THAN a hill. The defining feature is that you cannot understand what the relative IS without reference to something else. A parent cannot be conceived except in relation to a child. This makes relations depend on their terms — but it does not make them unreal.

Aristotle’s relatives raise a question that philosophers have grappled with ever since: are relations reducible to properties of the things they connect, or are they fundamental features of reality in their own right? If you say “A is taller than B,” are you describing a property of A (A’s height) and a property of B (B’s height), and inferring the relation? Or is “taller than” itself a real fact — a fact about A-and-B together, not about either one separately?

Bertrand Russell (1903) argued that relations are irreducible. You cannot explain “A is taller than B” by listing A’s properties and B’s properties separately. “Taller than” is not something you can derive from A alone or from B alone. It is a fact about A and B together. Russell wrote: “Relations are not adjectives, and therefore cannot be subsumed under the category of qualities.” A relation is a kind of thing — but not a substance or an individual. It is something that connects individuals.

This creates a problem: if a relation connects A and B, what connects the relation to A and B? Must there be a further relation doing the binding? F. H. Bradley (1893) argued that if relations are things, this leads to infinite regress. To avoid the regress, Bradley concluded that relations are not real — they are appearances, projections of how our minds organize experience. Russell rejected this. Relations are real and primitive. They do not require further relations to bind them to their terms. The relation just is the connection; that binding is constitutive.

Not all relations are the same. Some relations depend essentially on the nature of their terms. “A is larger than B” depends on A’s size and B’s size — the relation is grounded in the intrinsic properties of what it connects. Other relations seem more arbitrary. “A is 10 miles from B” depends on where A and B happen to be located, and the location could change without changing what A or B fundamentally are. Russell distinguished internal relations (dependent on the nature of their terms) from external relations (contingent on accidents of position or circumstance). But even this distinction is unclear. It is difficult to say which relations are essential and which are accidental.

There is another way to think about relations: not as connections between pre-existing things, but as constitutive — as what makes the things what they are. Alfred North Whitehead (1929) argued in Process and Reality that all relations are internal in this deeper sense. Nothing exists in isolation. Each actual entity — each genuine individual — is constituted by its relations to other entities. He called these relations “prehensions” (grasps). An entity is its network of prehensions. A person is not an independently existing being who happens to be related to parents, community, and the world. The person is constituted by her prehensions — her grasp of her parents’ presence, her appropriation of cultural meanings, her responsive engagement with others. Sever these relations, and you do not have a person in isolation. You have an abstraction, a remainder that lacks the reality of a living individual.

William James (1912) reached a similar conclusion from a different angle. In Essays in Radical Empiricism, he argued that relations are directly experienced, not inferred. When you see one person stand next to another, you do not first observe two isolated humans and then infer the relation “beside.” You perceive the relation directly. The “and” between two things is as real as the things themselves. Relations are immediate facts about the world.

Charles Sanders Peirce distinguished dyadic relations (relations between two things) from triadic relations (relations among three things). “A is taller than B” is dyadic — reducible to comparing heights. But “A is between B and C” is triadic — A’s position as the middle term cannot be reduced to dyadic relations. You cannot decompose “A is between B and C” into pairs without losing what makes the relation what it is. Peirce called this irreducible three-part structure “Thirdness” — mediation, continuity. It appears wherever meaning or interpretation is involved. In predication, “Socrates is wise” is not a dyadic fact. It involves a subject (Socrates), a predicate (wise), and what the predicate interprets in relation to the subject. Remove any of the three parts, and you lose the meaning.

The Buddhist philosopher Nāgārjuna (c. 150 CE) argued in the Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way) that nothing has intrinsic self-nature (svabhāva, literally “own-being”). Everything exists only through dependent origination (pratītyasamutpāda) — through relations to other things. “When this is, that is; when this is not, that is not.” A subject only exists in relation to objects, just as objects only exist in relation to subjects. A part is only a part in relation to a whole. The distinction between any two things is not given intrinsically but emerges through dependent origination. This is not mysticism. It is clarity about the structure of how things depend on each other. Nothing stands alone. Everything that is stands in a network of relations, and it is through that network that anything becomes what it is.
