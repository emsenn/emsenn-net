Catuṣkoṭi

2026-03-05 The four-cornered logical structure in which a proposition may be affirmed, denied, both affirmed and denied, or neither — deployed by Nāgārjuna to demonstrate that all phenomena are empty of inherent existence.

The catuṣkoṭi (Sanskrit: “four corners”; also called the tetralemma) is a logical structure in which a proposition is evaluated against four possibilities: it may be affirmed, denied, both affirmed and denied, or neither affirmed nor denied. The structure appears across Indian philosophy — in Jain anekāntavāda, in early Buddhist Abhidharma classification, in the Nyāya tradition’s accounts of inference — but receives its most radical deployment in Nāgārjuna’s Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (c. 2nd century CE), where it becomes the primary instrument for demonstrating that all phenomena are empty (śūnya) of inherent existence.

Nāgārjuna’s method is systematic. Given any metaphysical thesis T about the nature of a phenomenon — that it exists, that it arises from causes, that it has self-nature (svabhāva) — the catuṣkoṭi examines four exhaustive positions. T cannot be affirmed, because affirmation leads to contradiction (inherent existence cannot coexist with dependent origination). T cannot be denied, because denial leads to its own contradiction (the denial of existence is itself a metaphysical commitment). T cannot be both affirmed and denied, because conjunction of contradictions is not stable. And T cannot be neither affirmed nor denied, because that too constitutes a position on T’s status. The result is not a fifth position but the exhaustion of the space of positions — the demonstration that T never had the kind of determinate truth value that metaphysical commitment requires.

This is what śūnyatā (emptiness) means in Madhyamaka: the absence of inherent existence. Emptiness is not nihilism — Nāgārjuna is emphatic that emptiness itself is empty, that treating emptiness as a metaphysical thesis would be a misunderstanding worse than all others. Emptiness is what the catuṣkoṭi reveals when it systematically destroys every attempt to ground a thesis about the nature of things.

The catuṣkoṭi’s pre-Nāgārjunian history matters. In the early Pāli texts, the Buddha uses a similar four-cornered structure (the avyākata — undeclared questions) to refuse metaphysical questions: is the world eternal? is the self identical with the body? The Buddha’s refusal is not ignorance but a therapeutic intervention — the questions are badly formed, and answering them in any of the four corners would reinforce attachment. Nāgārjuna formalizes and generalizes this therapeutic stance, applying it to every metaphysical proposition rather than to a limited class of undeclared questions.
