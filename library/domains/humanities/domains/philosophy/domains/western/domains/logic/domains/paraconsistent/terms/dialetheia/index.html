Dialetheia

2026-03-05 A proposition that is both true and false — a genuine feature of reality rather than an error, requiring the failure of explosion to be non-trivially assertible.

A dialetheia is a proposition that is both true and false. Dialetheism is the philosophical position that dialetheia exist — that some contradictions are not merely apparent or the result of error but are genuine features of reality or of our best theories about it.

The position is most associated with Graham Priest, whose In Contradiction (1987, 2nd ed. 2006) argues that the liar sentence (“this sentence is not true”) is a dialetheia: it is true if and only if it is not true, and the correct response is to accept that it is both. The liar paradox is not an anomaly to be dissolved by semantic restrictions (Tarski’s hierarchy) or pragmatic dismissal but a window into the structure of truth itself.

Dialetheism is stronger than paraconsistency. One can adopt a paraconsistent logic without accepting that any contradictions are true — treating paraconsistency as a tool for reasoning in situations where information is inconsistent without commitment to the metaphysical claim that reality is contradictory. Dialetheism accepts the metaphysical claim: some contradictions hold in the world, not merely in our representations of it.

The semantic framework for dialetheism is typically LP (Logic of Paradox), a three-valued logic where propositions can take the values true only, false only, or both true and false. The “both” value is designated (counts for purposes of validity), so a dialetheia can appear in the premises and conclusion of a valid argument. Crucially, explosion fails: from a dialetheia, not everything follows, because the “both” value does not propagate unconditionally through the connectives.

Dialetheism has deep connections to Buddhist logic. Nāgārjuna’s catuṣkoṭi (tetralemma) — a thing may exist, not exist, both, or neither — admits the “both” case that classical logic excludes. Whether the catuṣkoṭi represents genuine dialetheism or a distinct logical framework is debated, but the structural parallel is real: both traditions recognize that the exhaustive dichotomy of true/false may not capture the full space of how propositions relate to reality.

For this research program, dialetheism matters because the founding gesture of the relational derivation is a productive contradiction: the attempt to posit relationlessness produces a relation. This is not a bug to be patched but the engine of the derivation. The influence of dialetheism on the research program’s ontology is explicit.
