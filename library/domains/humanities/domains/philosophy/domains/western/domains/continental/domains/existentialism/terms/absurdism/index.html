Absurdism

2026-03-02 Camus's position: the absurd arises from the confrontation between human desire for meaning and the universe's indifference, and the proper response is revolt — living fully without transcendent justification.

Absurdism is Albert Camus’s philosophical position, articulated most directly in The Myth of Sisyphus (1942). The absurd is not a property of the world and not a property of human consciousness. It arises from their confrontation: humans desire meaning, coherence, and purpose; the universe is indifferent to that desire. The absurd is the gap between the two — not resolvable by eliminating either side, because doing so would eliminate the confrontation that constitutes it.

Camus identifies three possible responses to the absurd. The first is literal suicide — refusing to live with meaninglessness. Camus rejects this as capitulation: if the absurd arises from the confrontation between human desire and cosmic indifference, then suicide eliminates one term of the confrontation without resolving it. The second is what Camus calls philosophical suicide — adopting a faith, a philosophical system, or an ideology that provides meaning at the cost of intellectual honesty. Kierkegaard’s leap of faith, Husserl’s transcendental reduction, and any system that promises to reconcile the human desire for meaning with the universe’s silence all count as philosophical suicide: they close the gap by pretending it is not there.

The third response is revolt. Revolt means continuing to live fully in the face of the absurd, without transcendent justification, maintaining the tension between desire and indifference without collapsing it in either direction. This is Sisyphus pushing his boulder up the hill, knowing it will roll back down, and continuing anyway. Camus insists we must imagine Sisyphus happy — not because the task is meaningful but because the engagement with it is freely chosen and the refusal to stop is itself the revolt.

Absurdism occupies a specific position relative to nihilism and existentialism. Where nihilism confronts the absence of meaning, absurdism responds with revolt rather than acceptance or despair. Where Sartre’s existentialism claims that humans create meaning through choice and commitment, Camus insists that the absurd persists regardless of what one chooses — meaning is not created but lucidly refused. The revolt is not a project of meaning-construction but a stance of defiance that does not require meaning to sustain it.

Camus extended absurdism into political territory in The Rebel (1951), arguing that revolt must set its own limits: the rebel who murders in the name of justice has betrayed the revolt that gave rise to the demand for justice. This insistence on self-limiting revolt put Camus in direct conflict with Sartre and the revolutionary Marxist tradition, a conflict that ended their friendship and defined the political stakes of mid-century French philosophy.
