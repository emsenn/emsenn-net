Demarcation Problem

2026-03-31 The problem of distinguishing science from non-science, foundational to philosophy of science and unresolved despite major attempts by Popper, Kuhn, Lakatos, and Feyerabend.

The demarcation problem is the question of what distinguishes science from non-science. It asks whether there is a criterion — a line, a test, a set of necessary conditions — that separates scientific knowledge from metaphysics, pseudoscience, ideology, and common sense. The problem is foundational to philosophy of science. It is also unresolved.

Karl Popper (1934) proposed falsifiability: a theory is scientific if and only if it makes predictions that could, in principle, be shown false by observation. Psychoanalysis and Marxism fail because they accommodate any evidence; Einstein’s relativity succeeds because it risks refutation. The criterion is elegant but too narrow — it excludes legitimate scientific practices like taxonomy and historical geology that do not generate falsifiable predictions in Popper’s strict sense.

Thomas Kuhn (1962, revised 1969) replaced the single criterion with the disciplinary matrix: a discipline is scientific when it possesses shared symbolic generalizations, metaphysical commitments, values, and exemplars. Science is defined not by method but by the social structure of its community — a shared paradigm that constrains and enables normal research. The boundary between science and non-science becomes a matter of degree: how developed is the disciplinary matrix, how stable the consensus?

Imre Lakatos (1970) proposed research programmes as the unit of demarcation. A research programme has a hard core of unfalsifiable assumptions protected by a belt of auxiliary hypotheses. A programme is scientific when it is progressive — its protective belt generates novel predictions that are sometimes confirmed. It degenerates when the belt only accommodates anomalies after the fact. Demarcation becomes dynamic: the same programme can shift from scientific to pseudoscientific over time.

Paul Feyerabend (1975) argued that no single method defines science. Historical examination shows that every proposed methodological rule has been productively violated by successful scientists. The boundary between science and non-science is conventional, maintained by institutions and power rather than by epistemological criteria. Feyerabend did not conclude that science is arbitrary — he concluded that its success cannot be captured in a rule.

The problem matters for knowledge organization because any classification of knowledge implicitly answers it. Deciding which domains count as sciences, which as humanities, and which as professional practice is a demarcation judgment, whether or not the classifier acknowledges it.

Key texts

  • Popper, K., The Logic of Scientific Discovery (1934/1959)
  • Kuhn, T., The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (1962, 2nd ed. 1970)
  • Lakatos, I., “Falsification and the Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes” (1970)
  • Feyerabend, P., Against Method (1975)
