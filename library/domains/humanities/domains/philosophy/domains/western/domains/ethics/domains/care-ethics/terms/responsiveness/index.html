Responsiveness

2026-03-05 The fourth phase of Tronto's care model — the criterion by which care's adequacy is judged, asking whether the cared-for actually received what was needed.

Responsiveness is the fourth and final phase in Joan Tronto’s model of care and the criterion by which the adequacy of care is judged. After care has been noticed (attentiveness), taken up as responsibility (taking care of), and performed as concrete labor (care-giving), responsiveness asks: did the care actually meet the need? How does the cared-for receive and respond to the care given?

Responsiveness rejects the assumption that the carer’s intentions or efforts are sufficient. Good care is not defined by what the carer meant to do but by what the cared-for experienced. A meal prepared with love but that the person cannot eat has failed the responsiveness test. A policy designed to help a population but that the population experiences as invasive has failed. The carer’s sincerity is real but not the measure — the cared-for’s response is.

This builds a feedback structure into care ethics that prevents it from collapsing into paternalism. Without responsiveness, care degrades into imposition — the carer decides what the other needs and delivers it without checking. Medical paternalism, colonial “development,” and institutional charity all share this structure: care is given on the giver’s terms, and the receiver’s response is treated as gratitude owed rather than information about whether the care worked.

Responsiveness also connects to Noddings’s insistence that the caring relation requires completion by the cared-for. The cared-for must receive and recognize the care for the relation to be fully enacted. When care is given but not received — when the loop does not close — something essential is missing, even if the carer acted well. This is not a moral failure of either party but a structural feature of care: care is relational, and relations require more than one party.

The political implications are significant. If responsiveness is the standard, then care systems must be designed to hear back from those they serve. Top-down welfare programs that measure their success by inputs (money spent, services delivered) rather than by the responses of recipients are structurally unresponsive. Care ethics demands that the adequacy of care be judged by those who receive it.
