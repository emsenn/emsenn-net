Intertheoretic Reduction

2026-03-02 The claim that one scientific theory can be derived from or explained by another, typically more fundamental, theory — connecting vocabularies through bridge laws.

Intertheoretic reduction is the claim that one scientific theory can be derived from or explained by another, typically more fundamental, theory. The classical model, formulated by Ernest Nagel in The Structure of Science (1961), requires that the laws of the reduced theory be logically derivable from the reducing theory, with the help of bridge laws — statements connecting the vocabularies of the two theories. Temperature is mean molecular kinetic energy. That identification is the bridge law that makes the reduction of thermodynamics to statistical mechanics possible.

The aspiration behind reduction is the unity of science: if every higher-level theory reduces to a lower-level one, then ultimately all of science reduces to physics, and the apparent diversity of scientific disciplines is an artifact of convenience rather than a fact about the world’s structure. Chemistry reduces to physics. Biology reduces to chemistry. Psychology reduces to neuroscience. Each bridge law eliminates a level of explanation by showing that the higher-level regularities are consequences of lower-level mechanisms.

Opponents of reduction argue that higher-level theories capture patterns that are invisible at the lower level. Jerry Fodor’s 1974 argument is the sharpest: psychological kinds cross-classify physical kinds. The same psychological state (believing it will rain) can be realized in indefinitely many physical configurations (different brain states in different organisms, perhaps even in non-biological systems). This “multiple realizability” means that the psychological generalization captures a pattern that no physical-level description can state — the category “believes it will rain” groups together physically disparate states on the basis of their functional role, not their physical constitution. If the higher-level pattern is real and irreducible, then reduction fails not because we lack the bridge laws but because the bridge laws cannot exist.

Philip Kitcher extended this defense of “explanatory autonomy”: even when reduction is in principle possible, the higher-level explanation may be better — more general, more informative, more perspicuous — than the lower-level one. The debate connects to broader questions about explanation, emergence, and whether the unity of science is a methodological aspiration or a metaphysical claim.
