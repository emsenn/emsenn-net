Mereology

2026-03-03 The study of the relations between parts and wholes — what it means for one thing to be a part of another and how parts compose wholes.

Mereology is the study of the relations between parts and wholes. It asks what it means for one thing to be a part of another, how parts compose wholes, and whether composition is governed by universal principles or context-dependent conditions. The word derives from the Greek meros (part), and the discipline was formalized by Stanisław Leśniewski in the early twentieth century as an alternative to set theory for representing structure.

The central relation is parthood: x is a part of y. Classical mereology (Leśniewski, later Goodman and Leonard) treats parthood as a partial order — reflexive, antisymmetric, and transitive — and typically adopts unrestricted composition: any collection of things, however arbitrary, composes a whole. Your left shoe and the Eiffel Tower compose a mereological whole, even if no one would normally recognize it as an object. This yields a Boolean algebra of parts where the mereological sum of any two things exists, and any object can be decomposed into arbitrary parts.

The philosophical debates in mereology are about whether this formal liberality reflects reality. The “special composition question” (Peter van Inwagen) asks: under what conditions do things compose a whole? Unrestricted composition says: always. Restrictivists argue that composition occurs only under specific conditions — when the parts are in physical contact, when they participate in a common causal process, when they sustain a life, or when some other non-trivial criterion is met. Nihilists about composition deny that composition ever occurs — there are no wholes, only “simples” (fundamental particles or whatever bottoms out the physical hierarchy) arranged in various ways.

Process philosophy offers a different approach: wholes are not aggregates of pre-existing parts but products of concrescence. Parts acquire their identity through the process that integrates them, not prior to it. This makes mereological structure dependent on the occasion of experience rather than fixed in advance — a position that aligns with relational ontology, where relations constitute their relata.
