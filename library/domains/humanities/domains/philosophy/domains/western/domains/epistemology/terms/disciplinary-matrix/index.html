Disciplinary Matrix

2026-03-31 Kuhn's 1969 replacement for 'paradigm,' specifying four components — symbolic generalizations, metaphysical commitments, values, and exemplars — that constitute a scientific discipline.

The disciplinary matrix is Thomas Kuhn’s specification, introduced in the 1969 postscript to The Structure of Scientific Revolutions, of what he had loosely called a “paradigm.” Critics (notably Margaret Masterman) had identified over twenty distinct senses of “paradigm” in the original 1962 text. Kuhn responded by replacing the single term with a structured concept: a discipline is its disciplinary matrix.

The matrix has four components:

  • Symbolic generalizations — the formal or quasi-formal expressions shared by the group without question. In physics: F=ma, I=V/R. These function partly as laws, partly as definitions. They are the mathematical or logical skeleton of the discipline.

  • Metaphysical commitments — shared beliefs about the kinds of entities that populate the domain and the kinds of processes they undergo. Examples: “heat is the kinetic energy of molecules,” “chemical compounds are produced by atoms bonding.” These are not testable hypotheses but background assumptions that make hypotheses possible.

  • Values — criteria for evaluating theories, shared broadly across sciences but applied differently within each discipline. Accuracy, consistency, scope, simplicity, and fruitfulness are Kuhn’s canonical list. Values explain why scientists in the same discipline can disagree about particular theories while sharing standards of judgment.

  • Exemplars — the concrete problem-solutions that students learn in training and that serve as models for future work. Exemplars are the most important and most overlooked component. Students learn what counts as a legitimate problem and what counts as a good solution not from rules but from worked examples: the inclined plane, the pendulum, the Bohr atom. New research extends these exemplars by analogy.

The disciplinary matrix reframes the demarcation problem. A community is scientific not because it follows a method but because it shares a matrix. Revolutions occur when the matrix breaks down — when anomalies accumulate that the current exemplars cannot absorb — and the community reconstructs its shared commitments around new generalizations, new metaphysics, and new exemplars. The values persist across revolutions, which is why science remains recognizable despite fundamental theoretical change.

Key texts

  • Kuhn, T., “Postscript—1969” in The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (2nd ed., 1970)
  • Masterman, M., “The Nature of a Paradigm” in Criticism and the Growth of Knowledge (1970)
