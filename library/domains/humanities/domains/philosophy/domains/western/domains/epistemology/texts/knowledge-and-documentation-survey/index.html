Knowledge and Documentation: A Survey of Their Relationship

2026-04-05 Survey of competing accounts of how knowledge relates to its recording — from Plato's separation of knowledge from writing through Briet's documentation theory, Polanyi's tacit dimension, Dewey's inquiry, archival diplomatics, and Haraway's situated knowledges. Maps the terrain between knowledge as a mental state and documentation as its constitutive practice.

¶The Contested Relationship

If knowledge is what a mind possesses when it knows, and a document is an object that records what is known, then documentation is derivative: first one knows, then one writes it down. The writing is a trace, a copy, a shadow of the prior mental achievement. This picture is intuitive, widespread, and contested by nearly every serious tradition that has examined the two concepts together.

The contest is not over semantics. It concerns what knowledge is and what documents do. Does knowledge exist prior to and independent of its inscription? Can a record constitute what it records rather than merely describe it? Do documents preserve knowledge or only its residue? What is lost — or made — when knowing becomes written? These questions matter because any system that manages knowledge through records presupposes an answer. Libraries, courts, scientific journals, software repositories, and administrative archives all encode a theory of the relationship whether they articulate it or not.

This survey maps five traditions that have answered the question in mutually incompatible ways. Each tradition has an account of knowledge, an account of the document, and — crucially — an account of the operation that joins them.

¶The Separability Tradition: Knowledge as Mental, Documents as Trace

The dominant Western picture treats knowledge as a state of the knower and the document as its external residue. Its clearest statement is Plato’s, and its deepest tension is also Plato’s.

In the Theaetetus (c. 369 BCE), Plato defines knowledge as epistēmē — justified true belief requiring logos, a rational account that can be articulated and defended. Knowledge lives in the soul; it is held, tested, possessed. In the Phaedrus (c. 370 BCE), Plato extends this into a famous attack on writing. Through the myth of Theuth, Socrates argues that writing produces forgetfulness, not memory. Written marks cannot answer questions, cannot respond to their reader, cannot distinguish the fit audience from the unfit. They give the appearance of wisdom without its reality. Real knowledge is the living speech of a knower who can defend and adapt it; writing is a feeble image, “the same thing forever.”

This is the separability thesis at its most extreme: knowledge exists in minds, documents are lesser copies, and the relationship between them is one of degradation. The tradition softens over centuries without abandoning its core. Cartesian internalism locates knowledge in clear and distinct ideas held by a thinking subject. Classical analytic epistemology, following Plato through to the twentieth century, treats knowledge as a property of a believer — justified, true, belief — with documents playing no constitutive role in the definition.

Within this tradition, the operation joining knowledge to documents is transcription. The knower writes down what is already known. The document’s value is fidelity to the prior mental state. Degradation is the expected direction of travel.

¶The Documentary Constitution Tradition: Records Make the Fact

The opposing tradition grew out of librarianship and archival practice: documents are not traces of knowledge but the material through which certain kinds of knowledge come into existence. Its founding figure is Suzanne Briet (1951), whose slim treatise Qu’est-ce que la documentation? argues that a document is “any physical or symbolic sign, preserved or recorded, for the purpose of representing, reconstructing, or proving a phenomenon.” Her famous example — an antelope in the wild is not a document, but an antelope in a zoo is — locates documenthood not in the object but in the act of intentional preservation that sets it apart as evidence.

Michael Buckland (1991) sharpened this with his distinction among information-as-process (the experience of being informed), information-as-knowledge (what is known), and information-as-thing (objects treated as informative). Buckland’s move is philosophical: he insists that information-as-thing is not reducible to the other two. Documents are genuinely third-personal, persistent, material. They can be organized, authenticated, damaged, authenticated again. The document is not a window onto a prior mental state; it is a thing in the world with its own history.

Luciana Duranti (1989) draws on the ancient discipline of diplomatics — the study of the formal characteristics of documents that establish their authenticity — to push further. A record, for Duranti, is created in the course of activity as evidence of that activity. Its meaning is inseparable from the transaction that produced it. Provenance, original order, and respect des fonds are not archival aesthetics but epistemic commitments: a record stripped from its context of creation is not merely disorganized but degraded in what it can be known to mean.

John Searle (1995) gives this tradition a general philosophical frame. Institutional facts — money, marriage, corporate personhood, scholarly credit — exist because of collective declarations of the form “X counts as Y in context C.” Such facts must be recorded to persist, and the recording is constitutive: an unrecorded transaction has not institutionally happened. A court’s written judgment does not describe guilt; it constitutes guilt as an institutional fact. A committed git hash does not report that code was merged; it is what makes the merge real for the repository and its dependents.

Within this tradition, the operation joining knowledge to documents is constitution. Documents do not follow knowledge; certain kinds of knowledge come into existence through the evidential act of recording. Fidelity is replaced by authenticity, integrity, and provenance.

¶The Tacit Tradition: Knowledge Exceeds Documentation

A third tradition argues that the most important kinds of knowledge cannot be documented at all. Gilbert Ryle (1949) distinguished knowing-that from knowing-how — propositional knowledge from practical skill. You can know that a bicycle requires balance without knowing how to ride one; you can know how to ride one without being able to state the principles. Knowing-how resists reduction to knowing-that.

Michael Polanyi (1966) generalized Ryle’s distinction into his claim that “we know more than we can tell.” Tacit knowledge is embodied, practical, and implicit. A surgeon’s touch, a craftsperson’s feel, a native speaker’s grammatical judgment, a scientist’s sense for which experiments will matter — all are known but not fully articulable. Polanyi traces this to the structure of attention itself: we attend from particulars (the feel of the handle) to an integrated whole (the capacity to use the tool), and the from-terms resist explicit statement because attending to them disrupts the integration they support.

Ikujiro Nonaka and Hirotaka Takeuchi (1995) carried this into organizational theory with the SECI model, which describes knowledge creation as cycles between tacit and explicit modes: socialization (tacit-to-tacit, through shared practice), externalization (tacit-to-explicit, through articulation and modeling), combination (explicit-to-explicit, through documentation and synthesis), and internalization (explicit-to-tacit, through practice that absorbs the explicit into habit). Documents, in this picture, occupy only the middle phases. They are products of externalization and raw material for combination, but they are neither the source nor the destination of knowing.

Within this tradition, the operation joining knowledge to documents is partial externalization. Documents record what can be made explicit and miss what cannot. The relation is one of systematic loss — not failure, but an ineliminable remainder. Apprenticeship, not archive, transmits the remainder.

¶The Inquiry Tradition: Documentation as a Moment in Process

A fourth tradition treats knowledge not as a state but as the outcome of a process, with documentation playing a functional role within that process rather than standing outside it. John Dewey (Logic: The Theory of Inquiry, 1938) argued that knowledge is “warranted assertibility” — the settlement of an inquiry that began with an indeterminate situation and transformed it into a determinate one. To know is to have gone through inquiry; what one knows is what inquiry has settled.

For Dewey, records and documents are instruments of inquiry. They stabilize intermediate results so further inquiry can build on them. They make inquiry social by letting one investigator’s settlement become another’s starting point. They allow a community to return to and test prior warrants. But they are not the knowledge itself; knowledge is the settled capacity for warranted action that inquiry produces and documentation supports.

Charles Sanders Peirce (1878) laid the ground: truth is what the community of inquiry converges on in the long run, and belief is a habit of action stabilized against doubt. The pragmatic maxim — that the meaning of a conception lies in its practical consequences — ties knowledge to procedure and consequence rather than to inner states. Documentation, on a Peircean reading, is the infrastructure that makes communal, self-correcting inquiry possible across time: the published paper, the recorded experiment, the indexed citation.

Within this tradition, the operation joining knowledge to documents is stabilization. Documents are neither shadows of prior knowledge (separability) nor the constitutive act of its existence (documentary constitution). They are instruments that hold the results of inquiry steady enough for further inquiry to build on. The picture is cyclical: inquiry produces warrants, documents stabilize warrants, stabilized warrants feed new inquiry.

¶The Situated Tradition: Relational Knowledge and the Violence of the Archive

A fifth tradition argues that both the separability and the documentary-constitution traditions miss something fundamental: knowledge is always bound to a particular standpoint, place, and web of relationships, and documentation — especially bureaucratic, universal, or propositional documentation — systematically distorts relational knowing.

Donna Haraway (1988) argued against the “god trick” of a view from nowhere. All knowledge is situated, produced from a partial perspective with particular interests, blindnesses, and embodiments. Objectivity is not achieved by stripping away the situation but by honestly accounting for it. Haraway’s concern with documentation is that archives typically erase situation in the name of universality, preserving claims while losing the standpoints that produced them.

Robin Wall Kimmerer (2013) articulates a related view from Indigenous epistemology. Knowledge, in traditions like those of the Potawatomi, is relational, reciprocal, and place-based: knowledge of how to live well with particular land, particular relatives, particular conditions. Such knowledge is held across generations through story, ceremony, and practice — not primarily through written records. Written documentation is not neutral; extraction into archive can sever the relationships that made the knowledge knowable in the first place.

Geoffrey Bowker and Susan Leigh Star (1999) formalized the critique in information science. Classification systems and documentary infrastructures are never neutral; they encode the interests and blindnesses of their makers. What gets recorded, how it is categorized, what fields exist on the form — these decisions make some realities visible and others unspeakable. Documentation is an act of power as well as preservation.

Within this tradition, the operation joining knowledge to documents is translation with loss and distortion. Some knowledge can be documented only at the cost of the relations that constituted it. Documentation is not innocent; it is a situated practice with political consequences, preserving some forms of knowing and foreclosing others.

¶Cross-Cutting Tensions

The five traditions disagree about what knowledge fundamentally is — a mental state (separability), an institutional construct (documentary constitution), an embodied capacity (tacit), a settlement of inquiry (pragmatist), or a relational inhabitation (situated). They disagree about what documents fundamentally do — transcribe, constitute, partially externalize, stabilize, or distort. These disagreements are ontological, not merely terminological.

Three cross-cutting tensions emerge clearly.

The direction of fit. Does the document answer to the knowledge (separability, tacit), or does the knowledge answer to the document (documentary constitution)? The inquiry tradition splits the difference: the document answers to the warrant it stabilizes, but the warrant gains institutional life only through the stabilization.

The completeness of capture. Can documentation be complete in principle? Separability says yes in principle but no in practice (writing is a lesser copy). Documentary constitution says yes for institutional facts (the record is the fact). Tacit knowledge says no in principle (the from-terms of attention resist explication). Situated knowledge says no in principle (relations exceed record). The pragmatist tradition is silent on completeness — only on adequacy to further inquiry.

The politics of the record. Is documentation neutral or political? Separability and tacit traditions tend to treat it as neutral (a medium, well or poorly used). Documentary constitution treats it as authoritative but within institutional legitimacy. Situated and pragmatist traditions insist documentation is political: what is recorded, by whom, under what classifications, constitutes what can be known and who can claim to know it.

¶What This System Needs

A repository that treats itself as a knowledge system inherits all five traditions and cannot simply choose one. It needs the documentary-constitution account for its own operation: committed records, append-only logs, provenance, and diplomatic authenticity are how institutional facts persist across sessions and across contributors. It needs the tacit tradition to explain why documentation is necessary but not sufficient — why apprenticeship, practice, and embodied judgment cannot be replaced by archive. It needs the inquiry tradition to understand why records stabilize intermediate warrants rather than terminating inquiry. It needs the situated tradition to keep honest about what its classifications foreground and what they foreclose. And it needs the separability tradition, if only as the default picture to correct: the naïve view that first one knows, then one writes, remains the strongest attractor and requires continual resistance.

The survey does not resolve the tension. It locates it. Any knowledge system that pretends the relationship between knowing and recording is settled has chosen one tradition and forgotten the others.
