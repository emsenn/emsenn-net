Provenness

2026-03-02 The epistemic status of having been established through demonstration, verification, or validation — distinct from truth, which concerns correspondence rather than evidence.

Provenness is the property of having been established through a process of demonstration, verification, or validation. Unlike truth (which concerns correspondence or coherence), provenness concerns the epistemic status of a claim relative to a community’s standards of evidence and reasoning. A claim may be true without being proven, and in some formal systems, the gap between truth and provenness is itself a subject of study.

In formal logic and mathematics, provenness is sharply defined: a statement is proven if there exists a valid derivation from accepted axioms. The concept is precise because the standards are explicit — the axioms, the rules of inference, and the criteria for a valid derivation are all specified in advance. A proof is a finite sequence of steps, each licensed by the rules, terminating in the statement to be proved. Whether a given sequence constitutes a proof is mechanically checkable.

Gödel’s incompleteness theorems (1931) demonstrate that in any sufficiently expressive formal system, there exist true statements that are not provable within the system. Truth and provenness come apart in principle. This gap is not an accident of any particular axiom system — it is a structural feature of formal reasoning above a certain threshold of expressiveness. The gap motivates constructivist and intuitionist approaches that identify mathematical truth with provenness: a statement is true if and only if there is a proof of it. On this view, the Gödelian “true but unprovable” statement is not true in any constructively meaningful sense — it is simply undecided.

In broader epistemology, provenness functions as a social and institutional category. What counts as proven depends on evidentiary standards, which vary across disciplines, legal systems, and communities of practice. The distinction between “proven” and “established beyond reasonable doubt” in law, or between “proven” and “supported by the preponderance of evidence” in science, or between “proven” and “peer-reviewed and replicated” in experimental research reflects different institutional calibrations of the relationship between evidence and confidence. Each calibration is a decision about how much evidence is enough — and that decision is not itself provable but is a matter of institutional agreement about acceptable risk.
