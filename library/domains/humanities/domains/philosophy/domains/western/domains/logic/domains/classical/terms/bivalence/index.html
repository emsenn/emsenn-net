Bivalence

2026-03-05 The semantic thesis that every declarative statement is either true or false — exactly two truth values, every proposition receiving exactly one.

Bivalence is the semantic thesis that every declarative statement is either true or false — there are exactly two truth values and every proposition receives exactly one of them. It is a foundational commitment of classical logic and the philosophical ground on which truth-functionality, truth tables, and model-theoretic semantics rest.

Bivalence should be distinguished from the law of excluded middle, though the two are often conflated. Excluded middle is a logical law: the formula A ∨ ¬A is valid. Bivalence is a semantic principle: every proposition has one of two truth values. In classical logic they are equivalent, but they come apart in other settings. A three-valued logic might validate excluded middle (by treating the third value as “designated”) while violating bivalence, or it might preserve bivalence in a restricted domain while rejecting excluded middle for a broader class of statements.

Aristotle’s discussion of future contingents in De Interpretatione (Chapter 9) is the classical challenge to bivalence. The statement “there will be a sea battle tomorrow” appears to have no determinate truth value now, because the future is not yet settled. If bivalence holds, then the statement is already true or already false, and fatalism seems to follow. Aristotle’s response — variously interpreted as rejecting bivalence for future contingents, or as maintaining it while distinguishing necessity from truth — has generated two millennia of philosophical debate.

The intuitionistic rejection of bivalence is different in character. Brouwer and Heyting do not deny that propositions have truth values; they deny that truth is independent of proof. A proposition is true when proved and false when refuted. For propositions that are neither proved nor refuted, no truth value is assigned — not because truth is indeterminate, but because truth is constructive. The gap between bivalence and constructive truth is formalized in the difference between Boolean algebras and Heyting algebras.
