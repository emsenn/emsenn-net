Process

2025-12-31 The fundamental character of reality in Whitehead's metaphysics: becoming rather than being, change rather than persistence — entities are achievements of processes, not substrates that undergo them.
Defines Process

In process philosophy, process names the fundamental character of reality: becoming rather than being, change rather than persistence, relation rather than substance. To say that reality is processual is not merely to say that things change — which even substance metaphysics acknowledges — but that becoming is ontologically prior to being. Entities are achievements of processes, not substrates that undergo processes. The river is not a thing that flows but a flowing that we identify as a river. The organism is not a substance that metabolizes but a metabolizing that we identify as an organism.

This reversal has consequences across philosophy. If process is primary, then identity is not a given but a pattern maintained through ongoing activity. A person is not a fixed self that has experiences but a stream of experience that produces the continuity we call a self. An institution is not a structure that persists through time but a pattern of activity that is continuously reproduced by its participants. When the activity stops, the “thing” ceases — not because it was destroyed but because it was never anything other than its activity.

Alfred North Whitehead’s Process and Reality (1929) provides the fullest metaphysical elaboration. For Whitehead, the ultimate constituents of reality are actual occasions — momentary events of experience that constitute themselves through prehension (grasping prior occasions) and concrescence (growing together into a determinate unity). Each occasion is a process: it arises, achieves a definite character, and then perishes as a subject, becoming an object available for future occasions. The process is the reality; the achieved result is already past.

Process philosophy dissolves the problem of how static substances can enter into dynamic relations, because there are no static substances. There are only processes at various scales of stability. A rock is a very stable process (the atomic-level interactions that maintain its crystalline structure). A thunderstorm is a less stable process. A conversation is still less stable. But each is a process, and each exists only so long as its constituent activities sustain it.
