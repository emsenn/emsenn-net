Existentialism

2026-03-02 The philosophical tradition emphasizing that existence precedes essence — human beings constitute themselves through choices, actions, and commitments in concrete situations rather than fulfilling a pre-given nature.

Existentialism is the philosophical tradition organized around the claim that existence precedes essence: human beings are not born with a fixed nature or purpose but constitute themselves through their choices, actions, and commitments in concrete situations. There is no human essence that determines what a person should do or become. What a person is emerges from how they live, and the responsibility for that emergence cannot be deflected onto God, nature, society, or history.

The tradition has several distinct strands, united by this shared commitment. Søren Kierkegaard, writing in the 1840s against Hegel’s systematic philosophy, insisted on the individual’s irreducible confrontation with choice — particularly the choice of faith, which cannot be grounded in reason and must be made in anxiety and uncertainty. Martin Heidegger’s Being and Time (1927) reframed the question: human existence (Dasein) is not a subject contemplating a world but a being already thrown into a world of concerns, projects, and relations with others. The question is not “what should I choose?” but “what does it mean that I exist at all?” — and the answer involves confronting one’s own mortality as the condition that gives existence its urgency.

Jean-Paul Sartre’s Existentialism Is a Humanism (1946) gave the movement its public manifesto. Humans are “condemned to be free” — there is no essence, no God, no human nature that determines what one should do, and this freedom is inescapable. Even the refusal to choose is a choice. Bad faith (mauvaise foi) is the self-deception by which people deny their freedom — claiming they had no choice, that circumstances forced them, that they are simply following their nature. Authenticity is the willingness to own one’s freedom and the anguish that accompanies it.

Simone de Beauvoir demonstrated that existentialist analysis applies to the situation of women under patriarchy. “One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman” — femininity is not an essence but a situation, produced by social structures that constrain women’s freedom while pretending that constraint is nature. De Beauvoir’s The Second Sex (1949) is existentialist philosophy applied with political precision, showing that the claim “existence precedes essence” has consequences for how oppression works: oppression operates by treating the contingent as natural, the constructed as essential.
