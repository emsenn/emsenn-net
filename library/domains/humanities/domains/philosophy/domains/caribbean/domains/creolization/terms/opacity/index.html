Opacity

2026-03-03 Glissant's concept for the right of every person, culture, and knowledge-form to refuse translation into the terms of dominant understanding — not obscurity but a relational stance.

Opacity, as Édouard Glissant develops it in Poétique de la Relation (1990), is the right of every person, culture, and form of knowledge to not be made transparent — to refuse translation into the terms of dominant understanding. It is not obscurity or vagueness but a relational stance: the refusal to submit oneself to the demand for comprehension as a precondition for being granted existence, value, or political standing.

The demand for transparency is not neutral. When settler institutions demand that Indigenous knowledge be made legible — translated into curricula, rendered as data, formatted as policy input — they are exercising a form of power that positions one mode of knowing (Western, institutional, propositional) as the medium through which all others must pass. The knowledge that cannot pass through this medium is treated as nonexistent. Opacity refuses this exchange. It insists that some knowledge is not available for extraction — not because it is secret but because its meaning depends on the relations, places, and practices that sustain it.

Glissant distinguishes opacity from secrecy. Secrecy hides content behind a barrier and thereby accepts the framework of hidden content that could, in principle, be revealed. Opacity holds that the demand for content is itself the problem. One can relate to what one does not understand. In fact, the insistence on understanding before relation is a form of violence: it makes the other available only to the extent that they can be grasped. Relation, for Glissant, does not require transparency. The richest relations are those that preserve the opacity of each participant — where the encounter produces something new precisely because neither party has been reduced to the other’s terms.

This makes opacity the epistemological counterpart of refusal. Where refusal is a political act — declining imposed structures, withdrawing from extractive exchanges — opacity is the epistemic condition that refusal protects. The knowledge that lives in opacity is not waiting to be made legible. It is functioning fully within the relations that sustain it, and its opacity to outside frameworks is a feature of its embeddedness, not a deficiency to be overcome.

Opacity is also foundational to Glissant’s concept of creolization. Creolization produces irreducible novelty precisely because the elements that encounter each other retain their opacity. If every culture were transparent — fully translatable into every other — then contact would produce assimilation, not creolization. Opacity is what ensures that encounter generates the genuinely unpredictable.
