Overrepresentation of Man

2026-04-27 Wynter's term for the structural condition in which the modern Western secular subject — Man, in two successive overrepresentations (Man1 of the rational political subject, Man2 of the bio-economic subject) — has been mistaken for the human itself, with the consequence that other genres of the human are positioned as subhuman, prehuman, or insufficiently human. The overrepresentation is not a correctable bias but the structural form of modern knowledge, governing what counts as rational, scientific, and fully human.

The overrepresentation of Man is Sylvia Wynter’s term for the structural condition in which the modern Western secular subject — Man, in two successive overrepresentations (Man1 of the rational political subject, Man2 of the bio-economic subject) — has been mistaken for the human itself. The mistake is not an error of perception or a correctable bias. It is the structural form of modern knowledge: the conditions under which something counts as rational, scientific, fully human, normatively standard. Other genres of the human are positioned, by the overrepresentation, as the constitutive outside that the figure of Man requires in order to remain Man. To unsettle the overrepresentation is to begin the work of constructing a new descriptive statement of the human — a project Wynter calls “the human, after Man.”

¶Etymology and conceptual sources

The term Man in Wynter’s usage carries a deliberate weight. It is not gendered (in English) merely; it is the figure of the European secular bourgeois subject as the universal human. The capitalization marks the figure as a specific ethnoclass occupying the position of the universal. Overrepresentation names what happens when a particular figure is treated as the general — when the conditions, capacities, virtues, and rationality proper to one ethnoclass are read as the conditions, capacities, virtues, and rationality proper to humans as such.

Wynter’s framework synthesizes three lineages. From the Caribbean negritude and decolonial tradition (Césaire, Fanon, Glissant), the analysis of how European civilization’s self-presentation as the human required the colonial subject’s positioning as the not-fully-human. Fanon’s sociogenic principle — the claim that the human is constituted not just biologically (phylogenically, ontogenically) but by the symbolic order in which subjects are formed — is the foundation. From a wide reading of European intellectual history (the Renaissance humanists, Foucault, Darwin and Malthus on the bio-economic subject), the historical reconstruction of how Man1 and Man2 were successively constituted. From a heterodox reading of natural science and the philosophy of biology, the demand for a new science of the human that would treat the symbolic-cultural dimension as part of, rather than supplemental to, what humans are.

The locus classicus is the 2003 essay “Unsettling the Coloniality of Being/Power/Truth/Freedom” — a comprehensive synthesis where Wynter distinguishes Man1 from Man2 and makes the overrepresentation thesis explicit. The earlier “1492: A New World View” (1995) provides the historical narrative of Man1’s constitution; Wynter’s broader corpus, much of it collected and contextualized in McKittrick (2015), develops the framework across decades.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a condition as the overrepresentation of Man.

Particular figure occupying the universal position. The figure of Man is a particular ethnoclass — modern Western, secular, bourgeois, originally European — whose conditions, capacities, and norms are read as the conditions, capacities, and norms of the human. The particular’s claim to universality is not argued for; it is structurally enacted in the disciplines (philosophy, economics, anthropology, biology, psychology) that take Man’s conditions as the default and other conditions as deviations.

Other genres of the human as the constitutive outside. The figure of Man requires its outside — the irrational, the savage, the primitive, the underdeveloped, the infrahuman — in order to be itself. The outside is not merely excluded; it is structurally produced by the overrepresentation. The Indigenous, the African, the colonized, the racialized, the peasant, women — these are positioned as the not-fully-human against whose deficiency the figure of Man is constituted as the standard.

Successive overrepresentations across modernity. Wynter distinguishes Man1 (early-modern, the rational political subject of the Cartesian-Hobbesian-Lockean tradition, constituted against the irrational and the savage) from Man2 (post-Darwinian, the bio-economic subject of liberal-capitalist science, constituted against the unfit and the underdeveloped). The transition is structural rather than additive: Man2 inherits Man1’s overrepresentation while shifting the terms by which the outside is produced. The shift from religious-political to bio-economic categorization is a refinement of the overrepresentation, not its overcoming.

¶Examples

The discipline of economics as the science of the human. Economics, as it was constituted in the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries, takes the bio-economic subject (Man2) — the rational utility-maximizer optimizing scarce resources — as the model human, and reads other relations to material life (gift economies, kin-based provisioning, ceremonial circulation) as deviations or precursors. This is the overrepresentation of Man at work in disciplinary form: a particular ethnoclass’s relation to material life is read as the universal human relation, and other relations are read as the not-yet or the inadequate.

The category “modern subject” in liberal political philosophy. When liberal political philosophy poses its questions about consent, rights, autonomy, and contract, it presupposes a subject — propertied, individuated, severed from kinship and place, contracting from a position of formal equality — that is a particular ethnoclass’s subject. The framework reads other peoples’ political life through this presupposition and finds them lacking in modernity, autonomy, individuality.

The development paradigm. “Underdevelopment” presupposes a developmental trajectory, with Man2 (the modernized bio-economic subject) at the destination. Peoples whose social forms do not move toward this destination are positioned as developmentally arrested. The overrepresentation operates at the level of the very category by which their condition is described.

The contrast: Wynter’s demand for a new science of the human. The work of unsettling the overrepresentation is not the inclusion of additional figures within Man’s frame but the construction of a different frame: a descriptive statement of the human that takes the symbolic-cultural-sociogenic dimension as part of, not supplemental to, what humans are. The work is theoretical, political, and scientific simultaneously; Wynter does not believe the three are separable.

¶Adjacent concepts

The overrepresentation of Man is not the same as Eurocentrism in the broader sense. Eurocentrism names a tendency to center European experience in narratives, syllabi, and institutions; the overrepresentation of Man names the structural condition under which European experience has been mistaken for human experience as such. The latter is more specific and more thoroughgoing: it is not a bias to be corrected but the form of modern knowledge.

The overrepresentation of Man is not the same as anthropocentrism. Anthropocentrism names the centering of the human (against animals, plants, ecosystems); the overrepresentation of Man names the centering of a particular human (against other humans). The two operate at different scales and license different counter-projects, though they interact.

The overrepresentation of Man is not the same as Western universalism in the abstract-philosophical sense. Western universalism is a doctrine about the scope of moral and political claims; the overrepresentation of Man is a structural condition that operates whether or not particular subjects subscribe to Western universalism. A subject who refuses Western universalism doctrinally may still operate within the overrepresentation in their disciplinary practice.

¶Open questions

What does a descriptive statement of the human “after Man” actually look like? Wynter’s framework names the demand without claiming to have completed the project. The contemporary literature (McKittrick 2015, Weheliye 2014, decolonial-philosophy work more broadly) develops the question without consensus. The framework’s open question is whether the project requires institutional infrastructure that has not yet been built.

How does the overrepresentation of Man interact with the late-liberal apparatus’s metabolizing of all coherent subject-positions? If platforms metabolize anti-Man critique as a genre within the same metabolism — making the unsettling of Man into content that funds the apparatus — the closure question gets sharpened. The cybernetic-postliberal framework reads this risk explicitly; the question of what unsettling can survive metabolization is live.

What is the relation between the overrepresentation of Man and the genring operations the cybernetic-postliberal framework analyzes? Both are operations that reduce living people to the categories the dominant frame requires. The cybernetic-postliberal framework treats Wynter’s analysis as foundational; the precise relation (whether genring is one operation under Man’s overrepresentation, or whether the overrepresentation is one historical case of genring more broadly) is a question the framework holds open.
