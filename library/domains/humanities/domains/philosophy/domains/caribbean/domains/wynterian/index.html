Wynterian Analysis

¶Wynter

Sylvia Wynter (1928–) is a Jamaican philosopher, cultural theorist, and former dancer and novelist, whose work develops a comprehensive critique of the modern Western subject and a corresponding demand for a new science of the human. Her foundational essay, “Unsettling the Coloniality of Being/Power/Truth/Freedom” (2003), is the canonical synthesis of a body of work spanning decades.

The framework’s central claim is that the modern Western secular subject — what Wynter calls Man, in two successive overrepresentations (Man1, the rational political subject of the early-modern period; Man2, the bio-economic subject of the post-Darwinian period) — has been overrepresented as if it were the human itself. The overrepresentation is not a mistake to be corrected by inclusion; it is the structural form of modern knowledge, governing what counts as scientific, what counts as rational, what counts as fully human. Other genres of the human — Indigenous, African, Asian, peasant, women, the colonized, the racialized — are positioned, within the overrepresentation, as the constitutive outside that the figure of Man requires in order to be Man.

Wynter draws on Frantz Fanon’s work, particularly the sociogenic principle (the claim that the human is constituted not just biologically but by the symbolic order in which subjects are formed), on Aimé Césaire’s analysis of the colonial subject, and on a wide reading of European intellectual history (Foucault, the Renaissance humanists, Darwin and Malthus on the bio-economic subject). The framework’s distinctive contribution is the insistence that the human as such is at stake in colonial-racial critique: the work of decolonization is not just the redistribution of resources or the inclusion of excluded subjects, but the construction of a new descriptive statement of the human after Man.

For cybernetic postliberalism, Wynter is a constitutive upstream source. The genring operations the framework analyzes — the reduction of living peoples to genre figures, the elimination of the temporal middle, the saturation of the subject in inherited categories — are operations that presuppose Wynter’s overrepresentation of Man as the implicit measure. The framework’s account of californication is the account of how Man’s overrepresentation is reproduced affectively under platformal-energetic conditions.

Central thesis

The modern Western secular subject — Man, in two successive overrepresentations (Man1 of the rational political subject, Man2 of the bio-economic subject) — has been overrepresented as if it were the human itself, with the consequence that other genres of the human are rendered subhuman, prehuman, or insufficiently human. The work of decolonization is the work of unsettling this overrepresentation and constructing a new descriptive statement of the human, after Man.
