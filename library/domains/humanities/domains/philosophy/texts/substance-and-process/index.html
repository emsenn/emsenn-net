Substance and Process: Two Traditions in Ontology

2026-04-03 Two ancient philosophical traditions answer the question 'what is real?' in opposing ways. Substance philosophy says reality is made of persistent things. Process philosophy says reality is made of events and becomings. Neither alone captures how reality works. Both are needed.

Philosophy has always struggled with a fundamental question: what is real — stable being or constant becoming? Two incompatible traditions have emerged from this struggle, and they reach opposite conclusions.

Substance philosophy answers: reality is made of things — persistent entities with properties and natures. Aristotle, Locke, Leibniz, Russell: these philosophers ground reality in being, in what things ARE, in their intrinsic character and identity.

Process philosophy answers: reality is made of events and becomings — actualization, activity, creative emergence. Whitehead, Bergson, Peirce, Deleuze: these philosophers ground reality in activity, in what things DO, in their continuous creative unfolding.

The two traditions are philosophically incompatible. Yet any adequate account of reality requires both. Here is why, and how they work together.

¶What Substance Philosophy Says

Substance philosophy asks: what IS a thing? The answer: a thing is a persistent entity with qualities and capacities. Identity is intrinsic—grounded in what the thing is in itself, independent of what happens to it.

In Aristotle’s Metaphysics, a thing is what persists through change. A marble statue persists through all its properties—shape, color, mass, the sculptor’s signature. If you remove the marble, you remove the statue. The marble is the substance; the shape is what you add when you sculpt. The substance is what stays; the qualities are what vary.

John Locke in An Essay Concerning Human Understanding divides qualities into primary (intrinsic to the object—extension, shape, solidity) and secondary (dependent on the observer’s perception—color, taste, warmth). But both kinds belong to things. A rock is extended whether anyone observes it; the rock is also colored, though the color is how light bounces off the rock’s surface into our eyes. The rock itself is the substance; the properties are what we discover about it.

The core insight: a thing has an identity independent of our observation. A rock is a rock whether anyone looks at it. A person remains the person they are even when asleep, even when no one is watching. Identity is intrinsic—it belongs to the thing itself, not to the network of relations surrounding it.

This gives substance philosophy tremendous power for reference and cataloging. You can name a thing, track it, list its properties. “This rock is hard, gray, half a pound, two billion years old.” You are describing what the rock IS—its internal character. This matters. It is how you distinguish one thing from another. Without identity, you have no way to track anything across time.

¶What Process Philosophy Says

Process philosophy asks: what does a thing DO? The answer: a thing is not a persistent substrate. A thing is a pattern of recurring acts and relations. Identity is not given in advance; it emerges through the process of becoming.

In Whitehead’s Process and Reality, the fundamental units of reality are not things but “actual occasions”—moments of actualization. Each occasion IS its activity. A person at this moment is not a substance standing behind her thoughts, feelings, and perceptions. The person IS the complex act of taking in the world (her perceptions, memories, relationships, bodily sensations) and creating herself through response. When the occasion passes, it is complete and fixed in the past. The next moment inherits it, prehends it (takes it into account), carries it forward.

What we call a “person” is a society of occasions—a temporal series with a common character. The person is not an unchanging soul persisting through bodily change. The person IS the continuous pattern of occasions, each becoming and perishing, each carrying forward the previous one’s character. Interrupt the pattern (coma, death), and the person ceases to be. The pattern IS the person.

Henri Bergson in Creative Evolution argues that change is the fundamental reality, not a secondary phenomenon. What we perceive as a “state”—a person standing still, water in a bucket—is an artificial snapshot we take of continuous becoming. Real time is durée, duration, qualitative and irreversible. The past lives in the present, shaping what comes next. The future is genuinely open. To divide duration into discrete states is useful, but it abstracts from the real flux underneath.

The core insight: identity is constituted through process, not given in advance. A river IS the flowing. A person IS the pattern of lived experience, bodily process, and social interaction. A conversation IS the exchange of words, thoughts, attention. Nothing has identity apart from what it does. Identity is relational—it emerges from the network of dependencies and interactions that constitute the thing.

This gives process philosophy tremendous power for understanding change, development, creativity. How does something become what it is? Not through the working-out of a pre-existing essence, but through the creative unfolding of possibilities in response to conditions. This is how evolution works. How learning works. How relationships form and transform.

¶Why Reality Needs Both

Here is the difficulty: a description with only nouns is static. A description with only verbs is incoherent. Reality requires both.

Substance gives you nouns. Rock, river, person, conversation. You can point at something and name it. You can distinguish one thing from another. You can ask “what is this?” and expect a coherent answer: “This is a person.” “This is water.” Without the substance perspective, you have no stable reference point. Everything dissolves into flux. You cannot say “I am the same person I was yesterday” because there is no continuous I—only a series of momentary occasions. You cannot catalog properties because there is no stable object to catalog. Language collapses into present-tense babbling.

Process gives you verbs. Acting, changing, becoming, relating. You can ask “what is happening?” and understand change, not as an external event imposed on a thing, but as the thing’s own self-actualization. You can explain development—how a child becomes an adult, how a seed becomes a plant, how confusion becomes understanding. Without the process perspective, change is a mystery. Things would have properties at one moment and different properties at another moment, but you would have no way to explain the transition. Change would be utterly alien to what the thing IS, because the thing’s identity is just its intrinsic properties, and those either persist or don’t. You would be left saying “things persist and also change, but these are inexplicable facts.”

An adequate account of reality needs both.

Substance perspective: A person has height, weight, genetic code, a name, a date of birth. These properties ground her identity across time. Without them, there would be no way to distinguish one person from another or to track a person across time. This perspective answers the question: “Who are you?” with reference, identity, and intrinsic character.

Process perspective: A person is a pattern of metabolic events, cognitive activity, social interaction, emotional responses, bodily development. She is continuously becoming herself through living. Without this perspective, you could never explain how she grows, learns, ages, responds to circumstances, develops character. This perspective answers the question: “How do you become who you are?” with reference to activity, development, and relational unfolding.

Both are true. They answer different questions. Neither alone suffices.

¶Four Concrete Examples

A person:

Substance view: A person is a human organism with a genetic code, a name, psychological continuity, and a history. Her identity is grounded in what she IS—her biological and psychological character, her relationships, her actual origins. Even when she is sleeping or unconscious, she remains that person because these essential properties persist.

Process view: A person is a pattern of lived experience—continuous becoming through thought, perception, emotion, bodily sensation, interaction with others. She is metabolic process (heart beating, neurons firing, cells renewing). She is social process (conversation, recognition by others, assumption of roles). She is existential process (projects, choices, self-creation through action). She is not a static entity with properties; she is the ongoing actualization of possibility through living.

Both are true. You need both to understand what a person is. A medical examination uses the substance view—measuring height, weight, blood pressure, genetic markers. Psychotherapy uses the process view—understanding how you create and recreate yourself through choices, relationships, and engagement with life. Neither alone suffices.

A river:

Substance view: A river is a geographical feature with a name, a course, a source, a mouth, banks, a measured depth and width. It has properties: location, direction of flow, temperature, water chemistry. It has continuity—the Nile is the Nile from source to Mediterranean, whether or not anyone observes it. The river IS this geographical entity.

Process view: A river IS the flowing. The water is always different; no two moments have the same water. What persists is the pattern of flow—the valley shape, the slope, the dynamics of fluid motion. The river is not a thing that flows; it is the flowing itself. To be a river is to flow. Stop the water, and you have water in a valley, not a river. The river is the continuous process of water moving downslope under gravity.

Again, both are true. A geographer names and maps the river using the substance view. A physicist studying river dynamics uses the process view. A poet describing a river’s personality—its moods, its power, its relation to the land—uses both simultaneously.

A conversation:

Substance view: A conversation is an event with participants, a location, a duration, and a topic. It has properties: formal or informal, serious or playful, public or private. It can be recorded, transcribed, analyzed. You can ask “who was involved?” and list names. You can ask “what was it about?” and summarize the topic.

Process view: A conversation IS the exchange itself. It exists only in the moment-to-moment flow of speech, listening, response. Each participant is continuously creating their contribution based on what they hear; each speaker shapes what comes next in the exchange. The conversation has no fixed identity apart from this unfolding process. It is constituted by the mutual attention and responsiveness of the participants—stop that, and the conversation ceases to exist, even if the same people remain in the same room.

Both perspectives are needed. Sociologists studying conversation patterns use the substance view—counting turns, measuring pauses, analyzing utterance types. Conversational participants experience it through the process view—attending to the other person, responding to what they hear, co-creating meaning in real time. Neither alone captures what a conversation is.

A rock:

Substance view: A rock is a mineral object with mass, density, hardness, chemical composition. It has intrinsic properties independent of observation. The rock is a rock whether or not anyone looks at it. You can know what it IS by examining its composition and structure.

Process view: A rock is a pattern of atomic and molecular activity—electrons in orbitals, chemical bonds, thermal vibrations. It is the continuous (at human timescales) actualization of quantum potentials. Its “solidity” is not intrinsic but relational—the rock is solid relative to human bodies, but at quantum scales it is mostly empty space. What it IS depends on the scale and context of observation.

Both are true. A geologist examining rocks uses the substance view. A physicist studying materials uses the process view. The rock itself is neither static nor flowing; it is both, relative to different observational frameworks.

¶Why Both Traditions Are Necessary

Substance philosophy gives you a framework for reference, identity, and property. Without it, you cannot name things, distinguish one from another, or track them across time. Substance philosophy grounds language in stable entities: “this person,” “that river,” “the rock.”

Process philosophy gives you a framework for understanding change, development, becoming, and creativity. Without it, change is a mystery. You cannot explain how anything becomes what it is, why anything develops, or how anything relates to anything else. Process philosophy grounds understanding in activity: becoming, acting, transforming.

The two traditions are philosophically incompatible. Aristotle and Whitehead cannot both be right at the most fundamental level. But they answer different questions. The same reality can be read both ways:

  • As composed of persisting entities with intrinsic properties and natures
  • As composed of becoming occasions with relational dependencies

Neither reading is wrong. They illuminate different aspects of how the world works.

A complete understanding needs both. A description with only nouns (substance perspective) is static and can only catalog. A description with only verbs (process perspective) is dynamic but cannot establish stable reference. Reality requires both perspectives to be understood adequately.
