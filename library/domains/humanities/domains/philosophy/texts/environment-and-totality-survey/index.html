Environment and Totality: The Local and the Whole

2026-04-04 A survey of how environment and totality relate — how the local surroundings of a thing scale (or fail to scale) into a whole that contains everything. Covers situation semantics, Hegel's concrete universal, nesting and context, and the boundary that separates inside from outside.

¶Introduction: From Here to Everything

Two companion surveys in this collection treat environment and totality separately. The environment survey traces what surrounds a thing — from Aristotle’s topos through Uexküll’s Umwelt, Gibson’s affordances, and process-relational accounts where organism and world co-constitute each other. The totality survey examines the whole — mereological sums, set-theoretic paradoxes, Whitehead’s creative advance, and relational ontologies that refuse to totalize.

This survey asks the question that connects them: how does the local become the whole? An organism has an environment. That environment sits within a larger environment. At some point the nesting must either terminate in a totality or dissolve into something that resists closure. The transition from local to global is where the deepest problems lie — problems of context, nesting, boundary, and scale.

The question matters practically. Any system that organizes knowledge — a library, an encyclopedia, a repository — faces the same structure: content exists in local contexts (domains, files, terms), and those contexts nest into a whole (the repository, the collection, the corpus). How the local relates to the whole determines what you can find, what you can say, and what coherence means.

¶Hegel’s Concrete Universal: The Whole in the Part

The most ambitious attempt to think environment and totality together comes from Hegel. His central claim is that the universal is not abstract (a bare concept that applies to many instances) but concrete: the universal is the structured whole that expresses itself through its particular instances and is fully present in each of them.

In the Science of Logic, Hegel develops this through the movement from being to essence to concept. At the level of being, things simply are — they have qualities, quantities, measures. At the level of essence, things are what they are through their relation to what they are not: a thing’s identity depends on its difference from other things, and its environment is constituted by this field of difference. At the level of the concept, the thing and its environment are grasped as moments of a single self-developing whole.

What this means concretely: an acorn is not just a small object in a field. The acorn is the oak tree in potential. The oak tree is the acorn actualized. The forest is the whole within which acorn and oak are moments. And the forest is not external to the tree — the tree is the forest expressing itself at a particular location and time. The environment (forest) is not something the tree sits inside; the environment is what the tree is a determination of.

Hegel calls this the “concrete universal” because the universal (the forest, the species, the ecosystem) is not a bloodless abstraction hovering above its instances. It is the actual structured process that the instances are. The whole is present in each part — not as a copy or miniature, but as the determining context that gives the part its meaning and identity.

This has a direct consequence for the relationship between environment and totality. In Hegel’s account, the environment of a thing is not merely its local surroundings. The environment is the totality as it appears from the perspective of that thing. Every local environment is the whole, refracted through a particular standpoint. And the totality is not something that exists beyond or above all environments — it is the self-organizing process that produces all environments as its own differentiations.

The strength of this account is that it dissolves the scaling problem. You do not need to explain how local environments compose into a totality, because each local environment already is the totality in a particular determination. The weakness is that Hegel’s totality is self-enclosed: it has no genuine outside, no residue that escapes the system. Critics from Kierkegaard onward have argued that this totalization suppresses the contingent, the singular, the genuinely new.

¶Situation Semantics: Partial Worlds

Jon Barwise and John Perry’s Situations and Attitudes (1983) offers a very different approach. Instead of starting with the whole and showing how parts express it, they start with partial information and show how meaning arises from situations that are always incomplete.

A situation, in their framework, is a partial state of affairs — a fragment of reality that supports certain facts and is silent about others. When you walk into a room and see a cat on a mat, you are in a situation. That situation supports the fact that the cat is on the mat. It does not support the fact that the Prime Minister is in London or that it is raining in Tokyo. Those facts may be true, but they are not part of this situation.

This is the key departure from possible-worlds semantics, which models meaning in terms of complete possible worlds — total assignments of truth values to every proposition. A possible world settles everything; a situation settles only what is local and relevant. Barwise and Perry argue that this better matches how agents actually operate: we never have access to a complete world, only to the situations we find ourselves in.

The connection to environment is immediate. An organism’s environment, in situation-semantic terms, is the situation it finds itself in — partial, perspectival, structured by what the organism can perceive and act on. A tick’s situation supports facts about butyric acid and temperature. A human reader’s situation supports facts about text content, link structure, and domain organization. Each is a genuine situation — not an incomplete version of some total situation, but a self-standing partial world.

How do situations relate to each other? Through a relation Barwise and Perry call infon containment. A situation s₁ is part of a larger situation s₂ if every fact supported by s₁ is also supported by s₂. The room I am in is part of the building, which is part of the city — each a larger situation containing the smaller one. But — and this is critical — there is no maximal situation, no “situation of all situations.” The hierarchy of containment is open-ended. You can always embed a situation in a larger one, but you never arrive at a complete world.

This directly addresses the environment-totality relationship. Environments are situations — partial, local, structured by the capacities of the agent in them. You can nest environments: the file is in a domain, the domain is in the library, the library is in the repository. But the nesting does not terminate in a totality. There is no final, complete situation that contains all others. The hierarchy is open at the top.

Keith Devlin extended this framework in Logic and Information (1991), formalizing how situations constrain and are constrained by information flow. A situation is not just a static set of facts; it is a node in a network of informational constraints. The constraints determine what can be inferred within the situation and what requires stepping out into a larger context.

¶Context: The Frame Around the Situation

If a situation is a partial world, context is what determines which situation you are in and how to interpret what you find there.

John McCarthy’s 1993 paper “Notes on Formalizing Context” proposes treating context as a first-class object in logical systems. A proposition is not true or false absolutely; it is true or false in a context. The statement “it is raining” is true in the context of London on a particular afternoon and false in the context of the Sahara. Context is not just background; it is a parameter that changes what propositions mean and which inferences are valid.

McCarthy’s key insight is lifting: you can move between contexts by making the context explicit. “In context c, p is true” is itself a statement that can be evaluated in a larger context. This creates a hierarchy of contexts — local contexts embedded in broader ones, each providing a frame within which meaning is determinate.

Giunchiglia and Ghidini (2001) develop this into a full theory of contextual reasoning based on two principles: locality (reasoning happens within a context, using only what that context provides) and compatibility (contexts can be related to each other through bridge rules that translate facts between them). A biologist’s context and a chemist’s context may describe the same organism, but the facts they support, the vocabulary they use, and the inferences they license are different. Bridge rules let you move information between them without collapsing them into a single super-context.

This is exactly the structure of nested environments. Each domain in a knowledge system is a context — it has its own vocabulary, its own relevant facts, its own inferential patterns. The philosophy domain and the ecology domain may both discuss “environment,” but they mean different things and draw different inferences. Understanding how they relate requires bridge rules — cross-references, shared terms, explicit translations — not the assumption that they are fragments of one unified context.

Dan Sperber and Deirdre Wilson’s relevance theory (1986) adds a pragmatic dimension. Context is not fixed in advance; it is constructed on the fly by the cognitive agent processing an utterance. When you hear “the bank is closed,” you construct a context from available assumptions (you are near a river? near a building?) that makes the utterance relevant — that is, that yields cognitive effects for the least processing effort. Context is not a pre-existing frame that you look up; it is something you build from the interaction between what you already know and what you are encountering.

¶Nesting: Environments Within Environments

The environment-totality relationship is fundamentally a nesting problem. Environments contain sub-environments. A forest contains microhabitats. A city contains neighborhoods. A repository contains domains. Each level has its own structure, its own boundary conditions, its own affordances.

The question is whether this nesting is well-behaved. In mereology, composition is straightforward: if A is part of B and B is part of C, then A is part of C. Transitivity holds. But environments do not always compose transitively. The affordances of a microhabitat (a rotting log in a forest) are not simply a subset of the affordances of the forest. The log affords things (shelter for insects, substrate for fungi) that the forest-as-a-whole does not afford in the same way. The local environment has its own character that is not reducible to its position in the hierarchy.

This is where Uexküll’s insight becomes structural. Each organism has its own Umwelt — its own environment constituted by its capacities. A tick on a branch and a bird in the canopy are in the same forest but in different environments. Their environments overlap spatially but differ in what they afford. The nesting of spatial containment (branch within tree within forest) does not map neatly onto the nesting of environments-as-experienced.

Gibson’s affordances sharpen this. An affordance is relational: it exists at the interface between an agent’s capacities and the environment’s structure. A staircase affords climbing to a human, obstacle to a wheelchair user, and nothing relevant to a fish. The same physical structure participates in different environments depending on who encounters it. Nesting by spatial containment and nesting by affordance are two different hierarchies that do not necessarily align.

This creates a genuine problem for totality. If you try to define the totality as the nesting of all environments, you get a tangle: overlapping, crisscrossing Umwelten that cannot be arranged in a single hierarchy. The tick’s environment and the bird’s environment cannot be composed into a single super-environment that contains both, because they are structured by incompatible capacities. The totality, if it exists, is not a super-environment — it is something categorically different from any environment.

¶Boundary: The Act of Distinction

The relationship between environment and totality turns on boundary. An environment has a boundary — it marks what is inside (relevant, perceivable, actionable) from what is outside (irrelevant, imperceptible, inert). A totality, by definition, has no outside. The boundary question is: how does something with a boundary relate to something without one?

G. Spencer-Brown’s Laws of Form (1969) reduces this to its simplest expression. The primary act of cognition is distinction — drawing a boundary that creates two sides. Before distinction, there is nothing to say. After distinction, there is a marked side and an unmarked side, an inside and an outside. All further structure builds from this primary act.

Spencer-Brown’s insight is that the boundary is not a thing between two pre-existing sides. The boundary creates the sides. Before you draw the boundary, there are not two things waiting to be separated. The act of distinction brings into being the very difference it marks. This is close to Barad’s agential cuts: the boundary between system and environment is not found but enacted.

What happens when you try to draw a boundary around everything? Spencer-Brown shows that the form re-enters itself. When you mark the totality, the mark is inside the totality (because the mark is something, and the totality includes everything). So the totality contains the act of its own distinction. This is the formal version of the self-reference problem that Russell’s paradox makes vivid: the set of all sets must contain itself.

Spencer-Brown treats this not as a paradox but as a feature. The re-entry of the form into itself generates time — the oscillation between marked and unmarked, the inability to settle on a fixed state. The totality is not a static container; it is a self-referential process that perpetually re-distinguishes itself.

This connects environment to totality through the concept of boundary. Every environment is constituted by a boundary — an act of distinction that separates what is relevant from what is not. The totality is what you get when you try to draw the final boundary — the one that has no outside. But this boundary cannot hold still; it re-enters itself and generates process. The totality is not a super-environment with a super-boundary. It is the self-referential dynamic that all boundary-drawing participates in.

¶Whitehead’s Actual World: The Bridge

Whitehead’s process philosophy provides perhaps the most precise bridge between environment and totality. Each actual occasion has an “actual world” — the totality of prior occasions that it prehends (grasps, incorporates). This actual world is the occasion’s environment. But different occasions have different actual worlds, because they come into being at different points in the creative advance and therefore inherit different pasts.

The actual world of occasion A is not the same as the actual world of occasion B, unless A and B are contemporaneous and share exactly the same past. Every actual world is a genuine totality — it is everything that exists for that occasion. But it is also genuinely local — it is structured by the specific standpoint of the occasion that prehends it.

This dissolves the opposition between environment and totality. The environment is the totality, seen from a particular standpoint. The totality is the set of all environments, but this set is not a fixed inventory — it grows with each new occasion. There is no absolute totality outside all occasions; the extensive continuum (Whitehead’s name for the condition of togetherness) is the medium in which all actual worlds overlap and interpenetrate, but it is not itself an actual world.

The extensive continuum is real but not actual. It is the condition that makes environments possible — the continuous background against which boundaries are drawn and occasions achieve definiteness. It does not have boundaries of its own; it is what boundaries are drawn in. This makes it the closest thing to a totality in Whitehead’s system, but it is not a container. It is the potentiality for containment, the medium of relation itself.

¶Connection to Systems of Knowledge

These philosophical accounts map onto the practical structure of any organized knowledge system.

A domain in a library is an environment. It has a boundary — certain terms and texts belong to it, others do not. It has affordances — the vocabulary and inferential patterns available within it. It has a perspective — the disciplinary tradition that structures what counts as a relevant fact. A philosophy domain and an ecology domain are different environments in the same way that a tick’s Umwelt and a bird’s Umwelt are different environments in the same forest.

The repository as a whole functions like Whitehead’s extensive continuum. It is the condition of togetherness — what makes it possible for domains to cross-reference each other, for terms in one domain to be used in another, for a text in philosophy to cite a text in ecology. It is not itself a domain; it is the medium in which domains exist. It has no fixed boundary (new domains can be added, old ones reorganized), but it is the background against which all domain boundaries are drawn.

A fiber — a local environment structured by a particular perspective on a particular entity — is a situation in the Barwise-Perry sense. It supports certain facts and is silent about others. The fiber that a build system sees (templates, configuration, output paths) differs from the fiber that a reader sees (content, arguments, references). Both are genuine partial worlds. Neither is a degraded version of some total view.

The nesting of domains within domains mirrors the nesting of environments. A subdomain is a more specific context within a broader one. Bridge rules (cross-references, shared terms, explicit links) connect contexts without collapsing them. The whole is not the sum of its domains — it is the relational structure that makes domain boundaries meaningful.

¶Conclusion: Local First, Whole as Process

The relationship between environment and totality is not one of part to whole in the mereological sense. The totality is not the sum of all environments. Instead:

  1. Every environment is a perspective on the totality. Following Hegel’s concrete universal and Whitehead’s actual world, each local environment is the whole as it appears from a particular standpoint. The forest is the tree’s totality; the domain is the term’s totality.

  2. The totality is not an environment. It has no boundary, no outside, no affordance structure. It is the condition that makes environments possible — Spencer-Brown’s unmarked space, Whitehead’s extensive continuum, the repository that is not itself a domain.

  3. Situations are partial and genuine. Following Barwise and Perry, local contexts are not incomplete descriptions of a complete world. They are self-standing partial worlds with their own facts, their own inference patterns, their own relevance structure.

  4. Nesting is real but not mereological. Environments nest within environments, but the nesting is structured by affordances and perspectives, not by spatial containment alone. Different agents encounter different nestings of the same physical or informational space.

  5. Boundary is enacted, not found. Every environment exists because a distinction has been drawn. The act of drawing the distinction creates both the environment and what lies outside it. The totality is what remains when you try to draw a boundary around everything — a self-referential process, not a static container.

The practical consequence: build systems of knowledge from local environments outward, not from a total schema downward. Let each context be genuine and self-standing. Connect contexts through explicit bridges, not by assuming they are fragments of a pre-given whole. The whole will emerge from the relational structure of the contexts — not as a completed inventory, but as the ongoing process of connection itself.
