Action and Change: A Survey of Substance and Process Accounts

2026-04-03 Survey of philosophical accounts of action and change from Aristotle through contemporary process philosophy, examining competing claims about what is more fundamental: the things that act, or the acts of becoming themselves.

What is an action? What is change? These questions matter because they concern the most basic features of the world we inhabit. Everything we observe involves change—seasons shift, bodies age, thoughts emerge and fade. Everything we do involves action—we choose, move, speak, create. Yet philosophy has produced not one coherent account but a fundamental divergence between two pictures: substance philosophy, which asks “what ARE things, and what do they DO?” and process philosophy, which asks “how do things BECOME, and is activity more fundamental than being?”

This divergence is not mere academic disagreement. It is a collision at the deepest level of ontology. For Aristotle, a thing is prior to any act; the thing is the substance, and its action is secondary. For Whitehead, actual occasions (acts) are the fundamental reality, and things are derivative—they are the patterns of activity left behind when moments of actualization pass into the past. The two traditions offer competing visions of which is more real: the persistent thing or the evanescent event. This survey presents both traditions with equal rigor, drawing on twenty-five centuries of philosophy from ancient Greece through process philosophy and Buddhist thought.

¶Substance Philosophy: The Thing Does; The Act Manifests

Aristotle’s act/potency distinction (energeia/dunamis) is the foundational substance account and remains the most developed framework for understanding action. In Metaphysics Book IX, Aristotle distinguishes two ways a thing can exist: dunamis (potentiality) and energeia (actuality). A piece of oak wood is potentially a house; a completed house is actually a house. A person with the medical knowledge and skill to heal is potentially a doctor (has the capacity); a person actively healing a patient is actually a doctor (is in the process of exercising that capacity). Potentiality is the capacity to be or do something. Actuality is the realization of that capacity in the world.

Critically, Aristotle distinguishes two types of change (metabole): kinesis and energeia. A kinesis is a motion or activity directed toward an end that is still incomplete. A runner engaged in running is in kinesis—the act of running is unfinished until they reach their goal. An energeia is an activity that is complete at every moment of its occurrence. The pleasure of seeing, the joy of thinking—these are energeiai because they are fully realized at every instant; you do not wait for them to finish in order to enjoy them. The distinction illuminates different modes of action and change: some actions are means to an end (kinesis) while others are their own end (energeia).

Aristotle’s Physics (Books III–IV) develops a rich taxonomy of change types: generation and corruption (coming to be and ceasing to be), alteration (a quality of a thing changing—growing pale, warming), growth and diminution (quantity changing—getting larger or smaller), and locomotion (change of place). Each type involves the actualization of a different kind of potentiality. This taxonomy reveals that “change” is not a univocal concept. The change from not-existing to existing (generation) is ontologically different from the change of a thing’s color while it persists. Understanding which type of change is occurring is essential to understanding what is happening in any situation.

What makes Aristotle’s account a substance account: the thing is primary. Change is something that happens TO or BY a thing. The thing pre-exists the act. A vessel is a vessel whether it holds cargo or sits empty; holding is what it DOES, not what it IS. A person persists whether acting or at rest; action is an expression of their capacities. The substance is the persistent entity that has the potential and realizes it through action.

Davidson’s event-causal theory (1963, 1980) inherits Aristotle’s substance framework but places it in a modern setting of causation and mental states. For Davidson, an act is an event caused by beliefs and desires. A belief is a mental state representing how the world is. A desire is a disposition to bring about a state of affairs. Together, they cause a bodily movement. The caused bodily movement IS the act. The act is an event—something that occurs at a time and place.

But critically for substance philosophy: the event is distinct from the beliefs and desires that cause it. The event is something that happens to or by an agent, not identical to the agent. The agent is the substance; the action is the event that the agent brings about.

Davidson’s key insight—that “there are no bare acts, only events under descriptions”—cuts deep. The same bodily movement (raising an arm) counts as many different acts depending on context: it is a greeting, a signal, a vote, a medical examination, a gesture of surrender, a distress signal. The act is not in the movement itself but in how the movement is described in relation to the agent’s beliefs and desires. A single event can fall under multiple act-descriptions simultaneously. This plurality of descriptions is not an artifact of language; it reflects a real feature of action itself.

Anscombe’s action under a description (Intention, 1957) deepens this insight without relying on Davidson’s causal account. An action is given by a description under which the agent acts knowingly. You know that moving your finger is an act under the description “pulling the trigger.” You may not know that pulling the trigger counts as killing, if your gun was loaded and aimed, and you were unaware of this fact. Yet the act of killing is an act of yours even though you did not do it knowingly. Actions nest under multiple descriptions simultaneously: moving my finger, pulling the trigger, firing the gun, killing my friend—all one action, but described at different levels of intentionality and knowledge.

Anscombe calls the basic action (the one you do directly, without doing anything else first) the terminal action. Everything else is derived from chains of basic actions. You move your finger (basic action) by contracting muscles (description of the same act at a physiological level), thereby pulling the trigger (description at the mechanical level), thereby firing the gun (description at the event-outcome level), thereby killing your friend (description at the social-moral level). The descriptions are not layers you peel back to find the “real” act underneath; they are authentic multiple descriptions of the same act, each true and informative in its context.

Arendt’s trichotomy (The Human Condition, 1958) distinguishes labor, work, and action as three fundamental human activities, each with its own temporal and ontological character. Labor sustains life. It is cyclical—you eat, digest, excrete, eat again—and its rhythm is biological necessity. The products of labor are consumed; nothing persists. Work builds durable things for the world. A table, once built, stays built (until it decays or is destroyed). Work is productive; it takes material from nature and produces an artifact with an independent existence. The worker withdraws after the work is complete; the artifact remains in the world.

Action discloses who you are. It is irreversible and unpredictable. You cannot take back what you have said; you cannot undo what you have done. No action can be undone by doing something else, as you can remake a table by woodworking. Actions happen between people, in public, and they reveal your identity in a way that labor and work do not. When you act, you show yourself to others—they learn who you are through what you do and say. This revelation is involuntary; you cannot fully control how others interpret your actions.

Arendt argues that the modern world has progressively downgraded action and elevated work and labor. We have become a society of workers and laborers, focused on productivity and consumption, forgetting that human dignity lies in the capacity for action—for appearing in public, for beginning something new, for exercising freedom in concert with others.

Goldman’s act-tree (A Theory of Human Action, 1970) provides the most systematic account of action decomposition within the substance framework. Goldman distinguishes between level-generating and simple causation. An act A is level-generated by act B if performing B is the way you perform A. Pulling the trigger is the way you fire the gun. Firing the gun is the way you shoot the person. The acts form a tree with the basic act at the bottom (what you do without doing anything else first) and increasingly complex acts higher up. The tree structure lets you formalize intuitions about action: which acts are means to which? which acts are basic (directly executable) versus derived (realized through other acts)?

The act-tree reveals that actions are hierarchically organized. You do not perform all acts at the same level. A complex act like “writing a book” is realized through a series of simpler acts: writing sentences, which are realized through striking keys, which are realized through finger movements. The hierarchy is not arbitrary; it reflects the real structure of how acts are performed—through level-generation, where a higher-level goal is achieved by performing appropriate lower-level acts in sequence.

¶Process Philosophy: Acts Are Primary; Things Emerge From Process

Whitehead’s actual occasions (Process and Reality, 1929) invert the substance picture entirely. For Whitehead, the ultimate units of reality are not things but actual occasions—“drops of experience.” An actual occasion is an act of becoming. It takes in (prehends) the world—its predecessor occasions, the contemporary world, the eternal objects (abstract possibilities)—and through a process of concrescence (growing together), it creates itself. The process is internal to the occasion itself; there is no external force molding the occasion into shape. The occasion is its own becoming.

When the occasion completes its becoming, it perishes as a subject and becomes data (past) for the next occasion. It is fully complete, fully determinate, fully what it is. It has created itself through its concrescence, and that self-creation is now fixed in the past. Subsequent occasions inherit it—by prehending it (taking it into account), they continue the process. The occasion’s perishing is not loss or destruction; it is the occasion achieving its full actuality and becoming available as data for future becomings.

Critically: there is no substance that does the acting. The actual occasion IS its activity. There is no occasion lying around waiting to act. The occasion is identical to its process of becoming-into-being. When Aristotle speaks of substance and act, Whitehead would say: the occasion just is the act of its own actualization. To ask “what is the occasion?” is to ask “what is it doing?"—and the answer is the occasion’s entire process of concrescence.

What we call a “thing” (a rock, a person, a tree) is, for Whitehead, a society of occasions—a series of occasions with a common characteristic that persists from occasion to occasion. The rock is not a substance that remains identical through time while its properties change. The rock is a temporal series of rock-occasions, each becoming into being and perishing, each prehending its predecessors. We perceive continuity because the occasions are causally linked: each occasion takes its predecessors into account and carries forward their character. The rock persists through the persistence of a pattern of occasions, not through the persistence of an underlying substance.

This dissolves the substance/act distinction. For Aristotle, a thing is primary and acts are secondary. For Whitehead, acts (occasions) are primary, and things are cognitive abstractions from extended temporal series of acts. The thing is a pattern of recurrence, not a substrate underlying the pattern.

Whitehead’s notion of subjective aim is his answer to what constitutes the directionality of an occasion. The subjective aim is not an external goal imposed on the occasion from outside. It is the occasion’s own internal direction—its orientation toward a goal. It is immanent to the occasion’s becoming, not separate from it. The subjective aim emerges through the occasion’s concrescence, shaped by what the occasion inherits from its predecessors and what possibilities lie available. For Whitehead, this explains how occasions can be both determinate (fully completed, with a definite character) and creative (each occasion is genuinely novel, not wholly determined by the past).

Bergson’s creative evolution (Creative Evolution, 1907; Time and Free Will, 1889) argues that change is the fundamental reality, not a secondary phenomenon. What we call “states” are artificial snapshots we take of a continuous flux. Real time is durée—duration, the interpenetration of moments. The past is not gone; it lives in the present, shaping what comes next. The future is genuinely open; it has not been created yet. The present moment is not a knife-edge between past and future; it is a thick duration in which past and future are folded together.

The élan vital—often mistranslated as “life force”—is better understood as the principle of creative advance. It is not a substance injected into matter. It is the dynamic principle that drives all becoming. Bergson argues that the appearance of teleology (goal-directedness, purpose) in living things is not an illusion or a projection but a real feature of creative process. Living things tend toward their own continuation and elaboration, but not according to a pre-existing plan. The plan emerges as the creature lives. The future is not predetermined; it is created through the living process itself.

This contrasts with mechanism (the view that the future is fully determined by the past and the laws of nature) and also with vitalism (the view that life contains a special non-physical substance). For Bergson, evolution is creative in the most literal sense: it produces genuine novelty that could not have been predicted from the past. The élan vital is what drives this creativity—not a thing or force, but the principle of creative advance itself.

Bergson’s account explains how novelty is real. If the future were fully determined by the past, no genuine novelty would be possible. If the future were random and lawless, we could not understand evolution’s apparent progress toward greater complexity. Bergson argues that creative evolution occupies a middle ground: the future is open and genuinely novel, but constrained by inherited structures and the continuity of creative advance.

Peirce’s Firstness, Secondness, and Thirdness provide a triadic framework that mediates between substance and process. Firstness is pure quality, pure possibility, pure monadic potential. Redness as a quale—the possibility of red, the redness-itself—exists at Firstness before anything is red. Secondness is brute fact, resistance, actuality, dyadic relation. When you press against something and feel resistance, you are at Secondness. It is the collision of two things with no mediation, no interpretation. Thirdness is mediation, interpretation, law, habit, triadic relation. A word means something because an interpreter reads it that way. A physical law (gravity) holds because there is a regular pattern that constrains what can happen.

Action sits at the boundary between Secondness and Thirdness. A bare bodily movement (muscle contraction, nerve firing) is Secondness—brute physical fact. But the movement counts as an act only at Thirdness—only when there is interpretation, intention, social meaning. A twitch and a wave look identical at Secondness; they differ utterly at Thirdness. The difference is purely the meaning—how the movement is interpreted in context.

This suggests that action is not purely physical. It requires interpretation. But it also is not purely mental or conventional. The bodily movement (Secondness) is real; the interpretation (Thirdness) is real. The act is the integration of both. You cannot have action without both the physical doing and the meaningful interpretation.

Simondon’s individuation (Individuation in Light of Notions of Form and Information, 1958/2005) challenges the hylomorphic picture (form stamped on matter) that underlies both substance philosophy and naive process accounts. Simondon argues that individuation is not the imposition of a pre-existing form onto matter. Rather, individuation is a process where form and matter both emerge together from a pre-individual field.

The classic example: a crystal does not form because a “crystal shape” descends from heaven and sculpts the atoms. The crystal forms through the dynamics of molecular bonding: local tensions, thermal gradients, and molecular affinities create a process where form and matter differentiate together. The crystal’s shape emerges. Neither the shape nor the matter was there at the start; both emerge through individuation.

This has profound implications. It means individuation is not instantiation—putting a pre-existing form into matter. It is emergence—the progressive differentiation of a pre-individual field into determinate form and determinate matter. The individual (crystal, organism, person) is the product of this process. More importantly: the individual is not fully determinate before it begins to individuate. The individual becomes determinate through individuation.

Moreover, individuation is not finished with the emergence of the individual. The individual continues to individuate—to differentiate, to resolve tensions, to develop. A person continues to individuate throughout their life. A text individuates as it is written and read and revised. An organism individuates as it grows and develops and responds to its environment.

Deleuze’s intensive and extensive differences (Difference and Repetition, 1968) provide a process account of change that avoids treating change as mere transition between static states. An extensive property is one that can be divided and aggregated. Length, weight, number. You can divide a meter into centimeters; you can add two kilos to another two kilos. Extensive properties are what you measure with. An intensive property is one that cannot be divided without changing in kind. Temperature, pressure, potential, speed. A temperature of 50°C cannot be divided in half: half of a 50°C fluid is still 50°C (or it is two portions of 50°C fluid, not half-temperatures). An intensity is a gradient, a difference, a tension—not a magnitude.

Deleuze argues that intensive differences are more fundamental than extensive magnitudes. They drive the process. When there is a temperature difference, heat flows. When there is a pressure difference, gases mix. When there is a potential difference, current flows. The intensive difference is the dynamism that produces change. The resulting extensive properties (the new temperature distribution, the mixed gases, the charge distribution) are products—already stabilized, already spatializable, already measurable.

This means that change is not transition between states (between extensive properties). Change is the resolution of intensive differences. A process is intensive; its products are extensive. Before extensive properties have settled, the situation is one of intensity—tension, gradient, tendency. The resolution of that tension produces a new extensive state.

Deleuze argues that this applies to quality as well. A quality like “color” or “sound” is intensive before it becomes a measurable wavelength. The lived intensity of a color (its affective quality, its saturation and brilliance) is intensive; the physical measurement is extensive. The intensive cannot be reduced to the extensive.

Heraclitus’s panta rhei (“everything flows”) is the oldest Western process ontology. “You cannot step in the same river twice, because both you and the river have changed.” Often misinterpreted as saying the river is always the same while the water changes, Heraclitus is making a stronger claim: the river is not a thing that flows; the river IS flowing. To call it a “river” is to create a linguistic continuity over something that is actually discontinuous and changeable.

The river right now is not the river it was a moment ago. Yet we call them the same river because of the continuity of process. The river persists as a flowing pattern, not as a substance. Similarly, a person is not a thing that remains identical while their body changes. A person is a persistent pattern of becoming.

For Heraclitus, the principle of change is not an external force acting on things. Change is intrinsic. Things perpetually become different. Fire is Heraclitus’s paradigm: fire is not a substance that burns. Fire is burning itself. To be fire is to be in the process of consuming and consuming oneself. A river is not a vessel through which water flows. A river is the flowing itself.

This implies that identity is not substantial. There is no permanent essence underlying the change. The river’s identity is in the pattern of flowing. Disrupt the pattern—dam the river, redirect it—and it is no longer the same river, even if the substance (water) remains. Conversely, even if the water molecules change (as water flows), it is still the same river.

Buddhist philosophy of action (karma, Abhidharma) dissolves the agent/act distinction that substance philosophy takes for granted. In Buddhist ontology, there is no permanent agent-substance that performs actions. What we call an “agent” is itself a process—a causally ordered sequence of mental and physical events. An action (karma) is intentional doing that produces consequences through dependent origination (pratītyasamutpāda).

The Abhidharma tradition analyzes mental processes into momentary dharmas (factors of experience) that arise and perish in rapid succession. A single mental act (deciding, remembering, intending) is not a substance doing something; it is a causal series of momentary events. Each dharma arises in dependence on causes and conditions and causes the next dharma to arise. The continuity is causal, not substantial. What we perceive as a persistent self is actually a seamless causal chain of momentary experiences.

For example, the experience of choosing to raise your arm is not a unified moment where you “decide” and then your arm rises. It is a rapid causal sequence: an intention-moment arises (dependent on your character, circumstances, and prior experiences), which causes an attention-moment, which causes a deliberation-moment, which causes a decision-moment, which causes a command-moment, which causes a movement-sensation-moment. Each moment is distinct and momentary; the continuity and unity are causal and conventional, not substantial.

Importantly, this account does not make action illusory. The intentional doing is real. The consequences are real. Karma (action) produces karmic consequences through the causal order of dependent origination. Your actions shape the world and your own future because actions have real causal efficacy. But the agent—the “I” who acts—is a convenient fiction we project onto the causal sequence. There is causal order without a causal agent-substance.

Moreover, karma is not fatalism. The Abhidharma framework allows for real indeterminacy. Each moment’s arising depends on conditions, but the future is not predestined. The conditions that determine what arises are themselves dependent on prior actions, which were not fully determined either. The future is constrained but open. Genuine novelty is possible within the causal order. This possibility of surprise, discovery, genuine learning—these are built into a process ontology that denies agent-substances but maintains the reality of action and its consequences.

¶The Two Traditions and Their Tensions

The divergence between substance and process philosophy is not an oversight in philosophical history. It reflects a real choice about what is more fundamental. Both traditions can point to genuine features of the world.

Substance philosophy captures:

  • Acts are describable at multiple levels, from basic to complex (Anscombe, Goldman)
  • Acts have agents, and agents can be held responsible
  • Change has types (Aristotle), and different types matter for different purposes
  • Things persist, and their persistence is real—not illusory

Process philosophy captures:

  • Becoming is more fundamental than being; the evanescent is real
  • Things are patterns that emerge from and depend on sustained process
  • Energy and directionality are immanent, not imported from external forces
  • Novelty is possible within the natural world; the future is genuinely open
  • Intensive differences and tensions drive change; extensive states are products

The question is not which tradition is correct but whether they can be integrated or whether they represent a genuine metaphysical fork in the road.

Some philosophers have attempted integration. Whitehead himself argued that his process philosophy preserves the substance-philosophical insights correctly understood. When Aristotle says “the substance is primary,” he means that there is a persistent principle. Whitehead agrees: the enduring society of occasions is the persistent principle. When Goldman describes act-trees, he is describing patterns of level-generation. Whitehead’s concrescence can accommodate this: occasions level-generate from one another.

Others argue the fork is genuine. One cannot truly hold both pictures simultaneously. To make things primary is to deny that becoming is fundamental. To make becoming primary is to deny that things are anything more than abstract patterns.

¶Action Without Substance: Agency Reconceived

One promising direction is to reconceive agency itself in process terms. If there is no agent-substance, how can there be intentional action?

The Buddhist and Peircean accounts suggest a way: intentionality is built into the process structure itself, not requiring a separate agent-mind. An action is intentional when it is oriented toward a goal, when it involves interpretation and meaning. This orientation and interpretation can be features of the causal process without requiring an external agent.

Consider: a plant’s growth toward light is oriented toward (in the sense of “aimed at”) capturing light, even though the plant has no mind. The growth is a process with a direction, a structure, a goal-relatedness. The plant succeeds or fails at getting the light. This goal-relatedness is real—it is not merely imposed by human interpretation (though it may be articulated by human interpretation).

Similarly, an animal’s behavior can be intentional—aimed at capturing prey, avoiding danger, finding shelter—without requiring the animal to be a substance with a mind. The behavior is structured toward these goals. It has the formal properties of action: it is responsive to circumstances, it aims at an end, it can succeed or fail.

An agent (human or otherwise) is a process with built-in intentionality—a process structured toward ends, responsive to circumstances, capable of learning and adaptation. This is not as mysterious as it might seem: neuroscience shows that the brain is a system with multiple levels of intentional organization, from basic drives (built-in goal-structures) to sophisticated abstract reasoning. At each level, the process is goal-oriented without requiring a central “I” looking at options and choosing.

This reconception does not eliminate agency. It relocates it. Agency is not the property of a substance that acts. Agency is a property of certain processes: processes that are goal-directed, adaptive, responsive to meaning. Some processes have it (living systems, deliberative systems), and some do not (a rock rolling downhill, a river flowing).
