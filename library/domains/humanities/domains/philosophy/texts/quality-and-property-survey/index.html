Quality and Property: A Survey of Substance and Process Accounts

2026-04-03 Survey of philosophical accounts of quality, property, and condition — from Aristotle's categories through Whitehead's eternal objects to Deleuze's intensive differences. Examines competing frameworks for understanding what qualities are, how they exist, and whether reality is fundamentally constituted by stable substances bearing properties or by processes actualizing potentials.

¶Introduction

Every thing possesses qualities. A rock is hard, heavy, gray. A promise is solemn, binding, abstract. A person is brave, measured, weary. Qualities are how things become distinguishable — how we tell one thing from another, how we describe what we encounter.

Yet “quality” is philosophically fraught. Is a quality a universal (the property of redness that many things share) or a particular (this specific redness here, unrepeatable)? Is it observable (color, temperature) or intrinsic (essence, nature)? Does a quality exist in the thing itself, or only in the mind of an observer? Does a thing have qualities as a substance has attributes, or does a thing consist of the qualities it realizes at a moment?

These questions matter because they shape how we understand reality itself. If qualities are intrinsic to things, then reality has determinate structure independent of observation. If qualities are properties substances bear, then substance comes first — there is something that is independent, that qualities attach to. If qualities are potentials that things realize, then actualization is primary, and substances emerge from their becoming.

This survey examines two major traditions. The substance tradition (Aristotle through Armstrong) treats quality as an attribute, a universal or particular that inheres in a substance. Quality is what a thing has. The process tradition (Whitehead, Simondon, Deleuze) treats quality as a pure potential (eternal object, abstract intensity) that a process or occasion realizes or becomes. Quality is not had; it is ingressed into, embodied, actualized.

The distinction cuts deeper than academic genealogy. It asks: when we describe a person’s health, are we recording a property the person carries, or are we tracking a phase of the person’s embodied becoming? When we say an organization is “mature,” are we recording a fact about its current state, or are we marking a threshold in its development? Does a person’s character precede their character-formation, or do they become who they are through action?

¶The Substance Tradition: Aristotle to Armstrong

¶Aristotle’s Categories

Aristotle’s Categories Chapter 8 provides the oldest systematic account. Aristotle divides quality (poiotes) into four species:

  1. Habit or disposition (hexis): a stable, relatively permanent condition. Virtue, knowledge, health. These can be lost or changed but persist over time.
  2. Natural capacity or incapacity (dynamis): a power to be affected or to act. The capacity of the eyes to see, of material to be shaped. These are intrinsic to the thing’s nature.
  3. Affective qualities and affections (pathē): sensible properties. Color, taste, heat, cold. These are what the senses perceive; some are alterable (the water changes temperature) but perceived as real.
  4. Shape and form (schēma): configuration, figure, arrangement. The shape of the body, the structure of a building. These endure unless externally altered.

Aristotle insists that qualities are not substances themselves — they depend on a substance to inhere in. You cannot have “whiteness” floating free; something must be white. Yet whiteness is not the substance itself; many things can be white.

Aristotle’s framework suggests that these four kinds are fundamentally different, not all collapsible into “observable properties.” A person’s courage is not a sensible property (you do not perceive courage the way you perceive color); it is a disposition, a stable orientation toward danger. A crystal’s solubility is a natural capacity, distinct from both its observed shape and its evident properties. A fruit’s ripeness is an affective quality — how it strikes the senses — yet distinct from its actual internal maturation. Shape is durable and intrinsic, while color may shift with light.

¶Locke’s Primary and Secondary Qualities

John Locke’s distinction in Essay Concerning Human Understanding II.8 splits qualities by their source of being. A primary quality is in the object itself: extension, shape, motion, number, solidity. Remove the observer and a lump of gold still has shape, mass, divisibility. A secondary quality exists in the observer’s mind: color, taste, sound, smell. The gold appears gold because of how it reflects light and how human eyes perceive that reflection. Remove the human observer and only the primary qualities remain.

Locke’s distinction has been criticized (if shape is in the object, why not color, which depends on surface microstructure?) but it gestures at something real: some properties seem intrinsic to things independent of observation (mass, extension); others seem observer-dependent (perceived hue, experienced heat).

Consider a piece of music. The vibration frequencies of its notes are primary qualities — they exist whether or not anyone hears them. But the perceived timbre, the emotional character, the sense of consonance or dissonance — these are secondary. They depend on human hearing and human sensibility. Yet both matter for understanding what the music is. A chemist measures the primary qualities of a substance; a person experiences its secondary qualities. Neither is false; they describe different aspects of the same thing.

Locke’s framework suggests that understanding any complex thing requires attending to both its intrinsic determinations and its relational properties — not what it is in itself alone, but what it is in relation to observers, to other things, to systems of use.

¶D.C. Williams and Trope Theory

D.C. Williams’s 1953 paper “On the Elements of Being” inaugurated trope theory: the view that qualities are particular, unrepeatable instances, not universals.

In traditional metaphysics, two red balls share the same universal — redness. Williams argues this is confused. What exists is not an abstract redness but two distinct, particular instances: the-redness-of-this-ball and the-redness-of-that-ball. These are tropes — particular qualities, resembling each other, but not identical. Each trope is unique, located in time and space, unrepeatable.

A substance, on this account, is not a substratum bearing properties. A substance is a bundle of tropes — a cluster of particular qualities held together by spatiotemporal unity. A person is the bundle {this-voice, this-manner-of-walking, this-laugh, this-temperament, this-body}. Another person with seemingly the same qualities is a different bundle {that-voice, that-manner-of-walking, that-laugh, that-temperament, that-body}. Resemblance between bundles (both are courageous, both are measured) is a matter of the tropes instantiating similar types, but the tropes themselves are irreducibly particular.

Trope theory thus denies the subject-predicate form: there is no substance (“the person”) that first exists and then has qualities (courage, wit). Rather, the person IS the bundle of particular tropes that constitute them. This is philosophically radical: it means there is no “subject” underneath or prior to the bundle. The bundle itself is the fundamental entity.

¶Armstrong and Universals

David Armstrong’s Universals and Scientific Realism (1978) defends realism about universals against nominalism. Universals are real, abstract entities that can be instantiated in multiple particulars. Redness is a real universal; this ball’s redness is an instantiation of that universal.

Armstrong grounds this in scientific realism: physics describes genuine universals (mass, charge, force). If physics is true, universals must exist. Moreover, resemblance requires universals: two things resemble each other not by accident or convention but because they instantiate common properties.

Armstrong distinguishes between monadic properties (properties a thing has in isolation) and polyadic relations (relations between things). A file’s word count is monadic; the relation between a term and its defining text (defines: relation) is dyadic.

Armstrong also distinguishes categorical properties (properties a thing has right now: current state, shape, color) from dispositional properties (powers, capacities, tendencies). This distinction matters. An electron has the categorical property of negative charge and the dispositional property of attraction-to-positive-charges. One is intrinsic state; the other is a power or tendency.

A person has the categorical property of standing six feet tall and the dispositional property of being able to run. A crystal has the categorical property of cubic structure and the dispositional property of dissolving in water. A building has the categorical property of being made of stone and the dispositional property of sheltering its inhabitants. These are not the same kind of fact. One describes an intrinsic state; the other describes what the thing can do or what happens to it under certain conditions. Armstrong’s framework clarifies that these are different kinds of properties and may need different logical treatment.

¶George Molnar on Powers

George Molnar’s Powers: A Study in Metaphysics (2003) develops powers ontology — the view that fundamental properties are powers (dispositions, capacities), not categorical properties. A power is a tendency to act under certain conditions. An electron doesn’t have negative charge as an inert categorical property; it has the power of electromagnetic repulsion.

Molnar argues that:

  1. Powers are intrinsic to their bearers (not mind-dependent)
  2. Powers are multiply realizable (the same power can be realized by different underlying properties)
  3. Powers have directions of fit: an electron’s power is a power-to-repel-positive (not the reverse)
  4. Powers are categorical properties’ complement, not reducible to them. You cannot analyze power away into categorical facts alone.

This matters philosophically because it means many apparently simple descriptions of things are really dispositional. A substance looks categorical (what a thing is) but is really a power-to-persist, a power-to-exclude-other-things, a power-to-maintain-coherence. A person’s character looks like a stable property but is really a power-to-act-consistently, a power-to-influence-others, a power-to-sustain-projects.

Molnar’s insight is that many apparently categorical properties are really powers. Brittleness is not a state of matter but a power-to-shatter. Solubility is not a permanent feature but a power-to-dissolve. Trustworthiness is not a property a person has but a power-to-act-reliably. This suggests that reality is more fundamentally characterized by powers and potentials than by static categorical properties.

¶Kim and Supervenience

Jaegwon Kim’s Supervenience and Mind (1993) examines supervenience relations between property families. A higher-level property supervenes on lower-level properties if any change in the higher-level property requires a change in the lower-level properties, and any two systems identical in all lower-level properties must be identical in the supervenient property.

Consider a person’s health. Health supervenes on lower-level physical properties: organ function, metabolic balance, immune response, structural integrity. Two people identical in all those lower-level properties must be identical in health. You cannot change health without changing something in the base properties. Yet health is not reducible to any single base property; it is a holistic, higher-order characterization that depends on the whole pattern.

Similarly, a crystal’s brittleness supervenes on its atomic structure and bonding. A color supervenes on surface microstructure and lighting conditions. Intelligence supervenes on neural architecture and cognitive organization. Kim’s framework clarifies the structure of relationships among properties: some are fundamental and intrinsic; others are dependent and higher-order. Understanding a thing requires understanding both levels and their dependencies.

¶The Process Tradition: Whitehead to Barad

The substance tradition asks: “What are qualities?” The process tradition asks: “How do qualities become actual?” The answer inverts the dependency.

¶Whitehead’s Eternal Objects and Ingression

Alfred North Whitehead’s Process and Reality (1929) Part III presents the most systematic answer. For Whitehead, qualities are not properties substances have; they are abstract potentials that actual occasions realize.

Whitehead calls these abstract potentials eternal objects. An eternal object is not a Platonic form in the traditional sense. It is pure potential, a pattern or determinant that could be realized in many ways. Redness is an eternal object. So is circularity, the property of being-soluble-in-water, the quality of harshness, the relation of being-larger-than.

An actual occasion is a concrete drop of experience or realization. A person at a specific moment is an actual occasion. When that occasion becomes, it grasps or prehends certain eternal objects out of the infinite multiplicity of possible potentials. It ingresses them — it actualizes them by embodying them. This occasion is not a substance that “has” these qualities; it consists of the eternal objects it realizes, together with its relations to other occasions.

This inverts causality. For substance philosophy, a thing pre-exists its properties; the thing is the ground. For Whitehead, the eternal objects are the ground; the actual occasion is their selection and realization. A person does not come first and then acquire the quality of being-courageous. Rather, in the becoming of this person-occasion, the eternal object “courage” ingresses — becomes part of the occasion’s definiteness.

Consider a musical moment. Traditionally, you might say: here is a moment in time that has the quality of being in C major. Whitehead says: the moment is the realization of the eternal objects {C-major-tonality, this-specific-timbre, this-tempo, this-harmonic-texture}. There is no moment prior to and independent of these ingressed eternal objects. The moment consists of them.

Crucially: when an eternal object ingresses, it is not in the occasion like a property in a substance. The occasion and its ingressed eternal objects are one. The occasion IS the realization of those eternal objects. This is why Whitehead’s framework is process-based: the quality is not something added to a pre-existing thing; the quality is essential to the thing’s coming-to-be.

This has a radical implication: there is no substance apart from its qualities. The “thing itself” does not exist prior to and independently of the qualities it realizes. A person is (at this moment) the bundle of realized potentials: their emotions, their actions, their thoughts, their bodily states, their relations to others. There is no person apart from what they are actualizing.

¶Simondon on Individuation

Gilbert Simondon’s Individuation in Light of Notions of Form and Information (1964, translated 2020) asks: when does an individual come to be?

The traditional answer is: there are individuals (substances), and they have properties. Simondon’s answer is: the individual is not prior to individuation. Individuation is a process, and the individual is what results from that process.

A crystal provides the exemplar. Traditionally, you might say: here is a crystal (substance) with the property of cubic-structure (quality). Simondon says: watch the process. Liquid is cooled. At the phase boundary, nucleation begins. Local order propagates. The structure emerges through the dynamics of the process; it is not imposed on the matter. The structure AND the individual crystal are constituted together through individuation.

Similarly, consider a person’s personality or character. Traditionally, you might say: there is a person who has the property of being courageous or prudent. Simondon says: watch the process. The person encounters challenges, responds, learns, adjusts. Their character emerges through this dynamical process; it is not fixed in advance. The quality of courage does not pre-exist the situation; it emerges in and through the situation and the person’s response.

Applied to qualities more generally: a quality is not a pre-given property of an already-formed individual. Qualities emerge through the individuation process. The quality “hardness” does not pre-exist the crystal; it emerges as the crystal forms. The quality “courage” does not pre-exist the person; it emerges through their actions and choices. Moreover, the individual itself — the crystalline form, the developed character — emerges through the same process.

This dissolves a common philosophical confusion: we sometimes speak as if a thing has a “true nature” or “true quality” underneath its current apparent form. Simondon’s framework suggests this is confused. A thing’s qualities are the phases it is in. There is no truer quality hidden underneath.

¶Deleuze on Intensive and Extensive Qualities

Gilles Deleuze’s Difference and Repetition (1968) distinguishes intensive qualities from extensive quantities.

Intensive qualities (temperature, pressure, intensity, potential) have a peculiar property: they cannot be divided without ceasing to be themselves. Half of a temperature is not a temperature; it is a different temperature. An intensive quality is a gradient; it drives processes. Heat causes things to flow, expand, transform. Intensity is generative — it is the condition of becoming.

Extensive quantities (length, mass, area, number of items) can be divided without loss of kind. Half of 100 meters is still length; it is 50 meters. Extensive quantities are products of processes; they are the result of difference being leveled or integrated.

Consider a musician learning an instrument. Their skill-level is intensive: there is no “half skill” that is still skill. At each moment, the musician has a certain capacity (no sound, weak sound, clear sound, masterful control). Crossing from one threshold to the next is not divisible; it is discontinuous. But the hours practiced is extensive: you can practice half the hours. The number of mistakes made is extensive: you can make half as many errors. Deleuze argues that intensive differences drive the processes (learning, development), while extensive quantities are the result of processes (time spent, errors accumulated). The intensities are primary; the extensities are secondary and derivative.

This matters for understanding change and becoming. All real change, all genuine becoming, is driven by intensive differences. A person’s emotional transformation is driven by intensive differences (confusion → clarity, despair → hope). A social movement’s power is driven by intensive potentials (fervor, collective will). The measurements we take afterward (number of participants, duration, amount of change) are sedimented results.

More radically: intensive and extensive are two different registers of being. Intensive differences are virtual potentials; extensive quantities are actual results. Confusing them is dangerous — it suggests that everything that matters can be measured, counted, divided. But the things that drive actual change (intensity, passion, will, vision) are precisely what cannot be measured.

¶James on Pure Experience

William James’s Essays in Radical Empiricism (1912) argues that the fundamental stuff of reality is not substances or minds but pure experience. Experience comes first. The division into “subject experiencing” and “quality experienced” is secondary, a way of analyzing pure experience after the fact.

A quality is not a property of a thing. A quality is an aspect of pure experience. When I taste bitterness, I do not perceive a substance (the coffee) that has the quality bitter. I experience the bitterness. Bitterness IS an aspect of the experience itself. The substance “coffee” comes later — it is a conceptual abstraction, a way of organizing and naming the flow of experience.

This matters for understanding how we encounter things at all. We do not first perceive an object and then notice it has qualities. We encounter the object as an experienced whole — as bitter or sweet, as heavy or light, as pleasant or repugnant. The qualities are not added to the object; they are the object as experienced. The object-itself (the substance, the thing) is an abstraction from the concrete lived encounter.

This dissolves a classic problem: how do minds know objects? If mind and object are separate, how can they connect? But James’s answer is: they are not separate to begin with. What is primary is the experience itself. Only by analyzing experience after the fact do we extract the divisions: “this is the subject’s experience” versus “this is the object’s property.” In the flow of lived experience, subject and object, quality and substance, are one.

This has radical implications for understanding qualities themselves. Qualities are not mysterious inner essences of things. They are the fundamental fabric of experience. To name and record a quality is to stabilize and share an aspect of lived encounter.

¶Peirce on Firstness

C.S. Peirce’s category of Firstness is the category of quality. Firstness is quality as pure possibility, prior to any actual instantiation or relation. Redness in itself, before anything is red. Hardness in itself, before anything is hard.

Peirce argues that Firstness is real and irreducible. It is not subjective (not “in the mind”) but also not actual (not embodied in any particular thing yet). It exists as pure potential. When a thing becomes red, a First (redness itself) ingresses into actuality.

This is remarkably close to Whitehead’s eternal objects (which Peirce influenced). But Peirce emphasizes that Firstness is prior to individuation. You cannot ask “what is red a property of?” in the realm of Firstness alone. Redness, as a First, is prior to any distinction between “substance” and “property.” Those distinctions only arise at the level of actuality and relation (Secondness and Thirdness).

For frontmatter: this suggests that qualities like “term-ness” or “readiness-phase” exist as pure potentials before being instantiated in actual file-occasions. The potential for a file to be a term, or to reach readiness, is a First — it is a real possibility prior to actualization.

¶Buddhist Abhidharma

Buddhist philosophy, particularly the Abhidharma (analytical exposition of dharma, or elements of existence), offers a process account independent of Western metaphysics. In the Abhidharma, stable objects are illusions. What is real are momentary dharmas — elements of experience arising and ceasing in dependence on conditions.

A “quality” like hardness is not an intrinsic property persisting in a substance. Hardness is a stream of momentary resistances, each arising as one dharma ceases and the next arises. At each instant, a new moment of resistance arises, conditioned by previous moments. The appearance of a persistent, hard object (a stone) is a conventionally constructed stability superimposed on a stream of momentary dharma-instants.

Importantly, dharmas are of different types: form-dharmas (color, shape, resistance), mind-dharmas (perception, feeling, intention), mental-factor dharmas (attention, discernment, resolve), and non-associated dharmas (space, impermanence, cessation). Qualities belong to different types depending on what they are (sensible vs. intentional vs. abstract). There is no single “quality” category; qualities are differentiated by their causal role and type.

Consider a person. Western philosophy asks: what is their essential nature? What persists through change? Buddhist analysis says: nothing persists. What you call a person is actually a stream of momentary experiences (perceptions, feelings, intentions, thoughts, sensations), each arising conditioned by previous moments. The appearance of a unified, persistent self is a construction, useful for coordination but not fundamental.

This applies to qualities. The quality “the person is generous” is not a permanent feature. It is a pattern in a stream of momentary intentions and acts. At each moment, generosity arises or does not, conditioned by what came before. The apparent stability is a name we apply to a pattern, not an intrinsic essence.

This has profound implications: it means all qualities are ultimately conditional and interdependent. Nothing has a quality in isolation or prior to conditions. Qualities arise in dependence on other conditions, and will cease when those conditions cease. This is radically different from substance philosophy, which locates qualities in enduring things.

Yet the Abhidharma is not a denial of qualities. It affirms that qualities are real, that they matter, that they structure experience. It simply insists that they are real as momentary, conditional phenomena, not as properties of enduring substances.

¶Three Fundamental Tensions in Quality Philosophy

These two traditions reveal tensions that any metaphysics must navigate.

¶Tension 1: Observable vs. Intrinsic Properties

The substance tradition distinguishes observable (affective, sensible) qualities from intrinsic capacities and natures. Some qualities are what the senses detect: color, temperature, hardness as felt. Others are structural or causal: a substance’s atomic composition, its capacity to dissolve, its susceptibility to breaking.

But the process tradition asks: what difference does the observable/intrinsic distinction make if qualities are realized potentials? If all properties are actualizations of eternal objects, then the distinction between what-is-observed and what-is-intrinsic becomes secondary. Both are aspects of actualization.

Yet the distinction remains important. Some properties depend on an observer: color depends on light and human vision. Others do not: mass exists whether or not anyone observes it. And some are strange: solubility is a power to dissolve, and you cannot observe a power as such; you observe only the results when conditions are right.

Understanding qualities requires honoring this complexity: some are intrinsic, some observer-dependent, some are powers that manifest only under conditions. None is simply “a quality” in a univocal sense.

¶Tension 2: Static Property vs. Dynamic Phase

Aristotle and Locke assume that qualities are relatively stable. A thing is white, hard, round — these persist (until altered by external force). But Whitehead, Deleuze, and Buddhist philosophy emphasize that qualities are phases in processes, not static states.

Consider a person’s emotional state. Is sadness a stable property they “have,” or a phase they “are going through”? If substance-philosophy, sadness persists as a state until something changes it. If process-philosophy, sadness is a passing phase in an ongoing stream of becoming. The person does not have sadness; the person is-sadness-ing.

This matters because it changes how we understand persistence and change. In substance-view, change means something loses a quality and gains another (like dye changing water’s color). In process-view, change means the process realizes different eternal objects, phases a different potential. Nothing loses or gains; realization simply shifts.

Most real situations involve both: some qualities persist stably (a crystal’s cubic structure, a person’s genetic constitution), while others phase through and transform (moods, fashions, skill levels). A complete metaphysics needs to accommodate both without reducing one to the other.

¶Tension 3: Are Qualities Intrinsic or Relational?

A final tension concerns what makes a quality real. Aristotle says a quality inheres in a substance. It is the substance’s property, intrinsic to that thing alone. But Whitehead and James suggest qualities are relational — they exist in the connection between occasions or between thing and observer.

If redness is a quality of an apple, does it exist intrinsically in the apple (even if no one sees it), or does it exist in the relation between the apple’s surface, light, and human eyes? If courage is a quality of a person, does it exist intrinsically in them, or only in their relations to challenging situations?

Neither extreme satisfies. Pure intrinsicism makes qualities seem mysterious — what are they apart from any manifestation? Pure relationalism makes them seem mind-dependent or context-dependent in ways that seem unstable. Yet the most productive philosophy sits in the tension: qualities have intrinsic bases (the apple’s microstructure, the person’s temperament) but manifest only in relations and contexts.

¶Conclusion

Quality is where ontology meets lived experience. The question “what is a quality?” is not merely academic. It shapes how we understand reality itself, how we describe what we encounter, and how we make sense of change.

The substance tradition teaches that qualities are real, that they are not mere appearance, and that they fall into natural kinds (observable vs. intrinsic, dispositional vs. categorical, primary vs. secondary). It insists that some properties are stable, some are intrinsic to things, and some persist across time.

The process tradition teaches that qualities are realized potentials, that things become through the actualization of abstract possibilities, and that what appears stable is actually a phase of ongoing becoming. It emphasizes that qualities emerge through processes, that intensity drives actuality, and that the real constituents of reality are not substances but occasions, processes, and streams of becoming.

Neither tradition alone suffices. A complete account of qualities must accommodate both:

  • Both enduring properties and transient phases: Some qualities persist stably (a crystal’s structure, a person’s genetic code); others transform continuously (moods, skill, understanding). We need a framework that honors both temporal modes.

  • Both intrinsic and relational aspects: Qualities have bases in things themselves (intrinsic) but manifest only in relations and contexts. This is not a weakness but a feature of reality.

  • Both substance-categories and process-accounts: Aristotle’s four species of quality (habit, capacity, sensible, shape) capture real distinctions within a substance framework. But Whitehead’s eternal objects and Deleuze’s intensive differences capture how qualities exist as potentials prior to and independent of any particular substance.

  • Both determinate and indeterminate qualities: Some qualities are fully actualized and definite. Others are genuinely open, unresolved, waiting for conditions to determine them. This indeterminacy is not epistemic (we just don’t know); it is ontological (the thing itself is not yet fully determined).

The productive question is not “which tradition is right?” but “what situations call for substance-thinking, and what situations call for process-thinking?” In biology, thinking of organisms as substances with properties is useful. In psychology, thinking of persons as processes of becoming is often more illuminating. In understanding change itself, we need both.

This is the philosophical depth of the question: how we answer “what is a quality?” determines what we see when we look at the world.
