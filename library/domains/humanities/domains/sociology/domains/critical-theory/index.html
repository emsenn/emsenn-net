Critical Theory

Critical theory, in its broadest sense, is the tradition of social analysis that does not merely describe social structures but asks whose interests they serve and how they could be otherwise. The Frankfurt School (Horkheimer, Adorno, Marcuse, Habermas) originated the term in the 1930s, fusing Marx’s critique of political economy with Freud’s analysis of the psyche to ask why people consent to domination — why revolution had not come where Marx expected it, and what cultural and psychological mechanisms sustained the existing order.

The tradition extended through Foucault, who displaced the Frankfurt School’s focus on ideology and consciousness with an analysis of how power operates through institutions, knowledge production, and the formation of subjects. Foucault’s concepts — power/knowledge, discourse, biopower, governmentality — provide the analytical vocabulary for understanding how populations are managed not through top-down command but through the production of the categories through which people know themselves and are known.

Povinelli extends this analysis to late liberalism — the phase of liberal governance that manages its own crises through recognition and procedural reform rather than structural change. Her concepts — economies of abandonment, endurance, quasi-events, geontologies — describe how power operates through withdrawal, through the governance of the Life/Nonlife boundary, and through the production of events that look like rupture but function as maintenance.

Both Foucault and Povinelli have their own subdomains here with full term and text collections.

Subdomains

Abolition
The school of thought and practice that argues the carceral state — prisons, police, surveillance — cannot be reformed and must be dismantled, with social needs met through non-punitive structures.
Affect Theory
The school that analyzes how pre-cognitive bodily intensities — tension, ease, attraction, repulsion — organize social and political life in ways that ideology critique and rational-actor models cannot capture.
Anarchism
The school of political thought and practice organized around the rejection of the state and all coercive hierarchy — proposing voluntary association, mutual aid, and direct action as the basis of social organization.
Biopolitics
The lineage of thought analyzing how modern power takes biological life as its object — governing populations through the management of who lives, who dies, and what counts as living.
Black Radical Tradition
The lineage of Black political thought analyzing racial capitalism, dispossession, and resistance — from slave revolt through Du Bois, C.L.R. James, Robinson, Davis, and Gilmore.
Feminism
The school of thought and practice analyzing how gender organizes social life — how patriarchy operates, how it intersects with race, class, and sexuality, and how it is contested.
Foucauldian Analysis
Michel Foucault's analytical framework — power/knowledge, discourse, archaeology, genealogy, biopower, governmentality — for understanding how institutions produce the knowledge, categories, and subjects they govern.
Marxism
The school of social analysis grounded in Marx's critique of political economy — historical materialism, class struggle, the analysis of capitalism as a system of exploitation, and the traditions that extended and contested this analysis.
Postliberalism
The school of thought arguing that liberalism has exhausted itself — from the right (Deneen, Dreher, Vermeule) through a critique of liberal individualism, secularism, and procedural neutrality as corrosive of community, tradition, and meaning.
Povinellian Analysis
Elizabeth Povinelli's analytical framework — late liberalism, economies of abandonment, endurance, quasi-events, geontologies, geontopower — for understanding how liberal governance operates through withdrawal, ontological classification, and the management of the Life/Nonlife boundary.
Queer Theory
The school of critical theory analyzing how heterosexuality is normalized, how gender and sexuality are produced through performance and regulation, and how queer existence contests the structures organized around reproductive futurity.
Situationist International
The school originating with Guy Debord — the critique of spectacular society, the analysis of recuperation, and the practice of détournement.
