Disaster Response

The sociological analysis of how communities respond to catastrophe — not as a technical problem of logistics but as a political question of who organizes response, for whom, and on what terms. The library’s content focuses on emergent disaster response: the self-organized, mutual-aid-based responses that arise from affected communities themselves, as distinct from top-down institutional response.

The domain covers the history and practice of mutual aid disaster relief, the centros de apoyo mutuo (mutual aid centers) that emerged in Puerto Rico after Hurricane María, anti-colonial reconstruction as a framework for rebuilding that refuses to reproduce the colonial conditions that made the community vulnerable in the first place, and dual-power disaster relief that builds autonomous infrastructure capable of operating independently of the state.

This is one of the library’s largest domains, reflecting the 561 Group’s practical engagement with disaster response and community resilience.

Central thesis

The sociology of disaster response analyzes catastrophe as a political question rather than a technical one — who organizes response, for whom, and on what terms — emphasizing emergent, mutual-aid-based, and anti-colonial models that operate outside or against state frameworks.
