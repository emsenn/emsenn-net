Ethics of Presence

2026-03-30 Evaluating action by its adequacy to present conditions and existing relations rather than by its contribution to a projected future — the temporal dimension of relational ontology.

An ethics of presence evaluates action by its adequacy to the present situation — the needs, relations, and constraints of this moment — rather than by its contribution toward a projected future or its conformity to an inherited ideal. You act for the relations that exist, not for the world you hope to build.

The concept draws on two converging sources. Vine Deloria Jr. contrasts Indigenous spatial-ethical thought with Western linear futurity: where Western political frameworks organize action around a timeline — progress, development, the arc of history — Indigenous thought grounds obligation in place and present relationship. The relevant question is not “does this advance our goals?” but “does this answer what the situation requires?” Glen Coulthard’s recognition refusal operates from the same temporal ground: Indigenous peoples need not orient their governance toward a settler-defined future in order for that governance to be real.

Anarcho-nihilism arrives at a parallel position from a different direction. Serafinski’s analysis of concentration camp resistance in Blessed Is the Flame shows that the Sonderkommando who revolted at Auschwitz did not act for a future they expected to inhabit. Their action was adequate to their present — the intolerability of domination in the moment was sufficient ground. The ethics of presence names this temporal structure: action complete in itself, not waiting for a future that justifies it.

Under collapse conditions, an ethics of presence has specific adaptive value. Frameworks that organize action around future outcomes — the cooperative society to come, the policy reform that will eventually arrive, the movement that will grow large enough to matter — are structurally vulnerable to the loss of that future. When ecological disruption, institutional failure, or violence forecloses the projected timeline, these frameworks lose their basis for action. An ethics of presence does not share this vulnerability because it was never organized around a destination. The present provides its own ground.

This does not mean indifference to consequences. It means that consequences are evaluated within the horizon of existing relations rather than against a timeline extending beyond them. You fortify the shelter because elders need it now, not because doing so contributes to a future movement. You preserve seeds because the land requires continuity, not because a plan says to. The action is its own justification — not because outcomes are irrelevant, but because the relevant outcomes are those the present situation makes visible.
