Genealogy

2026-01-01 Foucault's (after Nietzsche) method of tracing how present practices emerged from contingent historical struggles — exposing the power relations that produced what appears natural or necessary.

Genealogy is a method for tracing how present-day concepts, institutions, and practices emerged through contingency, conflict, and power rather than through rational development or natural necessity. Where most histories explain the present as the outcome of progress or design, genealogy shows that the present is the product of struggles that could have gone otherwise.

Friedrich Nietzsche originated the method in On the Genealogy of Morals (1887). He asked where the concepts “good” and “evil” came from. His answer was that “good” originally meant “noble” — a term the powerful used to describe themselves — and “evil” was the invention of the powerless, who revalued strength as morally corrupt. Moral concepts did not descend from universal reason. They descended from specific historical confrontations between groups with competing interests.

Foucault adopted this approach and applied it to institutions. In “Nietzsche, Genealogy, History” (1971) he formalized two concepts from Nietzsche and added a critical refusal. Descent (Herkunft) names the multiple, tangled sources that feed into a practice. What appears unified has heterogeneous origins. The modern prison did not come from one reformer’s plan — it drew on military drill, monastic timetables, hospital case files, and factory management, different practices developed for different purposes and later assembled into one institution. Emergence (Entstehung) names the specific confrontation or institutional shift that brought a concept or practice into being. Emergence is not invention by a visionary. It is the moment when competing forces produce something new as a byproduct of their struggle. The clinical examination emerged not from a physician’s insight but from the intersection of hospital administration, medical training reform, and state interest in population health. The refusal of origins (Ursprung) completes the method: genealogy refuses to explain a practice by identifying the moment someone designed it to solve a problem. Origin stories treat the present as inevitable. Genealogy insists it is not.

Foucault applied the method most fully in Discipline and Punish (1975) and The History of Sexuality (1976). In each case he showed that what appears natural — the prison as the obvious response to crime, sexuality as a biological given — is the product of identifiable institutional struggles. Where archaeology maps the rules of a discourse at a given moment, genealogy asks how those rules came to be and what forces sustain them.
