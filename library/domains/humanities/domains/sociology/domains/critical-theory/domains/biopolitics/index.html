Biopolitics

The lineage of thought originating in Foucault’s analysis of biopower — the form of governance that takes biological life itself as its object. Foucault identified the shift from sovereign power (the right to take life or let live) to biopower (the right to make live and let die) as the defining transformation of modern governance. The tradition that followed extended and contested this framework in five directions, each asking a different question about the governance of life and death.

Agamben asks: what is life stripped of political standing? His analysis of bare life — the homo sacer who can be killed but not sacrificed — reveals the sovereign exception as the hidden foundation of modern politics. Esposito asks: what happens when the community’s mechanisms of protection turn against the community itself? His immunitary paradigm shows how security logic becomes auto-immune. Butler asks: whose life is recognized as worth mourning? Her analysis of grievability shows how media frames and state rhetoric determine in advance which lives register as lost. Mbembe asks: how does sovereignty operate through the production of death itself? His concept of necropolitics names governance that works through killing rather than through the management of life. Povinelli asks: what governance precedes the Life/Nonlife distinction? Her geontologies reveals that biopolitics presupposes a prior sorting of the world into what can suffer and what cannot.

Each of these thinkers has a subdomain here with their own terms and texts.

Central thesis

Biopolitics names the form of modern governance that takes biological life itself as its object, and the tradition extending Foucault’s analysis asks five complementary questions: what is life stripped of political standing (Agamben), how does protection turn auto-immune (Esposito), whose life is grievable (Butler), how does sovereignty work through death (Mbembe), and what governance precedes the Life/Nonlife distinction (Povinelli).
