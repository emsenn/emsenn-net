Recognition Refusal

2026-03-30 The rejection of settler-colonial frameworks of recognition as the condition of Indigenous political existence — refusing to make Indigenous governance legible to the colonizer in order to be granted legitimacy.

Recognition refusal is the rejection of the demand that Indigenous peoples legitimate their governance, knowledge, and sovereignty by translating them into terms the settler-colonial state can recognize. Glen Coulthard (Yellowknives Dene) develops the concept in Red Skin, White Masks (2014), drawing on and inverting Frantz Fanon’s analysis of colonial recognition in Black Skin, White Masks (1952).

Fanon argued that the colonized subject who seeks recognition from the colonizer enters a trap: the terms of recognition are set by the colonizer, and what is recognized is not the colonized subject as they are but a version of them that the colonial framework can accommodate. Recognition does not end domination; it reformats it. The colonized subject becomes legible to the colonial order — and legibility, as Scott’s work shows, is the precondition for administration.

Coulthard applies this to the Canadian settler-colonial context. Liberal multiculturalism offers Indigenous peoples “recognition” — land claims processes, self-government agreements, cultural heritage protections — but on terms that presuppose the legitimacy of the Canadian state, the sovereignty of the Crown, and the evaluative framework of liberal-democratic governance. To be recognized, Indigenous governance must be translated into the language of rights, constitutionalism, and institutional procedure. What cannot survive that translation — governance grounded in specific relationships to land, ceremonial authority, oral tradition, and kinship obligation — is either excluded or reduced to cultural decoration.

Recognition refusal says: we do not need your recognition. Indigenous governance does not derive its legitimacy from the settler state’s acknowledgment. Grounded normativity — the ethical and political knowledge that arises from Indigenous relationships to specific places and practices — is self-legitimating. It does not require translation into universalist terms to be real.

Leanne Betasamosake Simpson’s As We Have Always Done (2017) develops the constructive dimension: if recognition is refused, what replaces it is not withdrawal but resurgence — the practice of Indigenous life, governance, and knowledge on Indigenous terms, grounded in the relations that sustain them. Resurgence does not seek a place within the settler framework; it operates outside it, generating political life from its own relational ground.

The concept connects directly to the critique of epistemic universalism. The demand for recognition is a demand that Indigenous knowledge pass through a universalist evaluative framework — that it be articulable, assessable, and ratifiable in terms accessible to settler institutions. Recognition refusal is the insistence that this demand is itself colonial. Opacity — the right not to be made transparent — is the epistemological face of recognition refusal.
