Anarchism

Anarchism is the political tradition organized around the rejection of the state, coercive hierarchy, and all institutions that maintain domination through force, law, or the threat of either. It proposes voluntary association, mutual aid, direct action, and prefigurative politics — the practice of building the world you want within the shell of the world that exists — as the basis of social organization.

The tradition is broader than its popular image as bomb-throwing chaos. Kropotkin grounded it in evolutionary biology, arguing that mutual aid is as fundamental to species survival as competition. Goldman grounded it in the lived experience of workers, women, and immigrants under industrial capitalism. Malatesta grounded it in the organizational question: how do people coordinate without command? Contemporary anarchism draws on Indigenous traditions of non-state governance, on queer and feminist critiques of the family as a site of domination, and on ecological thought that recognizes the state as structurally incapable of responding to environmental crisis.

The library’s anarchism domain is one of the largest in sociology, with subdomains covering communication practices, anarcho-nihilism, green anarchism, Indigenous anarchism, insurrectionary anarchism, organizational theory, the analysis of the state, strategic thought, and youth liberation. Each subdomain has its own terms and texts.

Central thesis

Anarchism treats the state and all coercive hierarchy as illegitimate, proposing voluntary association, mutual aid, direct action, and prefigurative politics — building the world you want within the shell of the world that exists — as the basis of social organization.
