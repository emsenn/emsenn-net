Decolonial Marxism

The school of Marxist analysis that takes colonialism not as a side effect of capitalist expansion but as constitutive of capitalism itself. European capitalism did not develop and then colonize — it developed through colonization. The primitive accumulation that Marx analyzed as the origin of capital was, concretely, the dispossession of Indigenous peoples, the enslavement of Africans, and the extraction of colonial wealth. Decolonial Marxism insists that any analysis of capitalism that brackets colonialism and race is analyzing an abstraction, not the actual system.

Fanon’s The Wretched of the Earth (1961) provides the sharpest formulation: the colonial situation produces its own forms of consciousness, violence, and liberation that European Marxist categories cannot capture without transformation. The colonized subject is not a proletarian in exotic dress — the structure of colonial domination (racial hierarchy, spatial segregation, epistemic violence, the production of the “native” as a category) requires analysis on its own terms. Mariátegui arrived at a parallel position from Peru in the 1920s, insisting that Latin American socialism must be grounded in Indigenous communal traditions, not imported wholesale from European theory.

Amilcar Cabral extended the analysis into revolutionary practice, arguing that national liberation is necessarily an act of culture — the reassertion of the colonized people’s capacity to make their own history after colonialism has declared them historyless.
