Settler Colonialism

Settler colonialism is not a historical event that happened and ended. It is an ongoing structure — a set of relationships, legal frameworks, and land arrangements that persist because settlers came not to extract and leave (as in extractive colonialism) but to stay and replace. Patrick Wolfe’s formulation is definitive: settler colonialism is organized around the logic of elimination. The goal is not the exploitation of Indigenous labor (though that occurs) but the removal of Indigenous peoples from the land so that settlers can claim it as their own.

This elimination takes many forms: genocide, forced removal, residential schools, blood quantum laws, the termination of tribal status, the conversion of collectively held land into individual private property, and the ongoing refusal to honor treaty obligations. Each of these is a mechanism of the same structure. The United States, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and Israel/Palestine are all settler-colonial formations, and the structure is not historical — it is the present arrangement of land, law, and sovereignty.

Tuck and Yang’s “Decolonization Is Not a Metaphor” (2012) insists that decolonization means the repatriation of Indigenous land and life — not a metaphor for other forms of justice, and not achievable through curricular reform, diversity initiatives, or settler guilt.

Central thesis

Settler colonialism is an ongoing structure organized around the logic of elimination — settlers came not to extract and leave but to stay and replace, and the dispossession of Indigenous peoples is not a historical event that ended but the present arrangement of land, law, and sovereignty.
