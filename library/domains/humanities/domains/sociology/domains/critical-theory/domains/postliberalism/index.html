Postliberalism

Postliberalism argues that liberalism has exhausted itself — that its core commitments (individual autonomy, procedural neutrality, the separation of public and private, the market as the organizing principle of social life) have produced the very conditions they were supposed to prevent: atomization, meaninglessness, the dissolution of community, and the inability to sustain the moral foundations on which liberal institutions depend.

The tradition is primarily right-wing in its current form. Patrick Deneen’s Why Liberalism Failed (2018) argues that liberalism did not fail to deliver on its promises — it succeeded, and the success is the problem. Liberal individualism dissolved the communal bonds that gave life meaning. Liberal secularism evacuated the public sphere of the moral frameworks that made shared life possible. Liberal procedural neutrality is not neutral — it actively destroys the substantive traditions it claims to leave undisturbed.

Postliberalism is distinct from emsenn’s cybernetic postliberalism, which analyzes the same exhaustion from a left/critical perspective rather than a communitarian/traditionalist one. Both diagnose liberalism’s crisis. They disagree about what produced it and what should follow.

Central thesis

Postliberalism argues that liberalism has exhausted itself — its core commitments to individual autonomy, procedural neutrality, and the market as organizing principle have dissolved the communal and moral foundations on which liberal institutions themselves depend.
