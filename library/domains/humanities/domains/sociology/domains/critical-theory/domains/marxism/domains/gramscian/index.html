Gramscian Analysis

The school of Marxist social analysis developed from Antonio Gramsci’s prison writings (1929–1935). Gramsci’s central question: why did revolution succeed in Russia but fail in Western Europe, where capitalism was more developed and the working class more organized? His answer: in the West, ruling-class domination operates not primarily through force (the state, the army, the police) but through hegemony — the production of consent through civil society (schools, churches, media, cultural institutions).

Hegemony is not brainwashing. It is the process by which the interests of the ruling class come to appear as common sense — as the natural way things are — so that dominated groups participate in their own domination not because they are deceived but because the alternatives seem unthinkable. The organic intellectual, in Gramsci’s framework, is the thinker who emerges from a social group and articulates that group’s experience in ways that challenge the ruling hegemony — not by imposing theory from outside but by making explicit the knowledge that is already embedded in the group’s practice.

Gramscian analysis has been extended by Stuart Hall (race and cultural studies), Ernesto Laclau and Chantal Mouffe (post-Marxist discourse theory), and the broader tradition of cultural studies that emerged from the Birmingham Centre.

Central thesis

Gramscian analysis holds that ruling-class domination in developed capitalism operates not primarily through force but through hegemony — the production of consent through civil society such that the interests of the ruling class come to appear as common sense.
