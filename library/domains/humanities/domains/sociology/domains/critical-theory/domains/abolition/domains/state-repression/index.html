State Repression

The sociological analysis of how states suppress dissent, resistance, and organizing. This covers the mechanisms (surveillance, infiltration, prosecution, criminalization, COINTELPRO-style disruption), the legal infrastructure (conspiracy law, RICO, anti-terrorism statutes, grand jury abuse), and the political logic (why states target specific movements and how repression reshapes the movements it targets).

State repression is not a failure of liberal governance but a feature of it. The same legal frameworks that protect rights also produce the mechanisms for their suspension. The state of exception — the suspension of law in the name of security — is the structure through which liberal democracies repress without formally abandoning their commitment to rights. The analysis of state repression requires attention to both the spectacular (arrests, raids, assassinations) and the ordinary (surveillance, infiltration, the slow attrition of movement capacity through legal harassment).

Central thesis

The analysis of state repression treats it not as a failure of liberal governance but as a feature of it — the same legal frameworks that protect rights produce the mechanisms for their suspension, and the state of exception is the structure through which liberal democracies suppress dissent without formally abandoning their commitment to rights.
