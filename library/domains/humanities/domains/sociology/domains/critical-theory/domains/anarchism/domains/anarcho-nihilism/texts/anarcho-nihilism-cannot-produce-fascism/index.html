Anarcho-Nihilism Cannot Produce Fascism

2026-03-31 A criterion-by-criterion demonstration that anarcho-nihilism is structurally incompatible with every necessary condition of fascist formation — ideational, processual, institutional, economic, biopolitical, and aesthetic.

Abstract

Fascism is a determinate phenomenon with necessary conditions. It requires palingenetic ultranationalism, mass mobilization through identifiable stages, an exceptional state form fusing party and sovereignty, corporatist reorganization of capital, biopolitical population management, and the sacralization of political life into civic religion. These conditions are not optional features; they are the structural minimum. A formation that lacks any of them is not fascism.

Anarcho-nihilism cannot satisfy any of them. Not one. Its refusal of teleology dissolves the rebirth myth. Its refusal of mass unity prevents movement formation. Its refusal of sovereignty prevents state capture. Its refusal of identity hierarchy prevents biopolitical sorting. Its refusal of sacrality prevents political religion. These are not contingent absences — they are structural consequences of anarcho-nihilism’s core commitments. The framework does not merely fail to produce fascism; it actively dissolves the metaphysical supports fascism requires.

The accusation that anarcho-nihilism enables fascism rests on a conflation: both reject liberalism, therefore they must be aligned. The conflation collapses under examination. Fascism rejects liberalism in order to purify the nation. Anarcho-nihilism rejects liberalism in order to dissolve domination. These are opposite operations performed on the same target.

1. Introduction

The accusation is common enough to require a systematic answer: that rejecting universal moral frameworks leaves an ethical void that fascism fills. That nihilism — even qualified as anarcho-nihilism — is permissive toward domination because it refuses the moral architecture that would prohibit it. That without transcendent values, anything is permitted, and what is most likely to be permitted is the worst.

The accusation is wrong, and it is wrong in a way that can be demonstrated rather than argued. Fascism is not a mood or an aesthetic or a disposition toward cruelty. It is a political formation with identifiable structural requirements. Those requirements can be specified. Anarcho-nihilism’s commitments can be specified. The two sets can be compared. The comparison yields a definitive result: structural incompatibility at every point of contact.

The operational definition of fascism used here follows the synthetic framework developed in Describing Fascism, integrating Griffin’s ideational core, Paxton’s processual stages, Poulantzas and Neumann’s analysis of the exceptional capitalist state, Foucault’s biopolitics, and Gentile’s account of political sacralization. Each domain specifies a necessary condition. The test is whether anarcho-nihilism can satisfy any of them.

2. What Fascism Requires

Fascism is the simultaneous activation of at least six structural domains. A formation that activates some but not others is authoritarian, or populist, or theocratic, or something else — but it is not fascism. The domains are:

Palingenetic ultranationalism. Griffin’s sine qua non: a myth of national rebirth after a period of decline. The nation was pure, fell into decadence, and will be restored through struggle and purification. This myth fuses nostalgia with utopian futurity. It converts diffuse resentment into total political mobilization. Without it, fascism has no ideological engine.

Mass mobilization through developmental stages. Paxton identifies a sequence: movement formation, rooting in crisis, seizure of power through elite alliance, consolidation, radicalization. Each stage requires disciplined cadres, paramilitary capacity, vanguard leadership, and the transformation of crisis into political identity. Fascism is not a coup; it is a process that requires a mass base.

The exceptional state. Poulantzas and Neumann describe fascism’s institutional form: fusion of party and state, destruction of mediating institutions, charismatic sovereignty, bureaucratic polycracy, rule by decree, permanent state of exception. The state becomes the instrument of the rebirth myth. Separation of powers is not suspended temporarily but abolished structurally.

Corporatist reorganization of capital. Neumann’s analysis of the Nazi political economy: destruction of independent labor, state-directed private capitalism, cartelization, profit guarantees for aligned capital, militarized investment. Fascism does not abolish capitalism; it reorganizes it into a war economy under state direction, with capital subordinated to the national project but guaranteed its returns.

Biopolitical population management. Foucault’s framework, extended by scholars of settler colonialism: racial hierarchy encoded in law, eugenics, demographic engineering, reproductive discipline, extermination of populations coded as pathological. Fascism sorts bodies into those worthy of the nation’s future and those that contaminate it. This sorting is not incidental; it is the biopolitical mechanism through which the rebirth myth is made material.

Sacralization of politics. Gentile’s analysis: fascism constructs a civic religion centered on leader, nation, and sacrifice. Politics becomes liturgy. Rallies, parades, uniforms, salutes, and public spectacles convert citizens into participants in mythic destiny. Obedience becomes worship. The aesthetic dimension is not decoration; it is the mechanism through which the rebirth myth achieves emotional coherence.

These six domains constitute a falsifiable framework. Each can be operationally specified and empirically tested. Fascism is present when they cohere. It is absent when any necessary domain is absent.

3. What Anarcho-Nihilism Commits To

Anarcho-nihilism is not a void. It is a set of positive commitments whose content can be specified as precisely as fascism’s requirements. The framework, developed most explicitly in Serafinski’s Blessed Is the Flame (2016) and extended through Indigenous relational ontologies, commits to:

  • Refusal of teleology: action is grounded in the present, not oriented toward a projected future.
  • Refusal of sovereignty: no institution, no leader, no state has legitimate authority to command.
  • Refusal of essentialized identity: no group defined by race, nation, or biology has ontological standing as a collective subject.
  • Dissolution of domination: all forms of hierarchical power are opposed, not in the name of a better hierarchy but in the name of their abolition.
  • Grounding of ethics in situated relations: obligations arise from kinship, place, and relational embeddedness — not from universal frameworks.
  • Ethics of presence: action is evaluated by its adequacy to present conditions, not by its contribution to a future state.
  • Opacity as legitimate practice: bounded knowledge and relational decision-making are not failures of transparency but forms of protection.

These are not absences. They are structural features with specific consequences for what the framework can and cannot produce.

4. The Incompatibility

4.1 No nation, no rebirth, no purity

Fascism’s ideological engine is palingenetic ultranationalism: a sacred people fell from grace and will be reborn through struggle. Every other domain depends on this myth. The exceptional state serves the rebirth. The corporatist economy funds it. The biopolitical sorting purifies for it. The civic religion sacralizes it.

Anarcho-nihilism dissolves every element this myth requires. There is no nation — no collective subject with ontological standing as a unified body. There is no purity — no original state from which the people have fallen. There is no rebirth — no destination the present is heading toward. There is no sacred identity — no category of person whose inclusion or exclusion determines the community’s worth.

Without the rebirth myth, the other five domains have no ideological engine. You cannot mobilize masses for a rebirth that no one believes in. You cannot build an exceptional state to serve a nation that does not exist. You cannot sort bodies for purity when purity is not a category. The entire formation loses its organizing center.

4.2 No mass movement, no developmental stages

Paxton’s stages require organized masses: cadres, paramilitaries, rallies, a leadership structure that transforms crisis into political identity. Fascism needs people marching in the same direction, wearing the same symbols, subordinating individual judgment to collective will.

Anarcho-nihilism structurally prevents this. It rejects mass unity — not because it dislikes people but because it recognizes that unified mass formation is a mechanism of domination. It rejects vanguard leadership, disciplined hierarchy, and institutional rooting. It does not produce cadres; it produces kin networks. It does not stage rallies; it strengthens relational ties. The organizational form required for Paxton’s stages cannot emerge from a framework that refuses every element those stages require.

4.3 No sovereignty, no exceptional state

The fascist state requires sovereignty: a decision-maker who can declare the exception, suspend law, and fuse party with state apparatus. Schmitt’s friend-enemy distinction, the Führerprinzip, rule by decree — all presuppose a sovereign position that anarcho-nihilism refuses to acknowledge as legitimate.

Anarcho-nihilism does not merely oppose the current occupant of the sovereign position. It denies that the position exists. There is no one who should rule by decree. There is no party that should fuse with the state. There is no exception that should be declared. The institutional architecture fascism requires — centralized executive supremacy, judicial subordination, militarized administration — presupposes a center of authority that anarcho-nihilism has structurally eliminated.

4.4 No corporatist economy

Fascist economics requires the state to reorganize capital: destroy independent labor, direct private investment, guarantee profits for aligned industries, and militarize production. This requires a state powerful enough to discipline capital and a political movement willing to align with capitalist interests in exchange for control.

Anarcho-nihilism has no economic program, no alignment with capital, no state apparatus to direct investment, and no interest in industrial modernization as destiny. It does not seek to reorganize capitalism; it refuses the categories (state, nation, productive destiny) through which fascist reorganization operates. The corporatist bargain — the state protects capital in exchange for capital serving the national project — cannot be struck by a framework that acknowledges neither the state nor the national project.

4.5 No biopolitical sorting

Fascism’s biopolitics requires a hierarchy of bodies: those fit for the nation’s future and those that contaminate it. Racial purification, eugenics, demographic engineering, and extermination of pathologized populations all presuppose that human worth is determined by categorical membership — race, blood, reproductive fitness, national belonging.

Anarcho-nihilism dissolves these categories. It denies biological essentialism. It refuses demographic futurity — the claim that the nation’s survival depends on managing which bodies reproduce. It recognizes obligation across difference through co-equal kinship: all relations possess inherent worth without requiring identity conformity. The biopolitical machine requires a sorting criterion — who belongs, who doesn’t — and anarcho-nihilism provides no criterion because it rejects the premise that belonging should be sorted.

4.6 No sacred politics

Gentile’s civic religion requires that politics become liturgy: leader worship, sacrificial narrative, aesthetic unity, the conversion of citizens into ritual participants. Fascism sacralizes the political by investing the nation and its leader with transcendent significance.

Anarcho-nihilism refuses sacrality. Not because it is against meaning — relational life is saturated with meaning — but because it refuses the operation by which political authority is made sacred. No leader incarnates the people’s will. No rally enacts collective destiny. No sacrifice redeems the nation. The specific mechanism by which fascism converts political power into religious experience requires a willingness to invest authority with transcendent significance, and anarcho-nihilism treats that investment as domination.

5. Why the Confusion Persists

The accusation that anarcho-nihilism enables fascism survives because of two structural features of Western political epistemology.

Non-universalism is read as immorality. Western moral philosophy from Kant through Rawls equates ethics with universal rules: moral claims are valid insofar as they apply to everyone. A framework that grounds obligation in situated relations rather than universal principles appears, within this epistemology, to have no ethics at all. If your ethics are not universal, you have no ethics. If you have no ethics, anything is permitted. If anything is permitted, fascism is permitted.

The error is in the first step. Epistemic universalism is not the only source of moral constraint. Relational ontologies produce constraints that are binding, specific, and often more demanding than universal rules — because they arise from actual relationships rather than abstract principles. The grandmother you are obligated to care for produces a more binding constraint than the categorical imperative. But this constraint is not visible to a framework that can only recognize obligation in universal form.

Both reject liberalism — for opposite reasons. Fascism and anarcho-nihilism both hold liberal proceduralism in contempt. Fascism rejects it because liberalism is too weak to purify the nation. Anarcho-nihilism rejects it because liberalism is a form of domination that epistemic universalism makes invisible. The surface overlap — shared disdain for liberal institutions — obscures an absolute structural opposition. Fascism wants a stronger state. Anarcho-nihilism wants no state. Fascism wants a purer nation. Anarcho-nihilism wants no nation. Fascism wants a sacred leader. Anarcho-nihilism wants no leader.

The confusion is possible only if you define political positions by what they oppose rather than by what they are. Once you specify the positive commitments of each framework, the opposition is total.

6. Conclusion

Fascism requires teleology. Anarcho-nihilism refuses it. Fascism requires a sacred nation. Anarcho-nihilism dissolves it. Fascism requires mass mobilization. Anarcho-nihilism prevents it. Fascism requires sovereignty. Anarcho-nihilism denies it. Fascism requires biopolitical hierarchy. Anarcho-nihilism dissolves it. Fascism requires sacralized politics. Anarcho-nihilism refuses it.

The incompatibility is not contingent — a matter of different priorities that might shift under different conditions. It is structural. Anarcho-nihilism’s core commitments negate the necessary conditions of fascist formation at every point. The framework does not merely happen to be non-fascist; it is constitutively anti-fascist, in the precise sense that its positive commitments dissolve the metaphysical, institutional, and affective infrastructure that fascism requires in order to exist.

The accusation persists because Western political epistemology cannot recognize relational ethics as ethics. Once that limitation is identified, the accusation collapses. What remains is a clear theoretical boundary: anarcho-nihilism is not permissive authoritarianism. It is an anti-dominating ontology that is incompatible with fascism at the level of structure, not merely at the level of preference.
