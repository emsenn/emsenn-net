Auto-Immunity

2026-03-06 The condition in which a system's mechanisms of self-protection turn against the system itself — defense that becomes destruction, preservation that produces the threat it was designed to prevent.
Defines Auto-Immunity, immunitary logic

Auto-immunity, in its philosophical sense, names the condition in which a system’s mechanisms of self-protection turn against the system itself — defense that becomes destruction, preservation that produces the threat it was designed to prevent. The concept draws on immunology but has been developed independently by Roberto Esposito and Jacques Derrida as a framework for understanding politics, community, and meaning.

Esposito, in Immunitas: The Protection and Negation of Life (2002), argues that modern political life is structured by immunitary logic: the community protects itself by incorporating controlled doses of the threat it fears. Vaccination is the paradigm — introducing a weakened form of the pathogen to produce resistance. The logic extends to politics: quarantine, deterrence, border control, and preemptive war all work by internalizing danger as a condition of security. The community that fears external contamination builds walls, screens, and checkpoints. The community that fears internal dissent develops surveillance, loyalty tests, and disciplinary institutions. In each case, the mechanism of protection requires the presence of the threat within the body being protected.

When this logic intensifies, it becomes auto-immune: the community’s defenses consume the community itself. Every safeguard injures; every protection erodes what it protects. The surveillance state intended to protect civil liberties destroys them. The military buildup intended to ensure peace produces the conditions for war. The immune system intended to protect the organism attacks the organism’s own tissue. Esposito argues that this is not a malfunction but the logical terminus of immunitary logic carried to its conclusion: a system that can only protect itself by incorporating the threat will, at sufficient intensity, become indistinguishable from the threat.

Derrida developed a parallel concept in his late political work, particularly Rogues (2003) and “Faith and Knowledge” (1998). For Derrida, auto-immunity names the structural vulnerability that belongs to any system that claims sovereignty or self-sufficiency. Democracy is auto-immune: it must protect itself against forces that would destroy it, but the mechanisms of that protection (censorship, exclusion, emergency powers) undermine the openness that makes democracy democratic. The system cannot defend itself without damaging itself. This is not a contingent political failure but a structural necessity — the condition of possibility for democracy is also the condition of its vulnerability.
