Emotional labor

2026-04-27 The labor of producing or suppressing feelings to meet the affective expectations of an institution, role, or audience — distinct from physical or cognitive labor in that what is being shaped is the worker's own feeling-state. Coined by Arlie Russell Hochschild in *The Managed Heart* (1983) to analyze the affective work of service-sector employees.

Emotional labor is the labor of producing or suppressing feelings to meet the affective expectations of an institution, role, or audience. What is being shaped is not just an external display but the worker’s own feeling-state. The concept was coined by Arlie Russell Hochschild in The Managed Heart (1983) to analyze the affective work of service-sector employees, particularly flight attendants whose job required them to cultivate warmth and patience continuously across long shifts under often-hostile conditions.

¶Etymology and conceptual lineage

The term combines emotional (relating to feelings) with labor (work that produces value, typically for compensation). The juxtaposition is intentional: feeling, on Hochschild’s account, is not a private matter that workers happen to bring to the job; under modern service-economy conditions, the management of feeling itself is part of what workers are paid to do. The labor is real; it produces value; it is exhausting.

Hochschild builds on Erving Goffman’s (1959) dramaturgical analysis of impression management. Goffman showed that everyday social interaction involves performance — front-stage display calibrated to audience expectations. Hochschild extends this in two ways. First, she insists that under service-sector conditions the performance is not just front-stage display but deep acting: the worker is required to actually feel what they display, not merely show it convincingly. Second, she names the resulting work as labor in the political-economic sense, with implications for analyses of exploitation, compensation, and burnout.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a working condition as requiring emotional labor.

Feeling is part of the job. The worker is required, as a condition of employment, to produce particular feelings (warmth, calm, sympathy, enthusiasm, deference) or to suppress others (anger, frustration, fear, contempt). The requirement is not a side-effect of the role; it is integral to the worker’s effective performance.

Surface acting and deep acting. Hochschild distinguishes surface acting (displaying a feeling without producing it internally) from deep acting (cultivating the feeling internally so the display is genuine). Modern service work increasingly demands deep acting: the customer can detect surface-acting and registers it as inauthentic, so the worker must actually feel the prescribed feeling to perform competently.

Cumulative cost to the worker. Sustained emotional labor produces specific costs: emotional dissonance (the gap between what one feels privately and what one is required to feel professionally), feeling-rule fatigue, and what Hochschild calls estrangement from one’s own emotional life. Over time, the worker may lose the ability to distinguish their own feelings from the feelings the job requires.

¶Examples

The flight attendant (Hochschild’s case). Hochschild studied trainee flight attendants at Delta Airlines. The training emphasized that flight attendants must cultivate, not merely display, calm and warmth toward passengers — including hostile or drunken ones. The trainees were taught techniques for deep acting: visualize a passenger as a frightened child, recall family-member-relationship dynamics, manage one’s own arousal in real time. The training was systematic, the labor was substantial, and Hochschild documented the costs: depersonalization, emotional exhaustion, and a recurring difficulty separating professional from personal feelings.

Care work. Nurses, eldercare workers, teachers, social workers, and others in care professions perform emotional labor as a major component of their work. The labor is compounded by the physical and cognitive demands of the role; it is often expected to be performed without explicit acknowledgment as labor (because care is treated as natural rather than waged); and it is heavily gendered.

Service-sector frontline work. Retail clerks, food-service workers, hospitality workers, and call-center operators perform emotional labor under direct surveillance (often through customer-feedback metrics). The labor is sometimes scripted in detail and the worker’s emotional state is monitored through the feedback metrics. Ehrenreich (2001) documented the conditions in which this labor compounds physical exploitation in U.S. low-wage work.

Domestic labor. Hochschild’s The Second Shift (1989) extended the analysis to unpaid household labor. The work of managing a household’s emotional climate — supporting children, sustaining a marriage, maintaining extended-family relationships — is emotional labor performed without wage, often by women, and frequently invisible as labor.

¶Adjacent concepts

Emotional labor is not the same as emotional intelligence in the Goleman sense. Emotional intelligence is a capacity (an ability to perceive, regulate, and use emotions); emotional labor is what the capacity is put to work doing. Emotional intelligence is supposedly a property of the worker; emotional labor is a relation between worker, employer, and audience.

Emotional labor is not the same as affective labor in Hardt’s (1999) autonomist-Marxist sense, though they overlap substantially. Hardt’s concept generalizes Hochschild’s to all post-Fordist immaterial labor that produces affective states (entertainment, care, advertising, social-media-platform engagement). Hardt’s category is broader; Hochschild’s is more specific and grounded in particular service-sector cases.

Emotional labor is not the same as emotion work. Hochschild herself distinguishes emotional labor (which is performed for a wage in commercial contexts) from emotion work (which is performed in private, non-commercial settings — friendships, families, communities). The distinction is analytical rather than hard, but it allows the political-economic analysis of the commercial form to remain sharp.

¶Open questions

How does emotional labor change under platform-mediated conditions? Gig-platform workers (rideshare drivers, food delivery couriers, content creators, online sex workers) perform emotional labor under different surveillance regimes than traditional service workers. The metrics that monitor and price the labor are continuous and algorithmic. Whether the analytical apparatus developed for traditional service work transfers cleanly is contested.

How is emotional labor distributed across the economy? Some readings (Hardt’s) treat emotional labor as the dominant form in late-capitalism; others insist on a more limited analytical scope. Empirically, the distribution varies sharply by sector, gender, and class.

What political-economic responses to emotional labor are operative? Worker-organizing efforts (UNITE-HERE in hospitality, recent care-worker movements) explicitly address emotional labor; legal frameworks generally do not recognize it as compensable distinct from physical or cognitive labor. The framework here gestures at the politics without resolving them.
