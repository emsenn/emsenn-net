Desire

2026-03-06 In Lacanian psychoanalysis, the mode of wanting organized around lack — desire targets a future object, is sustained by its own non-fulfillment, and is vulnerable to collapse when the object proves unattainable. Anarcho-nihilism distinguishes desire from drive to explain why hope-based resistance fails.

Desire is the mode of wanting organized around what you do not have. You desire what is absent — the revolution that has not yet come, the justice that does not yet exist, the freedom you have not yet achieved. Desire is structured by lack: it targets an object, pursues it, and is sustained by the gap between the wanting and the having. If the object were obtained, desire would not be satisfied — it would relocate to a new object, because desire is not really about the object at all. It is about the movement toward what is missing.

This structure comes from Lacan, who drew a sharp line between desire, need, and drive. Need is biological: the body requires food, water, warmth. Need can be satisfied. Desire is what exceeds need — the surplus that emerges when biological need passes through language, culture, and the symbolic order. A hungry person needs food. A person who desires a meal at a particular restaurant, prepared a particular way, shared with particular people, is operating in the register of desire: they want something that exceeds the biological requirement and that is shaped by the symbolic world they inhabit.

¶Why desire matters for politics

Most political movements are organized around desire. They desire a better world, justice, liberation, equality, the end of domination. This desire mobilizes action: people organize, protest, strike, and revolt because they want something they do not have. The structure of desire makes this mobilization powerful but also fragile, because desire depends on the plausibility of its object. If you desire revolution, your desire is sustained by the belief — however tenuous — that revolution is possible. If that belief collapses, desire collapses with it. What remains is despair: desire without an object, wanting without the possibility of getting.

This is the mechanism anarcho-nihilism identifies as the central vulnerability of hope-based politics. The demand for hope is a demand to sustain desire: keep wanting the better world, keep believing it is achievable, keep orienting your action toward a future in which the object of your desire is realized. When the conditions for that belief deteriorate — ecological collapse, consolidated state power, the manifest failure of every revolutionary project to produce lasting freedom — desire collapses into paralysis. If you cannot believe you will get what you want, you stop wanting, and if wanting is the ground of action, you stop acting.

¶Desire and recuperation

Desire is also vulnerable to recuperation precisely because it targets an object. Systems of domination can redirect desire by offering substitute objects: the desire for freedom becomes the desire for consumer choice; the desire for community becomes the desire for brand belonging; the desire for meaningful work becomes the desire for a fulfilling career. Capitalism is, among other things, a machine for producing substitute objects of desire — commodities that promise to fill the lack that desire is structured around, and that never do, because the lack is structural, not incidental. You buy the product, the lack persists, you buy another product. The circuit of desire and consumption is endless because desire’s non-fulfillment is its operating principle.

This is why the anarcho-nihilist turn from desire to drive matters strategically, not only philosophically. Desire can be redirected because it has an object; drive cannot, because it does not. The shift from desire-based to drive-based resistance is a shift from a form of political engagement that the system can capture to one that it cannot.

¶Desire is not bad

Desire is not something anarcho-nihilism rejects in favor of drive. Desire is how most of life operates: you desire connection, understanding, beauty, justice, the well-being of people you love. The point is not to eliminate desire but to recognize that a politics grounded exclusively in desire is structurally vulnerable — to despair when its object proves unattainable, and to recuperation when the system offers substitutes. Drive provides a ground for action that persists when desire fails.
