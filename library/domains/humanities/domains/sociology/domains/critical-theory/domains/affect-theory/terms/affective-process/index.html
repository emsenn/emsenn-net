Affective Process

2026-03-02 Any psychological or social process driven by affect — pre-cognitive bodily intensities that shape experience before interpretation as named emotions.

An affective process is any psychological or social process driven by affect — the pre-cognitive bodily intensities (tension, ease, agitation, attraction) that shape experience before they are interpreted as named emotions. Affect theory distinguishes affect from emotion: emotion is socially coded and linguistically structured (grief has scripts, anger has genres, joy has occasions); affect is the raw capacity to be affected and to affect, operating below and before the categories that language provides.

The distinction matters because it identifies a register of experience that ideology critique misses. If political alignment were a matter of belief, then correcting false beliefs would change political behavior. But people are moved to act, believe, and identify in ways that rational deliberation and ideological persuasion cannot account for. A political rally operates through affective processes — the transmission of intensity between bodies, the rhythmic synchronization of a crowd, the felt shift in what seems possible or necessary — that produce alignment, resistance, or discomfort independent of the propositional content being exchanged. The person who leaves a rally changed has not necessarily learned new facts. They have been moved.

The study of affective processes draws on Baruch Spinoza’s account of affects as increases or decreases in a body’s power to act. Spinoza’s framework was reworked by Gilles Deleuze into a theory of intensive difference, and developed into a political analysis by Brian Massumi, whose Parables for the Virtual (2002) treats affect as autonomic bodily response that exceeds conscious perception, and by Sara Ahmed, whose The Cultural Politics of Emotion (2004) traces how affects circulate between bodies and stick to objects, producing the surfaces of individual and collective identity.

The political stakes are significant. If social order is maintained not just through belief and coercion but through the organization of affect, then political transformation requires attending to how bodies are moved, not only to what people think. This reframing shifts the analysis of domination from the register of consent (Gramsci) and interpellation (Althusser) to the register of intensity, atmosphere, and the felt sense of what is possible. Affective processes are not the opposite of rational deliberation — they are the medium within which deliberation occurs, shaping what arguments feel compelling before any argument is consciously evaluated.
