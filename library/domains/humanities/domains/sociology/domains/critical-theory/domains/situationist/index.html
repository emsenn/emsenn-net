Situationist International

The Situationist International (1957–1972) produced a critique of postwar capitalism centered on the spectacle — the social relation in which lived experience is replaced by its representation. Guy Debord’s The Society of the Spectacle (1967) argued that the commodity form had colonized not just production but perception: life is lived through images rather than as direct experience. The spectacle is not a collection of images but a social relation between people mediated by images.

Recuperation names the mechanism by which the spectacle absorbs its own opposition. Radical gestures are commodified, critical ideas become marketing slogans, and resistance is packaged and sold back to the resisters. The Situationist response was détournement — the hijacking of spectacle’s own materials to reveal the social relations they conceal.

The Situationists’ influence exceeds their small membership: punk, culture jamming, Occupy, and contemporary meme culture all draw on Situationist methods, whether or not they know it. Hypernormalization — the condition in which everyone knows the system is failing but no one can imagine an alternative — extends the Situationist diagnosis into the present.
