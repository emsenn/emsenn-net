Political Economy

The analysis of economic structures as political arrangements rather than as natural laws. Political economy asks: who controls the means of production? How is surplus distributed? How does economic power translate into political power, and how does political power maintain economic arrangements? These questions precede the neoclassical separation of “economics” (the study of markets) from “political science” (the study of government) — a separation that political economy refuses because the separation itself is a political act that naturalizes the market.

The library’s political economy content currently focuses on specific investigations: the structure of organized crime and its relationship to legitimate capital, the analysis of specific corporate formations, and the application of Marxist categories to concrete economic situations.

Central thesis

Political economy treats economic structures as political arrangements rather than natural laws — refusing the neoclassical separation of economics from politics, since the separation itself is a political act that naturalizes the market.
