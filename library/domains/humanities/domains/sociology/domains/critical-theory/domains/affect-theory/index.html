Affect Theory

Affect theory studies how pre-cognitive bodily intensities — the felt registers of tension, ease, agitation, attraction — shape social and political life in ways that rational deliberation and ideological persuasion cannot account for. The tradition draws on Spinoza’s account of affects as increases or decreases in a body’s power to act, reworked through Deleuze into a theory of intensive difference, and developed into sociological analysis by Brian Massumi, Sara Ahmed, Lauren Berlant, and Kathleen Stewart.

The core claim: social order is maintained not just through belief and coercion but through the organization of affect. A political rally moves people through rhythm and intensity, not argument. A workplace maintains compliance through atmospheric pressure, not just rules. The insistence on understanding before relation is itself an affective stance — one that privileges cognition over the bodily registers in which most social life actually operates.

Berlant’s work on cruel optimism and the impasse of ordinary life represents the school’s most sustained engagement with political economy. Ahmed’s work on how affects circulate between bodies and stick to objects represents its most sustained engagement with race and migration. Massumi’s work on the autonomy of affect represents its most sustained engagement with the question of what affect is as distinct from emotion.
