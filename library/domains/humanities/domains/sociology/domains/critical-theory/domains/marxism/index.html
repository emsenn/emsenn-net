Marxism

Marxism is the school of social analysis grounded in Marx’s critique of political economy. Its central claim: the mode of production — how a society organizes the labor that sustains it — determines the structure of that society’s politics, law, culture, and consciousness. Capitalism is a specific mode of production in which those who own the means of production (capital) extract surplus value from those who sell their labor, and the entire apparatus of the state, law, and ideology exists to maintain this arrangement.

The tradition extends in multiple directions. The Gramscian school analyzes how ruling-class domination operates through cultural hegemony — consent manufactured through civil society — rather than through force alone. The decolonial Marxist tradition (Fanon, Cabral, Mariátegui) insists that colonialism and racial domination are not incidental to capitalism but constitutive of it. Autonomism (the Italian workerist tradition through Negri, Virno, Federici) centers the self-activity of labor and the analysis of social reproduction. Political economy applies Marxist categories to the analysis of specific economic formations.

Each of these has its own subdomain with terms and texts.

Central thesis

Marxism holds that the mode of production — how a society organizes the labor that sustains it — determines the structure of that society’s politics, law, culture, and consciousness, and that capitalism is a specific mode in which the apparatus of state, law, and ideology exists to maintain the extraction of surplus value from those who sell their labor.
