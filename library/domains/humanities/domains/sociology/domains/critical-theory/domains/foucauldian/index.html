Foucauldian Analysis

Foucault

Michel Foucault (1926–1984). His work asks not “who has power?” but “how does power work?” — and answers by tracing how institutions produce knowledge as they exercise power, how that knowledge produces the categories through which people are classified and governed, and how the subjects produced through those categories come to understand themselves through the very frameworks that manage them.

Foucault developed two methods across his career. Archaeology maps the rules that determine what can be said in a given period — the conditions under which statements become possible and authoritative. Genealogy traces how those rules came to be through contingent historical struggles rather than rational progress. Power/knowledge names the mechanism connecting them: knowledge is not neutral observation of a pre-existing reality but a product of the institutional practices that simultaneously study and govern their objects.

Biopower names the form of modern governance that takes biological life as its object — managing populations through statistics, public health, and the regulation of birth, death, and everything between. Governmentality names the specific rationality (liberal, neoliberal, welfarist) through which that management is organized. Both are developed in the Collège de France lectures of the late 1970s.

The terms and texts in this domain cover the full analytical vocabulary. The lesson texts provide a pedagogical sequence from overview through the specific concepts.
