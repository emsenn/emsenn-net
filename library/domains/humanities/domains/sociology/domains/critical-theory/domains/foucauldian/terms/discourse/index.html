Discourse

2026-01-01 Foucault's concept for the structured system of statements that constitutes what can be said, thought, and known within a given domain — not mere language but a power-laden practice that produces its own objects.

Discourse, in Foucault’s sense, is the set of rules, practices, and institutional conditions that determine what counts as a meaningful statement in a given domain. It is not language in general but the specific regime that governs what can be said, by whom, from what institutional position, and with what authority. A discourse is not defined by its subject matter (medicine, law, psychiatry) but by the rules of formation that produce its statements — the conditions under which a claim becomes intelligible rather than nonsensical.

Foucault developed the concept across The Order of Things (1966) and The Archaeology of Knowledge (1969). A discursive formation has four dimensions. First, the positions from which authorized speakers can make statements — the licensed physician, the certified expert, the judge. Second, the institutional sites where statements are produced — the clinic, the laboratory, the courtroom. Third, the conceptual grid that organizes the domain — disease categories, legal precedent, diagnostic criteria. Fourth, the strategies by which statements are combined into arguments, theories, and bodies of knowledge. Together, these four dimensions determine what can be said and what falls outside the thinkable.

Medical discourse, for example, is not “talk about bodies.” It is the configuration of qualified speakers, institutional sites, diagnostic categories, and evidential standards that makes certain statements about bodies possible and others unthinkable. A medieval physician’s claim that a patient’s humors are imbalanced is not wrong in the way a modern misdiagnosis is wrong. It belongs to a different discursive formation — one in which “humoral imbalance” is a valid category and “bacterial infection” is not yet a possible thought.

Discourse is the site where power/knowledge operates most directly. To control a discourse is to control what can be treated as true — not by censoring statements but by structuring the conditions under which statements become intelligible. Foucault treats discourses not as reflections of a pre-existing reality but as productive forces: they generate the objects they appear to describe. “The delinquent,” “the homosexual,” “the mentally ill” are not types that existed before the discursive formations of criminology, sexology, and psychiatry classified them. The discourses produced these categories, and the categories in turn reorganized the institutional practices that produced them.
