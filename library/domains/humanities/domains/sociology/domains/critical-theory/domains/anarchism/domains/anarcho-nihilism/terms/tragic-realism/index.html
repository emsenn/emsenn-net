Tragic Realism

2026-03-30 The acceptance that capacity limits are ecological facts rather than moral failures — permitting communities to make difficult decisions about who and what to protect without procedural paralysis.

Tragic realism is the acceptance that not everyone can be saved and that this is an ecological fact rather than a moral failure. It permits communities to make decisions about protection and priority under conditions where resources, time, and capacity are finite — without collapsing into the procedural paralysis that universalist frameworks generate when confronted with the impossibility of equal treatment.

The concept operates at the intersection of anarcho-nihilism and collapse adaptation. Anarcho-nihilism, as developed by Serafinski in Blessed Is the Flame (2016), demonstrates that resistance does not require hope — the intolerability of domination is sufficient grounds for action regardless of outcome. Tragic realism extends this into collective survival: adaptation does not require the guarantee that everyone will be adapted for. When shelter holds 350 and the neighborhood holds 1,000, the framework that insists on universal inclusion before action produces no action at all. Tragic realism permits the decision.

This is not triage in the clinical sense — a neutral professional sorting casualties. It is relational: the decisions about who to protect are made by people who know each other, who carry obligations that predate the crisis, and whose priorities reflect the relational structure of their community rather than an abstract ranking system. Elders are protected because they are elders, not because an algorithm scored their survival value. Knowledge-keepers are sheltered because the community’s continuity depends on what they carry, not because a committee ratified the criterion.

Tragic realism is structurally opposed to frameworks that treat capacity limits as occasions for moral crisis. Mutualist and liberal frameworks respond to the impossibility of universal inclusion by seeking better procedures — more deliberation, fairer criteria, broader consensus. These procedures consume the time and safety that survival requires. Tragic realism does not resolve the moral tension; it acts through it. The tragedy is real — people are not sheltered, losses are permanent — but the realism is that acting within limits is not the same as endorsing them.

The concept also connects to what disaster sociology consistently finds: in actual catastrophes, survival is driven by small-scale, relationally dense, improvisational networks (Klinenberg 2002; Solnit 2009), not by universalist coordinating procedures. Tragic realism names the ethical posture these networks already operate within — the unstated acceptance that you help who you can reach, protect who you are obligated to, and do not wait for the procedure that will authorize you to act.
