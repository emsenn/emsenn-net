Hannian Analysis

¶Han

Byung-Chul Han (1959–) is a Korean-German philosopher whose work extends Foucauldian analysis of power into the conditions of late-neoliberal subjectivity. Where Foucault analyzed disciplinary power as the molding of bodies through external constraint, Han analyzes a successor formation in which the subject internalizes the imperative to perform and turns the apparatus of self-discipline against itself.

The framework has three major components. The achievement-subject (Leistungssubjekt) names the subject who has displaced the disciplinary “you must” with a permissive “you can” — and who, finding the freedom of “you can” indistinguishable from the demand of “you must,” exploits themselves in the name of self-realization. The transparency society names the social formation in which visibility is the medium of value and opacity is positioned as resistance to circulation. Psychopolitics names the regime of power that operates through affective calibration and data-mediated self-knowledge rather than through the body’s direct constraint.

Han’s signature claim is that depression, burnout, and ADHD are structural features of the achievement society, not pathologies of individual subjects. The subject who cannot stop performing has no outside from which to refuse performance. The framework is consistent with the cybernetic-postliberal claim that the late-liberal apparatus formats its constitutive contradictions as personally manageable affect; Han’s contribution is the specific phenomenology of what that formatting feels like from inside.

Central thesis

Late-neoliberal governance does not discipline its subjects from outside; it produces them as achievement-subjects (Leistungssubjekte) who exhaust themselves in the name of self-realization. The subject is simultaneously project and product, exploiter and exploited, and the resulting depression is a structural feature of the system rather than an individual pathology.
