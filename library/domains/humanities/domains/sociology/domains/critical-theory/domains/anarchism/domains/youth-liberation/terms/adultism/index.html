Adultism

2026-03-04

Adultism is the systematic discrimination against young people by adults. The term was introduced in its current sense by psychologist Jack Flasher in a 1978 article, where he defined it as the abuse by adults of the greater power they have over children. It names not individual acts of unkindness but a structural condition: the set of behaviors, policies, and assumptions through which adults exercise unquestioned authority over young people and through which the perspectives, capacities, and interests of children are treated as subordinate to those of adults.

Adultism manifests across institutions. In families, it appears as the assumption that parental authority over a child’s life requires no justification beyond the parent’s age. In schools, it structures the relationship between teacher and student as one of command and compliance rather than dialogue. In legal systems, it underwrites the exclusion of young people from voting, contracting, and other forms of political participation. In the economy, it justifies restrictions on youth labor while simultaneously exploiting children’s unpaid domestic and educational labor.

The concept has been compared to racism, sexism, and other forms of systemic oppression. Like those systems, adultism operates through both explicit rules (age-based legal restrictions) and implicit norms (the assumption that children’s opinions are less valid than adults’). It is reinforced by the fact that every adult was once a child, which produces a naturalization effect: because adults experienced age-based subordination themselves, they tend to regard it as a normal and inevitable feature of human development rather than as a political arrangement.

Adultism is related to misopedy (the ideological antipathy toward children and childhood), childism (prejudice against children as a group), and adult supremacy (the political arrangement in which adults monopolize power). Where misopedy names the attitude and adult supremacy names the political structure, adultism names the discriminatory practice — the concrete ways in which adult power operates to constrain the lives of young people.
