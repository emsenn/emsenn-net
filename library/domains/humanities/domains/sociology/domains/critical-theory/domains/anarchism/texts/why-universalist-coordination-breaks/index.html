Why Universalist Coordination Breaks

2026-03-31 How four commitments shared by mutualist and liberal organizing — teleology, epistemic universalism, proceduralism, and cooperative reciprocity — form a chain that collapses under the conditions it claims to address.

¶Assumed audience

Anyone involved in or thinking about community organizing — particularly anarchist, mutual aid, or horizontal organizing — who has experienced or witnessed the frustration of good-faith coordination processes failing under pressure. No prior familiarity with political theory is assumed.

¶The chain

Most anarchist and left-radical organizing shares four commitments that appear independently reasonable but form a chain of dependencies. When the chain is loaded — when real pressure is applied — the weakest link fails and takes the rest with it.

The chain runs like this:

¶1. We want to cooperate as equals

Cooperative reciprocity is the starting commitment: social relations should be structured through voluntary exchanges among participants acting as equals. No one commands. No one extracts. People contribute and receive in rough balance. This is the intuition that draws most people to anarchist organizing in the first place.

The commitment is real and serious. But it carries a hidden dependency: it works when participants actually have comparable capacity, comparable access, and comparable standing. When they don’t — when some people are sick, old, disabled, traumatized, overburdened, or simply in a different situation — the framework either ignores the asymmetry (treating unequal people as if they were equal) or stalls while trying to procedurally accommodate it.

¶2. Decisions must be justifiable to everyone

Epistemic universalism is the next link: for a decision to be legitimate, it must be articulable in terms that everyone affected could accept. No one should be bound by arrangements they can’t evaluate on shared terms. This sounds like a protection against arbitrary authority — and it is. But it also means that knowledge, strategies, and priorities that can’t be translated into the shared evaluative framework are structurally excluded. Place-based knowledge, ceremonial authority, kinship obligation, tactical opacity — anything that operates on its own terms rather than universal ones — falls outside what the framework can recognize.

¶3. Legitimacy requires process

Proceduralism follows: since decisions must be justifiable to everyone, there must be a process through which justification happens. Assemblies, consensus rounds, transparent documentation, collective ratification. These procedures are the mechanism by which epistemic universalism is enacted. Without them, the universalist commitment is just an aspiration. With them, coordination gains legitimacy — but also gains a time cost, a safety cost (transparency), and a dependency on institutional infrastructure (meeting spaces, communication channels, shared language).

¶4. All of this is heading somewhere

Teleology is the deepest link and the hardest to see. The entire chain presupposes that there is a form of social life we are moving toward — cooperative, fair, inclusive, horizontal — and that our present actions are valuable insofar as they advance toward it. Actions that don’t serve the telos are illegitimate. Strategies that can’t be universalized are failures. Decisions that aren’t procedurally ratified are domination in disguise. The destination organizes everything.

¶How the chain breaks

Each link depends on a condition that stress removes:

  • Cooperative reciprocity depends on comparable capacity among participants. Collapse produces radical asymmetry.
  • Epistemic universalism depends on shared evaluative ground. Heterogeneous communities under crisis have no such ground.
  • Proceduralism depends on time and safety. Collapse compresses time and makes transparency dangerous.
  • Teleology depends on a reachable future. When the future the framework was organized around becomes unavailable, the entire basis for action dissolves.

Under normal conditions, these dependencies are invisible. There is enough time, enough shared ground, enough safety, enough symmetry for the chain to hold. Under collapse conditions — time compression, capacity limits, asymmetric vulnerability, hostile institutions, heterogeneous needs — the dependencies become load-bearing and the chain snaps.

The specific failure mode is worth naming: the community’s own procedures for preventing domination prevent it from acting. The assembly can’t decide who gets shelter. The transparent plan attracts municipal interference. The consensus process runs out of time. The framework designed to ensure no one is dominated ensures that no one is protected.

¶Why this matters

This is not an argument against cooperation, against fairness, or against process. It is an argument that these commitments have hidden prerequisites — and that those prerequisites are exactly what collapse destroys. If you are organizing in a context where time is compressed, resources are limited, vulnerability is unequal, and external institutions are hostile or absent, the universalist chain will not hold your weight.

The alternative is not abandoning coordination. It is grounding coordination in something other than universalist prerequisites: in relational ties rather than shared evaluative frameworks, in situated obligation rather than procedural legitimacy, in present adequacy rather than future destination.
