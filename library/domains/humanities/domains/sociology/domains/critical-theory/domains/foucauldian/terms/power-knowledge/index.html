Power/Knowledge

2026-01-01 Foucault's thesis that power and knowledge are mutually constitutive — power produces knowledge and knowledge enables power, with no neutral ground between them.

Power/knowledge (pouvoir/savoir) is the relationship in which power relations produce fields of knowledge, and knowledge in turn sustains and extends power. The two are not merely connected — they are mutually constitutive. There is no knowledge that is not produced within power relations, and no exercise of power that does not simultaneously produce knowledge. The common assumption that knowledge is neutral (scientists discover facts; politicians misuse them) and that knowledge liberates (the more we know, the freer we are) both fail to reckon with this entanglement.

Foucault developed the concept most directly in Discipline and Punish (1975) and the Collège de France lecture series through the 1970s. The examination is the paradigmatic mechanism: as practiced in the school, the clinic, the prison, and the workplace, the examination is simultaneously an exercise of power over the examined subject and a method of producing knowledge about that subject. The teacher who gives a test exercises authority over the student and produces data (scores, rankings, classifications) that become part of the student’s institutional identity. The physician who examines a patient exercises clinical authority and produces diagnostic knowledge that determines the patient’s treatment. In each case, the knowledge produced is not incidental to the power exercised — it is the mechanism through which power operates.

The knowledge gained through examination feeds back into techniques of management and classification. Test scores generate categories (gifted, average, remedial) that channel students into different institutional tracks. Diagnostic categories (neurotic, psychotic, personality-disordered) determine what treatments are administered and by whom. Criminal profiles (the recidivist, the juvenile delinquent, the sexual offender) shape sentencing, surveillance, and rehabilitation programs. These categories are not descriptions of pre-existing types. They are products of the institutional practices that observe and shape their objects simultaneously.

This means that contesting a regime of power requires contesting the knowledge that supports it. You cannot dismantle the prison system while leaving intact the criminological categories it produced. You cannot challenge psychiatric authority while accepting the diagnostic framework that constitutes it. The categories survive the individuals who hold positions of authority, because they are embedded in institutional practices, training programs, databases, and professional norms. Power/knowledge is structural, not personal.
