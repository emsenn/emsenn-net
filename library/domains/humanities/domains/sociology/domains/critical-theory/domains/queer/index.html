Queer Theory

Queer theory analyzes how heterosexuality is naturalized as the default organization of social life and how gender and sexuality are produced through repeated, regulated performance rather than expressed from an interior truth. The tradition originates in Foucault’s analysis of how “the homosexual” was produced as a category by 19th-century institutional practices, and was formalized by Butler (Gender Trouble, 1990), Sedgwick (Epistemology of the Closet, 1990), and the activist and intellectual responses to the AIDS crisis.

Butler’s concept of performativity — gender is not what you are but what you do, repeatedly, under regulatory pressure — is the school’s foundational contribution. Sedgwick’s analysis of the closet as an epistemological structure (not just a hiding place but a mechanism that organizes what can be known and by whom) is its methodological complement. Together they established that sexuality and gender are not natural facts to be described but social productions to be analyzed.

The library’s queer theory domain includes subdomains for feminist theory and queer-of-color critique, which extends queer analysis to the intersection of race, class, and sexuality.

Central thesis

Queer theory holds that heterosexuality is not the natural default but a social production, and that gender and sexuality are constituted through repeated, regulated performance rather than expressed from an interior truth.
