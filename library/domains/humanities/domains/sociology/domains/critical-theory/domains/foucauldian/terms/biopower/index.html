Biopower

2026-01-01 Foucault's term for the form of power that takes biological life itself as its object — governing populations through statistics, public health, and the management of birth, death, and everything between.

Biopower is a form of political power that takes the biological life of populations as its object. Where older sovereign power operated through the right to “take life or let live” — the king could execute or pardon — biopower inverts the formula: it operates through the right to “make live and let die.” The state no longer primarily threatens its subjects with death. It manages their lives — their health, reproduction, longevity, and the conditions under which they flourish or deteriorate.

Foucault introduced the concept in the final chapter of The History of Sexuality, Vol. 1 (1976) and developed it through the 1975–76 Collège de France lectures published as “Society Must Be Defended.” The shift from sovereignty to biopower is not a simple replacement. Sovereign power persists — states still execute, wage war, impose punishment — but it operates increasingly within a biopolitical framework where the justification for killing is the protection of life. Wars are waged to defend a population. Executions are justified by the danger the condemned poses to public safety. The right to kill is exercised in the name of the right to make live.

Biopower operates at two levels simultaneously. At the level of the individual body, disciplinary techniques — surveillance, examination, timetable, spatial organization — produce what Foucault calls “docile bodies”: subjects trained to behave in predictable, productive ways. The soldier, the student, the factory worker, the prisoner are each shaped by discipline specific to the institution that manages them. At the level of the population, regulatory techniques — demographics, epidemiology, public health campaigns, insurance — manage aggregate biological processes. Birth rates, mortality curves, endemic disease patterns, and rates of reproduction are tracked, modeled, and adjusted. Discipline individualizes; regulation totalizes. Together they constitute the two poles of biopower.

The concept of governmentality, which Foucault developed in subsequent lectures, names the rationality that organizes these techniques into a coherent art of government. Biopower is the what — power exercised over life. Governmentality is the how — the specific rationality (liberal, neoliberal, welfarist) through which that power is organized and justified.
