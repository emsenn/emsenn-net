Adult Supremacy

2026-03-04

Adult supremacy is the political arrangement in which adults hold power over children and young people, and in which this power is treated as natural, inevitable, and justified. The term draws a deliberate parallel to white supremacy and male supremacy: it names not individual prejudice but a system — a set of interlocking institutions, norms, and ideologies that concentrate political, economic, and social power in the hands of adults and exclude young people from participation in decisions that affect their lives.

The NO! Against Adult Supremacy zine series, published through anarchist networks, has been a primary venue for developing the concept. Contributors argue that adult supremacy is not a metaphorical extension of other forms of domination but a foundational one: the first hierarchy most people encounter is the hierarchy of adult over child, and it trains people to accept hierarchical social relations as normal. From this perspective, the family — as an institution in which adults exercise near-total authority over children — functions as the primary site where obedience to authority is learned and naturalized.

Adult supremacy operates through legal exclusion (children cannot vote, contract, or choose their own guardians), economic dependence (children are denied the right to work while their domestic and educational labor goes uncompensated), epistemic dismissal (children’s testimony, preferences, and judgments are treated as less credible than adults’), and physical coercion (corporal punishment remains legal in many jurisdictions, and children have fewer protections against confinement than adults).

The concept is related to adultism (the discriminatory practices through which adult power operates), misopedy (the ideological antipathy toward children and childhood), and childism (prejudice against children as a group). Where adultism names the practice and childism names the prejudice, adult supremacy names the political system — the arrangement of power that adultism enforces and childism legitimizes.

Toby Rollo’s work on misopedy connects adult supremacy to settler colonialism. If the colonial relationship is structured by the logic of adult supremacy — the assumption that the mature, rational, civilized subject is entitled to govern those deemed immature — then adult supremacy is not one oppression among others but a template for domination as such.
