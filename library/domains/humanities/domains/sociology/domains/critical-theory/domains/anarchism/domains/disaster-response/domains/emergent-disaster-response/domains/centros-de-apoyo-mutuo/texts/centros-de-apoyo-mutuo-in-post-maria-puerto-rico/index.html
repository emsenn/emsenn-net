Centros de Apoyo Mutuo in Post-Maria Puerto Rico

2026-03-08
Abstract

A research text on the self-managed mutual support centers that emerged in Puerto Rico after Hurricanes Irma and Maria.

The centros de apoyo mutuo that emerged in Puerto Rico after Hurricanes Irma and Maria are one of the clearest examples of emergent disaster response becoming territorial, autonomous, and explicitly political [@camcoconspirators2019; @cambuilding2020]. Faced with state collapse, FEMA abuse, and colonial abandonment, people organized self-managed spaces to meet urgent needs while building durable community power.

Self-managed spaces

Mutual Aid Disaster Relief’s CAM materials describe these centers as self-managed spaces created around the island after Maria [camcoconspirators2019]. This matters because the centers were not only distribution points. They were institutional forms through which communities could organize themselves rather than wait to be governed.

Aid linked to justice and political discussion

The Building Power in Paradise account makes this current especially clear. CAMBU in Bucarabones is described not just as a relief site but as a project rooted in social, economic, cultural, and environmental justice [cambuilding2020]. The centers hosted community kitchens, classes, workshops, cultural activities, and communal space while also serving immediate survival needs.

Seizing and defending space

This is Caguas emphasizes another distinguishing feature: some of these centers involved reclaiming and defending space itself [caguas2017]. In Caguas, a building was seized and defended by the community, then turned into a site for water infrastructure, mutual aid, and collective use. This adds a community-defense dimension to emergent disaster response that goes beyond service provision alone.

Infrastructure for future storms

The Solar Power to the People report shows that CAM-related efforts also built energy infrastructure meant to strengthen future resilience [solarpower2018]. Solar micro-grids, water systems, and shared facilities linked present disaster response to longer-term autonomous capacity. This makes the CAM current especially important for thinking about disaster response as institution building.

Significance

The centros de apoyo mutuo matter because they make visible a distinct political current within emergent disaster response. Disaster relief is organized here as self-management, territorial autonomy, and community infrastructure, not just as volunteer service or temporary solidarity.
