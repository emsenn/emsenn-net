Work

Work encompasses the practices, structures, and relationships through which humans organize productive activity, organized into governance (decision-making, authority, accountability), labor (forms of work, compensation, obligation), organization (institutional structures and coordination), and practice (the actual conduct of work and craft).

Teleology
The goal of work as a domain of study is to understand how humans organize productive activity — the practices, structures, and relationships through which collective effort coheres into outcomes.
Method
The method of work studies is comparative analysis of organizational forms and labor practices, drawing on philosophy of action, organizational theory, and ethnographic study of how work is actually done.

Featured texts

Philosophy of Endeavor
The philosophical grounding of endeavor in Chisholm's analysis of intentional action, Hornsby's trying-events, and O'Shaughnessy's sustained trying.
The Settlement Path
The settlement path — from inquiry to process — is the organizing principle of a settlement-carrier system. Every piece of infrastructure serves the movement from uncertainty to determinism.
The Temporality of Endeavors
How an endeavor's temporal character — aim-terminated, indefinite, or aim-open — determines the shape of its operational infrastructure: which planning horizons apply, what cadences are structurally necessary, how urgency manifests, and why multi-scale operations are required.
The Office as Normative Structure: Roman Law, Kantorowicz, and Constitutional Opposition
The formal model of office as a normative structure that persists across holders — the munus/honor/dignitas trinity from Roman law, Kantorowicz's body politic, Searle's status function declarations, and Madison's separation of powers as structural insulation between offices.
The Algebraic Structure of Work: Free Algebras, Ground Terms, and Operational Signatures
How universal algebra formalizes the work taxonomy — operations as signatures, work as term application, work units as ground terms, and the settlement path as substitution closure.
