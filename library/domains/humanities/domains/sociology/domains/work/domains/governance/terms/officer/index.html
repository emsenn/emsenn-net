Officer

An officer is the natural person currently holding an office. The officer is the body natural — mortal, particular, indexed to a specific time — wearing the body politic, which is the office itself.

What makes someone an officer

Three conditions must all hold:

  1. The office exists. There must be a defined normative position with powers, duties, privileges, and immunities independent of any holder.
  2. Investiture has occurred. A formal installation act has bound the person to the office’s deontic profile. This is what distinguishes an officer from someone who merely fills a role — role-filling is capability-matching; officership requires a constitutive normative event.
  3. The binding is current. The person has not vacated, been removed, or had their investiture revoked.

Officer vs. incumbent

The incumbent is the formal tuple — the office, the history, the occupant, and the incumbency proposition. The officer is the person in that tuple: the natural individual who bears the office’s authority for the duration of occupancy. “Incumbent” is the relational fact; “officer” is the person.

Officer vs. role-bearer

A role-bearer fills a role by demonstrating capability and accepting the role’s norms. No formal act is required. An officer holds an office by investiture — a constitutive speech act that creates normative relations not previously present. A surgeon is a role-bearer by virtue of surgical skill. A judge is an officer by virtue of being sworn in.

The difference is constitutive depth: role-filling can be informal and gradual; officership is created by a discrete normative event.

What an officer has

An officer acquires the office’s complete Hohfeldian profile upon investiture:

  • Powers — authority to create normative relations within the office’s jurisdiction
  • Duties — obligations imposed by the office, not by personal commitment
  • Privileges — liberties to exercise office functions without owing a duty not to
  • Immunities — protection from having the office’s normative structure altered by unauthorized agents

These belong to the office, not to the officer. When the officer vacates, the profile remains in the office, waiting for the successor.

The officer’s limitation

The officer may use the office’s authority but may not alter its normative structure. This is the usufruct condition: the officer enjoys the fruits of the office (jus utendi fruendi) while preserving the substance (salva rerum substantia). An officer who attempts to reshape the office to fit personal preferences violates the structural integrity that makes succession possible.
