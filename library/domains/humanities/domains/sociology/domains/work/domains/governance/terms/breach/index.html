Breach

2026-03-20 A Breach is a triple (D, t*, p) — a duty D, an assessment deadline t*, and a performance p whose performance-and-recognition predicates have not jointly reached affirming settlement at t*. Breach is the event of a gap that should have been zero remaining positive at the assessment point. It creates immediate Hohfeldian consequences: the breaching party acquires a new duty (to remedy) and the injured party acquires new rights (to remedial acts). The settlement position of p at t* gives a four-way classification of breach by which predicate(s) failed to settle.

¶Formal definition

A Breach is a triple (D,t∗,p)(D, t^*, p):

(D:Duty,  t∗∈T,  p)(D : \mathrm{Duty},\; t^* \in T,\; p)

where:

  • DD is the duty (or commitment) — the normative requirement whose satisfaction is being assessed; DD specifies the act-class φ\varphi that must be performed and the deadline t∗t^* by which it must be produced
  • t∗∈Tt^* \in T is the deadline — the assessment history; breach is evaluated at t∗t^*, not before
  • pp is the performance — the act produced by the duty-bearer in attempted satisfaction of DD; pp may be an actual performance act, or ∅\varnothing if no performance was attempted

Breach condition. (D,t∗,p)(D, t^*, p) is a breach iff the joint condition required by discharge — that both the performance predicate and the recognition predicate over pp have reached affirming settlement at t∗t^* — fails. That is, at least one of the two predication cells (p,pperform,D)(p, p_{\mathrm{perform}}, D) and (p,precognize,D)(p, p_{\mathrm{recognize}}, D) has not reached the truth-only corner t\mathbf{t} by the deadline.

Two breach species:

  1. Non-performance (p=∅p = \varnothing): no act of the required type was produced by t∗t^*. Both predicates remain in the absurd corner — no witnessing of either has occurred.

  2. Defective performance (pp exists but settlement is incomplete): a performance was produced but at least one of the two predicates has not reached affirming settlement. The duty-bearer attempted satisfaction but fell short. The unsettled predicate’s distance from the affirming corner is the breach quantum — how much was owed but not delivered.

¶Immediate normative consequences

Breach at (D,t∗,p)(D, t^*, p) generates new Hohfeldian positions immediately:

In the breaching party BB:

  • New duty D′D': the duty to remedy — BB must perform remedial acts to close the gap that breach created for the injured party CC. D′D' is created at t∗t^* and has a new deadline t∗∗>t∗t^{**} > t^*.
  • Existing liability: BB was already in a Hohfeldian liability position relative to CC’s power to invoke remedies; breach activates this liability.

In the injured party CC:

  • New power: the right to invoke remedies — CC gains the Hohfeldian Power to elect a remedy (specific performance, damages, rescission) and to trigger enforcement mechanisms within the normative system.
  • New claim-right: the right to the remedy — the correlative of BB’s new duty D′D'.

In the normative system NN:

  • Breach creates a record in the breach-history of NN: (D,t∗,p)(D, t^*, p) is committed as a settled fact that constrains future obligations and assessments.

¶Breach types by remedy

The remedy owed depends on the breach type and the governing normative system:

Breach type Typical remedy Settlement reading
Non-performance of a unique act Specific performance The breaching party must produce the original φ\varphi (not a substitute); the court enforces affirming settlement of both performance and recognition predicates at the new deadline
Non-performance with substitute possible Damages The injured party receives compensation equivalent in value to the breach quantum
Repudiatory breach (anticipatory) Rescission + damages CC may treat the contract as void and the obligations as discharged; the contract’s predicates are removed from the active settlement structure
Defective performance Cure or damages BB may cure (bring the unsettled predicate to affirming settlement within additional time) or pay damages for the gap

Repudiatory breach deserves special treatment: it is the breach of a condition so fundamental that the entire normative structure built on the contract collapses. Formally: a repudiatory breach removes the other party’s obligations from the active settlement entirely — the bilateral structure of duties and claims dissolves because the foundational condition of the relationship has failed.

¶The four-corner classification of breach

The settlement position of the performance pp across the two predicates (performance and recognition) at deadline t∗t^* classifies every breach instance into one of four structurally distinct types. The classification corresponds to the four corners of joint settlement on the two predicates:

Joint settlement of (pperform,precognize)(p_{\mathrm{perform}}, p_{\mathrm{recognize}}) Breach type Normative content
Both affirmingly settled No breach Complete discharge — both predicates at t\mathbf{t}
Recognition settled, performance not Paper-compliance breach Acknowledged but not actually performed — the act has been recognized as falling within the duty’s scope but the performance itself has not reached affirming settlement
Performance settled, recognition not Completed-but-unrecognized breach The act has been performed (and its performance carries forward) but the normative system has not yet acknowledged it as satisfying the duty
Neither settled Double breach Both predicates remain in the absurd corner — entirely unsettled, the maximal breach

Paper-compliance breach. The recognition predicate has reached affirming settlement (the normative system acknowledges this act-type as satisfying the duty), but the performance predicate has not (the act has not actually been carried out, or has been carried out only formally). The duty-bearer has produced something that passes the evaluative test at t∗t^* but is designed to be temporary, reversible, or otherwise not actually durable. Legal analog: a performance that complies with the letter of the obligation but not its spirit.

Completed-but-unrecognized breach. The performance predicate has reached affirming settlement (the act has been done and is forward-stable), but the recognition predicate has not (the normative system has not yet accepted it as satisfying the duty). The duty-bearer has delivered something durable, but formal acceptance is pending. Legal analog: substantial performance — the work is done and will stand, but formal acceptance is pending.

Double breach. Both predicates remain in the absurd corner — the performance is neither performed nor recognized at the deadline. This is the maximum breach. Legal analog: non-performance that is not merely incomplete but structurally inadequate in every measurable respect.

Diagnostic remedy mapping. The four-corner classification doubles as a diagnostic of which remedy applies:

  • Paper-compliance breach → remedy targets performance: the duty-bearer must produce a durable, actually-performed version of the act.
  • Completed-but-unrecognized breach → remedy targets recognition: the normative system must register the already-completed performance as satisfying the duty.
  • Double breach → remedy targets both: full remedy is required to bring both predicates to affirming settlement.

¶Approaching compliance is not compliance

A performance that approaches affirming settlement asymptotically — moving toward but never reaching the truth-only corner in finite witnessings — does not cure breach at the deadline. The deadline t∗t^* is a specific history; the assessment is whether both predicates have reached affirming settlement at t∗t^*. If either has not, the breach condition holds regardless of trajectory. The fact that the predicates would eventually settle does not cure the breach at t∗t^*.

This is the formal analog of the legal principle that approaching compliance is not compliance: a contractor who has completed 99% of a building by the deadline has breached if the remaining 1% is still pending.

¶Breach and accountability

Accountability determines who bears the normative consequences of breach. When a subordinate B′B' breaches a duty within scope SS, the breach record (D,t∗,p)(D, t^*, p) is a fact about B′B'’s performance. But the accountability function makes the commanding officer BB answerable for the breach even if BB did not cause it. BB is accountable; B′B' may be responsible. Both normative positions can exist simultaneously.

¶Breach vs. failure

Not every unsettled predicate is a breach. A predicate that has not reached affirming settlement at history tt is simply the normal condition of an active inquiry. Breach requires:

  1. A duty DD specifying that both performance and recognition predicates over the required act must reach affirming settlement by t∗t^*
  2. The fact that at least one predicate has not reached affirming settlement at t∗t^*

Without a duty, there is no breach — only an act with unsettled predicates. The moral: unsettled is everywhere; breach is the assignment of normative significance to specific unsettled predicates at a specific deadline under a specific duty.

¶Open questions

  • Whether defective performance triggers the same Hohfeldian consequences as non-performance, or whether there is a graduated structure where small gaps generate only cure-rights while large gaps generate full remedy-rights.
  • Whether breach of a duty that is itself a consequence of a prior breach (a contrary-to-duty obligation) generates its own breach record, and whether the breach-history can have cyclic structure or must remain a DAG.
  • Whether the assessment point t∗t^* must be stipulated by the duty DD or whether it can be determined by the normative system NN based on default rules (e.g., reasonable time), and whether a duty with no explicit deadline is unbreachable or has an implied deadline.
  • Whether the four-corner classification of breach is exhaustive in the same sense as the bilattice’s four corners — whether every breach instance occupies exactly one of the four positions, or whether interior settlements (partial witnessing on both predicates) constitute their own intermediate breach types.
