2026-03-20 A Novation is a triple (K₁, K₂, π) — an existing obligation or contract K₁, a replacement obligation or contract K₂, and an agreement π among the relevant parties that K₁ is extinguished and K₂ takes its place. The defining structure: novation is the site morphism that combines an extinction of K₁ (terminal morphism to ⊥) with a fresh genesis of K₂ (new site constitution), where at least one party or one material term changes (distinguishing novation from amendment, which preserves community identity G₁ = G₂). Formally: the composition of a termination morphism K₁ → ∅ followed by a genesis morphism ∅ → K₂; novation is not a morphism between K₁ and K₂ but a replacement of one object by another, mediated by the parties' agreement π.

Novation

Formal definition

A Novation is a triple N=(K1,K2,π)\mathcal{N} = (K_1, K_2, \pi):

N=(K1:Obligation,  K2:Obligation,  π:Agreement(K1,K2))\mathcal{N} = (K_1 : \mathrm{Obligation},\; K_2 : \mathrm{Obligation},\; \pi : \mathrm{Agreement}(K_1, K_2))

together with:

  • An extinction act ε:K1→∅\varepsilon : K_1 \to \emptyset — the termination of the original obligation; K1K_1’s obligations, rights, and claims are discharged in full at the moment π\pi is effective
  • A genesis act γ:∅→K2\gamma : \emptyset \to K_2 — the constitution of the replacement obligation; K2K_2 comes into existence simultaneously with K1K_1’s extinction
  • The agreement π\pi — the consent of all parties whose rights are extinguished or created by the novation; π\pi must be given by every party whose position changes materially

Four invariants. N\mathcal{N} is a valid novation iff:

  1. Extinguishment: K1K_1 is fully discharged at the moment π\pi is effective. No residual obligations, liabilities, or claims from K1K_1 survive into K2K_2’s operation. The extinguishment is clean: K1→∅K_1 \to \emptyset is a terminal morphism (the empty obligation, with no remaining force). This distinguishes novation from assignment (where K1K_1’s obligations persist, only the party changes) and from amendment (where K1K_1 continues as K2K_2 via a morphism, not a replacement).

  2. Genesis: K2K_2 comes into existence as a new obligation with its own validity, parties, and terms. K2K_2 is not derived from K1K_1; it does not inherit K1K_1’s defects, defenses, or history. A party who had a defense against K1K_1 (e.g., fraudulent inducement) does not automatically have that defense against K2K_2 — K2K_2 must be independently valid.

  3. Tripartite consent (standard case): all parties whose rights are extinguished or created must consent. In the standard novation of a contract with three parties — original debtor DD, creditor CC, and new debtor D′D' — consent of DD, CC, and D′D' is required. Without CC’s consent, DD cannot unilaterally transfer their obligation to D′D' (the creditor has the right to choose their debtor).

  4. Material change: at least one of (a) a party, (b) the subject matter, or (c) the consideration is different between K1K_1 and K2K_2. If nothing material changes, the act is not a novation but a restatement (a clarifying rewrite of the same obligation). The material-change requirement is what distinguishes novation from amendment with complete rewriting.

The four novation types

Roman law (Digest 46.2) distinguished four forms of novation, all still operative in modern common law and civil law systems:

Substitution of debtor (expromissio / delegatio)

DD (original debtor) is replaced by D′D' (new debtor). CC (creditor) releases DD and accepts D′D' as the new obligor. K1=(D→C:pay X)K_1 = (D \to C : \text{pay X}) is extinguished; K2=(D′→C:pay X)K_2 = (D' \to C : \text{pay X}) is constituted.

Delegatio (Dig. 46.2.11): DD authorizes D′D' to assume the obligation; CC accepts the substitution. The requirement: CC’s express consent — a creditor cannot have their debtor changed without agreeing to accept the new one. Nemo invitus compellitur ad novationem (Dig. 46.2.14: no one is compelled to novate against their will).

Substitution of creditor (cessio in novationem)

CC is replaced by C′C'. Less common because assignment of credits (K1→K1′K_1 \to K_1' with C′=CC' = C) achieves the same result without requiring DD’s consent. True novation of the creditor requires extinguishment of DD’s obligation to CC and creation of a new obligation to C′C', requiring DD’s consent to the new obligation.

Substitution of obligation (novation of terms)

Same parties; different terms. The subject matter, the consideration, or the conditions change. Example: a loan obligation is novated by a promissory note (Dig. 46.2.1: “si quis stipulatus sit… novatio fit”). The old claim is extinguished; the note creates a new claim with its own defenses and limitations period.

This is the type most easily confused with amendment. The test: is the community preserved? If the parties are the same and the new obligation is a morphism from the old one (continuous, conservative or otherwise), it is amendment (Amendment). If the new obligation extinguishes the old and reconstitutes entirely, even between the same parties, it is novation.

Addition of a surety or condition

A new party is added to an existing obligation, transforming a simple bilateral obligation into a three-party structure. This is novation because the original obligation (A owes B) is extinguished and replaced by a new tripartite structure. In modern law, this is more typically handled by surety addition without novation, but historically it required novation.

Novation vs. amendment

The central distinction (see Amendment):

Dimension Amendment Novation
Community identity Preserved (G1=G2G_1 = G_2) Changed (G1≠G2G_1 \neq G_2 — at least one party changes)
Continuity of obligation Obligation persists through change Original obligation extinguished; new obligation created
Prior defenses Carried forward Not automatically carried forward
Prior obligations Survive unless explicitly modified Discharged in full at extinction
Authorization Derived from obligation’s amendment procedure Requires fresh consent from all affected parties
Math Morphism in the category of charters Terminal morphism ∘ genesis — replacement, not morphism

The test for novation vs. amendment: “is there a morphism C1→C2\mathcal{C}_1 \to \mathcal{C}_2 that preserves community identity?” If yes, it is an amendment. If the only path from C1\mathcal{C}_1 to C2\mathcal{C}_2 passes through ∅\emptyset (extinction followed by genesis), it is novation.

Novation vs. assignment

Assignment transfers a right from one party to another without the obligor’s consent (the right to receive payment is alienable). Novation extinguishes the entire obligation and creates a new one. The difference:

Dimension Assignment Novation
Obligor’s consent Not required for assignment of rights Required (new obligation must be consented to)
Obligation continuity Original obligation persists; right changes hands Original obligation extinguished
Defenses Obligor retains all defenses against the original Obligor may lose defenses against new obligation
What transfers The right (a Hohfeldian claim) Nothing transfers — old extinguished, new created

Math grounding

The novation is the composition of two morphisms in the category of charters:

N=γ∘ε:K1→ε∅→γK2\mathcal{N} = \gamma \circ \varepsilon : K_1 \xrightarrow{\varepsilon} \emptyset \xrightarrow{\gamma} K_2

where:

  • ε:K1→∅\varepsilon : K_1 \to \emptyset is the terminal morphism — the unique morphism from K1K_1 to the empty charter (the charter with no obligations, no parties, no history); this is the categorical termination
  • γ:∅→K2\gamma : \emptyset \to K_2 is the genesis morphism — the unique morphism from the empty charter to K2K_2; this is the categorical constitution of a new obligation from nothing

In site-morphism terms (Amendment): ε\varepsilon collapses the site (TK1,JK1)(T_{K_1}, J_{K_1}) to the empty site (∅,∅)(\emptyset, \emptyset); γ\gamma constructs the new site (TK2,JK2)(T_{K_2}, J_{K_2}) from scratch. The agreement π\pi is the colimit of the diagram K1←ε−1∅→γK2K_1 \xleftarrow{\varepsilon^{-1}} \emptyset \xrightarrow{\gamma} K_2 — the party-wise consent that makes the composition effective.

The novation is not a morphism K1→K2K_1 \to K_2 (there is no site morphism between two charters with different communities — different community identity means different base categories, and there is no continuous functor between them unless the community is preserved). Instead it passes through the empty site.

Open questions

  • Whether the “tripartite consent” requirement (all parties must agree) corresponds to a limit condition in the category of charters: whether π\pi is the limit of the diagram consisting of all parties’ consent morphisms, and whether the novation is valid iff this limit exists.
  • Whether the requirement that K2K_2’s defenses are independent of K1K_1’s corresponds to the genesis morphism γ:∅→K2\gamma : \emptyset \to K_2 being an initial morphism (no information from K1K_1 is carried forward) rather than a partial morphism from K1K_1.
  • Whether there is a weaker notion — novation up to homotopy — where K1K_1 and K2K_2 are different but homotopy-equivalent charters (the same “up to continuous deformation”), and whether this corresponds to Morita equivalence of the corresponding sites.
