Commissioning

2026-04-01 The constitutive act by which an authority creates, authorizes, and purposes a vessel — installing a charter, naming a captain, and constituting the crew's mandate.

Commissioning is the act by which an authority constitutes a vessel for a purpose. A navy commissions a warship — the hull goes from being a construction project to being a ship in service, with a captain, a crew, and a mission. A confederacy commissions a ship by issuing a charter — the vessel goes from being a container to being a ship with an endeavor.

Commissioning is a constitutive speech act. Before commissioning, there is a vessel (a structure capable of holding things) and an authority (a group with the standing to purpose it). After commissioning, there is a ship (a vessel with a charter, a captain, and a crew). The act creates something that did not exist before — not the physical structure, but the institutional identity.

¶What commissioning does

Commissioning performs three acts simultaneously:

  1. Installs the charter. The commissioning authority declares the vessel’s purpose, governing policies, and scope. The charter is the document; commissioning is the act that makes it binding. A charter sitting in a drawer is a blueprint. A charter installed by commissioning is a constitutive document.

  2. Names the captain. The commissioning authority designates who commands. This is the investiture of the captain — the formal act that creates command authority. The captain’s authority flows from the commissioning, not from personal qualities or consent of the crew.

  3. Constitutes the mandate. The commissioning creates the authorization under which the crew operates. Before commissioning, people may be assigned to the vessel but they have no mandate. After commissioning, the crew’s work serves the charter’s purpose and falls under the captain’s command.

¶What distinguishes commissioning

From delegation. Delegation transfers existing authority from one party to another within an already-constituted structure. Commissioning creates a new structure. A captain delegates watch authority to a duty officer (the ship already exists). A confederacy commissions a ship (the ship comes into institutional existence through the act). Delegation is intra-structural; commissioning is constitutive.

From investiture. Investiture installs a person into an existing office. Commissioning creates the office, the vessel, and the mandate all at once. When a new captain is installed on an already-commissioned ship, that is investiture. When the ship is first constituted for service, that is commissioning. Commissioning includes investiture of the first captain but is not reducible to it.

From chartering. Chartering is the document; commissioning is the act. A charter can be drafted, revised, and debated without the vessel being commissioned. Commissioning is the moment the charter becomes binding — the speech act that constitutes the institutional reality. In admiralty: a charter party is a contract; commissioning is the ceremony that places the ship in service.

¶The commissioning authority

Not just anyone can commission. The act requires standing — the authority to constitute institutional reality. A confederacy has standing to commission ships because its member groups have delegated upward the authority to coordinate action. A sovereign has standing because sovereignty includes the power to constitute institutions. A private party can commission a venture (chartering a merchant ship) because the legal system recognizes their standing to contract.

The commissioning authority retains certain powers after commissioning: the power to relieve the captain, the power to amend the charter, and the power to decommission (withdraw the ship from service). These are not delegated to the ship — they are retained by the authority that constituted it. The captain commands the ship; the commissioning authority commands whether there is a ship.

¶Decommissioning

Decommissioning is the inverse act: the withdrawal of a vessel from service. The charter is dissolved, the captain is relieved, the crew is discharged. The vessel persists — the hull is still there — but the institutional identity is extinguished. A decommissioned ship is a vessel again: a container without a purpose.

Decommissioning requires the same authority that commissioned. A confederacy that commissioned a ship can decommission it. The captain cannot decommission the ship — that would be the captain dissolving their own authority, which is incoherent. The crew cannot decommission — they serve the ship, not the other way around.

¶Etymology

From Latin commissio — “a joining together, an entrusting.” Com (together) + mittere (to send, to let go). The commissioner is the person entrusted; commissioning is the act of entrusting. The naval commissioning ceremony — reading the orders, raising the ensign, the captain’s first command — ritualizes the constitutive moment: from hull to ship, from document to charter, from appointment to command.
