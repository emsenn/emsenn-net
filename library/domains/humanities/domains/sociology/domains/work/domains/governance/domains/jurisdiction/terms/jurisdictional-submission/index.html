JurisdictionalSubmission

Formal definition

A JurisdictionalSubmission is a triple (G,A,S)(G, A, S):

(G:Person,  A:Authority,  S⊆HJ)(G : \mathrm{Person},\; A : \mathrm{Authority},\; S \subseteq H_J)

where:

  • GG is the submitting party — the party who consents to AA’s jurisdiction; GG typically holds immunity from AA’s jurisdiction by default (personal jurisdiction requires consent or presence) and submission is the act that lifts that immunity
  • AA is the authority with jurisdiction J=(A,TJ,HJ,V)\mathcal{J} = (A, T_J, H_J, V) — the forum to which GG is consenting
  • S⊆HJS \subseteq H_J is the matter-scope — the specific sub-fiber within AA’s subject-matter jurisdiction that the submission covers; GG may submit to personal jurisdiction for some matters and not others

The submission act. JurisdictionalSubmission is an act δsub∈Htsub\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \in H^{\mathrm{sub}}_t that settles the personal jurisdiction component of VV:

δsub settled  ⇒  Vpersonal(t′,G)=⊤for all t′≥t,φ∈S\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \text{ settled} \;\Rightarrow\; V_{\mathrm{personal}}(t', G) = \top \quad \text{for all } t' \geq t, \varphi \in S

VpersonalV_{\mathrm{personal}} is the personal jurisdiction factor of the validity condition V:TJ×HJ→ΩV : T_J \times H_J \to \Omega. When VV decomposes as V=Vterritorial∧Vpersonal∧VspecificV = V_{\mathrm{territorial}} \wedge V_{\mathrm{personal}} \wedge V_{\mathrm{specific}}, submission settles Vpersonal(t′,G)=⊤V_{\mathrm{personal}}(t', G) = \top for the submitted scope SS — it does not affect the other factors.

Four routes to personal jurisdiction. JurisdictionalSubmission is one of four ways Vpersonal(t,G)=⊤V_{\mathrm{personal}}(t, G) = \top can be settled:

  1. Presence: GG is physically within TJT_J (territorial personal jurisdiction by presence)
  2. Domicile: GG is domiciled within TJT_J’s geographic scope
  3. JurisdictionalSubmission: GG explicitly consents to AA’s jurisdiction for SS
  4. Pre-existing relation: GG holds a role or status that constitutively implies submission (an employee submits to their employer’s authority over work matters; a member submits to their institution’s authority)

Only submission (route 3) produces a new fiber element δsub∈Ht∗\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \in H^*_t; the others are constitutive facts derivable from presence, domicile, or role-holding.

What submission does and does not do

JurisdictionalSubmission enables Case 1 of σtJ\sigma^{\mathcal{J}}_t. Before submission, a matter φ∈S\varphi \in S concerning GG at history t∈TJt \in T_J may satisfy all conditions except Vpersonal(t,G)=⊤V_{\mathrm{personal}}(t, G) = \top. The nucleus cannot fix JV(A,φ,t)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t). After submission, VpersonalV_{\mathrm{personal}} is settled; the remaining conditions determine whether the matter proceeds.

JurisdictionalSubmission does not expand subject-matter jurisdiction. GG’s submission to AA for matters SS does not give AA jurisdiction over matters outside HJH_J. JurisdictionalSubmission fills a gap in VV; it cannot expand TJT_J or HJH_J. An agreement between GG and AA purporting to give AA jurisdiction over matters outside HJH_J is ultra vires as a matter of institutional design — the normative system that constitutes AA determines HJH_J, not GG’s consent.

JurisdictionalSubmission is not authority delegation. Delegation transfers authority from AA to a subordinate. JurisdictionalSubmission transfers exposure from GG’s immunity to AA’s jurisdiction. The Hohfeldian positions are different: delegation exercises a power; submission waives an immunity.

Nuclear reading

Submission is the act by which a party GG fills the personal-jurisdiction component of the validity condition VV. At the nuclear level, the submission act is a fiber element δsub∈Ht\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \in H_t whose settlement in Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) represents GG’s consent being genuinely established.

Definition (submission as a fiber element). The submission act δsub∈Ht\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \in H_t is the proposition “party GG has consented to authority AA’s jurisdiction over matters in SS at tt.” The condition δsub∈Fix(σt)\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) means the consent is past-saturated: it carries the same restriction profile to all sub-histories and no further saturation can enlarge it. This is a definition, not a theorem.

Proposition 1 (submission does not expand Fix(σ_t) beyond H_J). Suppose δsub∈Fix(σt)\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) and let φ∈Ht\varphi \in H_t be a matter outside HJH_J (the subject-matter scope of J\mathcal{J}). The meet δsub∧φ\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \wedge \varphi is an element of HtH_t. By extensiveness (a≤σt(a)a \leq \sigma_t(a), see Saturation Nucleus) and meet-preservation:

σt(δsub∧φ)=σt(δsub)∧σt(φ)=δsub∧σt(φ).\sigma_t(\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \wedge \varphi) = \sigma_t(\delta_{\mathrm{sub}}) \wedge \sigma_t(\varphi) = \delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \wedge \sigma_t(\varphi).

Whether δsub∧φ∈Fix(σt)\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \wedge \varphi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) depends on σt(φ)=φ\sigma_t(\varphi) = \varphi, i.e., whether φ\varphi itself is in Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t). The nuclear axioms alone do not prevent φ∉HJ\varphi \notin H_J from being in Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t). The scope restriction φ∉HJ\varphi \notin H_J is a structural condition on the jurisdiction, not a consequence of the nuclear axioms. This confirms: submission cannot expand subject-matter jurisdiction, but the reason is the scope-boundary axiom, not the nuclear axioms alone.

Proposition 2 (settled submission conjoined with settled validity is settled). If δsub∈Fix(σt)\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) and JV(A,φ,t)=pos(A,t)∧φ∈Fix(σt)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) = \mathrm{pos}(A, t) \wedge \varphi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t), then δsub∧JV(A,φ,t)∈Fix(σt)\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \wedge \mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t).

Proof. By meet-preservation (see Meet Preservation):

σt(δsub∧JV(A,φ,t))=σt(δsub)∧σt(JV(A,φ,t))=δsub∧JV(A,φ,t).□\sigma_t(\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \wedge \mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t)) = \sigma_t(\delta_{\mathrm{sub}}) \wedge \sigma_t(\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t)) = \delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \wedge \mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t). \quad \square

The joint proposition “submission holds AND jurisdictional validity holds” is settled when each component is settled. This is the nuclear content of the claim that personal jurisdiction and subject-matter jurisdiction can both be satisfied simultaneously.

Proposition 3 (idempotence: submission does not compound). σt(σt(δsub))=σt(δsub)\sigma_t(\sigma_t(\delta_{\mathrm{sub}})) = \sigma_t(\delta_{\mathrm{sub}}).

Proof. Idempotence of σt\sigma_t (see Idempotence) applied to δsub∈Ht\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \in H_t. □\square

Applying the saturation nucleus to a submission act twice is the same as applying it once. There is no “more submitted” beyond the first saturation. A submission either reaches Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) or it does not; there is no progressive approach.

What is not derivable from the nuclear axioms alone. The claims that submission is irrevocable mid-proceeding, that it persists at all t′≥tt' \geq t, and that GG cannot withdraw consent once a matter is active are not consequences of the nuclear axioms. Irrevocability is a claim about the transfer nucleus: δsub∈Fix(Δt)\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t), meaning for every s⊥ts \perp t there exists b∈Hs⋆tb \in H_{s \star t} with H(is,t)(b)=δsubH(i_{s,t})(b) = \delta_{\mathrm{sub}} (see Transfer Nucleus). Whether δsub∈Fix(Δt)\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) is not derivable from the nuclear axioms; it depends on the extension structure of the fiber bundle and must be given as a structural property of the submission act. The commutation axiom σt∘Δt=Δt∘σt\sigma_t \circ \Delta_t = \Delta_t \circ \sigma_t (see Commutation) ensures the two conditions are compatible, but does not establish that δsub\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} satisfies both.

Remark (scope boundary of submission). The submission act δsub\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} is defined for matters φ∈S\varphi \in S. For φ∉S\varphi \notin S, there is no submission proposition in the fiber — δsub\delta_{\mathrm{sub}} simply does not exist for that matter. This is not a statement about σt\sigma_t failing to fix an existing element; it is a statement about which propositions exist in HtH_t. The nuclear axioms do not govern what propositions exist; they govern the closure structure of the propositions that do exist.

Open questions

  • Whether submission to one forum implicitly submits to its appellate hierarchy — whether GG’s submission to AA implies submission to A+A^+ (the authority that reviews AA’s decisions). This would be submission-upward-closure, a sheaf condition on the forum hierarchy.
  • Whether submission can be conditional: GG submits to AA’s jurisdiction only if AA applies rule RR. The formal question: can Vpersonal(t,G)V_{\mathrm{personal}}(t, G) be conditioned on the normative system N\mathcal{N} applying a specific rule, making submission a biconditional settlement rather than a one-way waiver.
