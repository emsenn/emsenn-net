The Temporality of Normative Systems

2026-04-04 How normative systems — norms, duties, mandates, governance structures — depend on temporal structure: norm lifecycles, governance recurrence, accountability horizons, and the mechanisms of legitimate normative change.

Every normative system — the structure of norms, duties, mandates, and governance that constrains human action — depends on temporal structure. Without explicit temporal boundaries, validity periods, discharge conditions, and recurrence patterns, governance systems collapse in practice. They may work on paper, with rules written down and authority structures documented, but they fail where time matters: in succession, in accountability after office, in the legitimacy of normative change, in the coordination of recurring decisions.

The error is treating temporal concepts as optional elaborations. They are not. Every governance concept has a temporal dimension. Ignoring it produces systems that fail under scrutiny.

¶Norm Lifecycles

A norm is not a timeless abstraction. It has a lifecycle: it is proposed, enacted, in force, amended, and repealed. This lifecycle is not a decoration on the norm; it is essential to understanding what the norm is.

H.L.A. Hart’s distinction between primary and secondary rules captures this. Primary rules impose duties directly: “stop at red lights,” “file a tax return,” “you must defend the constitution.” Secondary rules are rules about rules — they govern how norms are created, modified, and extinguished. Hart identifies three types of secondary rules:

Rules of recognition specify which norms are in force — the criteria by which a rule counts as valid. The rule of recognition is not itself a norm validated by a higher rule (that produces infinite regress); it is a social practice among officials. When a court declares “this statute violates the constitution and is therefore void,” the rule of recognition is at work: it specifies that constitutional conformity is the criterion of validity for statutes.

Rules of change authorize persons or bodies to introduce, modify, or repeal norms. A legislature’s authority to amend statutes, a constitutional convention’s power to rewrite the constitution, an elected board’s ability to adopt new policies — these are all rules of change. They answer the question: who can create new norms?

Rules of adjudication confer authority to determine whether a norm has been violated on a particular occasion. Courts, administrative agencies, disciplinary boards — these institutions embody rules of adjudication. They answer the question: who determines whether norms apply?

Without secondary rules, a normative system cannot change. It is static. Primary rules alone can sustain custom in a small, stable community (they know which rules apply, no one wants to change them, disputes are rare). But any complex modern institution requires rules of change: the written constitution must be amendable, statutes must be modifiable, regulations must evolve. The temporal structure — the ability to move from one set of norms to another — is what makes the system viable.

Hans Kelsen’s principle of lex posterior — the later norm overrides the earlier norm when they conflict — is itself a temporal rule. It specifies how norms persist, how they interact over time, and when older normative commitments are displaced by newer ones. Without lex posterior, a contradictory norm-set is incoherent. With it, the normative system has a built-in resolution: time orders conflicting norms.

¶Governance Recurrence

Democratic governance depends on a particular temporal structure: recurrence. Elections recur, terms expire and renew, audits repeat, reviews are periodic. Recurrence is not a luxury feature of governance — it is the temporal mechanism that prevents power concentration.

Consider the alternative. If a leader were elected once, for life, with no periodic re-election or review, the system is a monarchy, not a democracy. The difference is not in the powers granted to the leader (both grant extensive authority) but in the temporal structure: democracy imposes cyclical review and renewal of authority.

The Haudenosaunee Confederacy’s Great Law of Peace (Gayanashagowa) encodes recurrence at the generational scale: decisions are made in awareness of their effects seven generations forward. This is not mysticism — it is temporal horizon-setting. It constrains present choice by making future consequence salient. The recurrence is generational: every seven generations, the effects of present norms return, in a sense, as burden or benefit.

Modern democratic theory formalizes this differently: one-term limits (U.S. presidency), periodic elections (parliaments, congresses), regular audits (financial, operational, compliance), and scheduled reviews (policy review cycles, strategic planning). Each recurrence is a moment at which authority is recommitted (re-elected, reconfirmed, re-audited) or redirected (new policy). Without recurrence, accountability disappears: an official who cannot be removed, who faces no periodic review, who has no term limit, is accountable only to themselves.

Recurrence is the temporal mechanism of accountability.

¶Accountability Horizons

Accountability is defined as a triple: a bearer, a scope, and a total accountability function (every outcome in the scope is assigned to the bearer). But the definition omits a temporal question: does accountability persist after the bearer is relieved from their position?

The formal definition of investiture specifies that relief dissolves the role-binding — the outgoing holder no longer has authority. But does accountability persist?

The answer is no if we understand accountability as tied exclusively to the duration of office. The doctrine of “what I do not own, I am not responsible for” treats accountability as office-bounded: a captain is accountable for their ship’s crew, but after the watch ends, accountability passes to the next captain. The prior captain is no longer answerable.

But accountability can persist beyond relief if the normative system so specifies. War crimes tribunals hold former leaders accountable for acts committed during their tenure — accountability survives investiture’s relief act. Constitutional term limits impose a lifecycle on offices but not on accountability: a president’s impeachment and trial can occur after leaving office, extending accountability forward in time.

Sunset clauses impose lifecycles on norms themselves: laws that expire unless renewed force periodic legislative reconsideration. They make the temporal horizon of the norm explicit. A sunset clause says: “this norm is in force for five years, then it expires unless reaffirmed.” It embeds renewal as a design feature, not an afterthought.

The temporal horizon of accountability is a design choice, not a natural fact. The governance system must specify: does accountability for acts extend into post-office time? If yes, for how long? Until what trigger? The system’s answer determines whether accountability is office-bounded or perpetual, whether it is absolute or sunset-constrained.

¶Norm Evolution vs. Norm Revolution

The central problem: how do normative systems change without collapsing?

One path is gradual evolution through legitimate amendment. Hart’s rule of change specifies who can amend norms and how. When the rule of change is exercised, the old norm is explicitly replaced by a new one. The lifecycle continues: a norm is proposed, enacted, amended, re-enacted, amended again, then repealed. The system preserves continuity because the rules themselves (the secondary rules) do not change. Only the primary norms change, within the framework of the existing secondary rules.

The common law tradition exemplifies this pattern: each case is a recurrence of prior legal questions in new circumstances. A precedent is not a static norm but a template that each subsequent case modifies. The law evolves through interpretation and distinction, not through explicit amendment. The secondary rule — stare decisis, respect for precedent — governs how primary law evolves.

The statutory tradition exemplifies a different evolution pattern: explicit amendment. The legislature, exercising the rule of change, rewrites statutes. The temporal landmark is clear: before the amendment, the old text was in force; after, the new text is. The evolution is deliberate and documented.

Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh) evolves through ijtihad — reasoned interpretation that adapts norms to new circumstances while maintaining continuity with foundational texts (Quran and Sunnah). The secondary rule governing ijtihad permits qualified jurists to reason from principles to new cases. The norms evolve without abandoning the foundational sources.

All three patterns solve the same problem: maintaining the legitimacy of normative systems across time despite changing circumstances. They differ in their temporal mechanics: common law evolves through repetition with distinction, statute through explicit rewriting, Islamic jurisprudence through principled interpretation. But all three recognize that normative systems must change to survive. A system that refuses to evolve either dies (loses authority because it addresses dead problems) or experiences revolution (the system is abandoned and replaced, not evolved).

Revolution breaks the lifecycle — old norms are abolished, new ones created, often without continuity with the prior system’s secondary rules. The American, French, and Russian revolutions each broke with the prior system’s rules of recognition and rules of change. Whether revolution produces legitimacy (whether the new secondary rules are accepted) depends on factors beyond the temporal mechanics: consent of the governed, military force, ideological persuasion. But the temporal structure is clear: revolution is the failure of evolutionary mechanisms, not a replacement for them.

¶Mandates and Duties Without Temporal Boundaries

The governance domain defines mandate as a triple: an authorizing source, a prescribed act, and a compulsion condition (when triggered, performance is compelled). It defines duty as a five-tuple: a bearer, a position, a normative system, a required act, and a correlative party.

Neither definition includes a temporal boundary.

A mandate is “standing obligation” — the compulsion persists indefinitely once triggered. A duty is discharged when performed, but the definition does not specify when the duty itself arises, when it becomes active, when it expires.

This is an open gap. In practice, institutions fill it:

Duties arise at investiture. A duty officer’s watch-standing duty does not exist until they assume the watch; it ceases when they are relieved. The duty is bounded by the lifecycle of the office.

Mandates are bounded by statute or policy. A mandate to “file a report” within a government agency exists because a statute or regulation requires it. If the statute is repealed, the mandate expires.

But the formal definitions of mandate and duty do not yet encode these temporal boundaries. They leave a question open: how do duties and mandates persist through time? What makes them time-limited or standing? The domain recognizes the question but has not yet formalized the answer.

¶Norm Validity as Temporal Settledness

Kelsen’s account of norm validity — that a norm is valid when it was created by authorized procedure and is part of the hierarchy tracing back to the Grundnorm — is partly temporal and partly structural. A norm is valid (in force) when it has been properly enacted and when no later norm (under lex posterior) has repealed it.

But the nuclear model of the governance domain adds precision. A norm is valid (operative, in force) when it is in the saturation nucleus’s fixed point — when the entire accumulated history of the normative system has settled on its meaning. The norm persists despite violation because the nuclear model tracks whether a norm is meaning-settled, not whether it is obeyed.

This is the answer to a temporal puzzle: why does a violated norm remain a norm? Because the nuclear model distinguishes between the norm as a fixed point of the saturation nucleus (meaning-settled across the history) and the norm as executed in the transfer nucleus (actually performed, carried out). A norm can be meaning-settled without being execution-settled — obligatory but violated. The norm persists as meaning-settled even when execution fails.

This temporal structure — meaning-settlement vs. execution-settlement — is essential. It explains how legal systems function despite widespread violation: the norms are meaning-settled (everyone recognizes them as in force) even though execution is partial. The system does not collapse because norm validity is not behavior-dependent.

The temporal dimension of governance is not a supplement to the structural analysis of norms, duties, mandates, and accountability. It is constitutive. Every governance concept implies a temporal structure: when it arises, how it persists, when it expires, how it changes legitimately, when it recurs. Governance systems that ignore these temporal requirements — that fail to specify when authority transfers, when accountability ends, how norms evolve, when decisions are revisited — do not fail for lack of written rules; they fail for lack of temporal coherence.

The governance domain has the concepts of investiture, accountability, norm, and norm-conflict. What it is building toward is a comprehensive temporal framework: when do duties come alive? How do mandates expire? What does recurrence mean for accountability? How do normative systems legitimately evolve? These are not side questions. They are the center of what governance is.
