Leitourgia

2026-03-19 A Leitourgia is a quadruple (λ, φ, C, ι) — a designated agent in Hohfeldian liability position, a prescribed-form proposition, a witnessing community constituted by the gathering of compatible local witnesses, and a discharge proposition. Full discharge is joint settlement of two predicates: form-enactment (the act has been correctly performed) and forward-stability (the discharge carries forward into all continuations). The personal cost is the irreversible expenditure the leitourgos bears in performing the step that settles the discharge.

¶What it is

A Leitourgia is a quadruple (λ,  φ,  C,  ι)(\lambda,\; \varphi,\; C,\; \iota):

Λ=(λ,  φ,  C,  ι)\Lambda = (\lambda,\; \varphi,\; C,\; \iota)

where:

  • λ\lambda is the leitourgos — an Entity in Hohfeldian liability position with respect to the community CC: designated by the appointing authority’s power, not by self-selection. Olson’s positional compulsion (The Logic of Collective Action, 1965, p.2): the obligation follows from station, not consent. The leitourgos is not an agent who has decided to serve; the station designates the service.
  • φ\varphi is the prescribed-form proposition — a determinate normative specification of what correct enactment consists in. Austin felicity condition A.1 (How to Do Things with Words, 1962, p.14): “there must exist an accepted conventional procedure having a certain conventional effect.” The prescribed form is not a general contribution but a definite specification with a yes/no satisfaction condition.
  • CC is the witnessing community — not a pre-existing assembly but the entity constituted by the gathering of a coherent family of witnessing observations. Bell (Ritual Theory, Ritual Practice, 1992, pp.69–93): “ritualization constitutes the community rather than expressing it.” CC exists as CC because and only because the enactment is collectively witnessed across a coherent cover. Before the cover gathers there is no community — there are only local observers.
  • ι\iota is the discharge proposition — the claim that λ\lambda enacted φ\varphi before CC. The discharge proposition is what records whether the enactment has occurred; its settlement is what determines whether the leitourgia has been fulfilled.

Type note. φ\varphi and ι\iota are distinct claims. The prescribed form φ\varphi is a standing normative specification — it exists whether or not any leitourgos has acted. The discharge proposition ι\iota is contingent on λ\lambda’s actual performance. What earlier formulations conflated — the normative form and the enactment record — are distinct. This parallels the distinction in Incumbent between the incumbency proposition ι\iota (whether aa holds OO) and the office structure OO (which holds regardless of any occupant).

Full discharge is the condition that two distinct predicates over ι\iota have both reached affirming settlement at the discharge history tt:

  • Form-enactment — the predicate that φ\varphi has been correctly performed in front of CC
  • Forward-stability — the predicate that the discharge carries forward into continuations of tt (no successor will be called upon to discharge what λ\lambda has already discharged)

When only one of the two predicates has settled affirmingly, the service is partially discharged.

Personal cost. The enactment step that settles ι\iota carries a structurally irreducible cost: the witnessings λ\lambda produces about ι\iota are not retractable, and the resources λ\lambda commits to performing φ\varphi correctly are spent. The leitourgos bears this cost as a structural consequence of the step, not as a free parameter. Demosthenes (Against Meidias 21.155) documents the trierarch’s irreversible personal expenditure as the structural marker of genuine leitourgia. The irreversibility is not a defect but the defining mark distinguishing genuine leitourgia from a performance of leitourgia.

This irreversibility has a thermodynamic analog in Landauer’s principle (IBM Journal of Research and Development 5:183–191, 1961): logical operations that erase information in their final state require non-trivial physical resources. Whether the leitourgos’s personal cost is formally identical to the Landauer cost of the enactment step, or merely structurally analogous, is an open question — see below.

¶The Athenian structure

Leitourgia (Greek: leitos, public + ergon, work) was in classical Athens a legally required public service borne by wealthy citizens at their own expense: funding a trireme (trierarchy), equipping a dramatic chorus (choregia), provisioning a sacred embassy (theoria). Not a tax, which is distributed; not charity, which is voluntary. A positional obligation — compelled by civic standing, borne at irreversible personal cost, directed toward a specific form whose correct enactment benefited the whole community.

The structural features that persist from this civic institution:

  1. Designation by position, not choice: the liturgical class was determined by assessed wealth. A man who crossed the wealth threshold owed the service. Nomination by a rival (antidosis) was the mechanism for identifying who held the position (Davies 1971).
  2. A determinate prescribed form: not “contribute to the navy” but “provide a specific trireme, crewed and maintained, for one year.” The form had a yes/no satisfaction condition. Demosthenes Against Meidias 21.155 documents the legal content of correct trierarchic performance.
  3. A community constituted by the performance: the Athenian theatrical festivals were not performances for an audience that existed independently. The audience was constituted as the demos — the political body of Athens — by assembling in the theater and witnessing the choruses funded by the liturgy. Aristotle Politics 1280b30: the city is not a commercial alliance but is constituted by shared participation in its own institutions.
  4. Irreversible personal cost: the trierarch could not recover the expenditure. This irreversibility was the liturgy’s marker of genuine civic commitment — it distinguished it from a refundable levy or a performance for fee.

¶The two-stage discharge

The Incumbent spec derives two installation stages — institution and induction — from ecclesiastical law. The leitourgia has a structurally parallel two-stage discharge:

Form-enacted (perfectus actus): The leitourgos enacts φ\varphi in the correct form before CC. The accumulation of relational history — the gathering of the witnessing family — settles the form-enactment predicate over ι\iota in the affirming direction. The past has no further demand. This corresponds to Austin’s felicity condition A.2 (complete execution) — the procedure is complete.

Forward-stable (perpetua effectus): The discharge carries forward into continuations of tt; the forward-stability predicate over ι\iota has reached affirming settlement. Every future history inherits the discharge as already-accomplished. No successor can be called upon to discharge what λ\lambda has already discharged.

Stage Settlement state What it gives
Enacted only Form-enactment predicate settled affirmingly; forward-stability predicate not yet Past has recognized the form-enactment; the discharge is not yet carried into all continuations — future histories could re-open the question
Forward-stable only Forward-stability predicate settled; form-enactment predicate not yet All future histories carry the discharge forward; the accumulated past has not yet fully recognized correct enactment — anomalous, normally impossible without form-enactment first
Full discharge Both predicates settled affirmingly Past-settled and forward-stable; the community inherits the enacted service across all extensions; no further normative demand can be made

The ordering — form-enactment before forward-stability — is the normal sequence. The reverse order is structurally anomalous: ongoing forward-stability without past-recognition would be a discharge that propagates without ever having been performed.

¶Community-constitution

The witnessing community CC at history tt is constituted as follows: a family of local witnessing observations — one at each history in a coherent covering of tt — forms a compatible family iff the observations agree where their domains overlap. The framework’s covering structure on histories ensures that every compatible family gathers into a unique global witnessing record. The community CC is the entity indexed by this gathered record: it exists as a unified entity iff the witnessing observations cohere across the cover.

Before the covering gathers, there are local witnesses but no community. The community comes into existence at the moment the compatible family becomes a global record. This is the formal content of Bell’s claim that ritualization constitutes the community: the gathering of compatible witnesses is the constitution.

The claim that ι\iota has reached affirming settlement requires this community-constitution: not only must the enactment occur, but the witnessing must cohere. An enactment witnessed by an incoherent family — one that cannot be gathered into a unified record — does not discharge the leitourgia, because there is no community before whom discharge could be recognized. Austin’s felicity condition B.1 (How to Do Things with Words, 1962, p.15): “the particular persons and circumstances in a given case must be appropriate for the invocation of the particular procedure invoked.” Inappropriate witnesses = incoherent cover = no gathered record = no community = no discharge.

¶Leitourgia levels

Four levels of discharge stability, from least to most complete:

Level Condition What it names
Designated / pending Neither predicate settled λ\lambda is designated and the obligation exists, but the service has not been rendered
Form-enacted only Form-enactment predicate settled; forward-stability not yet The enactment has been performed and the accumulated past recognizes it; the discharge is not yet forward-inherited
Forward-stable only Forward-stability predicate settled; form-enactment not yet All future histories inherit the discharge; the past has not yet settled the recognition — structurally anomalous
Fully discharged Both predicates settled affirmingly Past-recognized and future-inherited; the service is permanently complete

¶Theological development

The Septuagint uses leitourgia for Temple priestly service (1 Chr 23:28; 26:30; Num 8:22); the New Testament and patristic literature extend it to corporate worship. The structural transformation: the designated minister enacts a prescribed form before the assembly; the cost shifts from financial (trierarchy’s outlay) to consecrated time, purity, and submission to form; the recipient shifts from the city to the divine. Bradshaw (The Search for the Origins of Christian Worship, Oxford, 2002, pp.1–30) traces how the Eucharist inherits the positional-designation structure from both civic and Temple antecedents.

The invariant structure persists across this transformation: designated leitourgos (λ\lambda), prescribed form (φ\varphi), community constituted by the assembly’s witnessing (CC), personal cost. The two-stage discharge — form-enactment recognized by accumulated normative tradition, then forward-stability inherited by all future histories — maps onto the distinction in sacramental theology between valid celebration (perfecta executio) and the permanent effect of valid celebration (character indelebilis: the priesthood, once conferred, cannot be rescinded). Whether this theological extension generates derivable claims or remains interpretive is an open question of the same kind as the institution/induction mapping in Incumbent.

¶Open questions

  • Whether the framework’s covering structure on histories captures social witnessing: The community-constitution hypothesis states that the witnessing family must form a coherent cover for gathering to occur. But the framework’s covering structure is defined by the step-commutation and history-extension relations on histories — it is not defined by social witnessing. Whether social witnessing (the presence of the relevant witnesses at tt) is captured by the existing covering structure, or whether a leitourgia-specific enrichment is required, is not yet derived.

  • Whether form-enactment settlement entails forward-stability for discharge propositions: The two-stage table above lists form-enactment and forward-stability as independent conditions. But for discharge propositions of a specific type ("λ\lambda enacted φ\varphi before CC"), the structural conditions may suffice to show that form-enactment settlement implies forward-stability. If this implication holds for the discharge proposition specifically, the two-stage distinction collapses to a single condition for leitourgia and the distinction from Incumbent is formal, not substantive. This derivation is not yet carried out.

  • Whether the leitourgos’s personal cost is formally identical to the thermodynamic cost of the enactment step: The hypothesis identifies the personal cost borne by λ\lambda with the irreducible cost of an irreversible logical step (Landauer 1961). This is motivated: the step produces witnessings about ι\iota that cannot be retracted; the leitourgos, occupying the position that performs the step, bears the irreversible production. But this identification requires a model of what it means for λ\lambda — an Entity at history tt — to bear the cost of a step that produces witnessings at the successor history. Whether the Entity λ\lambda is formally the system that undergoes the irreversibility, or merely occupies the position that triggers the step, is not yet derived.
