Principal

What it is

A Principal is the source from which delegated authority derives within a normative sub-system: a party whose authority propositions are not themselves derived from any superior authority fiber within the sub-system, and who may generate new authority relations — delegations — by creating restriction maps from their own fixed fiber into agent fibers.

The structural fact from which everything follows: within the sub-system, there is no restriction map descending from above that constitutes the principal’s authority. The principal’s fixed fiber HtP∗=Fix(σtP)∩Fix(ΔtP)H^{P*}_t = \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^P_t) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta^P_t) is the terminal stratum of the authority lattice within the sub-system. All agent authority is in the image of some restriction map originating from HtP∗H^{P*}_t.

Two structural asymmetries define principalship. First, restriction maps flow DOWN: delegation morphisms ρa:HP→Ha\rho_a : H^P \to H^a carry authority from the principal’s fiber into agent fibers, never the reverse. Second, accountability does not flow: the accountability proposition α∈HtnowP∗\alpha \in H^{P*}_{t_{\mathrm{now}}} is not in the image of any ρa\rho_a, and cannot be moved out of the principal’s fixed fiber by any act of any agent. The agent acts in the principal’s name; the consequences bind the principal; the principal cannot escape by pointing at the agent.

Formal quadruple

A Principal is a quadruple P=(HP,Q,ρ,α)\mathcal{P} = (H^P, Q, \rho, \alpha):

P=(HP:Top→HAnucl,  Q,  ρ:Ag→HomR(HP,H(−)),  α∈HtnowP∗)\mathcal{P} = (H^P : T^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{HA_{nucl}},\; Q,\; \rho : \mathrm{Ag} \to \mathrm{Hom}_R(H^P, H^{(-)}),\; \alpha \in H^{P*}_{t_{\mathrm{now}}})

where:

  • HPH^P is the original authority fiber doctrine — the fiber nuclear doctrine with saturation nucleus σtP\sigma^P_t and transfer nucleus ΔtP\Delta^P_t commuting at each t∈Tt \in T; the principal’s authority exists as a global structure over all of TT, not as a single proposition at one history
  • QQ is the action domain — the scope within which the principal’s authority operates; no authority extends beyond QQ
  • ρ\rho is the delegation profile — the function assigning to each agent a∈Aga \in \mathrm{Ag} a morphism ρa:HP→Ha\rho_a : H^P \to H^a of nuclear fiber doctrines in R=Sh(T,J)R = \mathbf{Sh}(T, J); ρa\rho_a carries a sub-quota of the principal’s authority into the agent’s fiber without depleting HtP∗H^{P*}_t
  • α∈HtnowP∗\alpha \in H^{P*}_{t_{\mathrm{now}}} is the accountability proposition — the doubly-stable element asserting that P\mathcal{P} holds original authority over QQ at tnowt_{\mathrm{now}}; it satisfies σtnowP(α)=α\sigma^P_{t_{\mathrm{now}}}(\alpha) = \alpha (meaning-settled: the accumulated normative history recognizes the authority) and ΔtnowP(α)=α\Delta^P_{t_{\mathrm{now}}}(\alpha) = \alpha (forward-stable: every extension of tnowt_{\mathrm{now}} carries the accountability)

Originality condition. P\mathcal{P} is a principal (not merely an agent) iff: for all t∈Tt \in T and all fiber doctrines HotherH^{\mathrm{other}} in the sub-system, there is no sheaf morphism ρ↑:Hother→HP\rho^{\uparrow} : H^{\mathrm{other}} \to H^P in RR whose image intersects HtP∗H^{P*}_t non-trivially. The principal’s fixed fiber is not constituted by any restriction from above within the sub-system. This is system-relative originality: externally, HPH^P may itself be constituted by a statute, an election, or a meta-authority — but within the sub-system under consideration, no such constituting map exists.

Accountability non-transfer. For all a∈Aga \in \mathrm{Ag}, α∉image(ρa(Hta∗))\alpha \notin \mathrm{image}(\rho_a(H^{a*}_t)). Delegation creates morphisms going down from the principal to agents; accountability propositions do not travel along those morphisms. This is the formal content of retained accountability: α\alpha is doubly stable in the principal’s own fiber, and no act of any agent can reduce α\alpha’s stability. The agent’s misuse of delegated authority does not discharge the principal.

Revocability. Removing ρa\rho_a from ρ\rho terminates the agent’s borrowed authority. Since ρa\rho_a is a morphism in RR, its withdrawal means HaH^a receives no restriction from HPH^P; the agent’s authority propositions revert to whatever their fiber independently contains. The principal’s own authority α∈HtnowP∗\alpha \in H^{P*}_{t_{\mathrm{now}}} is unaffected: it was never in HtaH^a_t to begin with.

The mandatum doctrine: Roman principal-agent

The Roman law of mandate (mandatum) in Gaius Institutiones 3.155–162 and Digest Title 17.1 provides the classical legal structure from which all modern principal-agent law descends, and maps onto the delegation morphism with unusual precision.

The mandate relationship. Gaius (Inst. 3.155) identifies the mandate’s scope: “Mandatum consistit sive nostra gratia mandemus sive aliena” — the mandate is constituted whether we direct something for our own benefit or another’s. The mandator (principal) creates the mandatarius’s (agent’s) authority by issuing the mandate; the mandate is the delegation morphism ρa\rho_a. Ulpian (Dig. 17.1.1 pr.) adds the crucial restriction: a mandate given purely for the benefit of the mandatarius is not a true mandate but merely advice (consilium). The mandate structure requires that ρa\rho_a carries authority for purposes that originate in HtP∗H^{P*}_t — a delegation that serves only the agent’s independent interests creates no restriction map originating in the principal’s fiber.

The scope boundary. Acting outside the mandate (extra mandatum) creates no obligation for the principal; the act binds only the agent. Ulpian (Dig. 17.1.5.2): “Si quis mandatum suscepit et ultra vel citra fines mandati egit, mandati actio nata est.” — if someone took a mandate and acted beyond or within its limits, the mandate action is born. The nuclear reading: acts in the image of ρa\rho_a bind P\mathcal{P} (via qui facit per alium); acts outside the image bind only HtaH^a_t. The actio mandati directa is the action the principal can bring against the agent for failing to execute the mandate (failure to deploy the delegated scope); the actio mandati contraria is the action the agent can bring for compensation beyond the expected costs. These are the two asymmetric sides of the morphism structure: the principal demands fidelity to ρa\rho_a; the agent demands indemnification for costs incurred within ρa\rho_a.

Negotiorum gestio as unauthorized delegation. Negotiorum gestio (Dig. 3.5) is the Roman analog of unauthorized agency: acting on another’s affairs without a mandate. The gestor has no delegation morphism ρa\rho_a from the principal, but acts in the principal’s interest under emergency necessity. The law creates quasi-contractual obligations between gestor and dominus. The nuclear reading: where no ρa\rho_a exists but the gestor’s act is within the domain QQ, the act creates a new obligation in HtnowH_{t_{\mathrm{now}}} — a proposition that is not doubly-stable (not in HtP∗H^{P*}_t) but that the law’s own nucleus operators (the Grothendieck topology JJ) will drive toward stability. Negotiorum gestio is the case where the sheaf condition on the covering sieve generates authority ex post that was not authorized ex ante.

Hobbes’s authorization and the Actor-Author structure

Hobbes’s Leviathan Ch. XVI (1651) gives the earliest systematic analysis of what it means to act in another’s name — and maps precisely onto the restriction-map structure.

Author and Actor. Hobbes distinguishes the Author (whose words and actions are owned) from the Actor (who performs actions belonging to another): “Of Persons Artificial, some have their words and actions Owned by those whom they represent. And then the Person is the Actor; and he that owneth his words and actions, is the AUTHOR.” The Author-Actor relation is the triple (P,a,ρa)(\mathcal{P}, a, \rho_a): the principal is the Author who owns; the agent is the Actor who performs; the delegation morphism ρa\rho_a is the authorization that constitutes the relation.

Authorization as right-conferring. Hobbes: “And as the Right of possession, is called Dominion; so the Right of doing any Action, is called AUTHORITY.” Authorization is the conferral of a Hohfeldian power (Hohfeld’s second quartet): the right to do an action within the authorized scope. In nuclear terms: ρa(αscope)∈Hta\rho_a(\alpha^{\mathrm{scope}}) \in H^a_t is the agent’s authority proposition — the element in the agent’s fiber that Hobbes calls the “right of doing.” The agent acting within ρa\rho_a’s image acts by authority; outside it, the agent acts in her own name only.

Unauthorized acts and the mecovenanter. Hobbes: acts “not by Commission, nor by any other Declaration of the Author, are his own.” An agent acting without authorization accumulates obligations in HtaH^a_t that are not in any restriction from HtP∗H^{P*}_t. The normative consequence of the unauthorized act is the agent’s alone. This is the formal content of delegatus non potest delegare: the composition ρb∘ρa\rho_b \circ \rho_a creates obligations in HtbH^b_t that are not in any restriction from HtP∗H^{P*}_t unless P\mathcal{P} separately authorizes — the unauthorized sub-delegation produces a chain of fibers that the principal’s accountability proposition does not reach.

Agency cost as nuclear gap: the Jensen-Meckling reading

The principal-agent problem of Jensen and Meckling (1976) arises from the separation of ownership and control: the owner (principal) cannot perfectly monitor the manager (agent), and the manager’s interests may diverge from the owner’s. In nuclear terms, this is a divergence between two fixed fibers.

The divergence structure. The principal’s fixed fiber HtP∗H^{P*}_t contains the propositions that the principal’s nuclei (σtP,ΔtP)(\sigma^P_t, \Delta^P_t) stabilize — what the principal recognizes as authoritative and what the principal regards as forward-committed. The agent’s fixed fiber Hta∗H^{a*}_t contains the propositions stabilized by the agent’s own nuclei (σta,Δta)(\sigma^a_t, \Delta^a_t). The delegation morphism ρa:HP→Ha\rho_a : H^P \to H^a carries the scope authorization from the principal’s fiber to the agent’s fiber. The divergence: ρa(HtP∗)≠Hta∗\rho_a(H^{P*}_t) \neq H^{a*}_t. What the principal stabilizes is not what the agent independently stabilizes. The agency problem exists whenever this inequality holds.

Monitoring as nucleus-convergence. Monitoring expenditures (Jensen-Meckling cost type 1) are costs incurred by the principal to drive ρa(αscope)\rho_a(\alpha^{\mathrm{scope}}) toward Hta∗H^{a*}_t — to make the agent’s exercise of the delegated scope converge with the principal’s own nucleus operators. The monitoring cost is proportional to the gap between ρa(HtP∗)\rho_a(H^{P*}_t) and Hta∗H^{a*}_t: the larger the nuclear divergence, the more costly it is to verify alignment. In the limit: if ρa\rho_a is a nuclear fiber doctrine morphism (a morphism that commutes with both pairs of nuclei), monitoring cost is zero — the agent’s nucleus operators are exactly the restrictions of the principal’s. The Jensen-Meckling framing of “perfect principal-agent alignment” corresponds to ρa\rho_a being a nuclear morphism.

Residual loss as permanent gap. The residual loss (type 3) = propositions in HtP∗H^{P*}_t that the agent’s acts cannot recover, because the nuclear divergence cannot be closed without changing the identity of the agent. This is the permanent cost of the separation of ownership and control: the principal would have to become the agent (eliminate ρa\rho_a and act directly) to close the gap entirely. The residual loss is the welfare equivalent of HtP∗∖image(ρa(Hta∗))H^{P*}_t \setminus \mathrm{image}(\rho_a(H^{a*}_t)) — the principal’s stable authority propositions that lie outside what the agent’s fiber can deliver.

The trust-revocation condition: Locke

Locke’s Second Treatise develops the most sustained classical argument for the revertibility of delegated authority. In Ch. XIII §149, Locke describes the legislature as holding “only a Fiduciary Power to act for certain ends” — the legislature is an agent, not a principal; its authority is a delegation from the people.

The fiduciary structure. The legislative authority is the delegation morphism ρleg:HPeople→HLeg\rho_{\mathrm{leg}} : H^{\mathrm{People}} \to H^{\mathrm{Leg}}. The people’s authority proposition α∈HtnowP∗\alpha \in H^{P*}_{t_{\mathrm{now}}} is the original accountability — it is never in HtLegH^{\mathrm{Leg}}_t. The Legislature has only ρleg(αscope)\rho_{\mathrm{leg}}(\alpha^{\mathrm{scope}}) — a bounded delegation of the people’s authority into the legislative fiber.

Forfeiture and revocation. Ch. XIX §221: when the legislature “transgress” the trust — when legislative acts exit the scope of ρleg\rho_{\mathrm{leg}} — the delegation morphism is forfeit. “There remains still in the People a Supreame Power to remove or alter the Legislative”: the people’s original authority proposition α∈HtnowP∗\alpha \in H^{P*}_{t_{\mathrm{now}}} was never transferred; it persists through any legislative failure, waiting to be expressed through a new ρleg′\rho_{\mathrm{leg}'}. The new legislature is a new restriction map from the same source fiber.

The principal as judge. §240 gives the clearest statement of the structural asymmetry: “The people shall be judge; for who shall be judge whether his trustee or deputy acts well, and according to the trust reposed in him, but he who deputes him.” The entity who constituted the trust is the only one with standing to evaluate whether it has been honored. No agent can judge its own compliance; no sub-institution can evaluate whether the principal’s terms have been met. This is the formal content of the accountability non-transfer: the principal’s accountability proposition α∈HtnowP∗\alpha \in H^{P*}_{t_{\mathrm{now}}} is the standard against which all agent fibers are measured, and the principal is the only one whose nuclei (σtP,ΔtP)(\sigma^P_t, \Delta^P_t) set that standard.

The non-transfer of sovereignty. Locke’s argument implies a condition stronger than revocability: the people’s sovereignty is not merely recoverable in case of forfeiture — it is structurally non-transferable. α\alpha is never in ρleg(HtLeg)\rho_{\mathrm{leg}}(H^{\mathrm{Leg}}_t) because ρleg\rho_{\mathrm{leg}} goes DOWN from the people’s fiber to the legislative fiber, and α\alpha is in the source (fixed fiber of the people’s doctrine), not the target. This is the Lockean version of Rousseau’s inalienability: the principal’s accountability proposition is not an element of any agent fiber; it is an element of HtnowP∗H^{P*}_{t_{\mathrm{now}}} from which all agent fibers receive restriction maps but which no agent fiber contains.

Delegatus non potest delegare: the fixed-fiber constraint

The canon law maxim delegatus non potest delegare is not merely a procedural rule — it expresses a structural constraint on the category of delegation morphisms.

Why composition fails. Let ρa:HP→Ha\rho_a : H^P \to H^a be the principal’s delegation to agent aa, and ρb:Ha→Hb\rho_b : H^a \to H^b be agent aa’s attempted sub-delegation to agent bb. The composition ρb∘ρa:HP→Hb\rho_b \circ \rho_a : H^P \to H^b is a morphism in RR. But this morphism is NOT in P\mathcal{P}’s delegation profile ρ\rho unless P\mathcal{P} separately grants it. The image of ρb∘ρa\rho_b \circ \rho_a in HtbH^b_t does not carry P\mathcal{P}’s accountability proposition: α∉(ρb∘ρa)(HtP)\alpha \notin (\rho_b \circ \rho_a)(H^P_t) in the relevant sense that α\alpha’s normative force has not been directly authorized to flow to HtbH^b_t by P\mathcal{P}.

Why the rule is structurally correct. The delegation morphism ρa\rho_a is bounded: it carries only αscope≤α\alpha^{\mathrm{scope}} \leq \alpha into HtaH^a_t. Agent aa cannot grant to bb what aa does not hold: ρb(ρa(αscope))≤ρa(αscope)\rho_b(\rho_a(\alpha^{\mathrm{scope}})) \leq \rho_a(\alpha^{\mathrm{scope}}) in HtbH^b_t (restriction is order-preserving). But more importantly: the sub-delegation ρb∘ρa\rho_b \circ \rho_a creates a further removed representation of P\mathcal{P}’s authority in HtbH^b_t, and P\mathcal{P}’s nuclei (σtP,ΔtP)(\sigma^P_t, \Delta^P_t) do not extend to HtbH^b_t via the composed map unless P\mathcal{P} explicitly constructs a morphism ρab:HP→Hb\rho_{ab} : H^P \to H^b. The canon law maxim is thus a theorem about morphism categories: implicit composition is not an authorized delegation.

Exceptions. The express authorization exception — when the original mandate includes the power to sub-delegate — corresponds to the case where ρa\rho_a is a functor in the enriched sense: it carries not just authority propositions but the power to create new restriction maps. In categorical terms: ρa\rho_a then maps the principal’s delegation-power into HtaH^a_t as a higher-order proposition, and ρb\rho_b then has legitimate backing. This requires the original delegation morphism ρa\rho_a to be a morphism not just of Heyting algebras but of nuclear pairs together with their own delegation-capacity structure — a strictly richer object than an ordinary sheaf morphism.

Nuclear reading: original authority as terminal fiber

The four quadrants of the nuclear structure classify authority positions in the system:

Fiber region Authority position What it names
HtP∗=Fix(σtP)∩Fix(ΔtP)H^{P*}_t = \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^P_t) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta^P_t) Original, doubly-stable authority Full principalship: recognized backward and committed forward; the accountability proposition α\alpha; what the principal cannot alienate
Fix(σtP)∖HtP∗\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^P_t) \setminus H^{P*}_t Recognized but not forward-committed Past normative recognition established; forward-stability not yet complete; the saturation gap is zero, the transfer gap non-trivial
Fix(ΔtP)∖HtP∗\mathrm{Fix}(\Delta^P_t) \setminus H^{P*}_t Forward-committed but not yet recognized Operational commitment without settled recognition; the transfer gap is zero, the saturation gap non-trivial
HtP∖(Fix(σtP)∪Fix(ΔtP))H^P_t \setminus (\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^P_t) \cup \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta^P_t)) Free shadow class Proposed but unsettled authority claims; both gaps non-trivial

Proposition 1 (Accountability proposition is in H^{P}_t — definitional):* The accountability proposition α∈HtP∗\alpha \in H^{P*}_t means σtP(α)=α\sigma^P_t(\alpha) = \alpha and ΔtP(α)=α\Delta^P_t(\alpha) = \alpha: original authority is both backward-recognized (restriction profile demands no more) and forward-committed (every extension already contains it). This is a definition, not a theorem: the principal is defined as the party for whom this holds within the sub-system.

Proposition 2 (Delegation does not deplete HtP∗H^{P*}_t): After creating the delegation morphism ρa:HP→Ha\rho_a : H^P \to H^a, the principal’s proposition α∈HtP∗\alpha \in H^{P*}_t is unaffected: σtP(α)=α\sigma^P_t(\alpha) = \alpha and ΔtP(α)=α\Delta^P_t(\alpha) = \alpha continue to hold.

Proof. ρa\rho_a is a morphism from HPH^P to HaH^a; it does not modify the fiber HtPH^P_t or its nuclear operators σtP,ΔtP\sigma^P_t, \Delta^P_t. These operators are determined by the restriction maps of the presheaf HPH^P, which ρa\rho_a does not alter. The image of ρa\rho_a lives in HaH^a; the source HPH^P is unchanged. □

Proposition 3 (The nuclear morphism condition and agency divergence): ρa:HP→Ha\rho_a : H^P \to H^a is a morphism of nuclear fiber doctrines iff it satisfies:

ρa∘σtP=σta∘ρaandρa∘ΔtP=ΔtP∘ρa.\rho_a \circ \sigma^P_t = \sigma^a_t \circ \rho_a \quad \text{and} \quad \rho_a \circ \Delta^P_t = \Delta^P_t \circ \rho_a.

When both conditions hold, ρa(HtP∗)⊆Hta∗\rho_a(H^{P*}_t) \subseteq H^{a*}_t: the principal’s stable authority maps to stable authority for the agent.

Proof. If σtP(α)=α\sigma^P_t(\alpha) = \alpha then ρa(α)=ρa(σtP(α))=σta(ρa(α))\rho_a(\alpha) = \rho_a(\sigma^P_t(\alpha)) = \sigma^a_t(\rho_a(\alpha)) (by the nuclear morphism condition), so ρa(α)∈Fix(σta)\rho_a(\alpha) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^a_t). Similarly for Δ\Delta. □

When the nuclear morphism conditions fail (the generic case for delegation across different institutional contexts), σta(ρa(α))>ρa(α)\sigma^a_t(\rho_a(\alpha)) > \rho_a(\alpha): the agent’s saturation nucleus demands more than the principal’s restriction map delivers. The gap [ρa(α), σta(ρa(α))][\rho_a(\alpha),\, \sigma^a_t(\rho_a(\alpha))] is the agency divergence — the space of additional nuclear closure the agent’s context requires beyond what the grant provides.

Proposition 4 (Immutability of σtP\sigma^P_t and ΔtP\Delta^P_t under agent acts): No act by any agent aa (under any delegation ρa\rho_a) can alter σtP\sigma^P_t or ΔtP\Delta^P_t.

Proof. σtP\sigma^P_t is determined by the restriction maps of the presheaf HPH^P over TT. Agent acts are elements of HtPH^P_t; the presheaf morphisms are not in the write-scope of any element of any fiber. The same holds for ΔtP\Delta^P_t. □

Consequence. The principal’s originality — the condition that no restriction from above constitutes α\alpha within the sub-system — is not a claim agents can undermine by acting. It is a structural fact about the presheaf, not a proposition within any fiber.

On Yoneda representability (structural claim, not yet a derived theorem): The principal’s fixed fiber HtP∗H^{P*}_t plays the role of the representing object for the sub-system’s authority-allocation structure: every agent authority in the sub-system is in the image of some delegation morphism from HPH^P. By the Yoneda lemma, if HtP∗H^{P*}_t represents the functor HomR(−,HP)\mathrm{Hom}_R(-, H^P), it is uniquely determined up to isomorphism. This would be the categorical statement of originality: no other object in the sub-system can represent the same functor. Whether the authority-allocation functor is indeed representable by HtP∗H^{P*}_t requires specifying the category precisely and verifying the universal property — this is an open derivation (see open questions).

Open questions

  • Is there always a unique original principal in a sub-system? The originality condition (no restriction from above within the sub-system) ensures that the principal’s fixed fiber is not in the image of any other fiber. But whether there is a unique such fiber, or whether multiple competing original-authority fibers can coexist in the same sub-system, depends on the structure of the authority lattice. Multiple non-overlapping original fibers would correspond to multiple incompatible Grundnormen — a situation Kelsen treated as a contradiction (p. 214). Whether the nuclear structure of the relational universe forces uniqueness of the original fiber, or allows plural original authorities (as in constitutional systems with separated powers), is not yet derived. If the separated-powers structure is a case of multiple cooperating originals rather than one principal with sub-delegations, the spec of Madison’s constitutional opposition as mutual nuclear insulation may require a more complex fiber-product structure.

  • Does the delegation profile ρ have a categorical universal property? The Yoneda reading suggests that the principal’s fiber represents the delegation functor. If this is right, the delegation profile ρ\rho should be a natural transformation from the representable presheaf HomR(−,HP)\mathrm{Hom}_R(-, H^P) to the authority-fiber functor. Whether the full delegation structure on a principal is a representable natural transformation — and whether the universal property uniquely determines ρ\rho up to natural isomorphism — would establish that the principal is a characterized object in the category of authority structures, not just a named element. This derivation is not yet given.

  • Does the Locke revertibility condition require the principal’s fiber to be an initial object? Locke’s argument that the people’s authority always reverts when the legislature forfeits corresponds to the claim that HtnowP∗H^{P*}_{t_{\mathrm{now}}} is never depleted by delegation and always recoverable. In categorical terms: the principal’s fixed fiber is an initial object in the authority lattice of the sub-system — all restriction maps originate from it, and it is terminal in the sense that revocation merely removes target fibers, leaving the source unchanged. Whether the originality condition implies initiality in the relevant category (the sub-system authority lattice with restriction maps as morphisms) is a derivation that requires characterizing the category precisely. If it holds, the Lockean revertibility is a theorem, not a political postulate.

  • Is delegatus non potest delegare a theorem or an axiom in this system? The argument above treats it as a structural consequence of the delegation morphism category: composition is not automatically authorized. But this depends on how the “category of delegation morphisms” is defined. If delegations are morphisms in the full category HomR(HP,Ha)\mathrm{Hom}_R(H^P, H^a) and composition is defined as usual in RR, then composition IS well-defined — the maxim is not automatically a theorem. The maxim instead becomes a constraint on which compositions are RECOGNIZED as valid delegations — a normative condition on top of the categorical structure. Deriving the maxim formally would require specifying the normative sub-category of delegation morphisms and showing that it is NOT closed under composition by default. This specification is not yet given.

  • What is the correct type of the Bratman gluing condition? The claim that Bratman’s three conditions correspond to naturality, compatibility, and gluing in Sh(T,J)\mathbf{Sh}(T, J) is structurally motivated but not formally derived. Bratman’s conditions are formulated for finite shared activities between individual agents; the sheaf-gluing condition applies to potentially infinite families indexed by covering sieves. Whether the correspondence goes through in full generality — whether every finite shared cooperative activity is a case of a finite covering sieve with compatible sections — requires spelling out the topology JJ and verifying that the agents’ intentions form a sheaf-theoretically compatible family. The identification of “meshing sub-plans” with the gluing condition in particular is intuitive but not yet a derivation.

  • What is the relationship between Pettit’s non-domination and the nuclear commutation condition? The non-domination condition was rendered as: ρa\rho_a commutes with the nuclei — ρa∘σtP=σta∘ρa\rho_a \circ \sigma^P_t = \sigma^a_t \circ \rho_a. But this is the condition for ρa\rho_a to be a nuclear fiber doctrine morphism. If non-domination = nuclear morphism condition, then every non-dominating principal-agent delegation is a nuclear morphism. Whether this is too strong (all real delegations involve some nuclear non-commutativity, which would make them all dominating by this criterion) or too weak (the nuclear morphism condition is necessary but not sufficient for non-domination) is not yet determined. A weaker condition — that ρa\rho_a does not map INTO the agent’s fixed fiber without passing through the agent’s saturation closure first — might be the correct rendering of contestability.

  • Is the accountability proposition α the same as the office incumbency proposition ι? The Incumbent spec defines the incumbency proposition ι∈Htnow\iota \in H_{t_{\mathrm{now}}} as asserting that agent aa holds office OO at tnowt_{\mathrm{now}}. The principal’s accountability proposition α∈HtnowP∗\alpha \in H^{P*}_{t_{\mathrm{now}}} asserts original authority over domain QQ at tnowt_{\mathrm{now}}. If the principal holds the authority through an office — as is typical in institutional settings — then α\alpha is a global section of the office’s fiber doctrine: α=(ιt)t∈T\alpha = (\iota_t)_{t \in T} with ιt∈Ht∗\iota_t \in H^*_t for all tt. If the principal’s authority is non-institutional (personal authority not mediated by an office — the original authority of a constitution’s framers, Kelsen’s Grundnorm), then α\alpha has no corresponding incumbency proposition in an Office spec. The relationship between principal-accountability and office-incumbency is therefore a spectrum rather than an identification, and the boundaries are not yet derived.
