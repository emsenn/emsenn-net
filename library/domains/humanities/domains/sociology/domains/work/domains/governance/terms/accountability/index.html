2026-03-20 Accountability is a triple (B, S, A) — a bearer B, a scope S, and an accountability function A: Outcomes(S) → {B} — a total map assigning every outcome within S to B as the answerable party. The defining structure: accountability is backward-looking, total, and non-transferable by delegation. B is answerable for everything that happens in S regardless of who caused it, who was delegated to handle it, or whether B was present. Delegation distributes authority; it never lifts accountability.

Accountability

Formal definition

Accountability is a triple Ac=(B,S,A)\mathcal{Ac} = (B, S, A):

Ac=(B:Person,  S:Scope,  A:Outcomes(S)→{B})\mathcal{Ac} = (B : \mathrm{Person},\; S : \mathrm{Scope},\; A : \mathrm{Outcomes}(S) \to \{B\})

where:

  • BB is the bearer — the single party who is answerable for all outcomes within SS; accountability is always personal — it attaches to a named individual, not a role in the abstract
  • SS is the scope — the precisely bounded domain (unit, vessel, command, area, project) within which AA operates; SS must be bounded for accountability to be determinate; “accountable for everything” with no scope boundary is not accountability but a rhetorical claim
  • A:Outcomes(S)→{B}A : \mathrm{Outcomes}(S) \to \{B\} is the accountability function — total, constant, and surjective; every outcome o∈Outcomes(S)o \in \mathrm{Outcomes}(S) maps to BB; there is no outcome in SS for which BB is not answerable

Four invariants. Ac\mathcal{Ac} is a valid accountability structure if and only if:

  1. Totality: AA is defined on all of Outcomes(S)\mathrm{Outcomes}(S). There is no outcome within SS for which accountability is unassigned. The “accountability gap” — outcome without an accountable party — is a structural failure of the normative system, not a description of a real accountability.

  2. Non-transferability by delegation: for any delegation D=(B,G,Dsub)\mathcal{D} = (B, G, D_{\mathrm{sub}}) from BB to a subordinate within SS, the accountability function AA is unchanged: A(o)=BA(o) = B for all o∈Outcomes(S)o \in \mathrm{Outcomes}(S). Delegation distributes authority; it does not modify AA. The canonical formulation (U.S. Navy Regulations, Art. 0802): “delegation of authority shall in no way relieve the commanding officer of continued responsibility.”

  3. Installation-anchoring: AA is binding from the moment of BB’s Investiture I+I^+ into the position that carries scope SS. Before I+I^+: AA does not apply to BB with respect to SS. After I+I^+: AA applies and cannot be suspended short of I−I^- (formal relief). Physical absence, incapacitation, or delegation does not suspend AA.

  4. Scope-exclusivity: within SS, only BB is accountable under this instance of Ac\mathcal{Ac}. Other parties may have duties, may face sanctions, and may be blamed — but accountability in the formal sense (the total function AA) belongs to BB alone for SS. Two parties cannot share one accountability without it being undefined which one is answerable for which outcome.

Accountability vs. adjacent concepts

The precision of accountability requires distinguishing it from several adjacent concepts with which it is routinely confused:

Accountability vs. Duty. A Duty is forward-looking: BB must perform act φ\varphi by deadline τ\tau. Accountability is backward-looking: BB is answerable for outcome oo that has already occurred or will occur. Duty says what BB must do; accountability says who is answerable for what happened. A duty can be discharged; accountability cannot be discharged — it persists through the outcome’s entire causal history. An officer can fully discharge every duty and still be held accountable for an outcome caused by a subordinate’s failure.

Accountability vs. Responsibility. Responsibility is causal and moral: BB is responsible for oo if and only if BB’s act caused oo and BB had the relevant intention or recklessness. Responsibility can be absent when causation is absent. Accountability is positional: BB is accountable for oo if and only if o∈Outcomes(S)o \in \mathrm{Outcomes}(S), regardless of causation. A captain is accountable for a navigation error made by a subordinate watchstander, even if the captain was asleep. The captain did not cause it; the captain is still accountable for it.

Accountability vs. Liability. HohfeldianPosition Liability is the condition of having one’s normative position alterable by another’s power — it is a second-quartet position. Legal liability (exposure to suit, penalty, or remedy) is a consequence of breach or wrongdoing. Accountability is the pre-breach condition: the position that generates liability when outcomes are bad. Being accountable is what makes one liable to be held accountable if outcomes are poor.

Accountability vs. Blame. Blame is a reactive attitude — a normative emotion expressed toward a party. Accountability is a structural position — a formal assignment of answerability. Blame may be appropriate even where formal accountability is absent (a bystander who could have helped); accountability may hold where blame would be inappropriate (a commanding officer whose unit failed despite all reasonable measures).

Nuclear reading

Definitions. The accountability proposition at history tt is:

Ac(B,o,t)  =  RAc ⁣(ι(B,OS,t0),  out(o,t))  ∈  Ht\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t) \;=\; R_{\mathcal{Ac}}\!\bigl(\iota(B, O_S, t_0),\; \mathrm{out}(o, t)\bigr) \;\in\; H_t

where ι(B,OS,t0)∈Ht\iota(B, O_S, t_0) \in H_t is the investiture record — the settled proposition that BB was installed in office OSO_S at t0≤tt_0 \leq t — out(o,t)∈Ht\mathrm{out}(o, t) \in H_t is the proposition that oo is a scope-SS outcome at tt, and RAcR_{\mathcal{Ac}} is the constitutive rule (Constitution) that installation in OSO_S makes one accountable for scope-SS outcomes.

RAcR_{\mathcal{Ac}} is a natural transformation: it commutes with all restriction maps of the presheaf. This means ρt′t∗(RAc(a,b))=RAc(ρt′t∗(a),ρt′t∗(b))\rho^*_{t't}(R_{\mathcal{Ac}}(a, b)) = R_{\mathcal{Ac}}(\rho^*_{t't}(a), \rho^*_{t't}(b)) for all one-step extensions t′≥tt' \geq t.

Proposition 1 (Accountability proposition is doubly stable when the investiture is doubly stable): If ι(B,OS,t0)∈Ht∗\iota(B, O_S, t_0) \in H^*_t, then Ac(B,o,t)∈Ht∗\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t) \in H^*_t for all o∈Outcomes(S)o \in \mathrm{Outcomes}(S).

Proof. Since σt\sigma_t is determined by restriction maps and RAcR_{\mathcal{Ac}} commutes with restriction maps (naturality), the restriction profile of Ac(B,o,t)\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t) across all t0<tt_0 < t is constituted by the profile of ι(B,OS,t0)\iota(B, O_S, t_0) and out(o,t)\mathrm{out}(o, t) through RAcR_{\mathcal{Ac}}. If ι(B,OS,t0)∈Fix(σt)\iota(B, O_S, t_0) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t), then the restriction profile of ι\iota already constitutes ι\iota as σt\sigma_t-settled. The naturality of RAcR_{\mathcal{Ac}} then implies the restriction profile of Ac(B,o,t)\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t) is likewise constituted as σt\sigma_t-settled: σt(Ac(B,o,t))=Ac(B,o,t)\sigma_t(\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t)) = \mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t).

For Δt\Delta_t: Δt(Ac(B,o,t))=Ac(B,o,t)\Delta_t(\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t)) = \mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t) iff the accountability proposition is in the image of every one-step forward extension restriction. Since ι(B,OS,t0)∈Fix(Δt)\iota(B, O_S, t_0) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) (investiture records are globally stable: settled at t0t_0 and in the image of every forward restriction until I−I^-) and RAcR_{\mathcal{Ac}} commutes with restriction maps, Ac(B,o,t)\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t) is in the image of every one-step forward extension restriction. Therefore Ac(B,o,t)∈Fix(Δt)\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t).

Together: Ac(B,o,t)∈Ht∗\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t) \in H^*_t. □\square

Consequence. For all t∈TSt \in T_S with t≥t0t \geq t_0: the entire scope Outcomes(S)\mathrm{Outcomes}(S) is Ac\mathrm{Ac}-covered at tt. The totality invariant holds at every history in TST_S.

Proposition 2 (Immutability — acts at tt do not modify σt\sigma_t or Δt\Delta_t): No act at history tt — delegation, assignment, order — modifies σt\sigma_t or Δt\Delta_t. Such acts are history steps ss generating t′=s⋆tt' = s \star t; the nuclei at tt are determined by the restriction maps of the presheaf up to tt, unchanged by steps at tt.

Proof. σt\sigma_t is determined by the restriction maps H(it0,t)H(i_{t_0, t}) for t0<tt_0 < t. A step ss at tt extends the presheaf diagram above tt without modifying the existing morphisms. □\square

Proposition 3 (Non-delegability): A delegation step sδs_\delta generates a new history t′=sδ⋆tt' = s_\delta \star t. At t′t':

Ac(B,o,t′)=RAc ⁣(ι(B,OS,t0),  out(o,t′))\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t') = R_{\mathcal{Ac}}\!\bigl(\iota(B, O_S, t_0),\; \mathrm{out}(o, t')\bigr)

and Ac(B,o,t′)∈Ht′∗\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t') \in H^*_{t'}.

Proof. The delegation step adds the delegation record δ\delta to the fiber Ht′H_{t'}. The constitutive rule RAcR_{\mathcal{Ac}} takes as inputs the investiture record ι(B,OS,t0)\iota(B, O_S, t_0) and the outcome proposition — δ\delta is neither of these inputs and appears in neither. By the naturality of RAcR_{\mathcal{Ac}}, the restriction-profile class of ι(B,OS,t0)\iota(B, O_S, t_0) at t′t' remains settled (delegation does not retract the investiture), so ι(B,OS,t0)∈Fix(σt′)\iota(B, O_S, t_0) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t'}). By Proposition 1 applied at t′t': Ac(B,o,t′)∈Ht′∗\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t') \in H^*_{t'}. □\square

Note. The non-delegability result does not rest on “σt\sigma_t not reading the authority sub-fiber” — an imprecise formulation that anthropomorphizes the nucleus. The correct statement: RAcR_{\mathcal{Ac}} has no clause taking a delegation record as input; σt′\sigma_{t'} is determined by restriction profiles; the restriction profile of Ac\mathrm{Ac} at t′t' is determined by ι\iota and RAcR_{\mathcal{Ac}}, neither of which involves δ\delta.

Proposition 4 (Conjunction of accountability propositions is doubly stable): If Ac(B,o1,t),Ac(B,o2,t)∈Ht∗\mathrm{Ac}(B, o_1, t), \mathrm{Ac}(B, o_2, t) \in H^*_t, then Ac(B,o1,t)∧Ac(B,o2,t)∈Ht∗\mathrm{Ac}(B, o_1, t) \wedge \mathrm{Ac}(B, o_2, t) \in H^*_t.

Proof. By meet-preservation of σt\sigma_t and Δt\Delta_t: σt(a∧b)=σt(a)∧σt(b)\sigma_t(a \wedge b) = \sigma_t(a) \wedge \sigma_t(b) and Δt(a∧b)=Δt(a)∧Δt(b)\Delta_t(a \wedge b) = \Delta_t(a) \wedge \Delta_t(b). □\square

Global section. Define the accountability presheaf AB:TSop→Set\mathcal{A}^B : T_S^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set} by AtB={o∈Ht:Ac(B,o,t)∈Ht∗}\mathcal{A}^B_t = \{o \in H_t : \mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t) \in H^*_t\}, with restriction maps ρt′t∗:AtB→At′B\rho^*_{t't} : \mathcal{A}^B_t \to \mathcal{A}^B_{t'} given by fiber restriction on outcomes. The accountability function AA is a global section of AB\mathcal{A}^B over TST_S: the assignment t↦Outcomes(S)∩Htt \mapsto \mathrm{Outcomes}(S) \cap H_t is compatible with all restriction maps, because ρt′t∗(Ac(B,o,t))=Ac(B,ρt′t∗(o),t′)\rho^*_{t't}(\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t)) = \mathrm{Ac}(B, \rho^*_{t't}(o), t') by naturality of RAcR_{\mathcal{Ac}}. The totality invariant is the global section condition: every outcome at every history in TST_S is in AtB\mathcal{A}^B_t, and these local assignments cohere.

Breach. When B′B'’s act φ′\varphi' has a non-trivial gap for an outcome within SS: the gap is a fact in Ht′H_{t'} (the act sub-fiber). The accountability presheaf AB\mathcal{A}^B is unchanged — Ac(B,o,t′)∈Ht′∗\mathrm{Ac}(B, o, t') \in H^*_{t'} still holds. What the accountability structure determines is that the normative consequences of the gap attach to BB’s answerability, not B′B'’s, for scope-SS outcomes.

Accountability and the captain

The Captain holds the most complete accountability structure in this system: AA is total over Outcomes(S)\mathrm{Outcomes}(S) with no exceptions, installation-anchored, non-transferable, and scope-exclusive. The captain is the limiting case because:

  1. Scope singularity: exactly one captain per scope SS, so AA is unambiguously total
  2. Anti-override authority: the captain’s decisions within Π⊆S\Pi \subseteq S cannot be overridden, so the captain cannot claim their accountability is diminished by external interference within Π\Pi
  3. Non-delegable duties: certain duties in Λ\Lambda cannot be delegated, so the accountability for those acts is even more tightly coupled — neither the authority nor the accountability can be passed down

The duty officer’s accountability is bounded: it applies only to Outcomes(W)\mathrm{Outcomes}(W) (outcomes during watch interval WW) and terminates at I−I^- (watch relief). The duty officer’s accountability is a restriction of the captain’s accountability to the watch interval.

Open questions

  • Whether accountability can be shared — whether two parties can both hold (B1,S,A1)(B_1, S, A_1) and (B2,S,A2)(B_2, S, A_2) with A1,A2:Outcomes(S)→{B1,B2}A_1, A_2 : \mathrm{Outcomes}(S) \to \{B_1, B_2\} respectively, and whether shared accountability generates any coherent normative structure or whether it collapses to no accountability (diffusion of responsibility).
  • Whether the accountability function AA can be partially defined over time: beginning at I+I^+ and contracting as sub-scopes are delegated, or whether the totality invariant requires AA to be constant throughout the installation period.
  • Whether accountability survives installation: once BB is relieved (I−I^-), does accountability for past outcomes in SS persist? In law, it does (past acts remain attributable to past commanders). Whether this requires AA to be defined over Outcomes(S)×TS\mathrm{Outcomes}(S) \times T_S (history-indexed) rather than just Outcomes(S)\mathrm{Outcomes}(S).
