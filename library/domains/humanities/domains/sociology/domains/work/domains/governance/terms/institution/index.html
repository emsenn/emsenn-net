Institution

What this is

An Institution is a system of constitutive rules of the form “X counts as Y in context C” that brings institutional facts into existence by collective acceptance. The mechanism is the Status Function Declaration: a speech act (or equivalent collective act) that installs a status-function — a deontic power, right, obligation, or permission — on an entity or agent relative to the institution. The institution is not reducible to its members; it consists of the generative rule-system that defines what positions exist and what it means to occupy them.

Formal tuple

An Institution is a quintuple I=(Pos,D,CR,Rec,BG)I = (\text{Pos}, D, \text{CR}, \text{Rec}, \text{BG}):

I=(Pos,  D:Pos→HPos,  CR,  Rec,  BG)I = (\text{Pos},\; D : \text{Pos} \to \text{HPos},\; \text{CR},\; \text{Rec},\; \text{BG})

where:

  • Pos\text{Pos} is the set of positions (named offices; see Office)
  • D:Pos→HohfeldianProfileD : \text{Pos} \to \text{HohfeldianProfile} assigns each position its deontic profile — the powers, duties, privileges, and immunities of any occupant
  • CR⊆{(X,Y,C)}\text{CR} \subseteq \{(X, Y, C)\} is the constitutive rule set — triples of the form “X counts as Y in context C” that generate institutional facts from brute facts
  • Rec\text{Rec} is the collective recognition predicate — the ongoing acceptance by participants that keeps the institutional facts operative; without Rec\text{Rec}, CR is a dead letter
  • BG\text{BG} is the background condition — the brute-fact substrate (physical, computational, material) within which the institution operates

An institution is operative at time tt if and only if: CR\text{CR} is consistent, Rect\text{Rec}_t holds for the relevant participant population, and BGt\text{BG}_t is satisfied.

Four invariants. II is an institution iff it satisfies:

  1. Constitutive rule sufficiency: CR\text{CR} must be rich enough to generate all the institutional facts required for II to be II — it must specify what positions exist, what actions count as exercises of which powers, and what conditions bring new institutional facts into existence. Regulative rules (rules that regulate pre-existing behavior) are not sufficient; the institution’s rules must constitute the facts they govern. Searle’s criterion: the rule-form “X counts as Y in context C” must appear; “do not do X” is regulative, not constitutive.

  2. Collective recognition persistence: Rec\text{Rec} must hold continuously over the relevant participant population for II to remain operative. The institution is not a physical object; it exists only insofar as it is collectively recognized. A monetary system ceases to function when participants stop accepting money as money. A legal system ceases to function when officials stop treating law as binding. Rec\text{Rec} is not a one-time act but an ongoing condition — the institution’s existence is constitutively dependent on sustained collective acceptance.

  3. Position occupancy independence: II persists through complete turnover of position-occupants. The positions Pos\text{Pos} and their deontic profiles DD exist independently of any particular person filling them. This is the formal content of institutional persistence: the institution is not the set of current officeholders but the rule-system that defines what offices exist and what occupancy entails. North’s formulation: the institution is the rules of the game; the players (occupants) are variable.

  4. Background condition satisfaction: BGt\text{BG}_t must be satisfied — the brute-fact substrate must support the institution’s operation. An institution requires a medium: physical, computational, or social. A property rights institution requires a physical world with objects that can be possessed and transferred. A computational institution requires a file-system or database substrate. Changing the medium (from paper deeds to blockchain) may preserve the institution if the enabling conditions are the same; but removing all media destroys the institution regardless of how well-specified its constitutive rules are.

Nuclear/sheaf translation. In the relational universe, an institution corresponds to a Grothendieck topology JJ on the history site (T,J)(T, J):

  • Each covering sieve S∈J(t)S \in J(t) is a constitutive rule: “the histories in SS collectively count as covering tt” — they together witness what is institutionally valid at tt
  • The sheaf condition on H:Top→HAH : T^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{HA} is the collective recognition condition: local sections cohere into global ones if and only if the institution’s recognition is consistent
  • A Lawvere–Tierney topology j:Ω→Ωj : \Omega \to \Omega is the internal version — the institution’s constitutive rules internalized as a closure operator on truth values

The three traditions (Searle, North, Hodgson) each emphasize a different component: Searle emphasizes CR; North emphasizes Pos and D; Hodgson emphasizes the feedback from Rec\text{Rec} back onto BG\text{BG} over time (reconstitutive downward causation — not captured in the static tuple).

Nuclear derivation

The constitutive rule proposition. For a rule (X,Y,C)∈CR(X, Y, C) \in \text{CR}, define the institutional validity proposition at history tt:

rXYC  ∈  Htr_{XYC} \;\in\; H_t

where rXYCr_{XYC} is the element witnessing “entity XX occupies status YY in context CC” in the fiber Heyting algebra at tt. The institution is operative at tt if and only if rXYC∈Ht∗r_{XYC} \in H^*_t for every (X,Y,C)∈CR(X, Y, C) \in \text{CR}.

Characterization via the fixed-fiber characteristic morphism. The characteristic morphism χH∗:H→Ω\chi_{H^*} : H \to \Omega classifies institutional validity as an internal truth value of the topos R=Sh(T,J)R = \mathbf{Sh}(T, J):

χH∗,t(rXYC)  =  { f:s→t  ∣  H(f)(rXYC)∈Hs∗ }\chi_{H^*, t}(r_{XYC}) \;=\; \bigl\{\, f : s \to t \;\bigm|\; H(f)(r_{XYC}) \in H^*_s \,\bigr\}

This is a sieve on tt. The rule is operative if and only if this sieve is the maximal sieve on tt:

rXYC∈Ht∗    ⟺    χH∗,t(rXYC)=⊤tr_{XYC} \in H^*_t \;\iff\; \chi_{H^*, t}(r_{XYC}) = \top_t

The maximal sieve condition is the sheaf condition: every restriction of rXYCr_{XYC} to a sub-history s≤ts \leq t is in Hs∗H^*_s. Institutional validity is globally coherent — the validity of a rule at tt is the validity of all its restrictions.

What σt\sigma_t does. The saturation nucleus is the backward-facing closure:

σt(rXYC)=rXYC    ⟺    rXYC∈Fix(σt)\sigma_t(r_{XYC}) = r_{XYC} \;\iff\; r_{XYC} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t)

This holds if and only if the accumulated sub-history tt already forces rXYCr_{XYC}: every sub-history that could witness the rule in force already does. In institutional terms, σt(rXYC)=rXYC\sigma_t(r_{XYC}) = r_{XYC} is the condition that Rect\text{Rec}_t holds for the rule: the community’s shared evidence at tt collectively settles the rule as valid.

What Δt\Delta_t does. The transfer nucleus is the forward-facing closure:

Δt(rXYC)=rXYC    ⟺    rXYC∈Fix(Δt)\Delta_t(r_{XYC}) = r_{XYC} \;\iff\; r_{XYC} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t)

This holds if and only if every one-step extension t′=t⋆mt' = t \star m already commits to rXYCr_{XYC}: the rule carries forward. In institutional terms, Δt(rXYC)=rXYC\Delta_t(r_{XYC}) = r_{XYC} is the condition that BGt\text{BG}_t is satisfied and the institutional structure is stable under future steps.

Operative condition as double fixed-point. Combining:

The three informal conditions — CR consistent, Rect\text{Rec}_t holds, BGt\text{BG}_t satisfied — collapse into the single condition that every constitutive rule’s proposition is doubly-stable.

The joint retraction. By the commutation theorem, πt=σt∘Δt=Δt∘σt\pi_t = \sigma_t \circ \Delta_t = \Delta_t \circ \sigma_t is a nucleus with Fix(πt)=Ht∗\mathrm{Fix}(\pi_t) = H^*_t. For any proposed rule rXYC∈Htr_{XYC} \in H_t:

πt(rXYC)  =  ⋀{ s∈Ht∗:s≥rXYC }\pi_t(r_{XYC}) \;=\; \bigwedge\bigl\{\, s \in H^*_t : s \geq r_{XYC} \,\bigr\}

This is the smallest doubly-stable proposition above rXYCr_{XYC} — the institutional closure of the proposed rule. The gap πt(rXYC)−rXYC\pi_t(r_{XYC}) - r_{XYC} measures how far the proposed rule is from institutional validity.

A rule operative at t′t' is operative at every t≤t′t \leq t'. The institution persists backward: operative rules are globally coherent over the site TT. This is the formal content of invariant 3 (position occupancy independence): the institutional structure is a global section of H∗H^* over the full site, not merely locally valid at one history.

Three theoretical traditions

Searle (1995, 2010): constitutive rules and status-functions. A constitutive rule does not merely regulate behavior that could occur without it — it creates the behavior it regulates. “Touchdowns score six points” does not regulate an independently existing event; it constitutes the event by defining it. An institution is a system of such rules. Status-functions are the deontic powers assigned by these rules: the right to vote, the duty to obey, the power to authorize. These exist only within the institutional context C.

North (1990): institutions as constraints (rules of the game) distinct from organizations (the players). The institution is the rule-set that structures interaction; an organization is a group of agents pursuing goals within that rule-set. The distinction is critical: a legal system is an institution (it defines the rules); a specific agent serving in a court is a player in the game the institution defines.

Hodgson (2006): institutions as enduring social structures with reconstitutive downward causation — they not only constrain agents but reshape agents’ dispositions and identities over time. An agent who serves long within an institution internalizes its constitutive rules; this is not captured by Searle’s synchronic account or North’s purely constraint-based view.

Structural components

Every institution has five structural components:

Component What it is
Positions The offices — named normative slots that agents can occupy (see Officer)
Deontic profiles The Hohfeldian rights, duties, powers, and immunities associated with each position
Constitutive rules The “X counts as Y in C” rules generating the institutional facts
Collective recognition The sustaining act — the ongoing acceptance that keeps the institution operative
Background conditions The brute-fact substrate within which the institution operates (the physical, computational, or material medium)

The institution is not any one of these alone. Remove the constitutive rules and you have only a social habit. Remove collective recognition and you have a dead letter. Remove the deontic profiles and you have a mere label system.

Institution vs. organization

North’s distinction bears emphasis: an institution is the rules; an organization is the players. A company (organization) operates within contract law, property law, and employment law (institutions). A scientific community (organization) operates within the norms of peer review, citation, and epistemic standards (institutions). A legal system is an institution — it defines rules, roles, and deontic powers. A specific court operating within that legal system is an organization — a group pursuing a goal within the institutional frame.

This distinction prevents a common error: conflating the persistence of an institution with the persistence of a particular group. The institution persists when the rule-system persists. The organization dissolves when its members disperse.

Relation to vessel

A Vessel is an institution in the Searlean sense: its constitutive rules assign the status “captain” to an agent (giving that agent the deontic power to issue orders the crew is obligated to follow), assign “crew” status to other agents (giving them duties of obedience and privileges of protection), and define the charter as the constitutive document. The vessel’s registry is the persistence mechanism of the institutional identity — it maintains the collective recognition even through complete crew replacement.

Relation to normative system

A NormativeSystem is the computationally precise formalization of an institution. Where the institution is defined by constitutive rules and collective acceptance, a normative system is defined by a formal tuple ⟨Ag, R, Sc, Π, Δ⟩ with explicit roles, scenes, and deontic constraints. Every institution can be approximated as a normative system; not every normative system captures the reconstitutive downward causation that Hodgson identifies in mature institutions.

Institutional acceptance as nuclear evaluation

The nucleus evaluation pipeline — the passage from raw proposition to σ-stable to Δ-stable to H* — is the mechanism by which proposed relations (“X counts as Y”) are evaluated for institutional acceptance: a relation enters H* when it has passed through both nuclei, meaning both the meaning-closure and the transfer-closure of the institution have recognized it.

MacIntyre’s distinction: enterprises (practices) are carried by institutions but are not identical to them. Institutions are concerned with maintaining their own structure and external goods; enterprises are concerned with pursuing internal goods. An institution can persist after its enterprise has degenerated (a university can persist as a bureaucratic institution after genuine scholarship has been replaced by credential-production). An enterprise cannot persist without at least a minimal institutional form (some set of constitutive rules and collective recognition).

Beck-Chevalley base-change: operating within vs. operating on

Source: Beck-Chevalley Tower.

The Beck-Chevalley conditions control whether base-change geometric morphisms commute with the institution’s nucleus operations. They distinguish the two fundamental institutional acts:

Operating within the institution (morphisms inside R=Sh(T,J)R = \mathbf{Sh}(T, J)) preserves the Grothendieck topology JJ: the constitutive rules remain fixed. Agents and outputs move through the institution’s fiber, but the institution’s recognition mechanism — its topology — does not change. This is the day-to-day institutional operation.

Operating on the institution (geometric morphisms f:R→R′f : R \to R' between toposes with different topologies J≠J′J \neq J') changes the constitutive rules themselves: a new topology J′J' is installed, altering what counts as a covering sieve, and therefore what the institution recognizes as valid. Constitutional amendments, fundamental charter revisions, and jurisdictional boundary changes are all operations-on-the-institution. They are not moves within the game but changes to the rules of the game (North’s distinction).

σ-BC at every finite level. The saturation Beck-Chevalley condition holds at every finite depth level RnR_n: for any depth-inclusion fn:Rn→Rn+1f_n : R_n \to R_{n+1},

fn∗∘σn+1  ≅  σn∘fn∗.f_n^* \circ \sigma_{n+1} \;\cong\; \sigma_n \circ f_n^*.

Operationally: backward-stability of constitutive rules is always preserved across scope expansions. A rule that has been collectively recognized at depth n+1n+1 is also recognized at depth nn — the institution’s past recognition is coherent.

Δ-BC fails at frontier, holds in colimit. The transfer Beck-Chevalley condition fails at frontier histories (histories at maximum depth in RnR_n): these are institutional positions at the edge of the current scope, whose forward extensions have not yet been committed at depth nn. The Δ\Delta-defect is concentrated exactly on these frontier positions. But by defect mobility — every frontier history at depth nn becomes an interior history at depth n+1n+1 — the BC failure is resolved one level later, and both conditions hold in the full relational universe R=Sh(T,J)R = \mathbf{Sh}(T,J).

Institutional consequence. An institution operating at finite scope always has a Δ-BC gap at its institutional frontier: new agents and entities at the edge of the institution’s reach have not yet had their forward commitments assessed by the institution’s transfer nucleus. This is the formal content of “the law cannot cover every case at the moment it is enacted.” The gap closes as the institution’s scope expands, and both BC conditions hold in the institution’s colimit — the idealized full scope.

Proposition (Constitutional amendment = change of topology). A constitutional amendment that changes which acts count as valid institutional moves is a geometric morphism f:R→R′f : R \to R' between toposes with different topologies. If the amendment is conservative (every rule recognized in RR is also recognized in R′R': J≤J′J \leq J'), then ff is an open geometric morphism and the old institution is a sub-institution of the new. If the amendment is non-conservative (some previously recognized rules are revoked), ff changes the sheaf condition and the transfer nucleus may not commute with restriction to the old scope.

Automorphic directed comonad: mature institutions as fixed-point generators

Sources: Automorphic Directed Comonad Self-Generation, Automorphic Directed Comonad History Separation.

A mature institution is one that has reached the automorphic directed comonad level: its constitutional fiber atom HhullH^\mathrm{hull} is a fixed point of the automorphic directed comonad GΣG_\Sigma:

GΣ(Hhull)  ≅  Hhull.G_\Sigma(H^\mathrm{hull}) \;\cong\; H^\mathrm{hull}.

The fixed-point condition means: applying the institution’s substitution symmetry Σ\Sigma to its constitutional fiber returns an isomorphic fiber. The institution’s core constitutional structure is invariant under its own pattern-generating dynamics. No new constitutional atoms appear under iteration of GΣG_\Sigma — the institution generates itself from a single isomorphism class of constitutional fiber.

Contrast with immature institutions. At the ground level, R=UG(R)R = U_G(R) holds but the generating fiber atom HH is not individually stable: applying GsG_s to HH gives Gs(H)(t)=H(s⋆t)≠H(t)G_s(H)(t) = H(s \star t) \neq H(t) in general. A new institution operates with a generating fiber that shifts under each step — its constitutional interpretation evolves with each new act or precedent. A mature institution has stabilized: GΣ(Hhull)≅HhullG_\Sigma(H^\mathrm{hull}) \cong H^\mathrm{hull} — constitutional interpretation is invariant under the institution’s own pattern of operation. Precedents no longer shift the fundamental fiber.

History separation as theorem. At the automorphic directed comonad level, history separation — the condition that no two distinct institutional positions are constitutionally indistinguishable — holds as a theorem rather than an axiom. The proof: the aperiodicity of the quasicrystal QHQ^\mathcal{H} means that for any non-identity automorphism απ\alpha_\pi of the position lattice, απ∗Hhull∗≇Hhull∗\alpha_\pi^* H^{\mathrm{hull}*} \not\cong H^{\mathrm{hull}*} — the permuted assignment of fibers to positions is not isomorphic to the original. Every position in the mature institution has a unique constitutional signature. No two offices are interchangeable without remainder.

Operationally: in an immature institution, positions with similar constitutional profiles might be swapped without obvious consequence — the institution cannot distinguish them by their fiber algebras. In a mature institution, the aperiodic quasicrystal structure guarantees that every position is constitutionally unique; there are no genuinely equivalent offices.

Open questions

  • Reconstitutive downward causation requires agent-indexed fiber extension. Hodgson’s claim is that institutions reshape agent dispositions over time — not just institutional facts but the agents’ own operative logic. The current framework has one nuclear pair (σt,Δt)(\sigma_t, \Delta_t) per institutional history tt; this is the institution’s nuclear pair, not any individual agent’s. Formalizing Hodgson’s claim requires agent-indexed fiber algebras Hp(t)H_p(t) for each agent pp at each history tt, with a convergence condition: σp(t)→σt\sigma_p(t) \to \sigma_t and Δp(t)→Δt\Delta_p(t) \to \Delta_t as pp’s participation in the institution accumulates. Whether such agent-indexed nuclear pairs are compatible with the sheaf axioms — whether they can be sheaves over the same site (T,J)(T, J) — and whether their convergence to the institutional nuclear pair is definable within the relational universe framework or requires a separate modeling layer, is open. What IS currently formal: the nucleus σt\sigma_t is the institution’s accumulated-recognition operator, and Δt\Delta_t is the institution’s forward-commitment operator. These shape what enters Ht∗H^*_t but do not model the feedback from Ht∗H^*_t back onto the agents’ own σp,Δp\sigma_p, \Delta_p.

  • Institutional maturity convergence: finite or transfinite. The automorphic directed comonad section establishes that a mature institution satisfies GΣ(Hhull)≅HhullG_\Sigma(H^\mathrm{hull}) \cong H^\mathrm{hull}. Whether every institution reaches this fixed point at a finite tower level — after a bounded number of GΣG_\Sigma iterations — or whether some institutions require transfinite iteration (a colimit over an ordinal-indexed orbit) to stabilize, is open. The extremally slow growth theorem constrains the growth rate: the nuclear quartet grows no slower than the RelationalHistoryFiberNuclearQuartetExtremallySlowGrowthSequence, so the hull exists. But the theorem says nothing about how many steps are needed to reach it. Whether the hull is reached in finitely many applications of GΣG_\Sigma for all institutions, or whether some sites (T,J)(T, J) with particularly complex commutation relations require infinitely many steps before stabilizing, is unresolved.

  • Institutional topology characterization: which covering conditions are institutional. The translation identifies institution with Grothendieck topology JJ on (T,J)(T, J). In the relational universe the RelationalHistorySiteCommutationTopologyGrothendieckCoverageJ is one specific topology with a definite institutional interpretation. The open question is whether every Grothendieck topology on a history site (T,⋆,I)(T, \star, I) (for any commutation relation II) corresponds to some institution — whether every coverage condition admits an interpretation as “these operations collectively count as witnessing tt” — or whether only certain topologies are institutionally interpretable. If some topologies are not institutional (e.g., because no set of constitutive rules generates them, or because the collective recognition predicate would have to be inconsistent), then the translation is not an isomorphism of concepts but only a partial embedding: institutions form a proper subclass of topologies on history sites.
