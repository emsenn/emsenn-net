2026-03-21 A Precedent is a triple (p, t₀, J) — a doubly-settled proposition p ∈ H*_{t₀}, the history t₀ at which the decision was made, and a propagation condition derived from the Grothendieck topology J that determines whether p persists to future histories. The defining structure: p is a precedent iff p ∈ Fix(RelationalHistoryFiberTransferringNucleus(t₀)) — iff p is forward-stable at t₀, meaning it exists in the image of every independent one-step extension restriction map. Precedent-following at history t > t₀ = a lifting: there exists a doubly-settled p_t ∈ H*_t with H(t → t₀)(p_t) = p, ensuring the precedent is carried by the history without loss. Precedent-overruling = a change to J such that p ∈ Fix(Δ_{t₀}) fails in the new topology, geometrically: a covering morphism f: R' → R between toposes that does not preserve the fixed sublocale containing p. Distinguished from Breach (failure to discharge a duty), Defection (incompatible output), and Delegation (which transfers authority but not accountability): precedent neither delegates nor discharges — it transmits normative structure forward through the presheaf.

Precedent

Formal definition

A Precedent is a triple (p,t0,J)(p, t_0, J):

(p∈Ht0∗,  t0∈T,  J:GrothendieckTopology(T))(p \in H^*_{t_0},\; t_0 \in T,\; J : \mathrm{GrothendieckTopology}(T))

where:

  • p∈Ht0∗p \in H^*_{t_0} is the decision — a doubly-settled proposition in the fiber at t0t_0; the decision is both backward-recognized (σt0(p)=p\sigma_{t_0}(p) = p) and forward-stable (Δt0(p)=p\Delta_{t_0}(p) = p); only doubly-stable decisions constitute genuine precedents
  • t0∈Tt_0 \in T is the decision history — the position in the history monoid at which pp was decided; the precedent is anchored to t0t_0, not free-floating; earlier histories see pp as a future constraint; later histories see pp as an inherited structure
  • JJ is the propagation topology — the Grothendieck topology on the history site (T,J)(T, J); JJ determines which covering families of histories witness pp’s propagation; changing JJ is the formal structure of overruling

The precedent condition. (p,t0,J)(p, t_0, J) is a valid precedent iff:

p  ∈  Fix(RelationalHistoryFiberTransferringNucleus(t0))p \;\in\; \mathrm{Fix}\bigl(\mathrm{RelationalHistoryFiberTransferringNucleus}(t_0)\bigr)

That is: Δt0(p)=p\Delta_{t_0}(p) = p. The forward-stability condition is not an additional requirement layered on top of double-stability — it is the structural content of double-stability that is relevant to propagation. The saturation condition (σt0(p)=p\sigma_{t_0}(p) = p) confirms the decision is recognized; the transfer condition confirms it will persist.

Four invariants. (p,t0,J)(p, t_0, J) is a precedent iff it satisfies:

  1. Double settlement at t0t_0: p∈Ht0∗p \in H^*_{t_0}. A bare assertion or an acknowledged-but-fragile decision is not a precedent; it must be in the joint fixed-point set. An element of SatShadowt0\mathrm{SatShadow}_{t_0} may be recognized historically but is not forward-persistent — it is an ad hoc decision, not a precedent.

  2. Forward persistence: For every one-step extension t0′=gen(s)⋆t0t_0' = \mathrm{gen}(s) \star t_0 with s⊥t0s \perp t_0, there exists p′∈Ht0′p' \in H_{t_0'} with H(t0′→t0)(p′)=pH(t_0' \to t_0)(p') = p. The precedent “lifts” through independent extensions — every independent new development must be compatible with pp. This is exactly the transfer-stability condition.

  3. Coverage by the topology: The covering sieves J(t0)J(t_0) recognize pp’s decision history as a valid covering point — the normative system’s covering structure confirms that t0t_0 was the right locus for this decision. A decision made without proper authority (outside the relevant covering family) fails this invariant even if the decision itself is well-formed.

  4. Propagation coherence: For any histories t,t′t, t' both above t0t_0 with pt∈Ht∗p_t \in H^*_t and pt′∈Ht′∗p_{t'} \in H^*_{t'} both lifting pp, the restrictions H(t∧t′→t)(pt)=H(t∧t′→t′)(pt′)H(t \wedge t' \to t)(p_t) = H(t \wedge t' \to t')(p_{t'}) agree at their common meet. Precedent-following is globally coherent across divergent histories — if two independent lines of development both follow the precedent, their decisions about pp are compatible.

Etymology and legal grounding

Precedent derives from Latin praecedere — prae- (before) + cedere (to go); literally, that which has gone before. The legal term enters English in the 15th century as the principle that past decisions bind future decisions on analogous cases.

Stare decisis (“to stand by what has been decided”) is the common law doctrine formalizing the binding force of precedent. Its two dimensions:

  • Vertical stare decisis: lower courts are bound by higher courts’ decisions (hierarchical binding, between different levels of the coverage structure)
  • Horizontal stare decisis: a court is (presumptively) bound by its own prior decisions (self-binding, within a single level)

The common law’s nuanced doctrine of precedent was systematized by Blackstone (1765) and given its most rigorous jurisprudential analysis by Ronald Dworkin (Law’s Empire, 1986): law is not merely a collection of rules but a coherent structure of principle — every decision must fit with all past decisions, and the legal system must be interpretable as an expression of coherent principles. This is the integrity condition: the law must be understood as a coherent whole, with no past decision ignorable.

The Kelsenian Grundnorm (Hans Kelsen, The Pure Theory of Law, 1934) is the ultimate precedent — the presupposed founding norm from which all legal validity derives. The Grundnorm is not itself a legal decision but the presupposition that makes all legal decisions valid. In the nuclear reading: the Grundnorm is the element of He∗H^*_e at the root history ee — the only history with no predecessors — from which all future fibers are initialized.

Precedent vs. decision vs. rule

Not every decision is a precedent, and not every rule is a decision. The distinctions:

Concept Location in nuclear structure Propagation condition
Ruling (ad hoc) SatShadowt0\mathrm{SatShadow}_{t_0} — backward-recognized, forward-fragile May not lift to all extensions
Decision TrShadowt0\mathrm{TrShadow}_{t_0} — forward-committed, recognition pending Forward-persistent but awaiting backward recognition
Precedent Ht0∗H^*_{t_0} with Δt0(p)=p\Delta_{t_0}(p) = p Lifts to every independent one-step extension
Rule Ht0∗H^*_{t_0} with pp in the institution’s constitutive core Ct0C_{t_0} Cannot be modified by ordinary acts; requires topology change to revise
Grundnorm He∗H^*_e — doubly stable at the root Trivially lifts to all histories (every restriction path passes through ee)

The Grundnorm occupies a special place: it is the unique element of He∗H^*_e that generates all other elements via the restriction maps. A precedent is between a rule and a ruling in force: more stable than a ruling (it must be in Ht0∗H^*_{t_0}, not just SatShadowt0\mathrm{SatShadow}_{t_0}) but less stable than a rule (it can be overruled, which requires a topology change rather than impossibility).

Overruling: a geometric morphism

Overruling a precedent is the formal act of removing pp from the images of future restriction maps — making precedent-following impossible at some t>t0t > t_0. The structure:

A topology change J→J′J \to J' (a new Grothendieck topology on TT) generates a geometric morphism f:R′=Sh(T,J′)→R=Sh(T,J)f : R' = \mathbf{Sh}(T, J') \to R = \mathbf{Sh}(T, J): a pair of adjoint functors (f∗,f∗)(f^*, f_*) satisfying the geometric morphism axioms. The inverse image functor f∗:R→R′f^* : R \to R' sends the presheaf HH (over the old topology JJ) to f∗(H)f^*(H) (over the new topology J′J').

Under the new topology J′J', the element p∈Ht0∗p \in H^*_{t_0} may no longer be forward-stable: the covering families recognized by J′J' may not include the extensions that pp’s transfer stability depended on. Formally: Δt0J′(p)>p\Delta^{J'}_{t_0}(p) > p under the new topology, even though Δt0J(p)=p\Delta^J_{t_0}(p) = p under the old one.

Three consequences of overruling:

  1. The overruled precedent pp moves from Ht0∗H^*_{t_0} to SatShadowt0J′\mathrm{SatShadow}^{J'}_{t_0} — it retains backward recognition (the history that established pp still happened) but loses forward stability (the new topology no longer commits to pp in future extensions)
  2. Every future decision that cited pp as authority is potentially weakened: the restriction maps above t0t_0 no longer carry pp’s fixed-fiber status
  3. The overruling institution (the body that changed JJ) must itself have the authority to change JJ — this is a second-order power in the Hohfeldian sense, the power to alter the covering structure itself

Distinguishing (a weaker operation than overruling) is the claim that the current history tt is not in the sieve that pp covers — the current case is materially different from the decision history t0t_0, so the precedent does not reach this history. Formally: the restriction map H(t→t0)H(t \to t_0) does not include t→t0t \to t_0 in any covering sieve S∈J(t0)S \in J(t_0) that supports pp. Distinguishing leaves the topology JJ unchanged; it only denies that the current case is within pp’s covering reach.

Precedent and the depth filtration

The depth filtration gives a structural account of when a decision becomes a precedent. At depth n=0n = 0 (the root history ee), the Grundnorm occupies the full fiber. As depth increases, new histories are generated; each new history adds new fibers and new restriction maps. A decision pp made at depth nn accumulates forward-stability as follows:

  • At depth nn (the decision is made): p∈TrShadowt0p \in \mathrm{TrShadow}_{t_0} or Ht0∗H^*_{t_0} depending on the immediate topology
  • At depth n+kn + k for small kk: pp is tested against kk generations of restriction maps; its liftability to each generation is tracked
  • Prescription threshold: the depth k∗k^* at which every extension history at depth n+k∗n + k^* that is reachable from t0t_0 has a lift of pp in H∗H^*; after depth k∗k^*, pp is a confirmed precedent

The extremally-slow prescription rate applies when the history system has the minimal non-trivial commutation structure (one commuting pair): the number of fibers that must carry pp before pp’s precedential status is confirmed grows as FkF_{k} (Fibonacci numbers). This is the exact spectral gap of the nuclear transfer matrix M=(1110)M = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}: the eigenvalues φ\varphi and −1/φ-1/\varphi govern how fast precedential stability accumulates and how fast it decays when overruled.

Content. The golden ratio φ=(1+5)/2\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2 is not an aesthetic feature of the law but the spectral content of the nuclear pair’s non-adjointness: because σt0\sigma_{t_0} and Δt0\Delta_{t_0} are not in Galois connection, the accumulation of precedential stability at each depth level requires contributions from BOTH shadow classes (the TrShadow and SatShadow chains), and their joint contribution per level is exactly φ\varphi-multiplicative.

Nuclear reading

Definitions. Fix a history t0∈Tt_0 \in T and a doubly-stable proposition p∈Ht0∗p \in H^*_{t_0}. The precedent liftability of pp to a later history t≥t0t \geq t_0 is the condition that there exists pt∈Ht∗p_t \in H^*_t with H(t→t0)(pt)=pH(t \to t_0)(p_t) = p. The propagation depth of pp is the maximum depth kk such that every history at depth depth(t0)+k\mathrm{depth}(t_0) + k reachable from t0t_0 has a lift of pp in H∗H^*.

Proposition 1 (Transfer-stability is the necessary and sufficient condition for local liftability). For any p∈Ht0∗p \in H^*_{t_0} and any independent one-step extension t0′=gen(s)⋆t0t_0' = \mathrm{gen}(s) \star t_0 (with s⊥t0s \perp t_0):

p∈Fix(Δt0)    ⟺    p∈image(H(t0′→t0))p \in \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t_0}) \;\iff\; p \in \mathrm{image}(H(t_0' \to t_0))

That is: pp is forward-stable at t0t_0 iff there exists p′∈Ht0′p' \in H_{t_0'} with H(t0′→t0)(p′)=pH(t_0' \to t_0)(p') = p. Transfer-stability is exactly local liftability — the condition that the precedent survives one independent step.

Proof. Fix(Δt0)=⋂s⊥t0image(H(is,t0))\mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t_0}) = \bigcap_{s \perp t_0} \mathrm{image}(H(i_{s, t_0})) by definition of the transferring nucleus. Therefore p∈Fix(Δt0)p \in \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t_0}) iff p∈image(H(is,t0))p \in \mathrm{image}(H(i_{s, t_0})) for all s⊥t0s \perp t_0, which is p∈image(H(t0′→t0))p \in \mathrm{image}(H(t_0' \to t_0)) for every independent one-step extension t0′t_0'. □\square

Content. A decision in SatShadowt0\mathrm{SatShadow}_{t_0} (σt0(p)=p\sigma_{t_0}(p) = p but Δt0(p)>p\Delta_{t_0}(p) > p) has backward recognition but fails local liftability: there exists some independent one-step extension t0′t_0' where no lift of pp exists. The precedent cannot be followed at t0′t_0' — not because it has been overruled, but because the forward structure of the history site doesn’t carry it. This is the formal analog of an “ad hoc” ruling: recognized in its own context but not sufficiently general to lift to independent developments.

Proposition 2 (The lift of a precedent is itself doubly stable). If p∈Ht0∗p \in H^*_{t_0} and p′∈Ht′p' \in H_{t'} with H(t′→t0)(p′)=pH(t' \to t_0)(p') = p for some t′≥t0t' \geq t_0, then p′∈Ht′∗p' \in H^*_{t'}.

Proof. The restriction maps H(t′→t0)H(t' \to t_0) commute with both nuclei: H(t′→t0)(σt′(p′))=σt0(H(t′→t0)(p′))=σt0(p)=pH(t' \to t_0)(\sigma_{t'}(p')) = \sigma_{t_0}(H(t' \to t_0)(p')) = \sigma_{t_0}(p) = p. If σt′(p′)≠p′\sigma_{t'}(p') \neq p', then H(t′→t0)(σt′(p′))=H(t′→t0)(p′)=pH(t' \to t_0)(\sigma_{t'}(p')) = H(t' \to t_0)(p') = p but σt′(p′)>p′\sigma_{t'}(p') > p', contradicting σt0(p)=p\sigma_{t_0}(p) = p (which forces the restriction of any element above p′p' to be above p=pp = p, but the restriction of σt′(p′)\sigma_{t'}(p') equals p=p = restriction of p′p', so σt′(p′)=p′\sigma_{t'}(p') = p' by the saturation-restriction intertwining). Similarly for Δt′\Delta_{t'}. Therefore p′∈Ht′∗p' \in H^*_{t'}. □\square

Content. Lifts of precedents are themselves precedents. The institution following a precedent does not degrade it to SatShadow or TrShadow — it carries it forward at the same full stability. This is the sheaf-cohomological content: the global section corresponding to pp is lifted coherently.

Proposition 3 (The coherence condition for multi-path precedent following). Let p∈Ht0∗p \in H^*_{t_0} be a precedent. Let t1,t2≥t0t_1, t_2 \geq t_0 be two histories both reachable from t0t_0, with lifts p1∈Ht1∗p_1 \in H^*_{t_1} and p2∈Ht2∗p_2 \in H^*_{t_2}. Let t12=t1∧t2t_{12} = t_1 \wedge t_2 (the meet of t1t_1 and t2t_2 in the history poset) be their greatest common sub-history above t0t_0. Then the restrictions of p1p_1 and p2p_2 to Ht12H_{t_{12}} agree:

H(t1→t12)(p1)  =  H(t2→t12)(p2)  =  p12H(t_1 \to t_{12})(p_1) \;=\; H(t_2 \to t_{12})(p_2) \;=\; p_{12}

and p12∈Ht12∗p_{12} \in H^*_{t_{12}}.

Proof. Both p1p_1 and p2p_2 restrict to pp at t0t_0 (by hypothesis). By the sheaf condition on HH, compatible local sections (sections that agree on their overlaps) have a unique amalgamation. The sections p1∈Ht1∗p_1 \in H^*_{t_1} and p2∈Ht2∗p_2 \in H^*_{t_2} are compatible because they both restrict to pp at t0≤t12t_0 \leq t_{12}. The amalgamation is p12∈Ht12∗p_{12} \in H^*_{t_{12}}. □\square

Content. Precedent-following is globally coherent: two courts that both follow the same precedent — even developing the law independently along different doctrinal paths — produce decisions that are compatible at their common ground. This is Dworkin’s integrity condition formalized: the law as a whole is coherent precisely because precedent-following satisfies the sheaf condition.

Proposition 4 (Overruling moves the overruled element from H∗H^* to SatShadow\mathrm{SatShadow} in the new topology). Let J′J' be a Grothendieck topology on TT with J′⊃JJ' \supset J (finer topology — more covering sieves). Denote the nuclei under J′J' as σt0′\sigma'_{t_0} and Δt0′\Delta'_{t_0}. If J′J' is chosen so that Δt0′(p)>p\Delta'_{t_0}(p) > p (the new topology does not commit to pp’s forward-stability), then p∈SatShadowt0J′p \in \mathrm{SatShadow}^{J'}_{t_0} — backward-recognized under J′J' (the history of the decision is still there) but no longer forward-stable.

Proof. Since J′J' is finer than JJ, there are more covering sieves in J′(t0)J'(t_0), potentially introducing new independent extensions that pp is not in the image of. Specifically: if J′J' adds a covering family {t0′:t0′→t0}\{t_0' : t_0' \to t_0\} for which p∉image(H(t0′→t0))p \notin \mathrm{image}(H(t_0' \to t_0)) for some t0′t_0', then Δt0J′(p)=min⁡{b∈Fix(Δt0J′):b≥p}>p\Delta^{J'}_{t_0}(p) = \min\{b \in \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta^{J'}_{t_0}) : b \geq p\} > p. Meanwhile σt0′(p)=p\sigma'_{t_0}(p) = p still (the backward recognition is unchanged — the decision history t0t_0 is still in the past). □\square

Content. Overruling does not erase history. The decision at t0t_0 remains part of the history; the saturation nucleus σt0′\sigma'_{t_0} still recognizes pp as backward-settled (the old cases still happened). What overruling removes is forward commitment: the overruled decision is no longer carried into future fibers. The overruled precedent enters SatShadow — recognized, not followed.

Proposition 5 (The Fibonacci prescription rate as spectral structure of precedent accumulation). In the extremally-slow-growth regime (minimal non-trivial commutation: one commuting pair (s1,s2)∈I(s_1, s_2) \in I), the number of histories at depth nn above t0t_0 that must carry a lift of pp before precedential status is confirmed grows as Fn+1F_{n+1} (Fibonacci numbers). The rate of confirmation per depth level converges to φ=(1+5)/2\varphi = (1+\sqrt5)/2 (the golden ratio eigenvalue of the nuclear transfer matrix M=(1110)M = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix}).

Proof. By the nuclear Verlinde eigenvalue correspondence (RelationalHistoryFiberNuclearVerlinde­EigenvalueCorrespondence): the eigenvalues of MM are φ\varphi and −1/φ-1/\varphi. The count dt0(n)d_{t_0}(n) of fibers at depth nn above t0t_0 that must carry a lift satisfies the recurrence dt0(n+1)=dt0(n)+dt0(n−1)d_{t_0}(n+1) = d_{t_0}(n) + d_{t_0}(n-1) — the Fibonacci recurrence driven by the two non-empty shadow classes. The dominant eigenvalue φ\varphi determines the asymptotic growth rate; the sub-dominant eigenvalue −1/φ-1/\varphi determines the oscillatory correction (the Binet formula). □\square

Content. The golden ratio is not an aesthetic feature of legal precedent but the spectral consequence of nuclear pair non-adjointness: the two shadow classes (TrShadow and SatShadow) each contribute independently to precedent accumulation, and their independence is what drives the Fibonacci recurrence. In legal terms: a decision becomes a precedent through two independent channels — backward recognition by subsequent decisions citing it (saturation direction) and forward commitment by the covering structure of the topology (transfer direction). The non-adjointness of these two channels is what gives precedent-setting its Fibonacci growth rate.

Precedent and the sheaf condition (Dworkin’s integrity)

The sheaf condition on H:Top→HAH : T^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{HA} is the formal content of Dworkin’s law-as-integrity. The sheaf condition requires: for any covering sieve S∈J(t0)S \in J(t_0), any compatible family of sections (at′)t′∈S(a_{t'})_{t' \in S} (sections at each history in the sieve that agree on overlaps) has a unique amalgamation at0∈Ht0a_{t_0} \in H_{t_0}.

Precedent-following is the special case where the covering family is the set of all histories above t0t_0 (the maximal sieve), and the compatible sections are all the lifts pt∈Ht∗p_t \in H^*_t. The sheaf condition guarantees that these local following-acts assemble into a global section — a coherent reading of the law as a whole that includes all precedents.

Dworkin’s integrity condition (“the law must be interpretable as an expression of coherent principle”) = the sheaf condition on the presheaf of legal validity: local legal decisions (sections at individual histories) must assemble into a globally coherent legal order (a global section over the full site).

What breaks integrity: A normative system fails the integrity condition when compatible local sections fail to assemble uniquely — when two lines of precedent-development that both follow some precedent pp arrive at incompatible conclusions at their meeting point t12t_{12}. This is a failure of the sheaf axiom and corresponds to a contradiction in the normative system’s coverage structure.

Open questions

  • Whether the prescription threshold k∗k^* — the depth at which a decision p∈TrShadowt0p \in \mathrm{TrShadow}_{t_0} (forward-committed but not yet backward-recognized) becomes a genuine precedent p∈Ht0∗p \in H^*_{t_0} — has a structural formula in terms of the covering topology JJ and the history’s commutation structure, or whether it is always case-specific.

  • Whether vertical stare decisis (higher-court decisions bind lower courts) can be formalized as a condition on the restriction maps between fibers at different levels of a hierarchical coverage structure — whether there is a topology JhierarchyJ_{\mathrm{hierarchy}} on TT that makes higher-level decisions automatically transfer-stable relative to lower-level extensions.

  • Whether the Grundnorm (Kelsen) — the root-level precedent pe∈He∗p_e \in H^*_e — has a constructive specification or whether it must be posited as an axiom: whether the history site (T,J)(T, J) with its initial fiber HeH_e has a canonical preferred element that plays the role of the Grundnorm, or whether the Grundnorm is always a choice.

  • Whether the second eigenvalue −1/φ-1/\varphi of the nuclear transfer matrix has a legal interpretation: the negative-valued, oscillatory correction to Fibonacci precedent-accumulation corresponds to an alternating structure in the precedent history — decisions that align with the SatShadow direction subtract from the TrShadow direction and vice versa. Whether this alternation corresponds to pendulum-like doctrinal swings in law (expansive interpretations followed by restrictive ones, cycling at the spectral frequency of −1/φ-1/\varphi).

  • Whether constitutional entrenchment — making certain elements of Ht0∗H^*_{t_0} immune to topology change (immune to overruling) — corresponds to a notion of a “super-sublocale” in Sl(Ht0)\mathrm{Sl}(H_{t_0}) that is preserved by all geometric morphisms into RR, and whether such super-sublocales are formal invariants of the topos Sh(T,J)\mathbf{Sh}(T, J).

  • Whether common law development (organic expansion of precedent by case-by-case application) and statutory law (top-down rules established by legislation) have distinct nuclear signatures: whether common law = the process of pp moving from TrShadow to H∗H^* through accumulated citations (saturation nucleus settling the decision), while statutory law = the process of pp being installed directly in H∗H^* by a constitutive act (declaration-type act bypassing the saturation accumulation).
