Waiver

2026-03-20 A Waiver is a triple (Q, C, ω) — a right-holder Q, a Hohfeldian claim or immunity C held by Q in H*_{t*}, and a waiver act ω that drives C from H*_{t*} to ⊥_{t*}. The defining structure: waiver is unilateral relinquishment of a right by the right-holder — Q alone can waive Q's own right; the correlative obligor's consent is not required and the obligor cannot prevent it. After ω, C is extinguished: the correlative duty that had burdened the obligor is discharged. Formally: waiver is the retraction of C's section from H*_{t*} to ⊥ via the transferring nucleus Δ_{t*} applied in the dissolving direction — the right that had transferred into the doubly-settled layer is driven back to the bottom element.

Formal definition

A Waiver is a triple W=(Q,C,ω)\mathcal{W} = (Q, C, \omega):

W=(Q:RightHolder,  C∈Ht∗∗,  ω:Ht∗∗→⊥t∗)\mathcal{W} = (Q : \mathrm{RightHolder},\; C \in H^*_{t^*},\; \omega : H^*_{t^*} \to \bot_{t^*})

where:

  • QQ is the right-holder — the party whose Hohfeldian claim or immunity CC is being relinquished; QQ must be the current holder of CC in Ht∗∗H^*_{t^*}
  • C∈Ht∗∗C \in H^*_{t^*} is the claim or immunity — the Hohfeldian position being waived; it is doubly settled: σt∗(C)=C\sigma_{t^*}(C) = C and Δt∗(C)=C\Delta_{t^*}(C) = C; the right exists and has transferred into the settled layer
  • ω\omega is the waiver act — the formal event (written release, explicit verbal relinquishment, or course of conduct clearly manifesting intent to waive) that drives CC from Ht∗∗H^*_{t^*} to ⊥t∗\bot_{t^*}; after ω\omega, CC is extinguished and the correlative duty on the obligor lapses

Three invariants. W\mathcal{W} is a valid waiver iff:

  1. Right-holder exclusivity: only QQ can waive CC. The correlative obligor cannot extinguish the right unilaterally; the obligor can only perform (discharging the duty by satisfaction) or breach (failing to perform, triggering remedial duties). Waiver is QQ’s unilateral act. The reason: CC is a Hohfeldian claim — a normative position held by QQ against the obligor; it belongs to QQ’s normative domain.

  2. Existence of the right: C∈Ht∗∗C \in H^*_{t^*} — the right must be currently settled before it can be waived. Waiving a non-existent right is void (C∉Ht∗∗C \notin H^*_{t^*} means there is nothing at CC’s position to drive to ⊥\bot). A right that has already lapsed (discharged by performance, or previously waived) cannot be waived again.

  3. Intentionality: ω\omega must manifest an intent to relinquish CC. Waiver requires knowledge of the right and voluntary choice to give it up. Inadvertent waiver (not knowing one had the right) is contested — courts vary on whether an unknowing waiver is effective, but the institutional structure requires that ω\omega be an act attributable to QQ’s intentional agency, not an accident.

Express waiver vs. implied waiver

Express waiver: QQ explicitly declares relinquishment — a written release, a formal statement “I waive my right to X.” This is the clear case: ω\omega is a discrete, documented act with identifiable moment of effect. In the formal framework: ω\omega applies Δt∗\Delta_{t^*} in the dissolving direction at the moment of declaration.

Implied waiver: QQ’s conduct unambiguously manifests intent to waive without an explicit declaration. Classic example: a party who proceeds with a contract despite knowing of a breach impliedly waives the breach. In the formal framework: the implied waiver is a sequence of acts whose propositional content, viewed as a whole, satisfies ω(C)=⊥\omega(C) = \bot — no single act is the waiver, but the combined section of QQ’s behavior drives CC to ⊥\bot by course of conduct.

Implied waiver creates a proof-of-intent problem: whether QQ’s conduct amounts to waiver is a factual question resolved by the forum (Standing) that adjudicates the dispute. Formal implied waiver requires the forum’s σ-settlement of the “waived” proposition — the forum’s nucleus determines whether the conduct counts as a waiver act.

Waiver and estoppel

Waiver and equitable estoppel (Estoppel, if specified) both result in a party being unable to assert a right. The distinction:

  • Waiver: QQ voluntarily relinquishes CC; CC is extinguished; the right is gone.
  • Estoppel: QQ is prevented by the forum from asserting CC because of prior conduct that led another party to rely to their detriment; CC may still exist but QQ cannot exercise it in this context.

In the formal framework: waiver drives CC to ⊥t∗\bot_{t^*} (the right is gone from the fiber). Estoppel blocks the path from C∈Ht∗C \in H_{t^*} to Ht∗∗H^*_{t^*} — the σ-nucleus refuses to settle CC for QQ in this forum — but CC may remain accessible elsewhere.

Waiver of procedural vs. substantive rights

Procedural rights (right to trial by jury, right to object to evidence, right to receive notice) are generally waivable by conduct. Failure to timely assert a procedural right typically constitutes waiver: missing the deadline for objection drives the right to ⊥\bot for purposes of that proceeding. In formal terms: the forum’s site topology JJ specifies the temporal window for the procedural right; outside that window, JJ-coverage is no longer available for the right’s assertion.

Substantive rights (property rights, fundamental constitutional rights, rights under mandatory law) may be subject to non-waiver rules — some rights cannot be waived even by the holder. These are rights where the legislature has determined that the right-holder’s consent to waiver is not sufficient: the right is held not just for QQ but for a class of persons, and individual waiver would undermine the collective protection. In formal terms: non-waivable rights are rights where the waiver-retraction ω\omega is blocked by a higher-level nucleus — the culture or normative system’s topology JJ will not allow the right to be driven to ⊥\bot regardless of QQ’s intent.

Math grounding

Waiver is the retraction of CC’s section from the doubly-settled layer Ht∗∗=Fix(σt∗)∩Fix(Δt∗)H^*_{t^*} = \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t^*}) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t^*}). The transferring nucleus Δt∗\Delta_{t^*} placed CC in Ht∗∗H^*_{t^*} by transfer from a lower level (Passage, forward pole — the carrier direction placed CC in the settled layer). Waiver reverses this: ω\omega applies Δt∗\Delta_{t^*} in the retraction direction, driving CC back to ⊥\bot.

In Section terms: CC was a global section of the right-fibration over QQ’s authority domain. Waiver retracts this section: the right inverse sC:{C}→Ht∗∗s_C : \{C\} \to H^*_{t^*} is collapsed to the zero section sC↦⊥t∗s_C \mapsto \bot_{t^*}.

The distinction between waiver (unilateral, right-holder) and revocation (unilateral, grantor) is that waiver retracts from the right side of the Hohfeldian relation — the claim — while revocation retracts from the power side — the grant. Both use the section retraction structure; they differ in which Hohfeldian position is being retracted.

Open questions

  • Whether a waiver of a waiver is possible — whether after ω\omega drives CC to ⊥t∗\bot_{t^*}, a subsequent act ω−1\omega^{-1} can re-establish CC in Ht∗∗H^*_{t^*} — and whether this depends on whether the waiver was express or implied and whether there was reliance by the obligor.
  • Whether the non-waivable right rule (some rights cannot be driven to ⊥\bot regardless of the holder’s consent) has a formal expression as a nucleus constraint: whether a right CC is non-waivable iff σt∗(C)=C\sigma_{t^*}(C) = C and the cultural topology JJ contains no covering sieve that would certify ω(C)=⊥\omega(C) = \bot — i.e., no JJ-covering makes the retraction locally valid.
  • Whether implied waiver (accumulated conduct) is the colimit of the sequence of partial-waiver acts in the category of section retractions, and whether the colimit exists iff the sequence of acts is cofinal in the σt∗\sigma_{t^*}-retraction order.
