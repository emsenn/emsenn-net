Communications Officer

2026-04-05 The office responsible for managing information flow between organizational units, translating between internal and external registers, and ensuring messages reach the right recipients in the right form.

A communications officer is the office responsible for the controlled flow of information between organizational units. The office exists because organizations are not single minds — they are composed of parts that operate on different schedules, in different registers, with different attention constraints. Without a dedicated communications function, messages degrade: they arrive late, in the wrong form, at the wrong recipient, or not at all.

¶What the office does

The communications officer manages three classes of information flow:

  1. Inter-unit messaging. Information that must pass between parts of the organization — status reports, requests, notifications, handoffs. The communications officer does not generate this information; she ensures it arrives. The distinction matters: the engineering office produces an engineering report; the communications officer ensures the captain receives it in time to act.

  2. External communication. Information that crosses the organizational boundary — messages to other organizations, public statements, formal correspondence. External communication requires register translation: internal shorthand must become intelligible to recipients who do not share the organization’s vocabulary or context.

  3. Protocol management. The rules governing who may send what to whom, in what form, through what channels. Not all information should flow freely. The communications officer maintains the routing rules — what is broadcast, what is point-to-point, what requires acknowledgment, what is classified by audience.

¶Organizational lineage

The role traces to the signal officer in military organizations — the person responsible for ensuring orders and intelligence move reliably between command and field units. Claude Elwood Shannon’s mathematical theory of communication (1948) formalized the core problem: transmitting a message selected at one point so that it can be reconstructed at another, across a noisy channel. The communications officer is the organizational embodiment of the encoding-channel-decoding structure.

In corporate governance, the function appears as the corporate secretary (managing board communications), the public relations officer (managing external messaging), and the internal communications function (managing organizational messaging). Chester Barnard’s The Functions of the Executive (1938) identified communication as the first function of the executive — not the content of decisions, but the system that makes coordinated action possible by connecting decision-makers to each other and to the field.

¶Fiber structure

The communications officer’s officium has a distinctive recognition-production pair:

σ fiber (what the office recognizes): Messages in transit — their sender, intended recipient, urgency, channel, and delivery status. The communications officer recognizes when a message has failed to arrive, when a channel is degraded, when a recipient is unreachable, and when a message is in the wrong register for its audience. Recognition also covers protocol violations: messages sent through improper channels, notifications that bypassed required recipients, acknowledgments that were never returned.

Δ fiber (what the office produces): Delivered messages — information that has arrived at the correct recipient, in the correct form, with confirmation of receipt. The communications officer also produces routing tables (who receives what), channel status reports (which pathways are functioning), and protocol documents (the rules of information flow). The output is not the content of the messages but the fact of their reliable delivery.

¶Formality level

F1 — low formality. The communications function requires a defined office (someone must be responsible for routing) but does not require the full Hohfeldian apparatus of powers, immunities, and formal investiture that higher offices carry. In small organizations, the function is often absorbed by the secretary or clerk. It becomes a distinct office when the organization is large enough that information routing is itself a full-time coordination problem — when the number of communication channels (n(n−1)/2n(n-1)/2 for nn units) exceeds what any single officer can manage as a secondary duty.

¶What distinguishes the communications officer

From the secretary. The secretary records and preserves organizational memory — minutes, decisions, records. The communications officer moves information between living agents. The secretary’s concern is the archive; the communications officer’s concern is the channel. In practice, both functions often share an office, but they are structurally distinct: one faces backward (what was decided), the other faces outward (what must be transmitted).

From the clerk. The clerk processes standard forms according to established procedure. The communications officer routes novel messages through appropriate channels. The clerk’s competence is procedural correctness; the communications officer’s competence is channel management and register translation.
