Feminist Organizational Theory: A Survey of How Governance Reproduces Gender Inequality

Mainstream organizational theory treats structures—hierarchies, roles, accountability chains, decision procedures—as if they operate on genderless agents. Feminist organizational theory rejects this fiction. The five strands covered here argue that governance structures are gendered at their foundations: they encode assumptions about what counts as work, who is capable of authority, what labor is visible and compensated, and who bears the costs of the organization’s functioning. Governance does not simply reflect gender inequality that exists elsewhere; governance actively produces it through formal procedures that appear neutral.

The problem is not individual bias—women in leadership, diverse hiring, anti-discrimination policy—though these matter. The problem is structural: organizations depend on uncompensated reproductive and care labor; they define “the worker” as a being without family obligations or bodily vulnerability; they require emotional labor that is disproportionately extracted from women while remaining invisible in job descriptions; and they organize authority as a public sphere separated from the private sphere where care happens. These are not accidents; they are engineered features.

Joan Acker: The abstract worker is male

Joan Acker’s “Hierarchies, Jobs, Bodies: A Theory of Gendered Organizations” (1990) inaugurates feminist organizational theory by showing that organizational structures are not gender-neutral containers. The central claim: organizations are built on an image of “the worker” that is implicitly male.

The abstract worker assumed by organizational thinking is a being with no body, no sexuality, no obligations to reproduction or care. This worker is available for unlimited hours. He has a wife at home who manages his biological needs—food, laundry, childcare, elder care. He has no menstrual cycle, no risk of pregnancy, no lactation. He never needs to leave work to care for a sick child. The organizational structure—schedules, career progression paths, advancement criteria—is designed for this body and this life. It is designed for men, and specifically for men of sufficient class position to have unpaid reproductive labor performed by women.

Acker identifies five processes that reproduce gender in organizations:

  1. The division of labor: certain jobs are segregated by gender—not by explicit rule but through hiring, socialization, and organizational culture that makes some positions “naturally” suited to women (administrative support, nursing, early childhood teaching) and others to men (management, engineering, technical roles). Gender segregation makes it possible to pay women less for the same work.

  2. Cultural symbols and images: organizations use metaphors of warfare, conquest, competition—masculine imagery that defines authority as masculine and makes masculine qualities the standard of leadership.

  3. Workplace interactions: everyday interaction patterns reproduce gender—men’s interrupting, taking up space in meetings, receiving credit for collaborative work; women’s deferential listening, their emotional labor at the service of team cohesion, their ideas credited to others.

  4. Individual identities: people internalize gendered identities as they move through organizational life. Women learn to self-edit, to present as less ambitious than they are, to prioritize others’ needs. Men learn to claim credit and to resist emotional vulnerability.

  5. Organizational logic: the structure itself—the separation of public (paid work) from private (home), the definition of commitment as unlimited availability, the measurement of productivity in hours rather than output—depends on the assumption that someone else is doing the unpaid work that makes participation possible.

The consequence: organizations produce gender inequality not only by discriminating against women, but by structurally depending on women’s unpaid reproductive and care labor. The organization can claim to be neutral and meritocratic while extracting value from gender-structured unpaid work.

Calás and Smircich: Management theory carries gendered assumptions

Marta Calás and Linda Smircich’s feminist readings of organizational theory (beginning with “From the Woman’s Point of View,” 1996) show that the key concepts of organizational thinking are gendered at the semantic level.

Take motivation. Mainstream theory defines motivation as individual drive toward organizational goals—the worker seeks advancement, status, compensation. This framework assumes a person with no competing claims on their time. It assumes motivation is competition, not cooperation or care. But many people’s primary motivation is care: caring for children, for elders, for community. The organizational theory that cannot see care as motivation cannot see women’s actual motivations; it can only pathologize care as distraction.

Or take leadership. Mainstream leadership theory defines the leader as a visionary, charismatic, assertive figure who exerts control over subordinates. This definition carries masculine imagery and masculine behavioral expectations: assertiveness rather than listening, individual vision rather than collective deliberation, emotional distance rather than emotional attunement. When women fail to fit this image, they are not seen as different leaders; they are seen as failed leaders.

Or take organizational culture. The metaphor of culture suggests shared meaning, identity, cohesion. But whose meaning? Organizational culture is often the culture of those in power—their humor, their communication styles, their values. Feminist organizations that attempt to operate non-hierarchically, that prioritize emotional awareness and collective process, that measure success by caring for people rather than expanding output—these are systematically defined as lacking organizational culture, or as having “weak” culture. The feminist organization is pathologized as disorganized precisely because it refuses to reproduce masculine competitive logic.

Calás and Smircich argue that feminist scholarship does not just add women to existing organizational frameworks. It asks: what does organizational thinking look like if we start from women’s actual experience—the multiplicity of obligations, the integration of care into work, the interdependence rather than autonomous individual decision-making? This is not a matter of bringing in more content; it requires rethinking the foundational concepts.

Patricia Yancey Martin: Gender is actively practiced, not just reflected

Patricia Yancey Martin’s work on “practicing gender” (2003 and after) shifts the question from “does this organization have gender inequality?” to “how is gender being actively produced in everyday organizational practice?”

Gender is not a characteristic that people have and bring into the organization. Gender is something people do, repeatedly, often without conscious awareness. In her fieldwork in corporations, Martin observes: the woman who laughs at the male executive’s jokes because not laughing would be read as unfeminine hostility; the man who uses displays of technical knowledge and professional distance to enact authority; the woman who performs warmth and nurture in team settings while performing professionalism in presentations to senior leadership; the meeting dynamic where men speak longer, receive fewer interruptions, and have their comments remembered.

These are not conscious strategies. They are unreflexive practices, done in concert with others, informed by what Martin calls “liminal awareness”—not fully conscious, but not entirely automatic either. The key insight: gender is made and remade in these daily practices. An organization can have formal policies of gender equality while its everyday practices systematically subordinate women.

Moreover, these practices are reciprocal and interdependent. Women’s practices of deference and emotional labor are not separable from men’s practices of authority-claiming and emotional distance. They constitute each other. The man cannot assert authority without the woman’s recognition of it; the woman cannot provide care without the man’s accepting it as his due. The governance structure that appears to be neutral—“we all follow the same rules”—depends on this gendered choreography to function.

Martin’s contribution to feminist organizational theory is to show that formal neutrality is not the solution. Naming the practices, making them visible, requiring reflexivity—who did what work in this meeting? who was credited? who was listened to?—is the method of interrupting the unreflexive reproduction of gender. But this requires sustained attention to the doing of gender, not just the structure.

Silvia Federici: Governance depends on the invisibility of reproductive labor

Silvia Federici’s analysis (2004, 2012) grounds feminist organizational theory in political economy. She argues that capitalist governance—the organization of work and production—depends fundamentally on the appropriation of uncompensated reproductive labor, primarily women’s labor.

In Caliban and the Witch, Federici traces how the transition to capitalism required not just the enclosure of common lands (expropriating peasants’ access to the commons), but the enclosure of women’s bodies—the systematic control of women’s sexuality, reproduction, and productive capacity. The witch hunts, Federici argues, were a political project aimed at women who resisted this enclosure—women with knowledge of contraception and abortion, women healers whose autonomy over reproduction threatened capitalist control, women who organized collectively for their own survival.

What emerged was a gender division of labor: men’s unpaid reproductive labor—growing food to feed workers, bearing and raising children, caring for the ill and old, cooking and cleaning—became women’s unpaid work, while men entered wage labor. This gender division made capitalism possible. A worker can be paid a wage that covers only his own survival because women’s unpaid reproductive work covers his children’s survival and his own care. The value that women create through reproductive labor is appropriated by both capital and patriarchy.

For governance specifically: formal organizational hierarchies, accountability structures, and decision procedures all presuppose that someone is doing the reproductive work that sustains the organization’s members. The meeting schedule assumes someone else is managing childcare. The expectation of unlimited availability assumes someone else is cooking and cleaning and maintaining the household. The career path that requires uninterrupted work assumes someone else is caring for dependents.

The consequence: governance structures that appear to be organized by criteria of merit, efficiency, and fairness are actually organized by gender. They are designed for the person whose reproductive and care needs are met by someone else—historically and currently, women. This is not a side effect; it is structural. Any organization that does not explicitly interrupt this structure—by redistributing care work, by accounting for reproductive labor, by building care into job design—reproduces it.

bell hooks: Governance theory must center those at the margins

bell hooks’ Feminist Theory: From Margin to Center (1984) insists that governance theory that does not center those most marginalized by organizational power is incomplete. The mainstream women’s liberation movement of the 1960s-70s spoke as if all women’s problems were the same—but the liberation of white professional women required very different solutions than the liberation of Black women, poor women, women of color working as domestic servants and fast food workers.

hooks argues that feminist governance theory must ask: who benefits from the current structure, and who bears the costs? A governance reform that increases women’s access to management positions may improve the situation for white professional women while leaving poor women and women of color more exposed to exploitation. A policy that supports “working mothers” through childcare might provide subsidized care for some women while requiring other women to leave their own children to perform care work for wages.

Governance theory that does not center this analysis falls into what hooks calls “liberal feminism”—the assumption that access and inclusion are sufficient, that if women enter power structures the problem is solved. But power structures are not neutral containers. They are organized through intersecting oppressions: race, class, sexuality, nationality, disability. A woman’s position in an organization is not determined by gender alone but by how her gender intersects with her race, class position, immigration status, disability, and other dimensions of social location.

What does it mean for governance to be accountable to those most marginalized by organizational power? It means: their experience must be treated as knowledge. Their critiques must be listened to, not merely heard. Their conditions must be improved, not managed. The governance structure itself must change—not by admitting more women to existing hierarchies, but by asking whether those hierarchies should exist in their current form.

Synthesis: Three claims about gendered governance

The five thinkers converge on three core claims about how governance reproduces gender inequality:

1. Formal neutrality masks structural dependence on gendered unpaid labor.

Governance structures present themselves as neutral—everyone follows the same rules, advancement depends on merit, decisions are made through rational procedure. But these structures depend on a prior division of labor: someone is doing the unpaid reproductive and care work that makes participation possible. As long as this labor is invisible—not named, not compensated, not accounted for—governance can claim neutrality while systematically extracting value from women.

2. Authority, work, and virtue are defined in gendered terms.

The concepts that organize governance—what counts as work, what displays competence, what demonstrates leadership, what constitutes commitment to the organization—are all defined in terms of masculinity. Care work is not “real” work; it is a personal responsibility. Emotional attunement is not leadership; it is weakness. Availability to family is not legitimate; it is distraction. These definitions are embedded in job descriptions, evaluation criteria, and promotion procedures. They appear objective, but they encode gender.

3. Interrupting gender reproduction requires visible, reflexive change to governance structures.

Gender inequality cannot be solved by removing individual bias or improving representation at the top. It requires structural change: making reproductive labor visible and valued, redesigning work around care rather than around the fiction of the boundless worker, redistribuing care responsibilities rather than expecting women to do it, and crucially, including those most marginalized in the decision-making about how governance will change. This is not a one-time policy change but an ongoing practice of making visible how current structures reproduce gender and actively choosing to reproduce them differently.

Open questions

  • How can governance structures account for and value reproductive labor without reinforcing the assignment of it to women?
  • Can formal neutrality in governance ever interrupt gender reproduction, or does true change require abandoning neutrality as an ideal?
  • How do governance structures interact with other systems of oppression—race, class, disability, colonialism—to produce compounded inequalities, and what does it mean for governance to be accountable to those most affected?
  • What would governance look like if designed by and for those who have historically borne the costs of organizational functioning—workers, cleaners, care workers—rather than those at the top?
