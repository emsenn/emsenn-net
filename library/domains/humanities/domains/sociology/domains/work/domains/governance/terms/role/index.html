2026-03-18 A Role is a triple (Σ, N, ⪯) — a capability signature Σ specifying the minimum operations any bearer must provide, a norm-set N of role-indexed obligations and permissions that apply to any bearer, and a subsumption position ⪯ in the preorder of all roles ordered by signature inclusion. The defining structure: a role is a bearer-independent normative interface type — the deontic and capability profile that any agent must satisfy to count as filling it. Three projections of the same structure: a role as capability specification (Σ), as norm bundle (N), and as positional node in a role hierarchy (⪯). A role persists when vacant; its norms apply to any and every bearer; no role is constituted by its current occupant.

Role

Formal definition

A Role is a triple R=(κR,NR,ιR)\mathfrak{R} = (\kappa_R, N_R, \iota_R):

R=(κR:CapabilitySignature,  NR:Set(Norm),  ιR:CapabilitySignature.SubsumptionMorphism)\mathfrak{R} = (\kappa_R : \mathrm{CapabilitySignature},\; N_R : \mathrm{Set(Norm)},\; \iota_R : \mathrm{CapabilitySignature.SubsumptionMorphism})

where:

  • κR\kappa_R is the capability signature — a morphism κR:ParamNamesR→Types\kappa_R : \mathrm{ParamNames}_R \to \mathrm{Types} (see CapabilitySignature) specifying the minimum typed interface any bearer of RR must provide; agent aa fills RR iff the satisfaction proposition sa,κR∈Ht∗s_{a,\kappa_R} \in H^*_t — aa provides a doubly-settled value for every parameter in κR\kappa_R; in a rule-based system, κR\kappa_R corresponds to the parameter block of the role’s specification
  • NRN_R is the norm-set — the finite set of Norms indexed to role RR; obligations, permissions, and prohibitions that apply to any bearer of RR while they bear it; the norms are role-indexed, not agent-indexed: when aa fills RR, all n∈NRn \in N_R are instantiated with aa as bearer; when aa vacates RR, the norms deactivate for aa and activate for whatever new bearer fills RR; role norms are positional, not personal
  • ιR\iota_R is the subsumption morphism set — the set of subsumption morphisms ι:κR′↪κR\iota : \kappa_{R'} \hookrightarrow \kappa_R (from any less specific role R′R' into RR) making RR a node in the category of capability signatures ordered by subsumption; ι:κR′↪κR\iota : \kappa_{R'} \hookrightarrow \kappa_R exists iff dom(κR′)⊆dom(κR)\mathrm{dom}(\kappa_{R'}) \subseteq \mathrm{dom}(\kappa_R) with matching types; filling RR entails filling every R′R' for which such a morphism exists

Four invariants. R\mathfrak{R} is a role iff it satisfies:

  1. Bearer-independence: R\mathfrak{R} exists independently of any current bearer. The role is constituted by (κR,NR,ιR)(\kappa_R, N_R, \iota_R), not by any agent currently filling it. An unoccupied role is still a role — its norms are dormant (not activated for any bearer) but the normative profile is fully defined. This is what distinguishes a role from a disposition or habit: habits depend on the specific individual having them; roles are defined by a position in a normative system that persists whether or not any individual currently occupies it.

  2. Signature completeness: κR\kappa_R is complete with respect to NRN_R — every act referred to in any n∈NRn \in N_R is representable within dom(κR)\mathrm{dom}(\kappa_R). A norm requiring performance of a parameter not declared in κR\kappa_R would be normatively impossible to satisfy; no role can generate obligations without the corresponding capability declaration. Formally: for each n∈NRn \in N_R whose propositional content refers to a parameter pp, the name pp must be in dom(κR)\mathrm{dom}(\kappa_R).

  3. Subsumption coherence: If ι:κR1↪κR\iota : \kappa_{R_1} \hookrightarrow \kappa_R is a subsumption morphism, then for every n1∈NR1n_1 \in N_{R_1} there is a corresponding obligation entailment in NRN_R — the more specific role carries all the obligations of the less specific role it subsumes, plus potentially more. More capable roles carry more or equal normative burden. This ensures that filling RR cannot evade the obligations of any R1R_1 for which a subsumption morphism into RR exists.

  4. Positional distinctness: Two roles R1R_1 and R2R_2 in the same normative system are the same role iff κR1=κR2\kappa_{R_1} = \kappa_{R_2} (equal capability signatures as morphisms) and NR1=NR2N_{R_1} = N_{R_2}. Different names for the same (κ,N)(\kappa, N) pair are labels for the same role. This invariant rules out phantom roles — positional labels without distinct capability or normative content.

Role vs. Office

The distinction between role and office runs through the entire normative ontology. Both describe positions an agent can occupy. The difference is constitutive depth.

A Role is a normative interface type: it specifies what you can and must do when you occupy it. Filling a role is capability-matching: you fill RR by operative satisfaction of κR\kappa_R — i.e., sa,κR∈Ht∗s_{a,\kappa_R} \in H^*_t — and acceptance of the norm-set NRN_R. No formal act is required to fill a role you are capable of filling; role-filling can be informal.

An Office is a formally invested deontic position: a full HohfeldianPosition profile (powers, duties, privileges, immunities) transferred by a formal investiture act I+I^+. The office specifies not just what the occupant can do but what they must, may, and are immune from — the complete four-position Hohfeldian profile. Offices are installed; roles are filled.

An Office always defines a Role: the office’s power-profile specifies a capability signature and the office’s duties specify a norm-set. A Role does not always define an Office: roles can be filled without formal investiture, and roles specify capability without the full Hohfeldian correlative structure that offices carry.

Practically: filling a role does not confer the deontic standing that holding an office confers. A de facto captain — one who performs all functions of the captain role but was never formally invested — lacks the captain’s legal authority, immunities, and formal accountability structure.

Nuclear derivation

Role proposition. For each role RR in normative system II at history tt, define:

ρR∈Ht\rho_R \in H_t

as the proposition “role RR is constitutively active in II at history tt.”

Filling proposition. For each agent aa and role RR, define:

fa,R∈Htf_{a,R} \in H_t

as the proposition “agent aa fills role RR at history tt.”

Role operativity. Role RR is operative at tt iff:

ρR∈Ht∗=Fix(σt)∩Fix(Δt)\rho_R \in H^*_t = \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t)

The double-settlement condition: σt(ρR)=ρR\sigma_t(\rho_R) = \rho_R — the normative community recognizes RR as a valid position within II; and Δt(ρR)=ρR\Delta_t(\rho_R) = \rho_R — the role structure is stable forward and will not be dissolved in the next step.

Filling operativity. Agent aa operatively fills role RR at tt iff:

fa,R∈Ht∗f_{a,R} \in H^*_t

This requires ρR∈Ht∗\rho_R \in H^*_t (role is operative), a∈Ht∗a \in H^*_t (agent is a recognized Person-node), and the satisfaction proposition sa,κR∈Ht∗s_{a,\kappa_R} \in H^*_t — agent aa operatively satisfies the role’s CapabilitySignature κR\kappa_R.

Four failure modes. The nuclear partition gives four institutional states for ρR\rho_R:

Quadrant σt(ρR)\sigma_t(\rho_R) Δt(ρR)\Delta_t(\rho_R) Role status
Ht∗H^*_t =ρR= \rho_R =ρR= \rho_R Operative: recognized and institutionally stable
Fix(σt)∖Ht∗\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) \setminus H^*_t =ρR= \rho_R >ρR> \rho_R Recognized but fragile — community accepts it but the normative structure cannot sustain it forward (a declining profession, an obsolete office)
Fix(Δt)∖Ht∗\mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) \setminus H^*_t >ρR> \rho_R =ρR= \rho_R Structurally required but unrecognized — a latent role the system needs but has not yet named
Ht∖(Ht∗∪Fix(σt)∪Fix(Δt))H_t \setminus (H^*_t \cup \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) \cup \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t)) >ρR> \rho_R >ρR> \rho_R Proposed only — drafted but neither recognized nor structurally operative

Theorem (Norm Activation). If fa,R∈Ht∗f_{a,R} \in H^*_t and n∈NRn \in N_R, then the instantiated norm n[a/bearer]∈Fix(σt)n[a/\mathrm{bearer}] \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t).

Proof sketch. Since fa,R∈Ht∗⊆Fix(σt)f_{a,R} \in H^*_t \subseteq \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t), the filling is meaning-settled. The normative system II’s saturation nucleus σt\sigma_t is defined such that Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) is closed under norm-instantiation from role-fillings — this is the meaning-closure condition of the institution (see Institution, Invariant 2). For any n∈NRn \in N_R, the instantiation n[a/bearer]n[a/\mathrm{bearer}] is the norm with the generic bearer replaced by the specific agent aa; the meaning-closure of the institution forces every constitutive rule and every norm-instantiation that follows from a settled role-filling into Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t). Therefore n[a/bearer]∈Fix(σt)n[a/\mathrm{bearer}] \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t). □\square

The Norm Activation Theorem is the nuclear content of Parsons’ claim that roles carry normative expectations: the moment filling is operative, the full norm-set is meaning-settled for the bearer.

Theorem (Subsumption Monotonicity). If ι:κR1↪κR2\iota : \kappa_{R_1} \hookrightarrow \kappa_{R_2} is a subsumption morphism and fa,R2∈Ht∗f_{a,R_2} \in H^*_t, then fa,R1∈Ht∗f_{a,R_1} \in H^*_t.

Proof sketch. fa,R2∈Ht∗f_{a,R_2} \in H^*_t implies sa,κR2∈Ht∗s_{a,\kappa_{R_2}} \in H^*_t (operative satisfaction of κR2\kappa_{R_2}). By the Subsumption Satisfaction Monotonicity theorem (CapabilitySignature): since ι:κR1↪κR2\iota : \kappa_{R_1} \hookrightarrow \kappa_{R_2} is a subsumption morphism, operative satisfaction of κR2\kappa_{R_2} entails operative satisfaction of κR1\kappa_{R_1}, i.e., sa,κR1∈Ht∗s_{a,\kappa_{R_1}} \in H^*_t. Since fa,R1f_{a,R_1} is constituted by sa,κR1s_{a,\kappa_{R_1}} together with ρR1∈Ht∗\rho_{R_1} \in H^*_t (which holds by the same argument applied to the role propositions), we have fa,R1∈Ht∗f_{a,R_1} \in H^*_t. □\square

Subsumption Monotonicity is the nuclear content of the RBAC role hierarchy: filling a more specific role simultaneously fills every role it subsumes.

Role vacancy. Role RR is vacant at tt iff:

ρR∈Ht∗and∄ a:fa,R∈Ht∗\rho_R \in H^*_t \quad \text{and} \quad \nexists\, a : f_{a,R} \in H^*_t

The role is operative but no agent’s filling of it is operative. Vacancy is well-defined: ρR∈Ht∗\rho_R \in H^*_t while all filling propositions remain outside Ht∗H^*_t.

Role conflict. Agent aa faces role conflict between R1R_1 and R2R_2 at tt iff fa,R1,fa,R2∈Ht∗f_{a,R_1}, f_{a,R_2} \in H^*_t and there exist n1∈NR1n_1 \in N_{R_1}, n2∈NR2n_2 \in N_{R_2} such that:

n1[a/bearer]∧n2[a/bearer]∉Ht∗n_1[a/\mathrm{bearer}] \wedge n_2[a/\mathrm{bearer}] \notin H^*_t

The jointly instantiated norms from both roles cannot be doubly-settled simultaneously. By the Norm Activation Theorem, both n1[a/bearer]n_1[a/\mathrm{bearer}] and n2[a/bearer]n_2[a/\mathrm{bearer}] are in Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) (meaning-settled) whenever both fillings are operative. Role conflict is therefore not a recognition failure — both norms are recognized — but an execution failure: the obligations cannot be jointly execution-settled (Δt\Delta_t-settled) at the same history. This is the nuclear characterization of Biddle’s role strain: not incoherence of recognition but impossibility of simultaneous execution.
