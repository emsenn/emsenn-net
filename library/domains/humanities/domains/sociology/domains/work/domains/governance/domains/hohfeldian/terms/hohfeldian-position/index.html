Hohfeldian Position

2026-03-20 A HohfeldianPosition is one of eight fundamental legal concepts organized in two quartets — (claim, duty, privilege, no-claim) and (power, liability, immunity, disability) — that together constitute the primitive vocabulary for any normative system. Every normative relation between two parties can be decomposed into one or more Hohfeldian positions. The fundamental distinction: first-quartet positions govern what parties must, may, and cannot do (act-level); second-quartet positions govern who can alter act-level normative relations (meta-level).

¶What this is

A HohfeldianPosition is one of the eight fundamental legal concepts identified by Wesley Newcomb Hohfeld (Fundamental Legal Conceptions as Applied in Judicial Reasoning, Yale Law Journal, 1913–1917). The insight: the word “right” is used to mean at least four distinct things; juridical confusion follows from failing to distinguish them. Hohfeld’s analysis provides the primitive vocabulary that makes normative relations precisely statable.

The eight concepts form two quartets. Each quartet has an internal structure of opposites (what you don’t have if you have this) and correlatives (what the other party has, necessarily, when you have this).

¶The two quartets

¶First quartet: act-level positions

These govern what parties may, must, and cannot do. They are positions with respect to acts.

Position Symbol Formal reading Opposite Correlative
Claim-right A Rφ BA \,\mathbf{R}_\varphi\, B AA has a right against BB that BB shall φ\varphi No-right Duty
Duty A Dφ BA \,\mathbf{D}_\varphi\, B AA has a duty toward BB to φ\varphi Privilege Claim-right
Privilege A Pφ BA \,\mathbf{P}_\varphi\, B AA has a privilege against BB to φ\varphi Duty No-claim
No-claim A ¬Rφ BA \,\neg\mathbf{R}_\varphi\, B AA has no right against BB that BB not φ\varphi Claim-right Privilege

Claim-right (right in the strict Hohfeldian sense): AA has a claim-right against BB that BB shall φ\varphi iff BB is under a duty to AA to φ\varphi. The claim-right is the correlative of duty: wherever there is a duty, there is a correlative right; wherever there is a right, there is a correlative duty. A claim-right imposes an obligation on its subject. Most uses of “I have a right” are claims in this sense: the speaker asserts that others are obligated with respect to them.

Duty: AA is under a duty to BB to φ\varphi iff BB has a claim-right against AA that AA shall φ\varphi. Duty is the correlative of claim-right. The existence of a duty entails the existence of a correlative right-holder. This is the analysis of Duty in the relational system: a duty D(a,φ,r)\mathbf{D}(a, \varphi, r) is never merely a relation between aa and an act — it is always a three-place relation involving the correlative party rr.

Privilege (liberty): AA has a privilege against BB to φ\varphi iff AA is not under a duty to BB not to φ\varphi. A privilege is the absence of a duty. The free person who is at liberty to do something has a privilege — there is no correlative duty not to do it. A privilege is not a right: AA’s privilege to φ\varphi does not mean BB must let AA φ\varphi; it means only that AA is not wrong to φ\varphi. A property owner has a claim-right that strangers not trespass and a privilege to use the property; the stranger has only a privilege to look (no duty not to look) but no privilege to enter (there is a duty not to trespass).

No-claim: the opposite of claim-right; the correlative of privilege. AA has no-claim against BB that BB not φ\varphi iff BB has a privilege to φ\varphi relative to AA. If you have a privilege to speak your mind about me in public, I have no-claim against you that you not.

¶Second quartet: meta-level positions

These govern who can alter first-quartet normative relations. They are positions at one level higher — about the structure of rights and duties, not about particular acts.

Position Symbol Formal reading Opposite Correlative
Power A Pwφ BA \,\mathbf{Pw}_\varphi\, B AA has the power to alter BB’s normative position re: φ\varphi Disability Liability
Liability A Liφ BA \,\mathbf{Li}_\varphi\, B AA’s normative position re: φ\varphi is alterable by BB Immunity Power
Immunity A Imφ BA \,\mathbf{Im}_\varphi\, B AA’s normative position re: φ\varphi cannot be altered by BB Liability Disability
Disability A ¬Pwφ BA \,\neg\mathbf{Pw}_\varphi\, B AA cannot alter BB’s normative position re: φ\varphi Power Immunity

Power: AA has the power to alter BB’s normative position with respect to φ\varphi. This is the capacity to create, modify, extinguish, or transform first-quartet relations — to make new normative facts. Powers are second-order: they operate on the space of rights and duties, not on acts directly. Examples: a judge’s power to convict (alters the defendant’s legal status); a sovereign’s power to legislate (creates new duties for all subjects); a contracting party’s power to offer (creates a power in the offeree to form the contract); an officer’s power to issue orders (creates duties in subordinates); a testator’s power to transfer property at death.

Exercising a power is not performing an act that is privileged — it is constituting a new normative fact. This is why powers cannot be analyzed as merely acting in a certain way. The judge’s pronouncement of guilt constitutes the conviction; it is not merely behavior that happens to be correlated with guilt.

Liability: AA is under a liability with respect to BB’s power iff BB has the power to alter AA’s normative position. Every correlative of a power is a liability. Being liable to another’s power is not bad in itself — being liable to an offer (having the power exercised on you by the offeror) is just the condition of being able to enter contracts.

Immunity: AA has an immunity against BB’s power iff BB cannot alter AA’s normative position with respect to φ\varphi. Immunities protect the first-quartet position from outside alteration. Constitutional rights as immunities: even the legislature (which has vast normative powers) cannot alter certain normative positions of individuals — those individuals are immune from legislative power in those domains. Judicial immunity: a judge cannot be sued for acts done in judicial capacity — those acts are beyond the reach of civil liability.

Disability: AA is under a disability iff AA lacks the power to alter BB’s normative position. The correlative of immunity. A minor is under a disability to form binding contracts; the minor’s attempted contracts are voidable because the minor lacked the power to alter the other party’s normative position in the required way.

¶The key analytical moves

“Right” is ambiguous across four positions. “I have a right to do X” may mean:

  1. I have a claim-right that you not interfere with my doing X (generates your duty not to interfere)
  2. I have a privilege to do X (no duty on me not to do X; says nothing about your duty)
  3. I have a power to do X and thereby alter your normative position (create duties, claims, liabilities)
  4. I have an immunity from your preventing me from doing X (you lack the power to alter my normative position)

These four are logically independent. Confusion between them generates the classic legal muddles: asserting a privilege when you only have a claim-right; asserting an immunity when you only have a privilege; treating a power as if it were an immunity.

Duties and claims are co-generated. There is no duty without a correlative claim, and no claim without a correlative duty. This has consequences for normative system design: you cannot create a duty without simultaneously creating a claim-holder; you cannot grant a claim without imposing a correlative duty.

Powers and liabilities are co-generated. There is no power without a correlative liability. When a legislature gains the power to tax, citizens gain the liability to be taxed. When an employer gains the power to hire, applicants gain the liability to be evaluated.

¶The second quartet and institutional authority

The second quartet is the formal apparatus of authority in the normative sense. Authority, in its core normative sense, is a collection of powers: the capacity to create, modify, and extinguish first-quartet positions in others. An Office is a second-quartet structure — a specification of what powers, liabilities, immunities, and disabilities the officeholder has.

The Principal holds original powers: the capacity to create agency relations, delegate scope, and constitute normative facts — powers not derived from any other party within the system. The Delegation from principal to agent is the exercise of a Hohfeldian power: the principal alters the agent’s normative position by granting new powers within the delegated scope.

Contract formation is also a second-quartet phenomenon: when P1P_1 makes an offer to P2P_2, P1P_1 exercises a power (the power to propose), and P2P_2 thereby gains a power (the power to accept) plus a liability (the normative position is alterable — P1P_1 can revoke the offer). Acceptance is P2P_2 exercising their power, which creates the first-quartet web of duties and claims that constitute the contract.

¶In the relational framework

The Hohfeldian analysis maps onto the framework’s predication and settlement structure.

First quartet at the predication level (object-level normative facts). The four first-quartet positions are joint settlement positions of two predicates — a recognition predicate (the normative system has acknowledged the obligation) and an execution predicate (the obligation carries forward into continuations). The four corners of joint settlement give the four normative readings:

First quartet position Recognition predicate Execution predicate Normative reading
Both claim-right AND duty settled Affirmingly settled Affirmingly settled Complete normative settlement: obligation fully recognized AND forward-committed
Claim-right recognized, duty not committed Settled affirmingly Not yet settled Meaning-settled obligation (the normative system recognizes the claim) but not yet execution-committed
Duty committed, claim not recognized Not yet settled Settled affirmingly Execution-committed (duty carries forward) but meaning-recognition pending
Normatively open Not yet settled Not yet settled Neither claim nor duty constituted; the normative position is indeterminate

The first quartet is therefore the four-corner joint settlement structure applied to normative propositions: the two axes are recognition (whether the system has acknowledged) and execution (whether the obligation propagates), and the four corners are the four extremal joint positions.

Second quartet at the structural level (operations on the normative system). The second quartet — powers, liabilities, immunities, disabilities — operates at one level higher: not on propositions within the framework’s settlement space, but on the structure of the normative system itself. Powers are operations that change which predicates are operative in the system; liabilities are positions whose predicate-settlement can be altered by such operations; immunities are positions that resist alteration; disabilities are the absence of such operational capacity.

Second quartet Structural role Framework reading
Power Operation on the normative structure An act that alters which predicates are operative in the system, or which witnessings count toward their settlement. Exercise of power constitutes a new normative fact, not merely an act within an existing one.
Liability Susceptibility to alteration The correlative of power: holding a normative position whose predicate-settlement can be altered by another party’s exercise of power.
Immunity Stability under alteration A position whose settlement cannot be moved by ordinary witnessings — only a change to the framework’s structural conditions (the office’s required predicates and laws, the normative system’s covering rules) could remove it. Constitutional rights are immunities in this sense: settled by structural commitments that no operational act of governance can dissolve.
Disability Absence of operational capacity No power available that would alter another party’s predicate-settlement. The minor’s inability to form binding contracts is a disability: the witnessings the minor’s act would normally produce do not, in this normative system, settle the contract-formation predicate.

The level distinction. The first quartet is object-level: positions of normative propositions within a fixed normative system. The second quartet is structural-level: operations on the normative system itself — which predicates are recognized as operative, which witnessings count, what makes a position revisable. Powers, immunities, etc. modify the structure; rights, duties, etc. live within it.

Co-generation from joint settlement. The correlativity thesis — that duty and claim are co-generated, that power and liability are co-generated — follows from the framework’s predication structure. A duty held by AA toward BB and a claim held by BB against AA are the same predication cell viewed from the two relata’s positions: the cell (A,powe,B)(A, p_{\mathrm{owe}}, B) recording that AA owes BB to φ\varphi. The same cell appears as a duty when read from AA’s perspective (the obligation runs from AA) and as a claim when read from BB’s perspective (the obligation runs to BB). Co-generation is structural: there is one predication cell, two relational positions on it.

¶Open questions

  • Whether the second Hohfeldian quartet has a precise categorical formulation in the framework — whether powers are morphisms in a category of normative systems (operations that alter the objects of the category) and immunities are objects stable under all such operations.
  • Whether the co-generation of duties and claims (the correlativity thesis) follows necessarily from the predication structure — whether, for any duty AA owes BB, the same predication cell always supports the correlative claim, and whether this is automatic in the framework or requires an additional structural axiom.
  • Whether there are “emergent” Hohfeldian positions — complex normative facts that are built from combinations of the eight primitives in ways that do not reduce to any single primitive; whether institutional personhood (the corporation’s Hohfeldian profile) is one such emergent complex.
  • Whether the distinction between first-quartet (act-level) and second-quartet (structural-level) positions corresponds to the distinction between predication cells (object-level facts) and structural operations on the normative system (which predicates are operative, what counts as a witnessing).
