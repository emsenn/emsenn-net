Custody

2026-03-19 A Custody is a triple (H, O, [t₀,t₁)) — a holder H who possesses an object O during a bounded interval [t₀,t₁) with a duty of care for O but without ownership of O. The defining structure: possession without title; the holder may use O only as necessary to fulfill the custodial purpose; accountability for O's condition runs from t₀ to t₁. Universal Quiescence reading: the duty of care is quiescence-preservation — the holder applies the joint retraction π_t = RelationalHistoryFiberSaturatingNucleus ∘ RelationalHistoryFiberTransferringNucleus to the held object at every step within W, maintaining O's membership in H*_t (the FixedFiberType quiescent layer). Possession without title is the formal structure of fiber-level quiescence preservation without topology-level authority: the holder can keep O in H*_t (preserve its fixed-fiber membership) but cannot alter the Grothendieck topology J that determines what H*_t is (that would be ownership). The return condition is the restoration of quiescence: O must be returned with σ_{t₁}(O) = O and Δ_{t₁}(O) = O — the same FixedFiberType as at t₀. Quiescence is not an external force imposed on the custodial relationship: it is what the custodial relationship mathematically IS. At every step t ∈ W, the holder applies π_t to maintain O; at t₁, the holder releases O back to the owner with π_{t₁}(O) = O.

¶Formal definition

A Custody is a triple C=(H,O,W)\mathcal{C} = (H, O, W):

C=(H:Holder,  O:Object,  W=[t0,t1):Interval)\mathcal{C} = (H : \mathrm{Holder},\; O : \mathrm{Object},\; W = [t_0, t_1) : \mathrm{Interval})

with conditions:

  • HH has possession of OO during WW: OO is physically or constructively within HH’s control
  • HH does not have ownership of OO: title to OO remains with the owner Ω≠H\Omega \neq H
  • HH has a duty of care for OO during WW: HH must preserve OO’s condition and return or deliver it at t1t_1
  • HH’s use of OO is purpose-bounded: HH may use OO only to the extent required by the custodial purpose

Five invariants. C\mathcal{C} is a custody iff it satisfies:

  1. Possession without title: HH controls OO during WW; title stays with Ω\Omega. This is the constitutive condition. If HH held title, the relationship would be ownership or sale. If HH had no control, there would be no custody.

  2. Duty of care: HH owes Ω\Omega (or the eventual recipient) a duty to maintain OO’s condition during WW. The standard of care varies: a bailee for hire is held to a higher standard than a gratuitous bailee; a professional custodian (carrier, warehouseman) is held to the standard of their trade. Formally: σt(Oreturned)=Oreceived\sigma_t(O_{\mathrm{returned}}) = O_{\mathrm{received}} — the object returned must be in the condition received (identity preservation).

  3. Purpose boundedness: HH’s use of OO during WW is limited to the custodial purpose. A carrier may stow cargo; they may not sell it. A trustee may invest trust property; they may not use it for personal benefit. A bailee may store an item; they may not consume it. Any use exceeding the custodial purpose is conversion.

  4. Temporal boundedness: custody is defined over W=[t0,t1)W = [t_0, t_1). At t0t_0, custody begins (typically by formal transfer of possession). At t1t_1, custody ends (by delivery, return, or legal discharge). During WW, accountability for OO’s condition is HH’s; outside WW, it is not.

  5. Return or delivery obligation: HH is obligated to return OO to Ω\Omega or deliver it to a designated third party at or before t1t_1. The obligation is not merely to preserve OO but to complete the custody by relinquishing it.

¶The bailment structure

In common law, custody is formally the bailment — the delivery of personal property by one person (bailor) to another (bailee) for a specific purpose, with a contract (express or implied) that the purpose will be fulfilled and the property returned or disposed of according to the bailor’s directions.

The bailment taxonomy:

Type For whose benefit Duty of care Example
Gratuitous bailment for bailor Bailor only Slight care Leaving item with friend
Gratuitous bailment for bailee Bailee only Great care Borrowing a tool
Mutual benefit bailment Both Ordinary/reasonable care Carrier, warehouseman

All three share the custody triple (H,O,W)(H, O, W) with the same five invariants. The duty of care standard varies; the structural relationship does not.

¶Custody vs. ownership vs. adjacent relations

The trust is the deepest form of custody: the trustee holds legal title and possession, but the beneficial interest belongs to others. The trustee’s custody is permanent (not merely transient) and involves a fiduciary duty more stringent than ordinary bailment.

¶In the cargo context

The cargo custody during transit is a mutual-benefit bailment:

  • HH = the carrier (vessel operator)
  • OO = the cargo
  • WW = the voyage (from loading to discharge)
  • Ω\Omega = the shipper/consignee

The carrier’s duty of care is the maritime law standard: “properly and carefully load, handle, stow, carry, keep, care for, and discharge the goods” (Hague-Visby Rules, Art. III Rule 2). Any shortfall is a breach of the custodial duty of care. The cargo manifest is the formal record of what entered custody (t0t_0) and what must exit (t1t_1).

Proposition (Ownership vs. custody = topology-level authority vs. fiber-level preservation). The distinction between the owner and the holder is a distinction of level within the universal quiescence structure:

  • The owner holds topology-level authority: the owner is the party who can alter the Grothendieck topology J that determines what RelationalHistoryFixedFiber is. This is universe-level power — the power to change which propositions count as quiescent. Ownership = the power to define the quiescent destination.
  • The holder has only fiber-level preservation authority: the holder can maintain O’s membership in RelationalHistoryFixedFiber (the quiescent layer as already defined by J) but cannot alter J itself. Possession without title = fiber-level quiescence-preservation without topology-level authority.

A transfer of title is a transfer of topology-level authority — the new owner can alter J. A custodial arrangement transfers only fiber-level maintenance capability. This is why conversion (using the object beyond the custodial purpose) is not a failure of quiescence-preservation but an exercise of topology-level authority without title: the holder alters what J-stable means for O, which is the owner’s exclusive power.

¶Open questions

  • Whether custody admits delegation — whether the holder HH can sub-bail OO to a third party H′H' without the bailor’s consent, and under what conditions this transfers the duty of care from HH to H′H' or creates joint and several liability.
  • Whether data (digital objects) admit a coherent custody structure — whether possessing a copy of a file is “possession” in the custodial sense, or whether custody of digital objects requires a different formal treatment.
  • The formal relationship between custody and the on-call officer’s reachability obligation — whether “being available” is a form of custody of one’s own attention and professional capacity for the benefit of the activating party.
