Normative System

2026-03-19 A Normative System is a structured collection of norms with authority, scope, and coherence conditions, within which norms operate and from which duties derive their force. It is the ambient order giving every obligation, permission, and prohibition its meaning and binding power.

What This Is

A Normative System is a structured collection of norms equipped with authority and scope conditions that determine which propositions are obligatory, permitted, or prohibited within a domain.

The key distinction: a norm is a triple (φ, O, N) where N is a normative system. But what IS N? A normative system is not a single norm — it is the ambient order within which multiple norms operate together. It is the organizing structure that gives those norms coherence, assigns them priorities, resolves their conflicts, and maintains their validity.

Hans Kelsen (1945, 1960) called this structure a normative order — a hierarchy of norms each deriving its validity from a higher norm until reaching the foundational Grundnorm. The system is not a loose collection: it is a closed structure where every obligation, permission, and prohibition receives its meaning from the system as a whole.

Formal Definition

A Normative System is a quintuple N=(Ag,Φ,⊢,J,β)N = (\mathrm{Ag}, \Phi, \vdash, J, \beta):

N=(Ag,  Φ,  ⊢⊆Φ×Φ,  J,  β)N = (\mathrm{Ag},\; \Phi,\; \vdash \subseteq \Phi \times \Phi,\; J,\; \beta)

where:

  • Ag\mathrm{Ag} is the agent set — those to whom the norms apply; the system’s jurisdiction over persons
  • Φ\Phi is the proposition set — the facts, acts, and states the system governs; Φ\Phi may include base propositions (atomic acts) and complex propositions (conditional norms, prioritized obligations)
  • ⊢⊆Φ×Φ\vdash \subseteq \Phi \times \Phi is the derivation relation — the entailment or implication ordering: if φ⊢ψ\varphi \vdash \psi, then ψ\psi is a normative consequence of φ\varphi within the system
  • JJ is the scope or jurisdiction — the spatial, temporal, or contextual boundaries within which the system is operative; what Φ\Phi applies to
  • β\beta is the authority grounding — the source (statute, constitution, custom, contract, divine law) from which the system’s normative force derives; without β\beta, the propositions in Φ\Phi are mere suggestions, not norms

Five invariants. NN is a normative system iff:

  1. Closure under entailment: if φ∈Φ\varphi \in \Phi and φ⊢ψ\varphi \vdash \psi, then ψ∈Φ\psi \in \Phi. The system is deductively closed — if the system includes a norm and acknowledges an entailment, it must include all normative consequences. This prevents implicit contradictions. (Alchourrón & Bulygin, 1971.)

  2. Consistency of the authority source: β\beta is determinate. The system may be silent on some propositions (permitting them by default) but cannot derive its authority from incompatible sources — no legal system can be simultaneously grounded in the statute and its explicit repeal. Authority is singular or hierarchical, never indeterminate.

  3. Coherence of derivation: the relation ⊢\vdash is transitive and reflexive — if φ⊢ψ\varphi \vdash \psi and ψ⊢χ\psi \vdash \chi, then φ⊢χ\varphi \vdash \chi. The entailment ordering constitutes a partial order on propositions, giving the system an internal logical structure.

  4. Jurisdiction boundedness: JJ is well-defined — the system applies to specific agents, acts, and contexts, not universally. A tax code applies to residents and transactions within a jurisdiction; family law applies to familial relations within a defined scope. Without JJ, the system is either trivial (applies to everything) or incoherent (applies to nothing definite).

  5. Authority persistence: β\beta does not change within the system’s operative timeline. A system with changing authority sources has fractured into multiple systems or lost its identity. This is what the rule of recognition (Hart, 1961) enforces — officials recognize which authority source is operative, and this recognition is constant enough for the system to be a system.

The Alchourrón & Bulygin Framework

Alchourrón and Bulygin (1971) define a normative system as the deductively closed set generated by a set of explicitly stated norms plus classical logic. Begin with a finite set of axioms — call it Σ\Sigma (the explicitly stated rules, statutes, principles). The normative system generated by Σ\Sigma is:

N(Σ)={ φ∈Φ:Σ⊢φ }N(\Sigma) = \{\, \varphi \in \Phi : \Sigma \vdash \varphi \,\}

This is the closure of Σ\Sigma under entailment. Every norm in N(Σ)N(\Sigma) is either explicitly stated in Σ\Sigma or derivable from it. The system is its own logical closure.

Key insight: the master system N(Σ)N(\Sigma) is typically infinite (infinite logical consequences from finitely many axioms), but the base Σ\Sigma is finite and enumerable. A legal code has finitely many statutes, but those statutes entail infinitely many consequences. The system is what matters — it includes everything that reasonably follows.

Two critical distinctions:

  1. Gap vs. contradiction: A proposition φ\varphi is a gap in N(Σ)N(\Sigma) if neither φ\varphi nor ¬φ\neg\varphi is in N(Σ)N(\Sigma) — the system is silent. A gap is permissibility by default. A contradiction arises if both φ\varphi and ¬φ\neg\varphi are in N(Σ)N(\Sigma) — the system is incoherent. A consistent normative system has no contradictions; it may have many gaps.

  2. Explicit vs. implicit: An explicit norm is a member of Σ\Sigma — directly stated. An implicit norm is a member of N(Σ)∖ΣN(\Sigma) \setminus \Sigma — a logical consequence not explicitly written. Legal reasoning often turns on implicit norms: does the logic of the existing statutes entail this consequence? The system generates the answer.

The Kelsen Structure: Validity Hierarchy

Hans Kelsen (1960) adds a hierarchical dimension. A normative system is not merely a set of closed propositions — it is a hierarchy of norms where each norm’s validity is derived from a higher norm:

φn is valid  ⟺  φn was created by the authority specified in φn−1\varphi_n \text{ is valid} \iff \varphi_n \text{ was created by the authority specified in } \varphi_{n-1}

The statute is valid because it was enacted pursuant to the constitution. The constitution is valid because… the Grundnorm (“the historically first constitution ought to be obeyed”) is the presupposed foundation.

The Grundnorm is not itself a positive norm — it is the transcendental presupposition that stops the regress. Without it, the question “what makes this norm valid?” regresses infinitely. The Grundnorm is not proved or enacted; it is presupposed as the condition for any norm’s validity to be intelligible.

Validity vs. truth: This is Kelsen’s revolutionary insight. A norm is valid (in force) or invalid (not in force). It is not true or false — those predicates apply to facts, not prescriptions. An unjust law may be perfectly valid (enacted through proper procedure and part of the chain of validity). A wise principle not enacted is not a valid norm in the legal sense. The system cares about validity (is-it-binding?) not truth (is-it-correct?).

Derogation: In Kelsen’s system, norms conflict when both are valid but have inconsistent prescriptions. Derogation is the explicit mechanism: a later norm can derogate (repeal or override) an earlier norm, removing it from the valid set. Without explicit derogation, both norms remain valid and the system faces incoherence.

Hart: Secondary Rules and the Rule of Recognition

H.L.A. Hart (1961) observes that Kelsen’s hierarchy cannot be fully explained in terms of norms alone — there must be a rule of recognition: a secondary rule that identifies which primary rules are in force.

A legal system requires three layers of secondary rules:

  1. Rule of recognition: the criterion by which officials identify which primary rules are valid. “A rule is law iff it appears in the statute books and has not been repealed.” The rule of recognition is not itself a law (not a positive norm) — it is an accepted practice among officials. It is a social fact — a shared acceptance that this is the test of validity.

  2. Rules of change: rules that authorize some authority to create, modify, or repeal primary rules. Congress is authorized to pass statutes; the states can amend the constitution through a specified procedure.

  3. Rules of adjudication: rules that authorize courts to determine when a primary rule has been violated. These create the power to make binding determinations.

The union of primary and secondary rules is the legal system. The unity of the system comes not from the Grundnorm (a presupposed fiction) but from the rule of recognition — a practice, not a norm, that officials accept and apply consistently.

The internal point of view: Hart’s distinction between the external point of view (observing that people follow rules) and the internal point of view (accepting rules as binding, using them to guide conduct). A legal system exists when officials adopt the internal point of view toward the rule of recognition — they treat it as binding, not merely describe it as a regularity.

Ostrom: Polycentric Normative Systems

Elinor Ostrom (1990) extends the framework to nested and polycentric systems. A normative system is not a single unified structure but a confederation of rule-systems operating at multiple levels:

N=Noperational∪Ncollective−choice∪Nconstitutional−choiceN = N_{\mathrm{operational}} \cup N_{\mathrm{collective-choice}} \cup N_{\mathrm{constitutional-choice}}

where:

  • NoperationalN_{\mathrm{operational}} governs day-to-day action — “do not cut more than your allocated timber”
  • Ncollective−choiceN_{\mathrm{collective-choice}} governs changes to operational rules — “the forest management committee decides on harvest quotas”
  • Nconstitutional−choiceN_{\mathrm{constitutional-choice}} governs changes to the collective-choice rules — “the constitution must be ratified by representatives of all communities”

Polycentricity: multiple normative systems (multiple governance structures) can operate over the same domain without a single central authority. Villages manage their commons under their own operational rules; the state’s rules provide a constitutional frame within which village-level systems operate. Neither is subordinate to a pure hierarchy — they are nested and partially overlapping.

Boella & van der Torre: Input/Output Logic

Gabriele Boella and Leendert van der Torre (2014) formalize normative systems using input/output logic — norms as input/output pairs rather than as propositions. A norm is (A,B)(A, B): “if AA (input condition), then obligate BB (output).”

The system is a set of such pairs:

N={ (Ai,Bi):i∈I }N = \{\, (A_i, B_i) : i \in I \,\}

An agent in situation AiA_i is obligated to perform BiB_i. If multiple norms apply (multiple input conditions are satisfied), their outputs combine according to a priority structure or aggregation rule — if two norms have conflicting outputs, the system specifies which takes precedence.

Advantage: input/output logic separates the mechanics of norm-applicability (which norms fire given the current conditions?) from norm-content (what do they prescribe?). This makes it easier to handle conditional norms, cascading obligations (one obligation triggers another), and norm-generation (norms that create new norms).

Nuclear Reading: Double Fixed-Point Operativity

A normative system NN is operative at history tt iff every norm N=(φ,O,N)\mathcal{N} = (\varphi, O, N) in NN is doubly-settled in the fiber HtH_t:

N operative at t    ⟺    ∀N∈N:φ∈Ht∗=Fix(σt)∩Fix(Δt)N \text{ operative at } t \;\iff\; \forall \mathcal{N} \in N : \varphi \in H^*_t = \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t)

where σt\sigma_t is the saturation nucleus (accumulated backward-facing recognition) and Δt\Delta_t is the transfer nucleus (forward-facing institutional commitment).

The normative system is the topology JJ on the history site (T,J)(T, J). The covering sieves S∈J(t)S \in J(t) are the normative coverings — the conditions under which a set of actions is collectively recognized as satisfying the norms. A covering sieve is a Grothendieck covering: the operations it contains, taken together, witness the normative closure of the scope.

Consistency of the normative system is now definable: NN is consistent iff the bottom element ⊥t\bot_t (the contradiction) is not a fixed point of both nuclei — ⊥t∉Ht∗\bot_t \notin H^*_t. A system that obligates everything (the absurd norm) is degenerate.

Open Questions

  • The Grundnorm in relational universe terms: Does the Charter constitute the Grundnorm, or is the Grundnorm presupposed before the charter is enacted? If constituted, what agency installs it? If presupposed, how does it avoid Kelsen’s regress problem in the tower structure?

  • Reconciling Kelsen and Hart: Kelsen’s presupposed Grundnorm vs. Hart’s rule of recognition (a social practice) seem incompatible. In the relational universe framework, are they two readings of the same structure — the Grundnorm as the transcendental presupposition, the rule of recognition as its immanent social manifestation?

  • Hodgson’s reconstitutive downward causation: Does a normative system reshape agents’ dispositions over time? If so, the operative condition must be extended: agent-indexed nuclei σp(t),Δp(t)\sigma_p(t), \Delta_p(t) for each agent pp under the system’s jurisdiction, with convergence σp(t)→σt\sigma_p(t) \to \sigma_t as pp’s tenure accumulates. Whether such agent-indexed systems are coherent with the sheaf axioms is unresolved.

  • Operativity vs. ideality: Every formal normative system (law, regulation, contract) distinguishes what is operatively settled (meaning-stable and forward-committed in practice) from what is ideally declared (in the books but not yet effective). The nuclear quartet localizes this distinction: elements in Ht∗H^*_t are operative; elements in SatShadowt\mathrm{SatShadow}_t or TrShadowt\mathrm{TrShadow}_t are ideal but not operatively settled. How do institutions manage the gap between declared norms and operative ones?

  • Multi-system equilibria: When multiple normative systems govern overlapping agents and acts (legal system + professional ethics + organizational policies), how do their nuclei interact? Is there a coherence condition for the union, or do agents bear the burden of navigating incompatible systems?
