Discharge

2026-03-19 A Discharge is a triple (d, a, t) — a duty d, a performance act a by the duty-bearer, and a history t at which the act has reached settled determination as both performed and recognized. Discharge is not merely performance — it is recognized performance; both performance and recognition predicates must reach affirming settlement for the discharge to count.

¶Formal definition

A Discharge is a triple D=(d,a,t)\mathcal{D} = (d, a, t):

D=(d:Duty,  a:Act,  t∈T)\mathcal{D} = (d : \mathrm{Duty},\; a : \mathrm{Act},\; t \in T)

satisfying the discharge condition: the act aa has reached settled determination on both the performance and recognition predicates of duty dd at history tt. In the framework’s bilattice-valued vocabulary, this is the joint condition that the predication cells (a,pperform,d)(a, p_{\mathrm{perform}}, d) and (a,precognize,d)(a, p_{\mathrm{recognize}}, d) have both reached the truth-only corner t\mathbf{t} of the settlement structure at history tt.

Components:

  • dd is the duty being discharged — a Duty (abearer,p,N,φ,r)(a_{\mathrm{bearer}}, p, N, \varphi, r) specifying what must be done
  • aa is the performance act — the act performed by dd’s bearer in satisfaction of φ\varphi
  • t∈Tt \in T is the settling history — the point at which discharge is evaluated; both predicates must have reached settled determination at tt

Three invariants. D\mathcal{D} is a discharge iff it satisfies:

  1. Performance settlement. The predication cell (a,pperform,d)(a, p_{\mathrm{perform}}, d) has reached affirming settlement — the act has been performed according to fixed procedure, the execution path is complete, the required steps have been taken. Without this, the duty is not yet performed.

  2. Recognition settlement. The predication cell (a,precognize,d)(a, p_{\mathrm{recognize}}, d) has reached affirming settlement — the performance is recognized by the governing normative system NN and by the correlative party rr as constituting satisfaction of φ\varphi. Without this, the act may have been performed but is not yet acknowledged as discharge. A payment the creditor does not recognize as payment is not discharge; a filing the court does not record is not discharge.

  3. Joint settlement. Both predicates must reach affirming settlement simultaneously. This is the discharge condition. A duty can be:

    • Executed but unrecognized (performance settled, recognition not yet) — procedurally complete, semantically open
    • Acknowledged but unexecuted — this case does not arise cleanly; recognition without execution is not discharge
    • Fully discharged — both predicates reach affirming settlement; the duty is normatively extinguished

¶The performance/recognition gap

The gap between performance settlement and recognition settlement is the most practically significant feature of discharge. Consider:

Scenario Performance settled? Recognition settled? Discharged?
Act performed and recognized Yes Yes Yes
Act performed, recognition pending Yes No No
Act performed, recognition refused Yes No No — dispute arises
Act not performed No — No

The gap creates the space for duty-discharge disputes: the bearer claims joint settlement; the correlative party claims recognition has not settled. The resolution requires the governing normative system NN to adjudicate — making the recognition predicate’s settlement clear.

¶Discharge vs. satisfaction vs. performance

These three terms are commonly conflated but are formally distinct:

  • Performance: the bearer does act aa — the execution, regardless of recognition.
  • Satisfaction: the correlative party rr accepts aa as meeting their claim — the recognition, regardless of whether aa was formally performed.
  • Discharge: both performance and recognition predicates have reached settled determination — the duty is normatively extinguished.

Discharge is the strongest condition: it requires both performance and satisfaction to be simultaneously settled. A payment satisfies the creditor (recognition) and discharges the debt (joint settlement) simultaneously. A substitute performance satisfies only if the normative system NN accepts substitution.

¶Discharge by operation of law

In some cases, discharge occurs without performance: statute of limitations, frustration of contract, impossibility, merger. These are recognition-settlements applied directly to the duty itself rather than to a performance act — the duty is moved to its settled state by the system’s recognition that it no longer applies, not by the bearer’s act.

Formally: the predication cell (∅,precognize,d)(\varnothing, p_{\mathrm{recognize}}, d) reaches affirming settlement without a corresponding performance act — discharge is achieved through recognition alone. This is the exceptional case: discharge is normally a triple with a non-trivial performance; discharge by operation of law is the degenerate case where the recognition predicate’s settlement is sufficient.

¶Relation to the inquiry trajectory

The discharge condition places the duty in the determined mode of the inquiry trajectory: both performance and recognition predicates have been witnessed enough that the duty’s settlement is at the affirming truth-axis pole and the determined knowledge-axis pole. An unperformed duty corresponds to the absurd mode (no witnessing); a procedurally specified but unexecuted duty corresponds to the possible mode (witnessing has begun on recognition but not on performance); a performed but unrecognized act corresponds to the necessary mode (performance witnessed but recognition still pending).

¶Open questions

  • Whether partial discharge is coherent — whether a divisible duty can be partially discharged by partial performance, and what the formal condition for partial discharge is in terms of partial settlement of the performance and recognition predicates.
  • Whether the recognition-settlement condition is sufficient when NN is contested — whether discharge requires recognition by a specific party (the correlative rr) or by the normative system NN more generally.
  • The formal relationship between discharge and the Clerk’s authentication function — whether the clerk’s entry of a judgment is the recognition act that settles the recognition predicate.
