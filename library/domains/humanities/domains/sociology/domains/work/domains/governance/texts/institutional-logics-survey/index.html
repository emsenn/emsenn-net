Institutional logics in organizations

2026-04-03 How multiple, often conflicting normative systems coexist and compete within organizations and fields.

¶Introduction

The governance domain treats normative systems and institutions as relatively stable structures: a set of norms that constrain and enable behavior within a bounded domain. But organizations and fields rarely operate under a single normative regime. A hospital must simultaneously satisfy patient care logics, medical research logics, financial/efficiency logics, and state regulatory logics. A public university navigates academic prestige logics, market competition logics, public service logics, and state funding logics. A professional services firm operates within market logics, professional logics, and corporate logics. What happens when these systems make conflicting demands?

Institutional logics theory provides a framework for understanding how multiple, often competing normative systems coexist within organizations and fields, how they shape attention and action, and what strategies organizations deploy when their demands conflict.

¶The foundational framework: Friedland and Alford

Roger Friedland and Robert Alford’s foundational 1991 essay “Bringing Society Back In” established the core insight: modern society is not unified by a single organizing principle, but rather constituted by multiple institutional orders, each with its own logic.1

An institutional logic is a set of material practices and symbolic constructions—shared assumptions, values, beliefs, and rules—that constitute the organizing principles of a particular institutional sphere. Capitalism has its logic: accumulation, profit, efficient allocation of resources. The state has its logic: regulation, rule of law, public order. Democracy has its logic: representation, participation, accountability. The nuclear family has its logic: intimacy, generational reproduction, emotional sustenance. Religion has its logic: transcendence, moral authority, community of faith.

Friedland and Alford’s crucial move was to recognize these as coexistent and fundamentally incommensurate. They are not subsystems of a larger whole, nor expressions of a master logic. They are autonomous spheres with their own organizing principles. An actor embedded in modern society must navigate between them. This is the condition of institutional multiplicity.

The framework shifted institutional theory from explaining how a single institutional regime shapes organizational behavior to asking: what happens when an organization or field is subject to multiple, often contradictory, institutional logics?

¶Institutional logics mature: Thornton, Ocasio, and Lounsbury

Patricia Thornton, William Ocasio, and Michael Lounsbury’s 2012 systematic treatment The Institutional Logics Perspective elaborated the framework’s implications for organizational analysis.2

They clarified that institutional logics operate at multiple levels: they are macro-level structures (sets of organizing principles governing entire sectors), meso-level phenomena (shaping organizational fields and industries), and micro-level forces (shaping individual cognition, attention, and decision-making).

A central contribution is their articulation of how logics shape what gets attended to. Different logics direct attention to different problems, solutions, and measures of success. A firm dominated by market logic attends to profit margins, competitive position, and shareholder value. The same firm, if dominated by professional logic, attends to craft excellence, reputation, and peer recognition. These are not just different emphases—they create different fields of perception. Market logic makes certain facts salient (share price, market share, cost ratios); professional logic makes others salient (quality metrics, professional standards, peer review outcomes).

Thornton, Ocasio, and Lounsbury also mapped the institutional orders more systematically. They identified ideal-type logics associated with distinct sectors: the capitalist market (profit, exchange, competition), the corporation (hierarchy, efficiency, control), the state (regulation, authority, public order), professions (expertise, autonomy, ethics), the family (loyalty, reproduction, care), and religious/community orders (meaning, transcendence, belonging).

¶The mechanisms of institutional conformity: DiMaggio and Powell

While Friedland and Alford explained institutional logics, Paul DiMaggio and Walter Powell asked a different question: if multiple institutional logics exist, why do organizations in the same field become increasingly similar?3

Their 1983 essay “The Iron Cage Revisited” identified three mechanisms of institutional isomorphism—the tendency for organizations to grow more similar:

Coercive isomorphism occurs when an organization faces external pressure—legal mandates, regulatory requirements, or normative expectations from resource dependencies—to adopt a particular form. A hospital must adopt electronic health records if government funding depends on it. A company must implement diversity initiatives if contracts require it.

Mimetic isomorphism occurs when organizations respond to uncertainty by copying successful peers. When the source of success is unclear, organizations imitate what appears to be working. In turbulent markets, firms copy competitors’ organizational designs, management techniques, or business models.

Normative isomorphism occurs through professionalization: shared education, professional networks, and shared certification standards create convergence. Engineers, accountants, and managers trained in similar institutions bring similar practices to their organizations.

DiMaggio and Powell’s framework explains how, at the field level, competing organizations under the same logics become institutionally similar—but it also raises an implication: coercive, mimetic, and normative pressure can make different organizations converge on different logics. A hospital’s patient care identity, a pharmaceutical firm’s research identity, and a medical device manufacturer’s commercial identity all operate under healthcare logics, but they may be shaped by different dominant institutional pressures.

¶Institutional complexity: Greenwood, Raynard, Kodeih, Micelotta, and Lounsbury

The question becomes acute when a single organization faces multiple institutional logics simultaneously. Royston Greenwood and colleagues’ 2011 synthesis “Institutional Complexity and Organizational Responses” directly addresses this condition.4

Institutional complexity exists whenever an organization confronts incompatible prescriptions from multiple institutional logics. The expectations of one logic contradict the expectations of another. The authors map field-level and organizational conditions that intensify complexity:

  • Plurality of logics: The more distinct institutional logics present in a field, the greater the potential complexity.
  • Incompatibility: Some logics are more fundamentally opposed (market logic vs. civic/public service logic) than others.
  • Power distribution: If one logic clearly dominates, complexity may be low even if multiple logics exist. When power is distributed, complexity is high.
  • Authority: When there is no clear hierarchy of authority that can arbitrate between logics, organizations must manage the conflict themselves.

The authors identify a repertoire of organizational coping strategies:

Decoupling: Operating two systems simultaneously—one for external compliance with one logic, another for internal operations serving a different logic. A nonprofit may maintain two accounting systems: one for grant reporting (accountability logic), one for operational budgeting (efficiency logic). A university may maintain accreditation documentation serving regulatory logic while teaching is actually organized around pedagogical logic.

Selective coupling: Pursuing some elements of each logic while downplaying others. A hospital might embrace patient-centered care (family/care logic) while also implementing cost-control measures (market logic), selectively coupling rather than choosing one.

Structural differentiation: Creating separate organizational units to serve different logics. A firm may have a research division organized around scientific/professional logic and a commercialization division organized around market logic. A university separates research operations from teaching operations.

Temporal separation: Serving different logics at different times. A pharmaceutical company may operate as a researcher during drug development (professional logic) and a marketer during commercialization (market logic).

These strategies reveal that institutional complexity is not necessarily resolved—it is managed. Organizations rarely escape the tension. Instead, they develop structures and practices that allow them to acknowledge multiple logics while limiting the damage they inflict on each other.

¶Competing justifications: Boltanski and Thévenot

Where institutional logics theory treats normative systems as structural properties of fields and organizations, Luc Boltanski and Laurent Thévenot approached the problem from the perspective of justification in everyday disputes.5

Their framework of orders of worth identifies six distinct regimes of evaluation that people invoke when justifying actions or criticizing others:

  • Civic order: Justification by appeal to collective welfare, democracy, and the common good (Rousseau).
  • Market order: Justification by appeal to efficiency, exchange, and profit (Adam Smith).
  • Industrial order: Justification by appeal to productivity, scale, and technical performance (Saint-Simon).
  • Domestic order: Justification by appeal to tradition, hierarchy, and personal loyalty (Bossuet).
  • Inspired order: Justification by appeal to passion, creativity, and authenticity (Augustine).
  • Order of fame: Justification by appeal to reputation, recognition, and visibility (Hobbes).

Boltanski and Thévenot’s key insight is that conflict arises precisely when agents invoke different orders of worth. A manager justifies laying off workers by appeal to market and industrial logic: the firm must remain competitive and efficient. Workers justify resistance by appeal to civic and domestic logic: collective welfare and reciprocal obligations are being violated. They are not disagreeing about facts—they are invoking incommensurable frameworks of worth.

When legitimate tests (ways to determine what is truly of worth) are unavailable within a single order, conflict can only be resolved through compromise: a fragile hybrid principle that acknowledges value in both orders while satisfying neither completely. An employer might guarantee severance and retraining rather than immediate termination—a compromise between market efficiency and domestic/civic welfare.

Boltanski and Thévenot’s framework parallels institutional logics theory but focuses on justification practices rather than structural organizing principles. It suggests that institutional complexity is not merely a structural problem for organizations to solve, but a persistent feature of modern life—one that becomes visible and contentious precisely when different orders of worth collide.

¶Synthesis: Multiple logics as the condition of governance

Institutional logics theory transforms how we understand governance. The governance domain previously modeled normative systems and institutions as relatively stable, internally coherent structures. Institutional logics theory shows that the real object of governance is not a single system but the coexistence and management of multiple systems.

This has three major implications:

First, multiplicity is structural: Organizations and fields do not typically operate under a single normative regime. They are inherently plural. Studying governance requires understanding not just what norms apply, but which logics are competing, how they relate to each other (compatible, contradictory, nested), and under what conditions one dominates or all persist.

Second, complexity is not always resolvable: Greenwood and colleagues show that organizations can manage institutional complexity through decoupling, selective coupling, and differentiation, but not eliminate it. The tension between market logic and professional logic in healthcare, between accumulation logic and care logic in education, between efficiency logic and democratic logic in government—these are not problems to be solved but conditions to be governed. Effective governance acknowledges this and creates structures that allow multiple logics to coexist while limiting their mutual damage.

Third, justification is a key governance tool: Where logics conflict, people invoke different grounds of worth. Boltanski and Thévenot’s framework suggests that governance involves creating legitimate spaces for these competing justifications to be heard, and developing compromise principles that acknowledge worth in multiple orders. An institution that suppresses one logic entirely in favor of another may be efficient, but it creates the conditions for legitimacy crises. An institution that genuinely acknowledges multiple orders of worth—even when they conflict—has a stronger foundation for acceptance and voluntary compliance.

¶Open questions

Several questions remain for future research and institutional development:

  • Historical specificity: How do institutional logics change over time? Friedland and Alford identified the major logics of their era. Do new logics emerge? Do old ones decline? How do technological change, social movements, and value shifts reshape the institutional landscape?

  • Power and domination: Greenwood et al. note that power distribution shapes complexity, but the framework underspecifies how power within an organization determines which logic dominates. Is the outcome determined solely by structural position, or do ideas, persuasion, and framing also matter?

  • Microfoundations: Thornton and colleagues gesture toward how logics shape individual cognition and decision-making, but the connection between macro institutional structures and individual attention/action remains underdeveloped. How exactly does a person internalize or resist an institutional logic?

  • Governance across fields: Most research examines complexity within a single organization or field. What happens at the boundaries between fields? How do logics translate across institutional domains? A person who works in healthcare (professional/care logics) and attends a market-logic firm’s business meeting is navigating across fields. How do people and organizations manage these transitions?

  • Alignment and drift: When do multiple logics reinforce each other, and when do they undermine each other? Under what conditions does institutional drift—gradual, unintended departure from established logics—occur?
