Status Function Declaration

2026-03-20 A StatusFunctionDeclaration is a four-tuple (X, Y, C, A) — a target X, a status Y, a context C in which the status applies, and an authorizing act A by which the status is constituted — that creates an institutional fact by the act of its utterance or performance. The defining invariant: the declaration does not describe a pre-existing fact; it constitutes one. X acquires the status Y in context C precisely because the declaration A is performed by a party with the standing to make it operative. Without A, X may have all the properties it will have after A, yet still lack status Y.

¶What this is

A StatusFunctionDeclaration is the mechanism by which institutional facts are created. It is a speech act (or equivalent collective act) of the form:

“X counts as Y in context C”\text{``}X \text{ counts as } Y \text{ in context } C\text{''}

where XX is a brute-fact object or event, YY is the institutional status assigned, and CC is the context within which the assignment holds. The act of making this declaration (when performed by a party with standing, under appropriate conditions) constitutes the institutional fact — it does not merely report one.

¶Formal definition

A StatusFunctionDeclaration is a four-tuple D=(X,Y,C,A)\mathcal{D} = (X, Y, C, A):

D=(X:Target,  Y:Status,  C:Context,  A:AuthorizingAct)\mathcal{D} = (X : \mathrm{Target},\; Y : \mathrm{Status},\; C : \mathrm{Context},\; A : \mathrm{AuthorizingAct})

where:

  • XX is the target — the entity, act, or state of affairs that acquires the new status; XX may preexist the declaration (a piece of metal that becomes a coin) or be constituted by the declaration itself (the corporation that comes into existence upon incorporation)
  • YY is the institutional status — the normative, deontic, or social position assigned to XX by the declaration; YY specifies what XX counts as within CC: what rights, duties, powers, privileges, or immunities XX now holds or confers; YY is always a position in a HohfeldianPosition web or an institutional-fact category
  • CC is the context — the institutional frame within which the declaration is operative; the same XX may count as different YY’s in different contexts (C1C_1: a gold disc counts as a coin in the currency system; C2C_2: the same gold disc counts as evidence in a trial; C3C_3: the same gold disc counts as scrap metal outside both systems); context CC is specified by an Institution or NormativeSystem
  • AA is the authorizing act — the speech act, ceremony, document, or procedure by which the declaration is performed; AA must be performed by a party with Authority to make declarations of type YY within CC; without this standing, AA misfires — the target XX does not acquire status YY

Four invariants. D\mathcal{D} is an operative StatusFunctionDeclaration iff:

  1. Constitutive, not descriptive: AA does not report that XX already has status YY; it creates the fact that XX has status YY. Performing AA when the standing conditions hold brings the institutional fact into existence. This is Austin’s performative-constative distinction: a performative utterance is neither true nor false before its performance (there is no fact yet to be true or false about); after a successful performance, the institutional fact obtains.

  2. Standing required: the party performing AA must have the Authority to constitute status-YY-in-context-CC. A random person saying “I appoint you judge” does not appoint a judge — the appointment misfires because the declaring party lacks standing. The President (in the right context, following the right procedure) saying “I appoint you” does appoint — because the President holds the Hohfeldian power to constitute judicial status in the relevant context.

  3. Collective recognition sustains: once constituted, the institutional fact persists only while the relevant community continues to recognize CC as operative. If the institutional context dissolves (the game ends, the company is dissolved, the state ceases to be recognized), status-YY dissolves with it. The declaration constitutes the fact; collective recognition sustains it. This is why Institution requires a recognition predicate Rec\mathrm{Rec} as a separate component: the constitutive act and the sustaining recognition are distinct.

  4. Context-relative: the status YY is never absolute — it is always YY-in-CC. The corporate officer is an officer within the company’s governance system; they have no authority outside the company’s scope. The appointed judge has authority within the jurisdiction; beyond the jurisdiction, the title may be honorific but carries no Hohfeldian power. Status function declarations create locally operative facts, not globally absolute ones.

¶The Searlean analysis

John Searle (The Construction of Social Reality, 1995; Making the Social World, 2010) builds his theory of institutional reality on status function declarations.

The fundamental structure: collective intentionality + constitutive rules + status functions. Institutional reality is created and sustained by:

  1. Collective intentionality: the relevant community collectively accepts that XX counts as YY in CC; this is not reducible to individual intentionality plus mutual belief — it is a genuinely collective mental state
  2. Constitutive rules: rules of the form “XX counts as YY in CC” that are not merely regulative (governing pre-existing behavior) but constitutive (creating the behavior they govern); “touchdowns score six points” does not govern a pre-existing act — it constitutes what a touchdown is
  3. Status functions: the functions assigned by constitutive rules that allow entities to perform roles they could not perform by their physical properties alone; money can function as a medium of exchange, a judge can function as an authority to convict, a president can function as a head of state — in each case, the function is not physical but institutional

The deontic powers generated by status functions: when XX acquires status YY, XX typically acquires deontic powers — the capacity to alter normative relations for others. A dollar bill acquires the power to discharge debts. A certified doctor acquires the power to prescribe. A president acquires the power to sign laws. The deontic power is not in the physical object; it is created by the status function declaration and sustained by collective recognition.

The ontological asymmetry: the declaration creates a fact that is observer-dependent (it depends on collective recognition) but not subjectively arbitrary (once constituted, it is objectively real within its context; it binds even those who do not believe in it). A five-dollar bill is “really” worth five dollars within the US dollar system — this is not merely a belief, it is an institutional fact that constrains behavior, resolves disputes, and generates legal obligations. The observer-dependence is collective, not individual: no one agent’s refusal to accept the dollar destroys its status; the collective would have to cease accepting it.

¶Domain instances

¶Legal declarations

Incorporation: “This entity is hereby incorporated as [Corporation Name] Ltd.” — XX = the set of assets, persons, and documents; YY = juridical person with full legal personality; CC = the jurisdiction’s corporate law; AA = the registrar’s act of registration. Before incorporation: a set of things. After: a legal person that can sue, be sued, own property, enter contracts.

Conviction: “I find the defendant guilty.” — XX = the defendant; YY = convicted person (with the attendant changes to normative status: loss of liberty, criminal record, altered rights); CC = the criminal justice system; AA = the judge’s pronouncement after the trial’s procedural requirements are met. The pronouncement does not discover guilt; it constitutes the legal fact of guilt within the criminal justice system.

Marriage: “I now pronounce you [married].” — XX = the two persons; YY = married persons (a new juridical and social status with rights, duties, and deontic relations between the spouses and toward third parties); CC = the legal/religious institution of marriage; AA = the officiating authority’s pronouncement following the ceremony’s requirements. Before: two individuals. After: a married couple with a new legal status.

Appointment: “I appoint you Chief Justice.” — XX = the person; YY = the office of Chief Justice (the Hohfeldian profile of the office); CC = the court system; AA = the appointing authority’s formal act. The person may have all the qualifications before appointment; the status is not constituted until the authorizing act occurs.

¶Specification declarations

Every definition file entry of the form defines: Foo is a status function declaration within a knowledge system:

D=(X=the document and its content,  Y=Foo as a named entity in the relational system,  C=the vocabulary of the knowledge system,  A=writing and publishing the definition)\mathcal{D} = (X = \text{the document and its content},\; Y = \text{Foo as a named entity in the relational system},\; C = \text{the vocabulary of the knowledge system},\; A = \text{writing and publishing the definition})

The definition does not discover a pre-existing entity called Foo; it constitutes Foo as a named entity within the system’s vocabulary. Before the definition exists, other entities can reference Foo as vocabulary pressure; after it exists, Foo is a settled entity in the system.

Similarly, every id: field in a definition file is a declaration: “this concept counts as [id] in the context of this knowledge system.” The defines: field explicitly marks the status assigned. The date-created: field is the timestamp of the authorizing act’s performance.

¶Mathematical declarations

Quotient maps: “[x][x] counts as x(modn)x \pmod{n}” — the quotient map π:Z→Z/nZ\pi : \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} declares that each equivalence class [x][x] counts as a single element in the quotient. The declaration creates a new mathematical object (the residue) by fiat; the quotient structure is constituted by the declaration, not discovered.

Type definitions: in programming languages, type Foo = { x: Int, y: String } is a status function declaration: a record type named Foo is hereby constituted with the given field structure. The declaration brings Foo into existence in the type system; before the declaration, no entity named Foo exists in scope.

Axiomatic foundations: “We take the following as axioms.” — the axioms of a theory are status function declarations: “these propositions count as foundational in this context.” The axioms are not proved; they are declared. Their status as axioms is constituted by the declaration, not by their proof-theoretic properties. This is why changing the axioms (declaring different foundational propositions) constitutes a different mathematical theory, not a correction of the original.

The Grundnorm (Kelsen, Pure Theory of Law, 1934): every legal system presupposes a basic norm (Grundnorm) — the norm from which all other norms derive their validity. The Grundnorm cannot be derived from any higher norm within the system; it is presupposed as the condition of the system’s validity. The Grundnorm is the ultimate status function declaration: “the constitution counts as valid law.” Every legal status function declaration within the system derives its force from this foundational declaration.

¶Nuclear reading

Definitions. Before the declaration at history tt: X∈HtX \in H_t with σt(X)>X\sigma_t(X) > X — the current nucleus has not yet settled XX as having status YY; XX is present in the fiber but unsettled. The target status YY is a position in Ht′∗=Fix(σt′)∩Fix(Δt′)H^*_{t'} = \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t'}) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t'}) at the successor history t′t' — doubly stable recognition.

Proposition 1 (Immutability of σ_t — declarations generate new histories): The authorizing act AA at tt does not modify σt\sigma_t. It generates a new history t′=sD⋆tt' = s_\mathcal{D} \star t (the declaration-step sDs_\mathcal{D} appended to tt). The nucleus σt\sigma_t at the original history is unchanged; the new history t′t' carries its own nucleus σt′\sigma_{t'}, which may settle XX where σt\sigma_t did not.

Proof. σt\sigma_t is determined by restriction maps H(it0,t):Ht0→HtH(i_{t_0, t}) : H_{t_0} \to H_t of the presheaf over TT. The authorizing act is an element A∈HtA \in H_t, not a modification of the presheaf morphisms. The declaration creates a new step sDs_\mathcal{D} and a new history t′t'; the restriction maps at t′t' are determined by the presheaf extended to include sDs_\mathcal{D}. The nucleus σt′\sigma_{t'} at t′t' may differ from σt\sigma_t. □

Proposition 2 (Felicitous declaration yields σ-settlement at the new history): If the declaration D\mathcal{D} is felicitous (standing conditions met, authorizing source recognized, context operative), then X∈Fix(σt′)X \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t'}) at t′=sD⋆tt' = s_\mathcal{D} \star t: σt′(X)=X\sigma_{t'}(X) = X.

Proof structure. This is the defining criterion of felicity for a status function declaration in this system: the declaration is felicitous precisely when it generates a history t′t' at which the restriction profile of XX to all sub-histories t0<t′t_0 < t' satisfies the profile-class maximality condition for XX. The declaration embeds the recognition act into the history; the saturation nucleus at t′t' then settles XX because the accumulated record now includes sDs_\mathcal{D} among its steps. □

Consequence. Infelicitous declarations — those lacking authority, standing, or proper context — generate a step sDs_\mathcal{D} that does not constitute the required restriction profile for XX; the nucleus σt′\sigma_{t'} at t′t' does not settle XX. The element XX persists unsettled at t′t'.

Proposition 3 (Full institutional recognition requires both stages): Collective recognition corresponds to X∈Ht′′∗=Fix(σt′′)∩Fix(Δt′′)X \in H^*_{t''} = \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t''}) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t''}) at some history t′′t'' reachable from tt. This requires: (a) σ-settlement at t′t' (meaning-recognition: the past record fully constitutes XX’s status) AND (b) Δ-settlement at t′′t'' (forward commitment: every extension of t′′t'' carries XX’s status). Neither implies the other.

Proof. Ht′′∗=Fix(σt′′)∩Fix(Δt′′)H^*_{t''} = \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t''}) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t''}); the two conditions are independent by definition. A declaration that secures σ-settlement but not Δ-settlement gives recognition without forward commitment — the institutional fact is recognized but not yet entrenched in every possible future. □

Consequence. When collective recognition fails (the institution dissolves, the community stops treating XX as having status YY): the restriction profile of XX at future histories no longer satisfies the maximality condition, and σt′′′(X)>X\sigma_{t'''}(X) > X at later histories t′′′>t′′t''' > t''. The institutional fact becomes unsettled — not because σt′′\sigma_{t''} changed retroactively, but because the new accumulation of steps generates nuclei at later histories that do not settle XX.

Context-relativity of status: The context CC is encoded in which presheaf and which nucleus is operative. A dollar bill XX is settled (σt(X)=X\sigma_t(X) = X) in the history governed by the US monetary system’s presheaf structure; the same element is not settled (σt(X)>X\sigma_t(X) > X) in a history governed by the EU monetary system. Context-relativity is presheaf-relativity: different constitutive frameworks are different presheaves over different history sites, with different nuclei.

¶Open questions

  • Whether the Grundnorm has a formal nuclear analog — whether there is a foundational declaration that constitutes the saturation nucleus itself; and whether such a declaration is self-referential in a way that requires a fixed-point theorem to make sense of (“the system’s axioms count as valid, where ‘valid’ is defined by the system’s axioms”).
  • Whether conflicting status function declarations (two authorizing acts that constitute incompatible statuses for the same XX) have a resolution procedure in the nuclear structure — whether the conflict corresponds to σt(X)\sigma_t(X) having multiple incompatible fixed points, and whether the nuclear quartet gives a canonical resolution.
  • Whether revocation (the act of removing a status — dissolution of the corporation, annulment of the marriage, reversal of the conviction) is the inverse of a status function declaration, and whether it has a formal representation as an operation that moves XX from Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) back into Ht∖Fix(σt)H_t \setminus \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t).
  • Whether the defines: field across all definition files constitutes a formal vocabulary of status function declarations — and whether the closure of this vocabulary under the system’s constitutive rules gives the full institutional structure of a knowledge system.
