Amendment

2026-03-20 An Amendment is a morphism (C, J) → (C', J') between charters — a pair (μ, ν) of a scope adjustment μ and a policy revision ν — that transforms one constituted community's governing policies into another while preserving the community's identity. The defining structure: an amendment changes what the charter says without dissolving and reconstituting the community; the community persists through the amendment. Formally a site morphism — a functor on the underlying blueprint category that is continuous with respect to both Grothendieck topologies.

¶Formal definition

An Amendment is a morphism A:C1→C2\mathcal{A} : \mathcal{C}_1 \to \mathcal{C}_2 between charters:

A=(μ:S1→S2,  ν:N1→N2,  α:A1→A2)\mathcal{A} = (\mu : S_1 \to S_2,\; \nu : N_1 \to N_2,\; \alpha : A_1 \to A_2)

where:

  • C1=(G1,N1,S1,A1)\mathcal{C}_1 = (G_1, N_1, S_1, A_1) is the source charter — the existing constituted community G1G_1, governing policies N1N_1, scope S1S_1, authorizing act A1A_1
  • C2=(G2,N2,S2,A2)\mathcal{C}_2 = (G_2, N_2, S_2, A_2) is the amended charter — the charter after modification
  • μ:S1→S2\mu : S_1 \to S_2 is the scope adjustment — a morphism between the scopes; it may expand, contract, or laterally shift the charter’s operative domain
  • ν:N1→N2\nu : N_1 \to N_2 is the policy revision — a morphism in the category of normative systems; it adds, removes, or modifies the governing policies
  • α:A1→A2\alpha : A_1 \to A_2 is the authority chain — the authorizing act that makes the amendment valid; an amendment requires its own authorization, derived from the amending authority

Three invariants. A\mathcal{A} is an amendment iff:

  1. Community identity preservation: G2=G1G_2 = G_1 — the community being governed is the same community; an amendment does not dissolve and reconstitute the community. If the community changes, the act is not an amendment but a novation (dissolution and fresh constitution). The community’s persistence through amendment is the formal definition of what makes a governance structure amendable rather than merely replaceable.

  2. Continuity condition: the scope adjustment μ\mu is continuous with respect to the Grothendieck topologies J1J_1 and J2J_2 — covering families in J1J_1 map to covering families in J2J_2. In the site-morphism sense (Relational Universe Morphism): μ\mu is a functor on the blueprint categories that preserves coverage. An amendment that disrupts covering families — that changes what counts as a valid operational completion — is a scope revision, not merely a policy change.

  3. Authorization chain: α:A1→A2\alpha : A_1 \to A_2 derives A2A_2 from A1A_1 — the amending authority is derived from the charter’s own authority structure. A charter amendment not authorized by the charter’s own amendment procedure is void: the community has not recognized it. This is Hart’s rule of change (Norm): secondary rules authorize the modification of primary rules; the amendment is valid only if performed via an authorized rule of change.

¶Conservative vs. non-conservative amendment

An amendment A:C1→C2\mathcal{A} : \mathcal{C}_1 \to \mathcal{C}_2 is:

Conservative: N2N_2 proves no new claims in the language of N1N_1 — N2⊢ϕN_2 \vdash \phi implies N1⊢ϕN_1 \vdash \phi for all ϕ∈L(N1)\phi \in L(N_1). A conservative amendment adds vocabulary or clarifies procedure without changing what obligations exist in the original normative language. The community retains all its prior commitments; the amendment only extends or clarifies without altering.

Non-conservative: there exists ϕ∈L(N1)\phi \in L(N_1) with N2⊢ϕN_2 \vdash \phi and N1⊬ϕN_1 \not\vdash \phi. A non-conservative amendment changes what is obligatory in the original language — it creates new obligations or removes existing ones. Examples: adding a new duty to the charter’s officer structure; removing a prior requirement; changing a permission to a prohibition.

The conservative/non-conservative distinction corresponds to the Extends spec’s theory-extension structure: a conservative amendment is a conservative extension of the normative system (no new theorems in the original language); a non-conservative amendment is a proper extension (new theorems emerge).

¶The site-morphism reading

A charter is formally a site (B,J)(\mathcal{B}, J) — a blueprint category B\mathcal{B} equipped with a Grothendieck topology JJ (Charter). A morphism of charters is a site morphism (μ,ν):(B1,J1)→(B2,J2)(\mu, \nu) : (\mathcal{B}_1, J_1) \to (\mathcal{B}_2, J_2): a functor μ:B1→B2\mu : \mathcal{B}_1 \to \mathcal{B}_2 that is continuous (J1J_1-covering families map to J2J_2-covering families) and a natural transformation ν\nu on the corresponding policy sheaves.

In the Relational Universe Morphism sense: an amendment is a morphism of relational universes restricted to the governance layer — a history category morphism μ\mu (scope adjustment) plus a natural isomorphism ν:H1→∼μ∗H2\nu : H_1 \xrightarrow{\sim} \mu^* H_2 of policy presheaves (the policy revision, pulling the amended policies back along μ\mu).

The amendment is valid (a genuine morphism, not just any transformation) when both the continuity condition (invariant 2) and the authorization chain (invariant 3) hold. An invalid amendment fails to constitute a morphism in the site category — it is not recognized as a legitimate governance act.

¶Amendment vs. novation

Dimension Amendment Novation
Community identity Preserved (G1=G2G_1 = G_2) Dissolved and reconstituted (G1≠G2G_1 \neq G_2)
Charter continuity The charter persists through change A new charter replaces the old one
Prior obligations Carried forward (minus explicit changes) Extinguished (must be re-established in new charter)
Authorization Derived from existing charter’s amendment rules New authorization from a constituting act
Math type Morphism in the category of charters New object in the category of charters

¶Open questions

  • Whether every charter morphism C1→C2\mathcal{C}_1 \to \mathcal{C}_2 decomposes into a composition of elementary amendments — whether the category of charters has a generating set of elementary morphisms (scope extension, scope contraction, duty addition, duty removal, authority delegation) such that every amendment is a finite composition of these.
  • Whether conservative extensions of charters (Extends) correspond to the charter morphisms that are fully faithful on the policy sheaf — i.e., injective on obligations — and whether non-conservative amendments correspond to morphisms that are only faithful (injective) but not full (may not cover all new obligations).
  • Whether there is a notion of charter equivalence — two charters that are mutual amendments of each other (each extends the other conservatively) — and whether this corresponds to Morita equivalence of the corresponding sites.
