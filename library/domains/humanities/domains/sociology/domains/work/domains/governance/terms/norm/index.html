2026-03-20 A Norm is a triple (φ, O, N) — a proposition φ expressing what agents in situation S should do or refrain from doing, a deontic modality O selecting among obligation, permission, and prohibition, and a normative system N within which the norm is operative and from which it derives its force. The defining structure: a norm is not a regularity (a pattern of behavior that happens to occur) but a prescription (a standard whose authority is independent of whether it is followed). Violated norms remain norms; violated regularities simply turn out not to be regularities.

Norm

Formal definition

A Norm is a triple N=(φ,O,N)\mathcal{N} = (\varphi, O, N):

N=(φ:PropositionContent,  O∈{Obl,Per,Proh},  N:NormativeSystem)\mathcal{N} = (\varphi : \mathrm{PropositionContent},\; O \in \{\mathrm{Obl}, \mathrm{Per}, \mathrm{Proh}\},\; N : \mathrm{NormativeSystem})

where:

  • φ\varphi is the propositional content — the act, state, or situation that the norm is about; φ\varphi may be specific (“pay your taxes by April 15”) or abstract (“honor your contracts”); complex norms have conditional structure: “if ψ\psi then O(φ)O(\varphi)” — the norm applies when condition ψ\psi holds
  • OO is the deontic operator — the modality that specifies the normative valence:
    • Obl(φ)\mathrm{Obl}(\varphi): it is obligatory to φ\varphi — the agent must perform φ\varphi or face normative deficiency
    • Per(φ)\mathrm{Per}(\varphi): it is permitted to φ\varphi — no norm in NN prohibits φ\varphi; the agent may φ\varphi without violation
    • Proh(φ)\mathrm{Proh}(\varphi): it is prohibited to φ\varphi — the agent must not perform φ\varphi; Proh(φ)≡Obl(¬φ)\mathrm{Proh}(\varphi) \equiv \mathrm{Obl}(\neg\varphi)
  • NN is the normative system — the NormativeSystem or Institution within which the norm is operative; norms are not free-floating — they exist within some normative order that specifies their scope, their priority, and their enforcement; the same proposition φ\varphi may be obligatory in N1N_1 and merely permitted in N2N_2

Four invariants. N\mathcal{N} is a norm iff:

  1. Independence from compliance: a norm remains a norm even when violated. If drivers routinely run red lights, the norm “stop at red lights” remains operative — it is not falsified by violation, only violated. This is what distinguishes a norm from a regularity: regularities are descriptive (they hold when they are observed to hold); norms are prescriptive (they hold by their authority, not by their compliance rate).

  2. Authority-derived force: the norm’s prescriptive force derives from its authority source NN — whether statute, contract, custom, or professional standard. Remove NN and φ\varphi is merely a suggestion or a prediction, not a norm. Authority is what makes the norm’s force content-independent: “you must pay your taxes” does not depend on whether you agree that taxation is wise.

  3. Deontic consistency (SDL Axiom D): Obl(φ)⇒Per(φ)\mathrm{Obl}(\varphi) \Rightarrow \mathrm{Per}(\varphi) — obligation implies permission. A norm cannot simultaneously oblige and prohibit φ\varphi within a consistent normative system. Genuine normative dilemmas (cases where both Obl(φ)\mathrm{Obl}(\varphi) and Proh(φ)\mathrm{Proh}(\varphi) appear to hold) are either inconsistencies in the normative system or apparent dilemmas that resolve under a priority rule.

  4. Normative force generates Hohfeldian relations: an operative norm Obl(φ)\mathrm{Obl}(\varphi) in NN creates Duty-type Hohfeldian relations for the agents subject to NN — they acquire a duty to φ\varphi with a correlative claim-right in the relevant counterparty. A norm is not a mere fact about the normative system; it actively constitutes the deontic profile of agents within it.

The Kelsen structure: conditional norms

Hans Kelsen (General Theory of Law and State, 1945; Pure Theory of Law, 1960) identifies the canonical structure of a legal norm as:

If [F1 (delict), then O(sanction F2)]\text{If } [F_1 \text{ (delict)}, \text{ then } O(\text{sanction } F_2)]

“If there is murder, then there ought to be punishment.” The norm does not predict what will happen; it prescribes what ought to happen. The primary fact F1F_1 (the delict) is the condition; the normative consequence F2F_2 (the sanction) is what the norm prescribes.

Two further structural features:

Validity: a norm is valid (in force) iff it was created by an authorized authority through the appropriate procedure and is part of the hierarchy of norms tracing back to the Grundnorm. Validity is not truth — a norm is valid when it is in force, not when its content is correct. An unjust law is a valid norm if properly enacted; an admirable principle not enacted is not a valid norm in the legal sense.

The Grundnorm: Kelsen’s solution to the regress of norm validity. Every norm derives its validity from a higher norm (“this statute is valid because it was enacted pursuant to the constitution”). The constitution is valid because… there must be a foundational presupposition. The Grundnorm is the presupposed foundation: “the historically first constitution ought to be obeyed.” It is not itself a positive norm; it is a transcendental presupposition that makes the normative system’s validity possible. Without the Grundnorm, the regress is infinite; with it, the system has a foundation — albeit a presupposed rather than enacted one.

Derogation: a norm in Kelsen is not retracted by a later inconsistent norm automatically — it must be derogated (explicitly repealed or overridden) or it remains valid alongside the inconsistent one, creating a normative conflict that requires resolution.

Hart: primary and secondary rules

H.L.A. Hart (The Concept of Law, 1961) distinguishes two types of norms:

Primary rules: rules that impose duties or confer permissions directly on individuals. “Do not steal,” “you must file a tax return,” “you may speak in your own defense” — these directly regulate human behavior. A society with only primary rules is possible (a pre-legal society of custom) but deficient in three ways:

  1. Uncertainty: no authoritative identification of which rules are the rules (no rule of recognition)
  2. Static quality: no mechanism for deliberately changing rules
  3. Inefficiency: no authoritative way to determine when a rule has been violated (no adjudication procedure)

Secondary rules: rules about rules — they confer powers to create, modify, or extinguish primary rules, or to determine questions of compliance. Secondary rules are the solution to the three deficiencies:

  1. Rule of recognition: a secondary rule that identifies which primary rules are in force; the rule of recognition is not itself a primary rule but a practice among officials — a shared acceptance of a criterion of validity
  2. Rules of change: secondary rules that authorize some person or body to introduce, modify, or repeal primary rules
  3. Rules of adjudication: secondary rules that confer authority on individuals to determine whether a primary rule has been violated on a particular occasion

The union of primary and secondary rules is what Hart calls a legal system: not merely a set of norms, but a system with a rule of recognition (providing unity and identity), rules of change (providing dynamism), and rules of adjudication (providing effective enforcement.

Hart’s insight about the rule of recognition: it is not itself a valid rule in the Kelsenian sense — it does not derive its validity from a higher rule. Instead, it is accepted as an ultimate criterion of validity by officials. This replaces Kelsen’s presupposed Grundnorm with a social fact about official acceptance. Legal systems are distinguished not by what their norms prescribe but by their having a rule of recognition that officials accept and apply.

Bicchieri: social norms and normative expectations

Cristina Bicchieri (The Grammar of Society, 2006) offers a game-theoretic account of social norm internalization. Her formulation: a norm is a behavioral rule RR in a population PP for situation SS such that:

  1. Most members of PP know RR exists (empirical expectation condition)
  2. Most members prefer to conform to RR if: a. They believe most others will conform (empirical expectations — descriptive, about what others will do), AND b. They believe most others think they should conform and might sanction non-conformance (normative expectations — prescriptive, about what others expect)

The critical distinction: a social convention (like driving on the right) requires only empirical expectations — you do it because others do it, and it works as long as most people coordinate. A social norm (like not cheating in tests) additionally requires normative expectations — you conform because you believe others think you should conform and would disapprove of violations.

A social norm is not merely a regularity plus its having been there a long time. It is a regularity sustained by two kinds of expectations together: descriptive (others will comply) and normative (others think I should comply). Bicchieri’s diagnostic: if you manipulate the normative expectations without changing the empirical expectations (by telling people that others comply out of fear, not genuine endorsement), social norms collapse; conventions do not. This shows that the normative expectation is a real, independent component.

Norm sensitivity: agents vary in their sensitivity to normative expectations. Some agents are “norm followers” — they comply when normative expectations hold, regardless of empirical expectations. Some are “conditional cooperators” — they comply only when both expectations hold. Some are “free riders” — they defect when they think they can. The distribution of types in a population determines whether a social norm is stable (enough norm followers to sustain normative expectations even with some free riding) or fragile (a small shock in normative expectations collapses the norm).

Lewis: conventions as coordination equilibria

David Lewis (Convention, 1969) gives the game-theoretic account of conventions — a restricted class of norms:

A convention in population PP for situation SS is a regularity RR such that:

  1. Everyone in PP conforms to RR in SS
  2. Everyone in PP believes others will conform to RR in SS
  3. Everyone prefers to conform given others do (coordination payoff)
  4. There is an alternative R′R' that everyone would prefer if everyone conformed to R′R' instead
  5. This is common knowledge

A convention solves a coordination problem: there was no pre-existing reason to use RR rather than R′R' (drive on the right vs. drive on the left), but once enough people use RR, everyone prefers RR because others use RR. The convention is self-reinforcing without being mandatory: no one has a duty to drive on the right (they do so because it is strategically rational given others’ behavior), though the convention generates an expectation.

Lewis’s framework is important for distinguishing moral norms (which bind regardless of others’ compliance — you should not murder even if everyone else does) from conventional norms (which bind only given sufficient compliance — you drive on the right because others do). The distinction is not absolute: some legal norms are conventional (traffic rules); others are not (prohibitions on murder). The game-theoretic account captures the conventional-norm floor; the Kelsen/Hart accounts capture the validity-based analysis.

Nuclear reading

Definitions. A norm N=(φ,O,N)\mathcal{N} = (\varphi, O, N) at history tt is given by:

  • Obl(φ)\mathrm{Obl}(\varphi) operative at tt iff φ∈Fix(σt)\varphi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t): the saturation nucleus σt\sigma_t has closed on φ\varphi — the restriction profile of φ\varphi to all sub-histories t0<tt_0 < t has reached equilibrium (Saturation Nucleus: σt(a)=max⁡{b∈Ht∣ρt0(b)≤ρt0(a) ∀ t0<t}\sigma_t(a) = \max\{b \in H_t \mid \rho_{t_0}(b) \leq \rho_{t_0}(a)\ \forall\, t_0 < t\}).
  • Per(φ)\mathrm{Per}(\varphi) at tt iff ¬φ∉Fix(σt)\neg\varphi \notin \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t).
  • Proh(φ)≡Obl(¬φ)\mathrm{Proh}(\varphi) \equiv \mathrm{Obl}(\neg\varphi) at tt iff ¬φ∈Fix(σt)\neg\varphi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t).
  • φ\varphi is violated at tt iff φ∈Fix(σt)\varphi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) but φ∉Fix(Δt)\varphi \notin \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t): the obligation is meaning-settled but not execution-settled — committed forward by the past but not yet present in every one-step extension.

Proposition 1 (Obligation-conjunction): If Obl(φ)\mathrm{Obl}(\varphi) and Obl(ψ)\mathrm{Obl}(\psi) are both operative at tt, then Obl(φ∧ψ)\mathrm{Obl}(\varphi \wedge \psi) is operative at tt.

Proof. φ,ψ∈Fix(σt)\varphi, \psi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) means σt(φ)=φ\sigma_t(\varphi) = \varphi and σt(ψ)=ψ\sigma_t(\psi) = \psi. By meet-preservation: σt(φ∧ψ)=σt(φ)∧σt(ψ)=φ∧ψ\sigma_t(\varphi \wedge \psi) = \sigma_t(\varphi) \wedge \sigma_t(\psi) = \varphi \wedge \psi. So φ∧ψ∈Fix(σt)\varphi \wedge \psi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t). □\square

This is the SDL axiom (C): Obl(φ)∧Obl(ψ)→Obl(φ∧ψ)\mathrm{Obl}(\varphi) \wedge \mathrm{Obl}(\psi) \to \mathrm{Obl}(\varphi \wedge \psi) — derivable here from the meet-preservation of the saturation nucleus.

Proposition 2 (Norm persistence at the moment of violation): Let φ∈Fix(σt)\varphi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) (the norm is operative at tt), and let a∈Hta \in H_t be an agent’s action with a≱φa \not\geq \varphi (non-compliance). Then σt(φ)=φ\sigma_t(\varphi) = \varphi — the saturation nucleus is unchanged.

Proof. By definition, σt(φ)\sigma_t(\varphi) is determined entirely by the restriction maps H(it0,t):Ht→Ht0H(i_{t_0,t}) : H_t \to H_{t_0} for all t0<tt_0 < t. These maps are part of the presheaf structure over the history site (T,J)(T, J); they are not modified by any element a∈Hta \in H_t. Since aa is an element of HtH_t and not a modification of any restriction map, the restriction profile of φ\varphi is unaffected: σt(φ)=φ\sigma_t(\varphi) = \varphi persists. □\square

This is the mathematical content of “violated norms remain norms.” The norm exists as a fixed point of the sheafification structure; a non-compliant act is an element that fails to reach φ\varphi, but this failure does not retroactively change the sheaf’s restriction profile.

Proposition 3 (Idempotence collapses the Kelsenian validity chain): For any n≥1n \geq 1 and any φ0∈Ht\varphi_0 \in H_t: σtn(φ0)=σt(φ0)\sigma_t^n(\varphi_0) = \sigma_t(\varphi_0).

Proof. By idempotence: σt(σt(φ0))=σt(φ0)\sigma_t(\sigma_t(\varphi_0)) = \sigma_t(\varphi_0). Inductively σtn=σt\sigma_t^n = \sigma_t for all n≥1n \geq 1. □\square

Consequence. The Kelsenian validity chain — statute validated by constitution validated by Grundnorm — cannot be represented as iterated application of σt\sigma_t on a single fiber, because every iteration after the first is a no-op. The hierarchical derivation must instead be encoded in the VERTICAL structure of the site: the Grundnorm is not a repeated application of σt\sigma_t but a proposition at a DIFFERENT HISTORY (a higher stratum in the tower), with restriction maps connecting strata. The chain φstatute≤σt′(φ0)=φ0\varphi_{\mathrm{statute}} \leq \sigma_{t'}(\varphi_0) = \varphi_0 (at a higher stratum t′t') is the tower-level reading of norm derivation, not within-fiber iteration.

Proposition 4 (Deontic consistency requires an additional axiom): The nuclear axioms (extensiveness, idempotence, meet-preservation, commutation) do not prevent Obl(φ)\mathrm{Obl}(\varphi) and Proh(φ)\mathrm{Proh}(\varphi) from being simultaneously operative at tt.

Proof. Suppose φ,¬φ∈Fix(σt)\varphi, \neg\varphi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t). By Proposition 1: φ∧¬φ=⊥t∈Fix(σt)\varphi \wedge \neg\varphi = \bot_t \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t), meaning σt(⊥t)=⊥t\sigma_t(\bot_t) = \bot_t. This is consistent with all nuclear axioms: extensiveness requires ⊥t≤σt(⊥t)\bot_t \leq \sigma_t(\bot_t), which holds with equality; idempotence and meet-preservation are satisfied trivially by ⊥t\bot_t being fixed. No nuclear axiom is violated. □\square

An additional axiom is required: a consistent normative system NN is one for which ⊥t∉Fix(σt)\bot_t \notin \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) — the bottom element is not meaning-settled as an obligation. This is a condition on the normative system NN, not on the nucleus σt\sigma_t in isolation. A fiber with ⊥t∈Fix(σt)\bot_t \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) is a degenerate (trivially obligating) normative system.

Hart’s secondary rules: topology, not fiber elements. Primary norms are elements of HtH_t. Secondary rules do NOT operate by “adding or removing elements from Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t)” — this would be incoherent, since Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) is determined by the restriction maps of the presheaf, which are part of the sheaf structure, not something that legal authorization can modify. The correct reading:

  • Rule of recognition: the Grothendieck topology JJ on TT — the specification of which covering families count as valid. JJ determines which compatible families of sections amalgamate to global sections in Sh(T,J)\mathbf{Sh}(T, J), i.e., which local normative facts count as globally valid. Hart’s “internal point of view” is the sheaf gluing condition: a normative fact is legally valid iff its local sections, wherever officials operate, cohere into a global section.
  • Rule of change: a condition on which STEPS s∈Σs \in \Sigma are legally operative — which actions generate new histories by extending tt to s⋆ts \star t. A legislative act is an authorized step that generates a new history sleg⋆ts_{\mathrm{leg}} \star t, at which σsleg⋆t\sigma_{s_{\mathrm{leg}} \star t} is freshly defined, and the new fiber Hsleg⋆tH_{s_{\mathrm{leg}} \star t} may contain new fixed points not present at tt. Changing norms is changing histories, not changing fixed-point sets.
  • Rule of adjudication: a covering sieve that makes a disputed norm’s validity decidable — an authorized procedure (a judicial covering) that forces the compatibility condition in JJ to resolve in a specific direction.

Open questions

  • Whether Kelsen’s Grundnorm has a formal nuclear representation — whether there is an element g∈Htg \in H_t such that σt(g)=g\sigma_t(g) = g and every other obligation in Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) is derivable from gg by closure; and whether gg’s status as Grundnorm is itself constituted by a StatusFunctionDeclaration or presupposed without constitution.
  • Whether Bicchieri’s two-tier expectation condition (empirical + normative) corresponds to the two nuclei: empirical expectations tracking Fix(Δt)\mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) (what has actually been done, executed, propagated) and normative expectations tracking Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) (what is meaning-settled as required). The social norm is stable when both nuclei fix the same elements; instability arises when Fix(σt)≠Fix(Δt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) \neq \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) — when what is normatively expected diverges from what is actually done.
  • Whether Hart’s rule of recognition is a global section of the sheaf of normative systems over the history site — a coherent specification of which norms are valid at every history that is compatible with how norms are recognized across contexts.
  • Whether there is a formal category of norms — with norms as objects and “derivation” or “entailment” as morphisms — and whether the Grundnorm derivability condition corresponds to the norm of norms being an initial object in this category.
