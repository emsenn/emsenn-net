Black Radical Governance

2026-04-03 A survey of the Black radical tradition's theory of governance: that governance systems carry racial inheritance, that formal equality within inherited structures reproduces domination, that abolition is a positive governance theory (replacing life-denying institutions with life-affirming ones), and that fugitivity and collective refusal are normative practices outside state capture.

¶What This Is

The Black radical tradition is not merely a historical phenomenon—it is a coherent theory of governance that begins from a premise foreign to the dominant Western political tradition: that all existing governance systems carry the inheritance of racial domination, and that formal equality operating within inherited structures reproduces that domination at deeper levels. This survey examines how five foundational thinkers—Cedric Robinson, Ruth Wilson Gilmore, Saidiya Hartman, Fred Moten and Stefano Harney, and W.E.B. Du Bois—construct a theory of governance that locates the problem not merely in bad rulers or unjust laws, but in the historical embedding of racial ordering within the very structures through which governance operates.

The Black radical tradition makes three interconnected claims. First, governance systems are not neutral instruments—they carry and regenerate the systems of racial capitalism that structure power at every level. Second, formal freedom within existing institutional structures is not liberation but a new form of captivity. Third, abolition is not a negative project but a positive governance theory: the building of life-affirming institutions to replace those organized around abandonment and death.

¶Racial Capitalism as Governance Infrastructure

Cedric Robinson’s Black Marxism (1983) establishes the theoretical foundation: that capitalism has always been racial, and that racial ordering is not an accident or aberration of capitalist systems but intrinsic to how they function. This is the concept of racial capitalism—a system in which value extraction, labor control, and political authority are organized through racial distinction.

What makes this a governance claim is Robinson’s argument that Western political systems—the state, the legal order, formal institutions of democracy—inherited this racial ordering from their feudal and mercantile antecedents. Robinson writes that capitalism did not break from the old order but evolved from it, producing a “modern world system of racial capitalism dependent on slavery, violence, imperialism, and genocide.” Governance does not stand outside this system evaluating it; governance systems ARE racial capitalism’s mechanisms of enforcement and reproduction.

This means that the state, as it currently exists, is fundamentally tied to the racial ordering of labor and value. A governance system built on the foundation of racial capitalism cannot become non-racial simply by declaring formal equality. The inheritance runs too deep. This is why Robinson’s work supports horizontally-organized resistance movements—not because such organization is always better in principle, but because vertical authority structures reproduce the hierarchies embedded in existing governance systems.

¶Organized Abandonment: The Governance of Life and Death

Ruth Wilson Gilmore’s work on organized abandonment completes the picture by showing how contemporary governance functions not through direct violence but through systematic exposure and deprivation. In Golden Gulag (2007) and subsequent essays, Gilmore demonstrates that the state governs by deciding which populations, lands, and futures are surplus to requirement and may be abandoned.

California’s prison expansion, Gilmore shows, was not a policy error but a governance solution to four simultaneous surpluses: land idled by drought and debt, finance capital seeking profitable investment, workers displaced by deindustrialization, and state capacity ready to confine and suppress. Prisons solved a crisis of racial capitalism by concentrating the surplus population in a way that extracted profit. This is governance through organized abandonment: systematic withdrawal of the conditions necessary for life, organized so that it appears natural or inevitable rather than a political choice.

Critically, Gilmore insists that abolition is not merely the negation of this system—it is the positive construction of alternatives. “Abolition,” she argues, “is about building life-affirming institutions.” This transforms abolition from a critique into a governance theory: the replacement of institutions organized around abandonment with institutions organized around the preservation and flourishing of life. This reorientation of governance toward care and life—rather than control and extraction—is the core of the Black radical tradition’s governance theory.

¶The Afterlife of Slavery: How Formal Freedom Reproduces Captivity

Saidiya Hartman’s concept of “the afterlife of slavery” reveals how formal freedom within existing governance structures perpetuates the conditions slavery created. In Scenes of Subjection (1997), Hartman examines the period after slavery’s formal abolition, showing how legal systems, police authority, and the state apparatus continued to govern Black life as captive—even after the status of slavery was formally ended.

The key insight is that a normative system inherited from slavery does not become non-slaveholding simply by changing its law. The governance structures remain organized around the control, containment, and disposal of Black people. What appears as freedom—the right to work, to move, to transact—is actually a new form of subjection, operated through apparently neutral legal mechanisms rather than explicit coercion.

In Wayward Lives, Beautiful Experiments (2019), Hartman shifts focus to show how escape from this captivity is possible—not through reform of existing institutions but through practices of refusal and alternative living. Young Black women and queer people, Hartman documents, created “beautiful experiments” in social life that were outside and against the state apparatus. These practices—finding joy, building relationships, creating new forms of sociality on the margins—are not external to governance theory but constitute it: they demonstrate what governance looks like when organized by those without formal authority, oriented toward life rather than control.

Hartman introduces the practice of fugitivity as a governance strategy. Fugitivity is not merely escape; it is the ongoing practice of evading state capture, creating social forms that elude the categories and mechanisms through which governance ordinarily operates. It is a normative practice—a way of organizing life and relationship according to different principles than those the state enforces.

¶The Undercommons: Governance from Below

Fred Moten and Stefano Harney’s The Undercommons (2013) theorizes governance as it operates outside formal institutions. They argue that the governed—those without formal authority—have always had their own systems of organization, study, and collective decision-making. The undercommons is not chaos or the absence of governance; it is governance constituted by those whom official systems seek to control, organized according to principles those systems cannot capture.

The concept of black study—collective intellectual work outside institutional frameworks—is itself a governance practice. When people gather to read, discuss, and learn together outside formal education and state recognition, they are not preparing for governance; they are engaging in governance—deciding what knowledge matters, how to transmit it, what questions to ask. This is the “logistics of the governed”: the systems, practices, and infrastructures through which people organize their collective life.

Moten and Harney emphasize that the undercommons operates through planning—but planning that cannot be captured by the state apparatus, planning that remains fugitive. It is planning organized around refusal: the refusal to participate in the reproduction of existing systems, the refusal to accept the categories through which the state seeks to govern. This is not anti-planning or disorganization; it is organized refusal.

¶Abolition-Democracy: Governance Reconstructed

W.E.B. Du Bois’s concept of abolition-democracy in Black Reconstruction (1935) offers the historical example of governance theory in practice. During Reconstruction (1865-1880), freed people and their allies built new governance institutions—schools, hospitals, agencies for housing and welfare—not as reforms within existing systems but as replacements for them.

Du Bois documents how the federal government created agencies to address the concrete needs of the emancipated and poor whites, building the first public schools in the South and attending to general welfare. This was not merely charity or welfare administration; it was the construction of institutions organized around the principle that governance exists to enable life, not to extract value or maintain control.

The term “abolition-democracy” captures the insight that building alternatives to oppressive institutions IS democracy—it is the governed deciding what their governance should be. This requires, first, the abolition of the institutions that organize abandonment and control; second, the construction of new institutions organized around collective care and flourishing.

Abolition-democracy reveals that what appears to be a negative project—abolition—is actually profoundly constructive. It is the work of building governance systems from scratch, grounded in different premises than those that shaped the existing order. Du Bois’s historical narrative shows that this is not theoretical; freed people actually did this work, and their institutions—though they were ultimately destroyed by the counterrevolution of white supremacy—demonstrated that alternative governance was possible.

¶Integration: The Black Radical Tradition as Governance Theory

These five thinkers construct a unified theory, though not a unified system. The theory proceeds as follows:

  1. Inheritance: Governance systems carry the inheritance of racial domination built into their foundational structures. This inheritance cannot be erased by changing individual laws or electing different officials. It shapes how the entire system operates.

  2. Reproduction: Formal freedom and legal equality within inherited structures do not end domination; they reorganize it. The state governs through a combination of direct violence (police, prisons) and organized abandonment (withdrawal of the conditions for life). Legal and institutional mechanisms appear neutral while enforcing the same racial ordering they inherited.

  3. Refusal: The governed—those whom existing systems seek to control—have always organized their own governance through practices of refusal: study, fugitivity, collective decision-making outside formal institutions, the construction of alternative institutions. These are not external to governance theory but central to it.

  4. Construction: Abolition is the positive work of building life-affirming institutions to replace those organized around death and control. This is not reform but replacement; it requires the governed to constitute new forms of collective authority, new normative systems, new ways of deciding what community requires.

The Black radical tradition therefore relocates the question of governance. It is not “how should formal institutions be improved?” but “how do the governed constitute their own governance when formal institutions are organized to prevent their flourishing?” This is not a question about better laws or fairer procedures; it is a question about the construction of collective life outside the reach of systems organized for domination.

¶Connections to the Repository’s Commitments

The 561 Group’s charter positions the system toward “the pursuit of Black Liberation and Indigenous Sovereignty.” The Black radical tradition on governance is directly relevant to this commitment. If the governing principle is liberation—understood not as formal freedom within existing systems but as the reconstruction of governance itself—then the institutional forms through which the Group operates cannot simply adopt existing governance structures and expect different results.

This survey suggests that the Group’s governance structures should be designed with the theory outlined here in mind: conscious of the inheritance they carry forward, skeptical of formal neutrality and procedures, organized around constructive alternatives rather than reform of existing systems, and grounded in the collective decision-making of those most affected by governance failure.

¶Open Questions

  • The temporal structure of abolition: If abolition-democracy requires the simultaneous abolition of existing institutions and the construction of new ones, what prevents either an interregnum where no governance operates, or the colonization of new institutions by the logic of the old? Du Bois’s Reconstruction shows this is possible historically, but what organizational and theoretical conditions make it possible?

  • Inheritance and knowledge: The Black radical tradition is itself a form of knowledge transmission—a tradition of thinking about governance passed from Du Bois through Robinson to Gilmore, Hartman, and Moten. How does this tradition operate? Where and how is it transmitted? What is the relationship between preserving this knowledge and the risk that institutionalization will domesticate it?

  • Fugitivity and scale: Hartman’s fugitive practices and Moten and Harney’s undercommons operate at the scale of small groups, neighborhoods, intellectual communities. How do these practices scale to governance of larger populations? At what point does fugitive planning become visible to the state apparatus and get recaptured?

  • The relational universe and abolition governance: The repository’s own mathematical foundations (fiber algebras, bilattice structures, relational geometry) construct governance around relations, not essences. How do these mathematical structures relate to the Black radical tradition’s theory of relational refusal and mutual constitution?
