Founding and Governing Documents: A Survey of Constitutional Instruments

Covers charters, constitutions, articles of incorporation, articles of agreement, bylaws, covenants, treaties, and cooperative constitutions, examining what functions each serves and how they relate to one another.

¶What This Is

Organizations come into being through documents. A founding document constitutes a community—it declares who the community is, what authority governs it, what rules bind its members, and what processes must be followed for the community to act with legitimacy. A governing document specifies the operational rules that flow from that constitution.

The distinction matters because it maps onto different legal and institutional roles. The same organization may require both a charter (the act of constitution granted by an external authority) and bylaws (the internal operating rules). Or it may require articles of incorporation (the legal charter created by the incorporators themselves) plus bylaws. Or it may be governed by a treaty (an agreement between sovereign entities) with no subordinate documents.

This survey examines seven major types of founding and governing documents: charter, constitution, articles of incorporation / articles of association, articles of agreement, bylaws, covenant, and treaty. It also covers cooperative constitutions, which blend principles across these traditional categories. For each type, this survey addresses: what governance functions it serves, what it must structurally contain, how it relates to other document types, and real historical examples.

The survey concludes by synthesizing a general theory: what governance functions must any founding document address, regardless of its type or context.

¶Part 1: The Seven Document Types

¶Charter: Constitution Granted by External Authority

What it does. A charter is a founding document issued by an external authority—a sovereign power, monarch, legislative body, or commissioning entity—that creates and constitutes an organization and grants it specific powers. The charter both creates the organization and declares its governing principles.

Historical origin. The word comes from Latin carta (paper, document). Royal charters in medieval England granted rights and privileges to towns, guilds, universities, and trading companies. A royal charter created a corporation, made it a legal entity, granted it specific rights (to hold land, to govern its members, to collect fees), and declared what constraints bound it. The document itself was the act of constitution.

Structural requirements.

A charter must contain:

  1. Name and purpose — what is being chartered and what is its core endeavor or function
  2. Authority statement — who issues the charter and by what authority they do so
  3. Constituted community — who belongs to the community being chartered (members, officers, beneficiaries)
  4. Powers granted — what the organization is authorized to do
  5. Scope of authority — the geographic, temporal, or domain-specific limits of the organization’s power
  6. Governing structure — how decisions are made, who holds authority, what offices exist
  7. Member rights and duties — what obligations bind members; what standing they have
  8. Amendment and dissolution — under what conditions the charter can be modified and who holds that power

Governance functions a charter serves:

  • Legitimation: The charter is the document that makes the organization’s existence and authority legitimate. Without a charter, the organization lacks standing.
  • Boundary setting: The charter declares what falls within the organization’s scope and what lies outside it. It answers: “what is this organization responsible for?”
  • Authority distribution: The charter specifies who has power to make which decisions, whether authority is concentrated or distributed, and what checks exist on power.
  • Scope governance: As the charter term explains, charters can declare fixed scope (no expansion without a new charter), phased scope (pre-authorized expansion contingent on demonstrated capacity), or organic scope (the organization can expand or contract as it judges necessary within the charter’s bounds).

Examples:

  • Royal Charter of 1535 issued by Henry VIII to the Royal College of Physicians established the college as a body corporate with power to license physicians and establish medical standards in England.
  • City charters in the United States grant cities the power to establish local government, levy taxes, provide services, and create local ordinances within a state.
  • Corporate charters issued by a state’s secretary of state create a corporation as a legal entity.
  • Ship’s charter (in the nautical sense): when a confederacy charters a ship, it is issuing a charter that establishes the ship as an entity with a specific captain, crew, and mission, operating under the confederacy’s directives.

Relationship to other documents: A charter often stands alone as the sole founding document for simple organizations. But many organizations operate under a charter plus subordinate governing documents. A university has a charter (issued by the state) that creates it as a body corporate, plus bylaws that specify how the board of trustees operates. A corporation has a charter (articles of incorporation) plus bylaws.

¶Constitution: A Normative System Declared as Foundational

What it does. A constitution is a document (or set of documents) that declares the fundamental normative system—the core rules, principles, rights, and procedures—by which an organization or polity governs itself. Unlike a charter, which is often granted by external authority, a constitution typically articulates the norms that an already-existing community has decided are foundational.

The philosophical distinction. In the formal sense, constitution is the relation “XX counts as YY in context CC” — a piece of paper counts as a legal tender, a person counts as a president, a sequence of words counts as an oath. But a constitution document is more specific: it is the document that declares the highest-level normative rules of a system, the rules from which all other rules derive authority.

Structural requirements.

A constitution must contain:

  1. Preamble or founding statement — why this constitution is being established, what values or principles justify it
  2. Fundamental rights and freedoms — what cannot be taken away from members or citizens
  3. Governmental structure — the branches, offices, or divisions of authority and how they relate
  4. Powers and limitations — what powers the government/organization holds, and what it cannot do
  5. Procedures for decision-making — how laws are made, how disputes are resolved, how power is exercised
  6. Citizenship or membership — who counts as a member, what rights and duties they have, how status is acquired or lost
  7. Amendment procedure — how the constitution itself can be changed; almost always more rigorous than ordinary legislation
  8. Supremacy clause (often) — statement that the constitution is the highest law and subordinate documents must comply with it

Governance functions:

  • Fundamental constraint: A constitution is meant to be unchangeable except through extraordinary process. Its rules bind even those temporarily in power. This prevents any single leader or majority from arbitrarily rewriting the rules for their own advantage.
  • Separation of powers: Most modern constitutions distribute power across multiple branches (executive, legislative, judicial) or multiple constituencies, creating checks and balances.
  • Rights protection: A constitution typically declares certain rights inviolable—property rights, freedom of conscience, due process—that no ordinary law can override.
  • Framework for ordinary law: All subordinate laws and policies must be consistent with the constitution. The constitution is the meta-level rule that makes other rules valid.

Examples:

  • United States Constitution (1787): A written constitution adopted after independence, establishing three branches of government (legislative, executive, judicial), enumerating specific powers, declaring fundamental rights in the Bill of Rights (ratified 1791), and requiring supermajority votes for amendment.
  • United Kingdom Constitution: An unwritten constitution composed of multiple documents (Magna Carta, Bill of Rights 1689, Acts of Parliament) and constitutional conventions, rather than a single document.
  • Australian Constitution (1901): Established a federal system distributing powers between national and state governments.
  • Organization constitutions: Nonprofits, cooperatives, and social organizations often adopt constitutions that articulate their foundational principles, member rights, and governance structure.

Relationship to other documents: A constitution is often accompanied by subordinate documents that elaborate its principles. The U.S. Constitution is accompanied by federal laws, regulations, and agency rules that implement it. An organization may have a constitution (foundational norms) plus bylaws (operational procedures). Some organizations use “constitution” and “charter” interchangeably; the distinction depends on context and jurisdiction.

¶Articles of Incorporation / Articles of Association: The Corporate Founding Instrument

What it does. Articles of incorporation (in U.S. law) or articles of association (in UK and Commonwealth law) are the legal document by which incorporators establish a corporation or association as a legal entity. The document is filed with the state/government and creates the organization as a matter of law.

Distinction from charter. Historically, a charter was granted by the sovereign authority (a king or legislature). Articles of incorporation are a charter that the founders file with the sovereign authority to create themselves as a corporation. The founders write the articles; the state registers them. This represents a shift from incorporation by permission to incorporation by registration.

Structural requirements.

Articles of incorporation must contain:

  1. Corporate name — the legal name under which the entity will operate
  2. Registered agent and address — the person and location to which legal process can be served
  3. Purpose or mission — what the corporation is organized to do (may be general: “any lawful purpose”)
  4. Stock structure (for corporations) — the number and types of shares authorized to be issued, voting rights attached to each class
  5. Membership structure (for associations) — who can be a member, voting rights per member, classes of membership
  6. Board of directors or equivalent — the governing body, its size, how directors are selected
  7. Incorporators — the names and signatures of those founding the corporation
  8. Duration — whether the corporation is perpetual or has a fixed term

Governance functions:

  • Legal personhood: Articles of incorporation create the corporation as a legal entity distinct from its owners. The corporation can own property, enter contracts, and sue and be sued in its own name.
  • Liability shield: Incorporation typically creates limited liability: owners/shareholders are not personally liable for the corporation’s debts beyond their investment.
  • Authority specification: Articles declare what authority the board of directors holds and what requires shareholder approval.
  • Public notice: By filing articles, the corporation gives public notice of its existence, structure, and registered agent, creating transparency for creditors and other parties.

Examples:

  • Facebook, Inc. (Meta): Filed articles of incorporation in Delaware in 2004, establishing the corporate structure and governance framework.
  • Nonprofit incorporation: Nonprofit organizations file articles establishing their mission, board structure, and tax-exempt status.
  • Cooperative corporations: Cooperatives file articles establishing member voting rights, the principle of democratic control, and allocation of surpluses.

Relationship to other documents: Articles of incorporation create the legal entity and declare its basic structure, but they are deliberately kept brief for regulatory simplicity. The bylaws then elaborate the articles, specifying procedures for elections, meetings, and operations. Articles must not contradict bylaws; bylaws must not contradict articles.

¶Articles of Agreement / Ship’s Articles: Employment and Operational Governance at Sea

What it does. Articles of agreement (also called “ship’s articles” or “shipping articles”) are the contract between the captain of a vessel and the crew members aboard that ship. Signed by each crew member before departure, the articles specify the terms of employment, compensation, and conduct that bind all aboard.

Origins in maritime law. Historically, ships’ articles developed from the Law Merchant (Lex mercatoria) governing trade. Early merchants’ ships were often joint ventures: crew members contributed capital, shares in cargo, or labor, and received payment in shares at voyage’s end. By the 18th century, ship’s articles became standardized written documents; by the 19th century, most maritime nations required them to be written and freely available to any sailor signing on.

Structural requirements.

Ship’s articles must contain:

  1. Ship identification — the vessel’s name, port of registry, tonnage, and identifying marks
  2. Voyage details — the route, duration, and nature of the voyage (trading voyage, whaling voyage, etc.)
  3. Crew manifest — the names, ages, and ratings (positions) of all crew members
  4. Wage/compensation terms — the monetary wages or share of cargo profit for each rating, plus board (food, housing, medical care)
  5. Port of discharge — where the voyage will end and crew will be paid off
  6. Rules of conduct — regulations governing behavior aboard ship and in port, with specified penalties for violations (drunkenness, theft, disobedience, desertion, etc.)
  7. Dispute resolution — procedures for handling disputes between crew members or between crew and captain

Governance functions:

  • Employment specification: Ship’s articles create the employment contract, specifying what work the crew owes, what compensation they receive, and under what conditions employment ends.
  • Operational authority: The articles establish the captain as the final authority aboard ship, with power to enforce rules, impose penalties, and make binding decisions about navigation and cargo.
  • Dispute resolution: They provide a framework for resolving conflicts between crew members and between crew and captain, creating predictability in an environment where legal recourse is limited.
  • Port authority verification: Articles are presented to port authorities and foreign consular officials to establish the legitimacy of the crew and the ship’s operations.

Examples:

  • British Merchant Shipping Act 1850: Standardized ship’s articles and required them to be written out and made available to any sailor signing on.
  • U.S. Code Title 46 (Shipping): Specifies requirements for shipping articles, crew lists, and maritime employment contracts.
  • Modern ship’s articles: Contemporary vessels continue to file articles with maritime authorities, specifying crew, compensation, and regulatory compliance.

Unique feature: Unlike other founding documents examined here, ship’s articles are not a constitution of a permanent organization but a governing agreement for a specific voyage. They establish a temporary hierarchy (the captain in command), specify rights and duties of all aboard, and create dispute resolution mechanisms for a bounded time period. In this sense, they are more like a contract than a charter.

Relationship to confederacy and ship commissioning: In the 561 Group’s framework, a confederacy charters a ship (issues a charter establishing the ship as an entity with a captain and mission); the ship then establishes articles of agreement with its crew. The charter is the founding document of the ship as an institutional entity; the articles of agreement specify employment and conduct aboard that ship.

¶Bylaws: Operational Rules Subordinate to Charter or Constitution

What it does. Bylaws are a set of rules that govern the internal operations, procedures, and decision-making of an organization. Unlike a charter or constitution, which establish the fundamental framework and authority of the organization, bylaws specify how that framework operates in practice.

Structural distinction. A charter or constitution says what offices exist and what powers they hold. Bylaws say how those offices are filled, how long terms last, what notice is required for meetings, what constitutes a quorum, how votes are counted, and what procedures must be followed for ordinary decisions.

Structural requirements.

Bylaws typically address:

  1. Membership — admission, categories of membership, rights and duties of members
  2. Meetings — when and how often meetings occur, notice requirements, quorum, voting procedures
  3. Officers — titles of officers (president, secretary, treasurer), their duties, how they are elected, term lengths, removal procedures
  4. Board of directors or governing body — size, election, terms, meeting procedures, powers, committees
  5. Finances — fiscal year, accounting procedures, authority to spend, approval required for major expenditures
  6. Amendments — how bylaws can be changed (usually by a lower threshold than constitutional amendment, often requiring only board or member majority)
  7. Dispute resolution — procedures for internal conflicts
  8. Dissolution — what happens to assets if the organization dissolves

Governance functions:

  • Operationalization: Bylaws translate the abstract principles of the charter/constitution into concrete procedures. They make governance workable.
  • Procedural fairness: By specifying how decisions are made, bylaws ensure that decisions are made through legitimate processes, creating accountability.
  • Flexibility without constitutional change: Bylaws can be amended more easily than charters or constitutions, allowing an organization to adjust its procedures without the rigorous process of constitutional amendment.
  • Administrative detail: Bylaws handle the administrative machinery that a charter would be too high-level to address—how many board members, what the treasurer’s duties are, how many days’ notice before a meeting.

Examples:

  • Nonprofit bylaws: A community organization’s bylaws specify that the board has 9 members elected annually, meetings occur quarterly with 60 days’ notice, and major expenditures over $5,000 require board approval.
  • Corporate bylaws: A corporation’s bylaws specify director election procedures, shareholder meeting notice requirements, quorum rules, and the powers of various officers.
  • University bylaws: Faculty governance bylaws specify how faculty committees are formed, how decisions are made, and what procedural protections apply to personnel decisions.

Relationship to other documents: Bylaws are always subordinate to a higher document (charter, constitution, or articles of incorporation). They cannot override the higher document. If a bylaw contradicts an article of incorporation, the article governs. If a corporation’s bylaws specify that shareholders need 40% presence for a quorum but the articles require 50%, the articles’ requirement applies.

¶Covenant: A Binding Agreement Between Equals

What it does. A covenant is a solemn, binding agreement between two or more parties who commit themselves to common principles or mutual obligations. Unlike a charter (granted by authority) or a contract (exchanging value), a covenant is morally and perpetually binding, emphasizing the commitments and intentions of the parties themselves rather than legal enforcement alone.

Philosophical distinction. A covenant differs from a contract in that a contract specifies an exchange of value and relies on external enforcement (law, courts), while a covenant is a promise made before witnesses (historically, often before God), relying primarily on the moral commitment and ongoing efforts of the parties to honor it. Covenants tend to be perpetual or long-duration; contracts are often time-limited and transactional.

Structural requirements.

A covenant typically contains:

  1. Preamble — why the covenant is being made, what values it serves
  2. Covenanting parties — who is entering into this covenant
  3. Mutual commitments — what each party promises to do or refrain from doing
  4. Principles — the foundational values or ideals that guide the covenant
  5. Term or perpetuity — whether the covenant is permanent or for a specified duration
  6. Enforcement and renewal — how the covenant is maintained, how disputes are handled, how it is renewed or reaffirmed
  7. Witnesses or public declaration — declaration before the community or authorities that the covenant is binding

Governance functions:

  • Legitimation through consent: A covenant between equals creates legitimacy through mutual agreement, rather than through external authority. Each party has chosen to enter the covenant.
  • Perpetual obligation: Unlike a contract (which expires), a covenant is understood as perpetual or very long-term, creating stability and commitment over generations.
  • Principle-driven governance: Covenants emphasize shared principles and values rather than detailed procedural rules, allowing flexibility in implementation while maintaining core commitments.
  • Moral binding: A covenant relies on the honor and commitment of the parties; violation is understood as a breach of trust and principle, not merely a legal default.

Examples:

  • Magna Carta (1215): King John agreed under pressure to observe written limits on his power and to uphold rights of barons and freemen. While King John repudiated it, subsequent reissues transformed it into a foundational covenant of English governance.
  • Social contract theory: Philosophers like Hobbes, Locke, and Rousseau described political society as a covenant or agreement among citizens to create government in exchange for protection.
  • Haudenosaunee Confederacy and the Great Law of Peace: The Great Law of Peace is a covenant among the six nations of the Haudenosaunee, binding them to consensus decision-making, the Seventh Generation principle, and mutual support. Each nation is autonomous in internal affairs but covenanted in external relations.
  • Church covenants: Many congregational churches are constituted by a covenant among members, binding them to mutual support, accountability, and shared discipline.

Relationship to confederacy: In the confederacy framework, covenants bind together the member groups as equals. The covenant specifies shared directives that all members follow, but it does not create a superior governing body. Each member group retains autonomy in internal affairs and can withdraw (though this is usually rare and considered grave).

¶Treaty / Compact: Agreement Between Sovereign or Autonomous Entities

What it does. A treaty is a binding agreement between two or more sovereign states (or in some contexts, between autonomous communities or organizations). Treaties establish mutual obligations, rights, and procedures that the parties agree are binding under international law.

Definition. The Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (1969) defines a treaty as “an international agreement concluded between States in written form and governed by international law, whether embodied in a single instrument or in two or more related instruments and whatever its particular designation.” Other names for similar instruments include convention, protocol, covenant, accord, charter, and pact, but the legal binding status depends on the parties’ intent, not the naming.

Structural requirements.

A treaty typically contains:

  1. Preamble — the parties involved, the date, the purpose and intent
  2. Substantive articles — the specific obligations, rights, and procedures each party commits to
  3. Definition of terms — clarification of key concepts used in the treaty
  4. Dispute resolution — procedures for handling disagreements about compliance
  5. Ratification or entry into force — when and how the treaty becomes binding (often requiring each party’s internal approval process)
  6. Duration — whether the treaty is perpetual or terminates at a specified time
  7. Withdrawal provisions — under what conditions a party can withdraw
  8. Amendments — how the treaty can be modified

Governance functions:

  • Inter-sovereignty coordination: Treaties create binding obligations between entities that have no superior authority over them. They are the primary mechanism for international governance.
  • Rule-making between equals: Treaties allow autonomous entities to establish common rules without surrendering sovereignty or creating a superior authority.
  • Dispute resolution: Treaties typically specify how disputes about interpretation or compliance will be handled (negotiation, arbitration, courts).
  • Perpetual obligation: Like covenants, treaties are often perpetual or long-term, creating stable expectations across administrations and generations.

Examples:

  • Treaty of Westphalia (1648): Ended the Thirty Years’ War and established the principle of sovereign equality among states.
  • United Nations Charter (1945): Treaty establishing the UN as an organization and specifying member rights, duties, and procedures.
  • Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (1968): Multilateral treaty binding signatories to not develop or transfer nuclear weapons (except for the five permanent UN Security Council members, who agreed to disarm).
  • International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (1966): Treaty binding signatories to protect specified human rights.
  • Tribal treaties: Treaties between indigenous nations and colonial/national governments (often violated but still binding in principle).

Distinctions from charter, constitution, and covenant:

  • Unlike a charter (granted by external authority), a treaty is negotiated between equal parties.
  • Unlike a constitution (governing a single entity), a treaty governs the relationship between multiple entities, each retaining autonomy.
  • Like a covenant, a treaty emphasizes the binding commitment of the parties; unlike a covenant, treaties operate in a formal legal framework and rely on international law for interpretation and enforcement.

¶Cooperative Constitutions: Democracy, Equity, and Mutual Aid

What it does. Cooperative constitutions are founding documents of member-owned organizations (cooperatives) that establish democratic control, member equity in capital and surpluses, and commitment to inter-cooperation and community benefit. Cooperative constitutions blend features of corporate articles (establishing legal entity and governance structure) with constitutional principles (declaring foundational values) and covenant language (emphasizing mutual commitment).

Origins. The Rochdale Pioneers’ cooperative, established in 1844 in Lancashire, England, created the first systematized cooperative constitution. Their principles—democratic control, open membership, member economic participation, autonomy, and concern for community—became the template for cooperatives worldwide.

Structural requirements.

Cooperative constitutions typically contain:

  1. Cooperative identity and principles — statement that the organization operates as a cooperative and commits to specified cooperative principles
  2. Open membership — who can join, how membership is acquired, any limitations
  3. Democratic control — voting structure (typically one member, one vote in primary cooperatives), election of board, member authority
  4. Member capital participation — how members invest capital, what return they receive, limitations on capital accumulation
  5. Surplus distribution — how the cooperative’s profits/surpluses are distributed (often: first to serve member needs, then to pay a limited dividend on capital, remaining to community benefit or reinvestment)
  6. Autonomy — commitment to self-governance and cooperation with other cooperatives but independence from external control
  7. Education and training — commitment to member education in cooperative principles and practice
  8. Community benefit — commitment to benefiting the broader community beyond members

Governance functions:

  • Member ownership and control: Unlike corporations (where owners are investors and control follows investment), cooperatives establish that members own and control the organization regardless of investment size.
  • Equity: Cooperative constitutions embed principles of equitable participation and benefit distribution, often specifying limits on inequality.
  • Democratic participation: Decision-making power is distributed among members, with protection against concentration of power.
  • Long-term community commitment: Cooperatives commit to serving their members and communities over generations, not maximizing profit for outside investors.

Examples:

Rochdale Pioneers (1844): The original cooperative established a constitution based on:

  • Open membership to any person of good character
  • Democratic control: one member, one vote
  • Limited dividend on capital: investment in the cooperative earned a modest return, but not the primary benefit
  • Surplus distribution: profits returned to members in proportion to purchases, or used for community benefit
  • Education: commitment to teaching members about cooperation

Mondragon Cooperative Corporation (founded 1956): The Mondragon group in Spain established a cooperative federation based on:

  • Sovereignty of labor: work, not capital, is the fundamental principle
  • Democratic management: worker members elect the board and make major decisions
  • Payment solidarity: wage ratios are limited (often 6:1 between highest and lowest paid), reducing inequality
  • Inter-cooperation: individual worker cooperatives cooperate with each other and support new cooperative formation
  • Community transformation: commitment to building cooperative alternatives to capitalist structures

International Cooperative Alliance Statement on Cooperative Identity (1995): Formalized the seven principles of cooperation:

  1. Voluntary and open membership
  2. Democratic member control
  3. Member economic participation
  4. Autonomy and independence
  5. Education, training, and information
  6. Cooperation among cooperatives
  7. Concern for community

Relationship to other documents: Cooperative constitutions function like a hybrid of articles of incorporation and bylaws: they establish the legal entity (like articles) while also declaring foundational principles (like a constitution). Many cooperatives file articles of incorporation establishing their legal form, then adopt a constitution specifying their cooperative principles. Some jurisdictions have specific legal frameworks for cooperatives that build these principles into law.

¶Part 2: Universal Governance Functions

Across all seven document types, certain governance functions must be addressed by any founding document that seeks to constitute a legitimate, operational organization or arrangement. These are not type-specific; rather, they are the functions that governance itself requires.

¶1. Constitution and Legitimacy

The first and most fundamental function: the document must constitute the organization or arrangement. It must declare what entity is being created, who its members/subjects/parties are, and by what authority (external grant, internal consent, mutual agreement) the entity comes into being.

Without a constituting document, there is no organization—merely people gathered together. The document is what makes the gathering into an entity with standing, authority, and legitimacy.

Examples across types:

  • A charter constitutes by external grant: “I, the King, hereby grant unto the merchants of London…”
  • A constitution constitutes by the declaration of principles by an already-existing community: “We, the People of the United States, do ordain and establish this Constitution…”
  • Articles of incorporation constitute by registration with the state: the moment the articles are filed and accepted, the corporation comes into legal being.
  • A covenant constitutes by mutual agreement: “We do hereby covenant together to…”

¶2. Membership and Standing

The document must specify who belongs to the organization and what standing they have. This includes:

  • Membership categories: Are there different classes of members (voting vs. non-voting, full vs. associate, etc.)?
  • Admission: How do new members join? Is membership automatic, by application, by invitation?
  • Member rights: What can members do? Vote, hold office, benefit from services, own equity?
  • Member duties: What are members obligated to do? Pay dues, attend meetings, follow rules, contribute labor?
  • Removal: Can members be removed or expelled? Under what conditions and by what process?

Examples:

  • A charter might specify: “Membership shall be limited to merchants trading in the specified goods.”
  • Articles of incorporation for a corporation specify share ownership and voting rights attached to shares.
  • A cooperative constitution specifies: “Any person engaged in the economic activity served by the cooperative may apply for membership.”
  • Ship’s articles specify the crew: captain, officers, ordinary seamen, and specify what each is obligated to do.

¶3. Authority and Power Distribution

The document must specify who has power to make binding decisions and how that power is distributed. This includes:

  • Executive authority: Who executes decisions, implements policy, directs operations? (A captain, a CEO, a president, a council?)
  • Legislative/deliberative authority: Who makes rules and policies? (An assembly, a board, the membership?)
  • Judicial/dispute authority: Who resolves conflicts and interprets the document? (A judge, an arbitrator, a board, an assembly?)
  • Checks and balances: What limits exist on any one person or body’s power? Can power be removed? Can decisions be overridden?
  • Delegation: Can those holding authority delegate it to others? Under what conditions?

Examples:

  • A constitution typically separates power: legislature makes laws, executive enforces them, judiciary interprets them.
  • A charter might concentrate power: “The captain shall have absolute authority to command the ship.”
  • Bylaws specify: “The board shall consist of 7 directors elected by the membership for 3-year terms, with not more than 2 consecutive terms.”
  • Ship’s articles specify: “The captain is the supreme authority aboard ship and may enforce regulations and impose penalties for violations.”

¶4. Decision-Making Procedures

The document must specify how decisions are actually made: who decides, what information is required, what notice must be given, what threshold of agreement is needed, and how decisions are recorded.

  • Who decides: Is it a single authority, a committee, a full assembly, or some combination?
  • Notice and deliberation: What warning must be given before a decision? What time for discussion?
  • Voting/consent: What level of agreement is required? Unanimity, supermajority, simple majority, consensus?
  • Recording: How are decisions documented and made publicly available?
  • Binding force: How is a decision made binding? By whose proclamation? By recording? By implementation?

Examples:

  • A constitution might specify: “A law shall not be enacted except by vote of the legislature recorded in the public record.”
  • Bylaws specify: “Meetings shall have 30 days’ notice, a quorum of 40% of members, and decisions shall pass by majority vote and be recorded in meeting minutes.”
  • A covenant might specify: “All decisions shall be by consensus of the covenanting parties; if consensus cannot be reached, the matter is deferred until it can.”
  • Ship’s articles specify: “The captain’s orders are final and binding; disputes shall be heard by the captain and adjudicated by the captain’s judgment.”

¶5. Rights and Protections

The document must declare what members/subjects/parties can count on: what cannot be taken from them without due process, what they are guaranteed, what protections exist against arbitrary power.

  • Fundamental rights: What cannot be violated? (Property rights, freedom of conscience, due process, etc.)
  • Due process: If someone is to be punished or deprived of something, what procedures must be followed?
  • Remedy for violation: If a right is violated, how can the person seek redress?
  • Limits on power: Are there things no authority can do, regardless of how they would otherwise be authorized?

Examples:

  • A constitution typically declares: “No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law.”
  • Bylaws specify: “No officer can be removed without a hearing and a vote of the board.”
  • A charter might declare: “Members of the guild shall have the right to practice their trade within the town.”
  • A covenant might specify: “Each party shall be treated with dignity and shall not be subject to unilateral exclusion.”

¶6. Amendment and Stability

The document must specify how it can be changed and what threshold of agreement is required. This balances two needs: stability (the document should not be constantly rewritten) and flexibility (circumstances change and the document must be able to adapt).

  • Amendment procedure: What process must be followed to change the document?
  • Who decides: Can any person/body amend it, or only certain authorities?
  • Threshold: What level of agreement is required? Unanimity, supermajority, simple majority?
  • Core protection: Are there provisions that cannot be amended, or are all provisions amendable?
  • Frequency: How often can amendments occur? Are there limits to prevent constant revision?

Examples:

  • The U.S. Constitution requires amendment by two-thirds vote in both houses of Congress (or a convention called by two-thirds of state legislatures) plus ratification by three-fourths of states—an extremely high bar.
  • Bylaws can often be amended by a simple majority of the board or membership, making them much more flexible than constitutions.
  • A charter granted by external authority often specifies that the authority can amend it (the sovereign power that granted the charter can change it), but the chartered organization cannot unilaterally amend it.
  • A covenant might require unanimous consent for amendment, reflecting the principle that the binding agreement between equals can only be changed by mutual agreement.

¶7. Dissolution and Succession

The document must specify what happens if the organization ends: what happens to its assets, what obligations continue, how the dissolution is managed.

  • Triggers for dissolution: What events cause the organization to dissolve? (Bankruptcy, unanimous vote, expiration of charter, loss of quorum, external conquest?)
  • Asset distribution: If the organization owns property or money, who receives it when it dissolves? (Members, creditors, the public, the state?)
  • Debt and obligation: Who is responsible for unpaid debts? (The organization if it still has assets, the members personally, no one?)
  • Records and history: What documents are preserved? How is the organization’s history recorded?
  • Successor organization: Is there an entity that continues the organization’s mission, or does it simply cease to exist?

Examples:

  • A constitution typically specifies what happens to state property if the government dissolves (usually it is held in trust for the people).
  • Bylaws specify: “Upon dissolution, the organization’s assets shall be distributed to members in proportion to their capital contributions, provided debts are first paid.”
  • A charter granted by a sovereign might specify: “If the chartered body dissolves, the charter reverts to the Crown.”
  • A cooperative constitution often specifies: “Upon dissolution, assets shall not be distributed to members but shall pass to another cooperative or be distributed to the community.”

¶Part 3: Synthesis and Relationships

¶The Hierarchy of Documents

In most governance systems, founding documents exist in a hierarchy where higher documents constrain lower ones. The pattern is typically:

  1. Charter or Constitution (highest level, granted by external authority or declared by the people)
  2. Articles of incorporation or association (legal establishment of the entity)
  3. Bylaws (procedural rules for operation)
  4. Policies and standing resolutions (administrative details)

In this hierarchy:

  • A bylaw cannot contradict an article of incorporation
  • Articles cannot contradict a charter
  • A charter cannot contradict a covenant between equals (because the covenant is the source of the charter’s authority)

¶Founding Documents for Different Organizational Types

Sovereign states: Constitute themselves through a constitution (often written but sometimes unwritten like the UK), which establishes their governmental structure and fundamental laws. States may be parties to treaties that create obligations.

Corporations: Are created by articles of incorporation filed with the state, operate under bylaws, and may be bound by charters if they are chartered corporations (less common now but still exists for some industries).

Cooperatives: Operate under a constitution establishing cooperative principles and a legal structure (either as a registered cooperative under cooperative law, or as a corporation filing articles that declare its cooperative status).

Ships and maritime vessels: Operate under a charter issued by the flag state (the sovereign that registers the ship), with articles of agreement establishing the employment contract with the crew.

Confederacies: Are bound together by a covenant or directive structure; they charter individual entities (ships, projects) that operate under that higher covenant.

International organizations: Are created by treaties that bind member states to support the organization and follow its procedures. The UN is an example.

Churches and religious organizations: Often constitute themselves through both a formal legal charter (articles of incorporation) and an internal covenant among members.

¶When Documents Conflict

Real governance situations often have conflicts between documents or ambiguities about which document governs. Principles for resolving these:

  1. Higher documents constrain lower ones: If bylaws contradict articles, articles win. If articles contradict a charter, the charter wins.
  2. Explicit override higher than implicit: If a document explicitly addresses a situation, it governs over documents that are silent on it.
  3. Original authority determines hierarchy: The document issued by higher authority ranks above documents issued by lower authority.
  4. Consent structures (covenants, treaties) typically require that amendments be by the consent of the parties, not by unilateral authority.

¶The Template: What Every Founding Document Must Do

If we extract the common pattern across all seven types, a founding document must:

  1. Constitute an entity or arrangement with standing and legitimacy
  2. Specify membership and the standing of members
  3. Distribute authority and specify who has power to decide
  4. Establish procedures for decision-making that are legitimate and predictable
  5. Declare rights and protections that constrain arbitrary power
  6. Enable amendment with sufficient stability and flexibility
  7. Provide for dissolution and succession

Any document that addresses all seven of these functions, adapted to its specific context, can serve as a founding document. The seven types examined here are variations on this template, shaped by their origins (external grant vs. internal consent), their scope (a single organization vs. arrangements between organizations), and their reliance on external vs. internal enforcement.
