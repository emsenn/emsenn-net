2026-04-05 The trajectory by which a piece of work moves from open inquiry to deterministic process as its meaning and execution become progressively settled.

A settlement path is the trajectory by which a piece of work moves from open inquiry to deterministic process. As questions get answered and methods get encoded, two dimensions settle independently: what the work produces (its meaning) and how it produces it (its execution). The path through these two dimensions defines the settlement.

The four work types mark positions on this path:

Execution settled Execution open
Meaning settled Process Procedure
Meaning open Derivation Inquiry

Settlement is not a single step but a series of transitions. A research question (inquiry) produces a finding that settles the meaning, yielding a derivation. Encoding the derivation into a conditional workflow settles the execution under known conditions, yielding a procedure. Removing the remaining conditions — handling all edge cases, formalizing all branch points — yields a process.

The concept draws on John Dewey’s theory of inquiry as the transformation of an indeterminate situation into a determinate one (Logic: The Theory of Inquiry, 1938). Dewey’s inquiry terminates when the situation is settled — the doubt that initiated it has been resolved. Settlement path extends this: the termination of inquiry is not the end of the work’s evolution but a waypoint. The settled inquiry becomes a derivation, which can itself be further settled into procedure and then process.

The connection to closure operators is structural, not metaphorical. Each settlement step applies rules that make implicit knowledge explicit — adding what is missing until nothing new appears. The closure of an inquiry under its resolution rules is a derivation. The closure of a derivation under its procedural encoding is a procedure. The closure of a procedure under its edge-case handling is a process. The full settlement path is the iterated closure of the work under progressively stronger completion rules.

Settlement is directional but not inevitable. Work can stall at any stage — an inquiry that never produces a finding, a procedure that never gets its edge cases formalized. Work can also regress: a process breaks when its assumptions change, reverting to procedure or inquiry. The path describes a tendency, not a guarantee. What makes it a useful concept is that it identifies where infrastructure investment produces returns: moving work one step along the settlement path reduces the judgment required to perform it, which is the core claim of progressive automation.
