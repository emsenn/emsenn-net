Gap

2026-03-20 A Gap is the structural distance between a claim's current settlement and its affirming-determination at a given history. The diagnostic of incompleteness in normative and operational systems: every unsettled claim has a gap, and that gap is what drives the work toward settlement.

¶What this is

A Gap is the structural distance between a claim’s current settlement and its affirming-determination at a given history. Every claim that has not yet reached the affirming corner of settlement has a gap: the claim is somewhere in the joint space of evidence, and the gap measures how much further it has to go.

The gap is not a failure — it is a position. Every non-settled claim has a gap; that gap is the productive tension (the Tension) that drives the work toward settled determination. Without gap, there is nothing to settle.

¶Two components of a gap

A claim’s gap has two structurally distinct components, corresponding to the two axes of the framework’s settlement structure:

  • The knowledge gap — the distance from the claim’s current evidence-amount to the affirming-determination position on the knowledge axis. A claim with a large knowledge gap has not yet been sufficiently witnessed; more evidence is needed.
  • The truth gap — the distance from the claim’s current evidence-direction tilt to the affirming pole of the truth axis. A claim with a large truth gap has been witnessed but the witnessings have not converged on affirmation.

A claim is at zero gap when both components are at the affirming-determination corner: fully witnessed AND tilted toward affirmation.

A claim can have a zero knowledge gap and a positive truth gap (the witnessings have accumulated but they tilt toward denial — the claim is not just unsettled but actively contraindicated) or a zero truth gap and a positive knowledge gap (the available witnessings tilt toward affirmation but more evidence is needed for full settlement).

¶Gap and discharge

Discharge of a duty is the event of reaching zero gap on both the performance and recognition predicates: the act has been done (knowledge axis on performance) and acknowledged (knowledge axis on recognition), and both witnessings tilt affirmingly. Before discharge, at least one predicate has positive gap. The discharge event is the transition from positive gap to zero gap on both predicates simultaneously.

¶Gap and friction

Friction in a normative system is the failure of the system’s own dynamics to preserve settlement: a claim that was at zero gap at one history may, at a later history, exhibit a positive gap because new evidence has unsettled it, or because the criteria for settlement have shifted. Friction is the introduction of gap by the system’s own evolution. A frictionless system would preserve settled claims indefinitely; real systems often do not.

¶Gap as measure of institutional compliance

In a NormativeSystem, the compliance of an agent’s output is the gap of that output relative to the system’s required settlement. A fully compliant output has zero gap. An obligation is discharged iff the agent’s performance has reached zero gap at the required history. The norm system is satisfied when all obligated outputs are at zero gap.

Most institutional work is the work of closing gaps — accumulating witnessings, aligning evidence direction, shepherding partially-settled claims toward full determination. The presence of gap is not a defect of the system but the natural condition of any system whose work is ongoing.

¶Single-component vs two-component gaps

A claim with a positive gap on only one component (e.g., zero knowledge gap but positive truth gap) is, in some sense, closer to settlement than a claim with positive gaps on both. Closing a single-component gap is conceptually simpler: only one kind of work is needed (more witnessing in the case of a knowledge gap; conflict-resolution in the case of a truth gap). A claim with two-component gap requires both kinds of work in sequence or in parallel.

Single-component gap closure is the typical structure of mature institutional work: most claims are well-witnessed (knowledge axis high) but their truth-axis position needs adjudication. The work of governance is largely the work of closing truth-axis gaps in claims whose knowledge-axis position is already adequate.

¶Gap as a quality

Gap is a Quality of claims: a structural feature that varies from claim to claim and changes as the work progresses. As a quality, gap is comparable across claims (one claim has a larger gap than another), measurable in principle, and addressable through specific kinds of work (more witnessing reduces knowledge gap; conflict resolution reduces truth gap).

The gap quality differs from substantive qualities (color, weight, kind) in that it tracks the relation between the claim and its target settlement, not an intrinsic property of the claim itself. The same claim can have a large gap in one system and a small gap in another, depending on the surrounding witnessings and the system’s settlement criteria.

¶Open questions

  • Whether the two gap components (knowledge gap and truth gap) admit a single combined measure that respects both — or whether they are genuinely orthogonal, with no canonical way to compare a claim with high knowledge gap to a claim with high truth gap.
  • Whether gap-closure has bounded rate in real institutional systems — whether there is a maximum rate at which knowledge gap can be reduced (witnessings come in at finite rate) or truth gap can be resolved (conflicts take real time to adjudicate), and whether these rates are universal or system-dependent.
  • Whether some claims have irreducible gap — claims whose knowledge or truth gap cannot be closed by any witnessing the system can perform, because the relevant evidence is structurally inaccessible. If such claims exist, the institution must learn to operate with permanent gap on them rather than treating gap as universally closable.
  • The formal relationship between gap and friction — whether friction is best understood as the introduction of gap, the failure to close gap, or both, and whether the distinction matters for designing institutions that minimize friction.
