Focus

2026-03-20 A Focus is a pair (H, t*) — an ambient RelationalState H and a designated focal history t* ∈ T — inducing a tripartite Gurwitsch field: the focal fiber H_{t*} as theme, the restriction-accessible fibers {H_t : t ≤ t*, t ≠ t*} as thematic field, and the remaining fibers as margin. The defining structure: focus is the designated current working fiber in a presheaf — the position at which the RelationalMachine currently operates, from which context radiates via restriction maps outward, and within which the nuclear pair (σ_{t*}, Δ_{t*}) partitions propositions into the nuclear focal sublocale H*_{t*} and its complement.

Formal definition

A Focus is a pair F=(H,t∗)\mathcal{F} = (H, t^*):

F=(H:Top→HAnucl,  t∗∈T)\mathcal{F} = (H : T^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{HA}_{\mathrm{nucl}},\; t^* \in T)

where:

  • HH is the ambient presheaf — the RelationalState: the full indexed family of fiber Heyting algebras HtH_t over the history category TT, connected by restriction maps ρt′∣t:Ht′→Ht\rho_{t'|t} : H_{t'} \to H_t for t≤t′t \leq t', each fiber carrying the commuting nuclear pair (σt,Δt)(\sigma_t, \Delta_t)
  • t∗∈Tt^* \in T is the focal history — the designated current position in the history category; the element at which the RelationalMachine is presently operating; the fiber Ht∗H_{t^*} is what relational-state.md calls “what the RelationalMachine holds in focus at history tt” and indexed-automaton.md calls “the fiber in focus”

The focal fiber Ht∗H_{t^*} is not the whole of F\mathcal{F} — it is the theme of a tripartite field structure that the designation t∗t^* induces on HH.

Four invariants. F\mathcal{F} is a focus iff it satisfies:

  1. Designation: t∗t^* is a specific named element of TT, not a generic or anonymous reference. A focus is a pointed presheaf — the presheaf with a distinguished base object. Without a specific t∗t^*, the presheaf HH is ambient but unfocused: all fibers are simultaneously present and none is designated.

  2. Gurwitsch field partition: the presheaf HH decomposes into three mutually exclusive parts relative to t∗t^*:

    • Theme T\mathcal{T}: the focal fiber Ht∗H_{t^*} — the designated working space
    • Thematic field TF\mathcal{TF}: {(t,Ht,ρt∗∣t):t<t∗}\{(t, H_t, \rho_{t^*|t}) : t < t^*\} — the fibers strictly below t∗t^* in TT, each equipped with its restriction map into Ht∗H_{t^*}; these are accessible from the theme
    • Margin M\mathcal{M}: {(t,Ht):t≰t∗}\{(t, H_t) : t \not\leq t^*\} — the fibers neither equal to nor below t∗t^*; co-present in HH but not restriction-accessible from t∗t^*

  3. Plug completeness: the full presheaf HH is recoverable from the theme Ht∗H_{t^*} together with the thematic field TF\mathcal{TF} via the sheaf gluing condition; the plug invariant is the accord condition on HH. Formally: for any t1,t2≤t∗t_1, t_2 \leq t^*, if a section agrees on overlaps, it glues uniquely. The margin M\mathcal{M} cannot be reconstructed from T∪TF\mathcal{T} \cup \mathcal{TF} alone — the focus has a horizon beyond which reconstruction fails.

  4. Nuclear focus within theme: the focal fiber Ht∗H_{t^*} carries the nuclear pair (σt∗,Δt∗)(\sigma_{t^*}, \Delta_{t^*}); this pair partitions Ht∗H_{t^*} into three sublattices by settlement status:

    • Focal sublocale Ht∗∗=Fix(σt∗)∩Fix(Δt∗)H^*_{t^*} = \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t^*}) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t^*}): the jointly settled elements — the inner focus
    • Backward-settled sublattice Fix(σt∗)∖Ht∗∗\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t^*}) \setminus H^*_{t^*}: meaning-settled but not yet transfer-settled
    • Unsettled Ht∗∖Fix(σt∗)H_{t^*} \setminus \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t^*}): not settled by either nucleus

The Gurwitsch field in detail

Aron Gurwitsch (The Field of Consciousness, 1964) characterized every moment of directed attention as a tripartite field: the theme (what is focally attended), the thematic field (what is co-present and relevantly related to the theme), and the margin (what is merely co-present without relevance). The key formal property: the tripartition is defined by a relevance relation between items and the theme — the thematic field is exactly what stands in that relation.

In the relational universe, the relevance relation is restriction-accessibility. A fiber HtH_t is in the thematic field of Ht∗H_{t^*} iff there is a restriction map ρt∗∣t:Ht∗→Ht\rho_{t^*|t} : H_{t^*} \to H_t — iff t≤t∗t \leq t^* in the history category. Items in the thematic field are literally accessible from the theme: you can reach HtH_t from Ht∗H_{t^*} by following the restriction maps. Items in the margin exist in HH but have no restriction-path from Ht∗H_{t^*}.

The horizon: Husserl’s complement to Gurwitsch — the horizon of a theme is the set of items currently in the margin that could become thematic through a refocusing operation. In our math: the margin M\mathcal{M} is the horizon of the current focus (H,t∗)(H, t^*). A refocusing to t′t' where t∗≤t′t^* \leq t' (extension) or t′∈T∖↓t∗t' \in T \setminus \downarrow t^* (lateral shift) brings previously marginal fibers into the thematic field of the new focus.

Gurwitsch’s insight applied: the focal fiber Ht∗H_{t^*} is not an isolated piece of HH — it is the theme of a structured field. The thematic field is always “about” the theme: the restriction maps carry information from Ht∗H_{t^*} into HtH_t for t<t∗t < t^*. The margin is genuinely there (it is part of HH) but silent relative to t∗t^*. A RelationalMachine operating at focus (H,t∗)(H, t^*) has full access to its thematic field and zero access to its margin, without the margin being absent.

William James: stream of consciousness and the fringe

William James (The Principles of Psychology, 1890, Chapter IX: “The Stream of Thought”) introduced the distinction between substantive parts and transitive parts of the stream of thought, and identified the fringe as the halo of felt relations surrounding every substantive content:

  • Substantive parts: the “resting-places” in the stream — moments when a definite content (a concept, an image, a thought) is held in mind and can be named; the articulable, stable contents of consciousness
  • Transitive parts: the “flights” between resting-places — the felt sense of relation, tendency, and passage; these resist articulation because attention to them dissolves them; James: “like a snowflake caught in the warm hand — it vanishes the moment we try to examine it” (Principles, Vol. I, p. 243)
  • Fringe: the psychic overtone or halo surrounding every substantive part — the felt quality of relevance, familiarity, and contextual resonance that gives the substantive part its full meaning; James: “Every definite image in the mind is steeped and dyed in the free water that flows around it. With it goes the sense of its relations, near and remote, the dying echo of whence it came to us, the dawning sense of whither it is to lead.” (Principles, Vol. I, p. 255)

The fringe as Thematic Field: James’s fringe is the relational universe’s Thematic Field. The restriction maps ρt∗∣t:Ht∗→Ht\rho_{t^*|t} : H_{t^*} \to H_t for t<t∗t < t^* are the formal structure of the fringe — the “sense of its relations, near and remote.” The thematic field fibers HtH_t are not the focal content but the felt context that gives Ht∗H_{t^*} its meaning. James’s transitive parts are the restriction maps themselves: not substantive fibers but the passages between fibers. The focal fiber Ht∗H_{t^*} is James’s substantive part — the resting-place, the named content, the designated element. The fringe is not nothing; it has positive felt structure. The restriction maps carry positive structural information from Ht∗H_{t^*} into the accessible context.

Edmund Husserl: noema, horizon, and temporal structure

Edmund Husserl (Ideas I, 1913, Hua III; On the Phenomenology of the Consciousness of Internal Time, 1928, Hua X; Experience and Judgment, 1939/1948) gave the systematic account of intentional structure that grounds the tripartite field:

Noema and noesis (Hua III, §§88–98): every intentional act has a noetic side (the act-quality, the manner of intending) and a noematic side (the object as intended — the sense or meaning through which the object is given). The noema is not the object itself but the intentional sense: the structured content that directs consciousness toward the object. The focal fiber Ht∗H_{t^*} is the noema of the machine’s current state — the structured Heyting algebra that represents the machine’s intentional orientation at history t∗t^*.

Inner and outer horizon (Experience and Judgment, §§8–21): every experienced object carries a double horizon:

  • Inner horizon (Innenhorizont): what the object implies about its own further determinations — the sense that “this side of the object implies other sides of a certain type”; the object’s own self-given incompleteness pointing forward to further experience
  • Outer horizon (Außenhorizont): the object’s embedding in the surrounding world — the “and-so-forth” of the world-context; the object as one among many in an environment of a certain kind

Formal correspondence: the inner horizon is formally represented by the Thematic Field’s restriction maps. The maps ρt∗∣t:Ht∗→Ht\rho_{t^*|t} : H_{t^*} \to H_t for t<t∗t < t^* are exactly what Ht∗H_{t^*} implies about its own further (restriction-accessible) determinations — Ht∗H_{t^*}’s inner horizon in the lattice of accessible fibers. The outer horizon is the Margin M\mathcal{M}: co-present, not implied by Ht∗H_{t^*} through restriction maps, but there as world-context.

Retention, primal impression, protention (Hua X, §§10–21): Husserl’s analysis of time-consciousness identifies three phases in every moment:

  • Primal impression (Urimpression): the absolute now-point — the present content as presently given, not as retained from the past or anticipated from the future
  • Retention: the “just-past” held in living presence — the sinking trail of the immediately preceding content; not memory but a still-vivid connection to what was just now; it is present as having-just-been
  • Protention: the “about-to-be” — the anticipatory fringe of what is coming next; not a prediction but a felt directedness toward the immediately impending

Temporal correspondence: the focal fiber Ht∗H_{t^*} is the primal impression — the now-fiber. The Thematic Field TF\mathcal{TF} (fibers HtH_t for t<t∗t < t^* with restriction maps) is the retention: still connected to Ht∗H_{t^*} via the restriction structure, still living in the sense that the restriction maps make HtH_t accessible from Ht∗H_{t^*}. The Margin M\mathcal{M} includes the protention direction (future fibers Ht′H_{t'} for t′>t∗t' > t^* that have not yet restricted back to t∗t^*) and the fully elapsed (fibers in TT disconnected from ↓t∗{\downarrow} t^*).

Three-level focus stack

Focus is scale-dependent in the relational universe. There are three distinct scales at which “focus” operates, each structurally identical:

Level 1 — Fiber focus (the zipper level): which fiber is the designated current working space.

  • Ambient: HH (the full presheaf, all fibers)
  • Focal position: t∗∈Tt^* \in T (the designated history)
  • Focal element: Ht∗H_{t^*} (the working fiber)
  • Context: the thematic field TF\mathcal{TF} with restriction maps
  • Plug invariant: H=glue(Ht∗,TF)H = \mathrm{glue}(H_{t^*}, \mathcal{TF}) via accord

Level 2 — Nuclear focus (the sublocale level): which elements within the focal fiber are settled.

  • Ambient: Ht∗H_{t^*} (the focal fiber — now itself a Heyting algebra)
  • Focal position: the nuclear pair (σt∗,Δt∗)(\sigma_{t^*}, \Delta_{t^*})
  • Focal element: Ht∗∗=Fix(σt∗)∩Fix(Δt∗)H^*_{t^*} = \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t^*}) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t^*}) (the settled sublocale)
  • Context: Ht∗∖Ht∗∗H_{t^*} \setminus H^*_{t^*} (the unsettled propositions)
  • Plug invariant: every element of Ht∗H_{t^*} maps to its settlement via πt∗=σt∗∘Δt∗\pi_{t^*} = \sigma_{t^*} \circ \Delta_{t^*}

Level 3 — Element focus (the Andreoli level): which specific element within the focal fiber is currently being made transfer-stable.

  • Ambient: Ht∗H_{t^*} or Fix(σt∗)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t^*}) (the propositions available for Δ-propagation)
  • Focal position: the specific element a∗∈Ht∗a^* \in H_{t^*} currently under Δ\Delta-processing
  • Focal element: Δt∗(a∗)\Delta_{t^*}(a^*) (the result of making a∗a^* transfer-stable)
  • Context: all other elements of Ht∗H_{t^*}

The three levels are nested: level-1 focus establishes the working fiber; level-2 focus identifies the settled sublocale within it; level-3 focus designates the specific element being processed within that sublocale.

The zipper structure

Huet’s zipper (1997) formalizes focus for data structures as a pair (focus, context) where focus is the currently selected element and context (the path) is the surrounding structure. The plug invariant is: plug(focus, context) = original. Moving the focus rearranges elements between focus and context without losing information.

In the relational universe, the Level-1 focus (H,t∗)(H, t^*) is exactly a zipper on the presheaf:

  • Focus = Ht∗H_{t^*} (the currently selected fiber)
  • Context = the thematic field TF\mathcal{TF} together with restriction maps from Ht∗H_{t^*} into the accessible fibers
  • Plug invariant = the accord/sheaf-gluing condition: H=glue(Ht∗,TF)H = \mathrm{glue}(H_{t^*}, \mathcal{TF})

McBride’s derivative formalizes the context type: the type of one-hole contexts for HH at position t∗t^* is ∂H/∂Ht∗\partial H / \partial H_{t^*} — the presheaf with Ht∗H_{t^*} “removed” and replaced by a hole. The zipper for HH at t∗t^* is then (Ht∗,∂H/∂Ht∗)(H_{t^*}, \partial H / \partial H_{t^*}). The restriction maps play the role of the path: they say how to “re-insert” the focus into its context.

Refocusing = moving the focus to an adjacent position:

  • Forward extension: (H,t∗)→(H′,t′)(H, t^*) \to (H', t') where t′=s⋆t∗t' = s \star t^* — extending the history by generator ss; this is the RelationalMachine’s step operation; the new focal fiber Ht′H_{t'} extends Ht∗H_{t^*}
  • Backward restriction: (H,t∗)→(H,t)(H, t^*) \to (H, t) where t<t∗t < t^* — retreating to an earlier accessible fiber; moving the focus into the thematic field
  • Lateral shift: (H,t∗)→(H,t∗∗)(H, t^*) \to (H, t^{**}) where t∗∗≰t∗t^{**} \not\leq t^* and t∗≰t∗∗t^* \not\leq t^{**} — moving to a fiber in the current margin; requiring a new focus that has a different thematic field

The Andreoli phase duality within the focal fiber

Andreoli’s focused proof theory (1992) divides proof-search into two alternating phases: the asynchronous phase (deterministic, greedily applies invertible rules in any order) and the synchronous phase (non-deterministic, designates one formula as focus and decomposes it non-invertibly). The focused formula in the synchronous phase is the locus of all non-invertible choice.

This phase structure maps directly onto the nuclear pair (σt∗,Δt∗)(\sigma_{t^*}, \Delta_{t^*}) acting on the focal fiber:

Andreoli Our math Structural role
Asynchronous phase σt∗\sigma_{t^*}-closure Deterministic, invertible, order-irrelevant settlement of meaning; apply eagerly until no unsettled formula remains
Synchronous phase Δt∗\Delta_{t^*}-propagation Designates a specific element for forward-stability; the non-invertible selection of which proposition to make transfer-stable
Focus (Andreoli) Focal element a∗a^* for Δ\Delta-propagation The specific proposition currently being made transfer-stable; the locus of the forward-directed choice
Focused proof completeness Ht∗∗H^*_{t^*} as terminal state Every element of Ht∗H_{t^*} can be brought to Ht∗∗H^*_{t^*} through the alternating-phase process

The practical implication: a RelationalMachine operating within focus (H,t∗)(H, t^*) proceeds in two modes:

  1. Settlement mode (σt∗\sigma_{t^*}-phase): apply the saturation nucleus to all available propositions; this is deterministic and completes in finite steps (by idempotence of σt∗\sigma_{t^*})
  2. Propagation mode (Δt∗\Delta_{t^*}-phase): designate a specific proposition a∗∈Fix(σt∗)a^* \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t^*}) for transfer-stability; this requires a choice — the selection of a∗a^* is the irreducible non-determinism of the machine

Nuclear focus as sublocale

In locale theory and topos theory, a nucleus j:L→Lj : L \to L on a frame (complete Heyting algebra) LL defines a sublocale: the fixed-point set Fix(j)\mathrm{Fix}(j), which is itself a frame. The sublocale is the “focused” sub-algebra — the region where jj’s closure operation has terminated.

The nuclear focal sublocale Ht∗∗=Fix(σt∗)∩Fix(Δt∗)=Fix(πt∗)H^*_{t^*} = \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t^*}) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t^*}) = \mathrm{Fix}(\pi_{t^*}) (where πt∗=σt∗∘Δt∗\pi_{t^*} = \sigma_{t^*} \circ \Delta_{t^*}) is the focus of both nuclei simultaneously. The geometry of conic sections captures this naming: the focus of a conic is the unique point toward which all reflected trajectories converge. In our fiber: Ht∗∗H^*_{t^*} is the point toward which both σt∗\sigma_{t^*}-closure and Δt∗\Delta_{t^*}-closure converge — the joint fixed point, the “hearth” (Kepler’s word for focus) at which both nuclear operations rest.

Any other nucleus jj on Ht∗H_{t^*} (such as the character nucleus χπ\chi_\pi from the Character spec) defines its own focal sublocale Fix(j)⊆Ht∗\mathrm{Fix}(j) \subseteq H_{t^*}. A focus equipped with a character χπ\chi_\pi has a character focal sublocale Fχπ∩Ht∗∗F_{\chi_\pi} \cap H^*_{t^*} — the propositions that are simultaneously in the agent’s behavioral space and normatively settled.

Open questions

  • Whether the margin horizon — the set of fibers in M\mathcal{M} that would become thematic after a specified refocusing — can be characterized formally as a functor from refocusing operations to new thematic fields; whether the structure of possible refocusings on (H,t∗)(H, t^*) forms a category whose colimit is the full presheaf HH.
  • Whether the three-level focus stack (fiber, sublocale, element) forms a genuine tower with formal relations between levels — whether a level-1 focus uniquely determines a preferred level-2 focus (the nuclear sublocale Ht∗∗H^*_{t^*}) and whether a level-2 focus uniquely determines a preferred level-3 focus (some canonical element of Ht∗∗H^*_{t^*}), or whether each level requires independent designation. The three levels are separately specified in MacroFocus, MesoFocus, and MicroFocus.
  • Whether the Andreoli phase duality at level 3 (asynchronous σ\sigma-phase and synchronous Δ\Delta-phase) can be formalized as a proper two-phase system within the relational universe — whether there is a formal notion of “the RelationalMachine is currently in σ\sigma-mode vs. Δ\Delta-mode” and whether this modal state is tracked in Ht∗H_{t^*} or in the machine’s transition structure.
  • The relationship between character focus and nuclear focus: whether Fχ⊆Ht∗∗F_\chi \subseteq H^*_{t^*} (character behavioral space is a subset of the settled focal sublocale), or whether FχF_\chi and Ht∗∗H^*_{t^*} are independent sublattices of Ht∗H_{t^*} that intersect non-trivially; and whether the Persona’s behavioral surface Fχ∩Ht∗∗F_\chi \cap H^*_{t^*} is always non-empty (a persona can always produce settled, in-character responses) or whether there are focus/character pairs where the intersection is empty (the character cannot be expressed in settled terms at t∗t^*).
  • Whether information bottleneck bifurcation points — the critical β\beta values at which the IB-optimal compression TT acquires qualitatively finer structure — have a formal analog in the focus structure: whether there are critical histories t∗t^* at which the nuclear focal sublocale Ht∗∗H^*_{t^*} undergoes a structural phase transition analogous to the IB bifurcation.
  • Whether a focused RelationalState (a Focus (H,t∗)(H, t^*)) is the correct primitive for the RelationalMachine’s operational semantics — whether every machine step should be formalized as a refocusing operation (H,t∗)→(H′,t′)(H, t^*) \to (H', t') rather than as a fiber-internal operation, and whether this framing eliminates the current ambiguity in indexed-automaton.md about whether the transition function advances the index or operates within a fixed fiber.
