Cadence

2026-04-03 The rhythm of recurring operational events — standups, reviews, retrospectives, planning cycles — that creates predictability and natural checkpoints across time scales.

Cadence is the rhythm of recurring operational events — the regular beats at which the operations loop runs its stages. A daily standup. A weekly review. A monthly retrospective. A quarterly planning cycle. Cadence creates predictability: everyone knows when things happen, which reduces coordination overhead.

Cadence operates at multiple time scales simultaneously:

Scale Cadence Loop stages emphasized
Turn Per agent message Tracking (environment composition)
Session Per work session All six stages within session scope
Daily Once per day Dispatch, tracking
Weekly Once per week Review, planning
Monthly Once per month Feedback, project review
Quarterly Once per quarter Goal review, strategy adjustment

Each level feeds the one above it. Daily tracking surfaces weekly themes. Weekly reviews feed monthly assessments. The information flows upward; the direction flows downward.

Reinertsen (2009) distinguishes cadence from synchronization. Cadence is the regular rhythm. Synchronization is the alignment of multiple rhythms. A sprint is cadence — it happens every two weeks. A release is synchronization — it aligns the output of multiple teams at a single point. The operations loop needs both: cadence for predictability within stages, synchronization for handoffs between stages.

Cadence should be encoded as scheduled events, not just habits. A weekly review that depends on someone remembering to do it will be forgotten. A weekly review triggered by a scheduled hook will happen. This is the settlement path applied to cadence: move from inquiry (we should probably review sometimes) through procedure (we review when we remember) to process (the system triggers a review every Friday at 3pm).
