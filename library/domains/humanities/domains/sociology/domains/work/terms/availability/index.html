Availability

2026-04-04 When an agent can work — the temporal constraint that rosters and schedules must respect, not the same as capacity or commitment.

Availability is the set of intervals during which an agent is able and willing to work. It is a constraint, not a commitment — being available does not mean you are assigned; it means you can be assigned. Availability is the temporal precondition for dispatch: no work can be assigned to an agent outside their available hours, regardless of capacity or organizational need.

Availability has multiple dimensions. Temporal availability specifies which hours and days the agent can work: Monday through Friday, 9am to 5pm; or overnight shifts; or on-call windows. Capability availability specifies which roles the agent is qualified to fill during those hours — a junior agent may be available full-time but only qualified for certain task classes. Energy availability acknowledges that not all available hours are equal: the same agent has higher cognitive availability in the morning than at 11pm, but may still be technically available. Goldratt (1997) treats availability as the binding constraint in multi-resource systems — if two tasks need the same agent, neither can be scheduled when the other is running, regardless of dependency structure.

Availability differs from capacity in a crucial way. Capacity is the amount of work an agent can handle in a given period — say, 40 hours per week. Availability is when that capacity falls on the calendar. Two agents with 40 hours/week capacity may have completely different availability patterns: one works mornings and has nights free; one works staggered shifts. The capacity pool is the same; the availability constraint is different.

Constraints on availability come from three sources. Personal constraints include family obligations, health needs, sleep requirements, and preferences (some agents work best early, others late). Legal constraints include maximum consecutive work hours without mandatory rest, protected leave (vacation, medical), and union agreements that may limit scheduling discretion. Organizational constraints include core hours when everyone must be present, recurring meetings, and ceremonies (standups, reviews) that occupy specific calendar slots.

In scheduling theory, availability gates the feasible region — it is the resource constraint that Critical Chain scheduling must respect. Harris and Rubin’s communication model (1957) shows that any system coordinating multiple agents must have an availability protocol: a way for agents to signal when they are available. Without it, dispatch becomes guesswork.

Availability degrades under load. An agent nominally available but already assigned to six concurrent tasks has lower effective availability for a seventh: they may be technically available on the calendar, but practically overcommitted. This is why WIP limits protect availability — they prevent the assignment stage from overcommitting what the execution stage cannot actually do. The roster specifies potential availability; the schedule operationalizes it by assigning specific work to specific intervals.

See Schedule, Roster, and Dispatch and Delegation Survey for how availability constrains planning and execution.
