Delegation

2026-03-19 The transfer of bounded authority from a principal to a beneficiary, where accountability remains with the principal. Authority moves; the duty to answer does not.

A delegation is the transfer of bounded authority from a principal to a beneficiary, where accountability remains with the principal. The principal can give the beneficiary the power to act; the principal cannot give the beneficiary the duty to answer for outcomes.

¶Four invariants

A delegation has four structural properties:

  1. Derivativity. The principal can only delegate what the principal holds. You cannot give authority you do not have. The Latin maxim: nemo dat quod non habet — no one gives what they do not have.

  2. Accountability non-transfer. Delegating authority does not reduce the principal’s accountability. The principal remains answerable for all outcomes attributable to the delegated authority, even those the beneficiary produces. The canonical statement (US Navy Regulations): “delegation of authority shall in no way relieve the commanding officer of continued responsibility.” The delegator cannot give away the duty to answer.

  3. Boundedness. The grant is strictly specified — what the beneficiary may do, within what limits, and optionally for how long. An unbounded delegation (“do whatever you think is necessary” without any specification) is an abdication, not a delegation, because it eliminates the structure that makes the principal accountable for how the grant is used.

  4. Revocability. The principal may revoke the grant at will unless constrained by an external obligation (contract, statute, institutional rule). The grant is the principal’s to give and the principal’s to take back.

¶The core asymmetry

What is delegated What is retained
Power to act within the grant Accountability for outcomes
Beneficiary’s capacity to bind the principal Principal’s duty to answer
The doing The answering

This asymmetry is why delegation is not the same as transfer. A full transfer moves both authority and accountability. Delegation moves authority only. The fiduciary, the trustee, the commanding officer — all can delegate authority to subordinates; none can delegate their accountability.

¶Delegation and trust

Delegation always involves extending trust. When a principal delegates to a beneficiary, the principal accepts vulnerability to the beneficiary’s exercise of the grant — the beneficiary could exploit the delegated authority in ways that harm the principal’s interests. The residual accountability is what makes this an asymmetric exposure.

¶Delegation as a Hohfeldian power

In the Hohfeldian framework, a delegation is the exercise of a power — the capacity to create new normative relations. The principal’s voluntary act (the delegation) creates a new relation in which the beneficiary now has the authority to act within the grant. The beneficiary’s counterpart position is a liability — susceptibility to having their normative position changed by the principal’s exercise of delegating power (including revocation).

¶Sub-delegation

When a beneficiary sub-delegates to a third party, the derivativity invariant constrains what can be passed on: the third party can receive at most what the beneficiary holds, which is at most what the principal delegated. Sub-delegation cannot amplify authority — it can only narrow it. The Latin maxim: delegatus non potest delegare — a delegate cannot delegate, unless the original grant explicitly permits it.
