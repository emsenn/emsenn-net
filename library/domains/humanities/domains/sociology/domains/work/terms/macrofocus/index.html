MacroFocus

Formal definition

A MacroFocus is a pointed presheaf Fmac=(H,t∗)\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{mac}} = (H, t^*):

Fmac=(H:Top→HAnucl,  t∗∈T)\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{mac}} = (H : T^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{HA}_{\mathrm{nucl}},\; t^* \in T)

where:

  • HH is the ambient presheaf — the full RelationalState: the family of fiber Heyting algebras (Ht)t∈T(H_t)_{t \in T} connected by restriction maps ρt′∣t:Ht′→Ht\rho_{t'|t} : H_{t'} \to H_t for t≤t′t \leq t'; each fiber carries the commuting nuclear pair (σt,Δt)(\sigma_t, \Delta_t)
  • t∗∈Tt^* \in T is the focal history — the designated current position in the history category TT

A MacroFocus IS a Focus — the pair (H,t∗)(H, t^*) is identical. MacroFocus names this structure at Level 1 of the three-level focus stack: the fiber-level designation. It answers the question: which fiber is the current working context? The answer is Ht∗H_{t^*}.

Four invariants. Fmac\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{mac}} is a macrofocus iff it satisfies:

  1. Designation: t∗t^* is a specific named element of TT, not a generic placeholder or existential witness. Without a specific t∗t^*, the presheaf HH is ambient and unfocused. Designation is the minimal act of focus: before any sub-fiber structure is resolved, the fiber is picked.

  2. Fiber accessibility: Ht∗H_{t^*} is an object in the presheaf — a Heyting algebra with commuting nuclear pair (σt∗,Δt∗)(\sigma_{t^*}, \Delta_{t^*}) — accessible as an object in the category of fibers. A MacroFocus is not a claim about what is inside Ht∗H_{t^*}; it is purely the selection of Ht∗H_{t^*} from the presheaf.

  3. Gurwitsch tripartition: the designation t∗t^* induces a partition of HH into three mutually exclusive parts:

    • Theme T=Ht∗\mathcal{T} = H_{t^*}: the designated working fiber
    • Thematic field TF={(t,Ht,ρt∗∣t):t<t∗}\mathcal{TF} = \{(t, H_t, \rho_{t^*|t}) : t < t^*\}: the restriction-accessible fibers, each equipped with a restriction map from Ht∗H_{t^*}; these are the MacroFocus’s context
    • Margin M={(t,Ht):t≰t∗}\mathcal{M} = \{(t, H_t) : t \not\leq t^*\}: fibers that exist in HH but have no restriction path from Ht∗H_{t^*}; co-present but inaccessible

    The tripartition is a structural consequence of the designation — it is not an additional choice.

  4. Horizon completeness: the margin M\mathcal{M} is the horizon of the MacroFocus — the set of fibers currently inaccessible that could become thematic by a refocusing operation. The full presheaf HH equals Ht∗H_{t^*} together with TF\mathcal{TF} and M\mathcal{M}; the MacroFocus (H,t∗)(H, t^*) is recoverable (via sheaf gluing / accord) from Ht∗H_{t^*} and TF\mathcal{TF} alone only for the restriction-accessible portion. The horizon cannot be reconstructed without an explicit refocusing.

The zipper reading

A MacroFocus (H,t∗)(H, t^*) is a zipper on the presheaf HH in the sense of Huet (1997): a data structure traversal that designates one element as the current focus and encodes the surrounding structure as context, with a plug invariant ensuring nothing is lost.

  • Focus = Ht∗H_{t^*} (the currently selected fiber — the zipper’s cursor)
  • Context = TF\mathcal{TF} together with restriction maps (the path “breadcrumbs” from the root down to t∗t^*)
  • Plug invariant: H∣↓t∗=glue(Ht∗,  TF)H|_{\downarrow t^*} = \mathrm{glue}(H_{t^*},\; \mathcal{TF}) via the accord/sheaf-gluing condition — the restriction-accessible portion of HH is fully recoverable; the horizon M\mathcal{M} is the part the zipper has not visited

The zipper reading makes the operational meaning of MacroFocus precise: a MacroFocus is a position in the presheaf, not a global state. It localizes computation to Ht∗H_{t^*} while keeping the full structure reachable via the context.

Refocusing operations

A MacroFocus can be refocused — its t∗t^* changed — in three structurally distinct ways:

Forward extension (H,t∗)→(H′,t′)(H, t^*) \to (H', t') where t′=s⋆t∗t' = s \star t^*:

  • Extends the history by a generator ss; produces a new fiber Ht′H_{t'} that extends Ht∗H_{t^*}
  • The old theme Ht∗H_{t^*} moves from theme to thematic field (it is now t′>t∗t' > t^*, so t∗<t′t^* < t' and Ht∗∈TFH_{t^*} \in \mathcal{TF} of the new focus)
  • This is the RelationalMachine’s step operation
  • The new margin may be larger or smaller than the old margin depending on TT’s structure

Backward restriction (H,t∗)→(H,t)(H, t^*) \to (H, t) where t<t∗t < t^*:

  • Retreats to a fiber currently in the thematic field; makes an accessible fiber the new theme
  • The old Ht∗H_{t^*} moves from theme to the margin of the new focus (since t∗≰tt^* \not\leq t when t<t∗t < t^* in a non-trivial partial order)
  • This is context inspection: moving focus into the accessible context
  • The thematic field shrinks: only fibers below tt are now accessible

Lateral shift (H,t∗)→(H,t∗∗)(H, t^*) \to (H, t^{**}) where t∗∗≰t∗t^{**} \not\leq t^* and t∗≰t∗∗t^* \not\leq t^{**}:

  • Moves to a fiber currently in the margin — a fiber not restriction-accessible from t∗t^*
  • Requires a genuinely new focus, since the new thematic field ↓t∗∗\downarrow t^{**} may be disjoint from ↓t∗\downarrow t^*
  • Context is not preserved: the old thematic field is not (in general) the new thematic field
  • This is a hard context switch — unlike forward extension or backward restriction, which preserve context continuity

MacroFocus as the Level-1 question

In the three-level focus stack from Focus, MacroFocus is Level 1. The three levels answer three distinct questions:

Level Answers Math object
Level 1 (MacroFocus) Which fiber is the current working context? (H,t∗)(H, t^*) — pointed presheaf
Level 2 (MesoFocus) What is settled within the working fiber? (Ht∗,j,Fix(j))(H_{t^*}, j, \mathrm{Fix}(j)) — nuclear sublocale
Level 3 (MicroFocus) What specific element is being processed now? (Ht∗,a∗)(H_{t^*}, a^*) — designated element

A MacroFocus is a precondition for both MesoFocus and MicroFocus: you must know which fiber is active before asking what is settled within it or which element is being processed. But MacroFocus does not uniquely determine a MesoFocus (there may be multiple nuclei on Ht∗H_{t^*}) or a MicroFocus (any element of Ht∗H_{t^*} could be designated).

Aron Gurwitsch: the tripartite phenomenal field

Aron Gurwitsch (The Field of Consciousness, Pittsburgh: Duquesne University Press, 1964; repr. in Collected Works of Aron Gurwitsch, vol. III, ed. Richard M. Zaner, Dordrecht: Springer, 2010) provided the formal account of consciousness as a structured field rather than a punctate spotlight:

The three zones: every moment of directed attention constitutes a field with three formally distinguishable zones:

  • Theme: the item that is the center of attention — the focal content, maximally determinate, upon which the current cognitive act is directed; the theme has the most vivid, most fully articulated presence; it is what the act is about
  • Thematic Field: items that are co-present with the theme and stand in a relation of intrinsic relevance to it — they are not currently attended but are organized around the theme as its immediate context; the thematic field is structured (organized by Gestalt laws operating relative to the theme) and its items are relevant to the theme in virtue of their content, not merely their proximity
  • Margin: items that are co-present but stand in no intrinsic relevance relation to the theme — they are “there” in consciousness as background but exert no organizational pull on the theme; the margin is unorganized relative to the current act

Formal criteria (Gurwitsch, Field of Consciousness, ch. 1):

  1. The tripartition is exhaustive and exclusive: every item in the phenomenal field is in exactly one zone; no item is simultaneously in two zones
  2. The tripartition is theme-relative: moving the theme (shifting attention) reorganizes the entire field; what was in the thematic field may move to theme or margin; the zones are not fixed properties of items but are constituted by the current act of attention
  3. The thematic field is defined by intrinsic relevance: its items are there as context for the theme; relevance is a matter of content-organization (not spatial proximity), explained by the Gestalt organizational laws
  4. The margin is defined by absence of intrinsic relevance: its items are experienced as background, not as context; they are present but without organization relative to the current theme

MacroFocus as Gurwitsch’s field: the MacroFocus (H,t∗)(H, t^*) instantiates Gurwitsch’s tripartite field at the presheaf level. The full presheaf HH is the phenomenal field; designation of t∗t^* constitutes the Theme (Ht∗H_{t^*}), Thematic Field (TF\mathcal{TF} — restriction-accessible fibers, each intrinsically relevant via restriction maps), and Margin (M\mathcal{M} — remaining fibers, co-present but without restriction paths from Ht∗H_{t^*}) simultaneously. MacroFocus is the scale at which Gurwitsch’s partition first becomes visible — MesoFocus and MicroFocus operate inside the Theme; MacroFocus is the level that sees all three zones at once.

Attention shifts as field reorganization: Gurwitsch argued that consciousness is dynamic — the field is always reorganizing as attention moves. The three refocusing operations (forward extension, backward restriction, lateral shift) are field reorganizations in Gurwitsch’s sense. Each refocusing reconstitutes the tripartite partition: forward extension makes the old Theme part of the new Thematic Field; lateral shift makes a formerly Marginal fiber the new Theme; backward restriction makes the old Theme Marginal (since t∗≰tt^* \not\leq t when moving to t<t∗t < t^*). The field does not merely repoint — it structurally reconstitutes.

Open questions

  • Whether the three refocusing operations (forward extension, backward restriction, lateral shift) can be given a categorical presentation as morphisms in a category of MacroFoci over HH — and whether the composition of such morphisms is well-typed (a composition of a forward extension followed by a backward restriction returns to the original position, but through a different path).
  • Whether a MacroFocus determines a canonical MesoFocus: the natural candidate is Ht∗∗=Fix(σt∗)∩Fix(Δt∗)H^*_{t^*} = \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t^*}) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t^*}), but this uses the machine’s nuclear pair (σt∗,Δt∗)(\sigma_{t^*}, \Delta_{t^*}) which is present in every fiber by the relational universe structure — so the canonical MesoFocus may always exist and be unique.
  • Whether the horizon M\mathcal{M} carries a natural structure — whether the set of margin fibers can be equipped with an ordering or metric that captures their “distance” from the current focus, giving a formal notion of how many refocusing steps are needed to reach each marginal fiber.
