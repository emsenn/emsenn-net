Horizon

2026-04-04 The temporal boundary beyond which planning, concern, or accountability does not extend — the limit of temporal visibility at a given scale.

A horizon is the temporal boundary beyond which planning, concern, or accountability does not extend. It is not a deadline (a specific point in time) but a limit of visibility — how far into the future the organization or agent is responsible for considering. You can see and plan within your horizon. Beyond it, the future is not invisible, but it is not your problem to solve right now.

Horizons exist at every scale. A task has a session or daily horizon — what can I finish in this work period? A plan has a weekly or monthly horizon — what commitments must complete by this date? A project has a quarterly or yearly horizon — where should we be at the end of this cycle? An endeavor has a generational horizon — what world are we building for our children and grandchildren? These are not separate concepts but instances of the same pattern at different scales.

Different work containers have different natural horizons. Bratman (1987) introduced the concept of a temporal limit on practical reasoning — agents can deliberate about the near future, but not infinitely far ahead without losing actionability. A task bounded to a morning or afternoon needs a horizon measured in hours. A plan binding multiple tasks needs a horizon measured in weeks. A project grouping related plans needs a horizon measured in months or quarters. Once you move beyond the natural horizon of a container, the planning degrades: the future becomes too uncertain to constrain present action, or the present decisions become too divorced from their consequences to be responsibly made.

Horizon and cadence are partners. Cadence is the rhythm at which you look — a daily standup, a weekly review, a quarterly planning session. The horizon is what you can see in that glance. At the daily cadence, your horizon is typically a few days. At the weekly cadence, your horizon is typically a month or quarter. At the monthly cadence, your horizon might stretch a year. Cadence without horizon is ritual without purpose. Horizon without cadence is aspiration without check-ins.

Horizon collapse is a temporal pathology — sacrificing long-term concerns to short-term urgency. Corporate quarterly earnings cycles force governance horizons down to 90 days, even when the consequences of decisions extend decades. Climate policy debates collapse multigenerational horizons into election cycles. Hospital staffing crises collapse care horizons into shift-by-shift survival. When the horizon shrinks below the natural scale of the work, short-termism follows: decisions optimize for immediate firefighting at the cost of systemic stability.

The Haudenosaunee principle of the Gayanashagowa (Great Law of Peace) establishes a governance horizon: decisions by the Confederacy must consider their effects seven generations forward. This is not metaphor but formal constraint — it sets the temporal boundary of accountability. A decision maker whose personal horizon is 20 years must still ask what harms or benefits their choice creates for people who will live 140 years hence. This horizon does not require perfect foresight. It means: you are morally answerable for reasonably foreseeable long-term consequences.

A horizon is defeasible — it can be extended or narrowed deliberately. An uncertain project might start with a monthly horizon (what must we deliver by month’s end?) and extend it to quarterly as confidence grows. An emergency response contracts its horizon to hours — all other planning pauses until the acute crisis passes. The key is that horizon shifts are deliberate acts, not passive compression under pressure. Horizon collapse happens when you stop choosing and start merely reacting.

Horizon is distinct from goal or objective. A goal says what success looks like. An objective measures progress toward it. A horizon says how far you are responsible for thinking ahead. You can have a quarterly planning horizon and a multiyear goal. The horizon constrains which objectives are active right now; the goal constrains what those objectives point toward.
