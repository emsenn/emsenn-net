Bottleneck

2026-04-03 The constraint that limits the throughput of the entire system — the single resource whose capacity determines how fast work flows.

A bottleneck is the constraint that limits the throughput of the entire system. It is not the slowest task or the busiest agent — it is the specific resource whose capacity determines how fast work flows from entry to completion. Everything upstream of the bottleneck produces faster than it can consume; everything downstream starves.

Goldratt’s Theory of Constraints (1984) provides the operational framework. The five focusing steps: (1) identify the constraint — find where work accumulates (the longest queue), (2) exploit it — ensure the constraint is never idle, never works on low-priority items, never wastes capacity on rework, (3) subordinate everything else to it — non-constraints serve the constraint’s needs, not their own optimization, (4) elevate it — invest in increasing the constraint’s capacity, (5) repeat — once this constraint is resolved, a new one emerges.

The identification is diagnostic: a bottleneck reveals itself through queue growth. If tasks accumulate before the review stage but flow freely through dispatch and execution, review is the bottleneck. If the dispatch queue is empty but the planning backlog grows, planning is the bottleneck. Little’s Law (1961) quantifies the relationship: longer queues at a stage mean that stage’s throughput is lower than its input rate.

Reinertsen (2009) adds a nuance: the bottleneck is not always a processing stage. It can be a decision point (waiting for approval), a shared resource (one specialist needed by multiple tasks), or a coordination mechanism (all teams must synchronize before release). Decision bottlenecks are especially insidious because they look like idleness — nothing is being processed, but no one notices because the queue is invisible (it exists as pending decisions in someone’s head, not as visible work items).

The tracking stage should identify bottlenecks by measuring queue length and cycle time at each stage of the operations loop. The review stage should evaluate whether the current constraint-management strategy is working. Common bottlenecks include decision latency, shared specialist availability, and session or context limits in automated systems.

See Planning and Scheduling Survey for constraint theory and Tracking and Measurement Survey for measurement.
