Incumbent

2026-03-19 An Incumbent is a quadruple (O, t_now, a, ι) — an Office, a designated current history, an occupant Entity, and an incumbency proposition asserting that a holds O at t_now. Full incumbency is joint settlement of two predicates: institution (recognized authority) and induction (corporeal possession).

¶What it is

An Incumbent is a quadruple (O,  tnow,  a,  ι)(O,\; t_{\mathrm{now}},\; a,\; \iota):

Inc=(O,  tnow∈T,  a:Entity,  ι)\mathrm{Inc} = (O,\; t_{\mathrm{now}} \in T,\; a : \text{Entity},\; \iota)

where:

  • OO is the Office — the normative structure defined over all histories in TT
  • tnow∈Tt_{\mathrm{now}} \in T is the current history — the designated base point for now; incumbency is a relation at a specific history, not a global structure
  • aa is the occupant — an Entity whose id is settled in the framework; the natural person currently wearing the body politic
  • ι\iota is the incumbency proposition — asserting aa holds OO at tnowt_{\mathrm{now}} — a claim whose settlement is what the rest of the structure tracks

Type note. The occupant aa is an Entity (a named individual), not itself a value in the framework’s settlement space. The settlement space carries claims about aa, not aa itself (Lambek–Scott 1986, Introduction to Higher Order Categorical Logic; Johnstone 2002, Sketches of an Elephant, Part D). The incumbency proposition ι\iota is the proposition about aa; aa is the individual the proposition names. What earlier formulations conflated — occupant individual and occupancy claim — are distinct.

Full incumbency is the condition that two distinct predicates over ι\iota have both reached affirming settlement at tnowt_{\mathrm{now}}:

  • Institution — the predicate that the office’s authority has been conferred; recognized authorization
  • Induction — the predicate that corporeal possession has been entered; forward-stable occupancy

When only one of the two predicates has settled, the incumbency is partial; see the two-stage installation section below.

¶The two-body doctrine

The deepest philosophical analysis of the office/incumbent distinction is Kantorowicz’s reconstruction in The King’s Two Bodies (Princeton, 1957) of the Tudor legal theory recorded in Plowden’s Commentaries (c.1571). The Plowden passage:

“The King has in him two Bodies, viz., a Body natural, and a Body politic… his Body politic is a Body that cannot be seen or handled, consisting of Policy and Government… utterly void of Infancy, and old Age, and other natural Defects.”

The body politic does not die. When the body natural expires, the body politic passes instantaneously to the successor — the King never dies. The natural person merely gives the continuous office temporary corporeal existence.

Formally: the body politic IS the Office’s normative structure over all of TT. It is not an element of any single history. It is the structure that governs incumbency claims and exists for all t∈Tt \in T whether or not any element inhabits it. The body natural is the current occupant Entity aa — indexed to tnowt_{\mathrm{now}}, not persisting to t′≠tnowt' \neq t_{\mathrm{now}} without an explicit transition. Dignitas non moritur is the condition that the office’s structure is defined over all of TT: no history can be vacated by the departure of an occupant.

The medieval canonists’ concept of dignitas (dignity, office-as-abstract-entity) corresponds to the Office’s structure itself: the formal normative content of the office, invariant across all holders, modifiable by no incumbent. Kantorowicz traces this through Baldus de Ubaldis and Johannes Andreae; the formal analog here is that no act controlled by the incumbent can alter the office’s required predicates or its laws.

The corporation sole (Blackstone Bk. I Ch. 18; Sutton’s Hospital, Coke C.J. 1613: “invisible, immortal… resting only in intendment of the law”) is the legal mechanism. The office is the juristic person; the natural person is the current occupant. Property vested in the corporation sole does not pass to the holder’s heirs but to the successor in office. This is perpetual succession: the office’s structure persists across the mortality of every individual element.

Hart’s rule of recognition (The Concept of Law, 1961, Ch. V) connects this to the philosophy of law: what survives succession is not the sovereign’s person but the rule that specifies which acts count as law. Hart: legal continuity is continuity of the rule of recognition, not of the natural person in the seat.

¶The two-stage installation

The ecclesiastical tradition developed the most formally explicit installation structure, revealing the two-component anatomy of incumbency (Phillimore, Ecclesiastical Law Vol. 1, 1895; Blackstone Bk. II Ch. 3):

Institution (institutio): The bishop confers cura animarum — care of souls — and the right to exercise spiritual functions. This is the act of normative recognition: the bishop’s act settles the institution predicate as affirming, placing ι\iota in a position recognized by the surrounding normative system. Blackstone: the clerk is now the incumbent for spiritual purposes.

Induction (inductio): The archdeacon puts the priest in corporeal possession — hand on the church door-ring, ringing the bell, sitting in the chancel stall. Blackstone: the parson is not complete incumbent till induction; for till then he has no complete freehold in the church. Induction settles the induction predicate as affirming: the occupancy is forward-stable, present in continuations of tnowt_{\mathrm{now}}.

These two stages map onto the two predicates whose joint settlement defines full incumbency:

Stage Settlement state What it gives
Institution only Institution predicate settled affirmingly; induction predicate not yet Spiritual authority without corporeal possession — recognized but not yet forward-stable
Induction only Induction predicate settled; institution predicate not yet Possession without authorization — forward-stable but not yet recognized
Full incumbency Both predicates settled affirmingly Both stages complete; the complete freehold

Succession condition. Once both predicates have settled at tnowt_{\mathrm{now}}, the framework’s settling monotonicity carries the settled state forward: the successor who inherits the office at t′=s⋆tnowt' = s \star t_{\mathrm{now}} receives the incumbency claim intact. The departing incumbent who has reached full incumbency leaves the office in a condition that permits clean succession — this is the formal content of the succession obligation.

The usufruct condition (Digest 7.1.1, Paul: jus alienis rebus utendi fruendi salva rerum substantia) is the negative face of the same structure: the incumbent may freely use the settled state at tnowt_{\mathrm{now}} (the fruits of the office — the stable resources at the current history) but may not modify the office’s normative structure (the substantia rerum — the substance of the thing enjoyed). The structure is not consumable. An incumbent who attempts to alter it violates the salva rerum substantia condition.

¶The Hohfeldian profile

An incumbent’s jural positions (Hohfeld, Yale Law Journal 23/26, 1913/1917; Finnis, Natural Law and Natural Rights, 1980, Ch. VIII):

Hohfeldian position With respect to Internal rendering
Power Subjects within the office’s jurisdiction Authorized acts that move predicates into affirming settlement at tnowt_{\mathrm{now}}
Duty The office itself Obligation to settle the office’s required predicates: full incumbency is what the duty demands; an unsettled predicate is a duty unfulfilled
Privilege Core office functions Liberty to exercise the office’s functions without owing anyone a duty not to
Immunity The Office’s normative structure The structure is not modifiable by ordinary agents; the incumbent cannot alter the office’s normative content unilaterally
Disability Office resources and successor’s inheritance Cannot alienate the office’s structure; cannot pre-commit successors beyond what the framework’s monotonic settling carries forward; cannot consume what belongs to the corporation sole
Liability The appointing body The appointing body has the power to install, discipline, or remove; the incumbent’s normative position is alterable by the legitimate authority

¶Incumbency levels

Four levels of incumbency stability, from least to most complete:

Level Condition What it names
Nomination / designation ι\iota exists; neither predicate has settled The occupancy claim is held but is not yet recognized or possessed; the appointment is pending
Institution only Institution predicate settled affirmingly Spiritual authorization complete; corporeal possession not yet forward-stable
Induction only Induction predicate settled affirmingly Forward-stable possession; meaning not yet settled — anomalous, normally impossible in canonical law
Full incumbency Both predicates settled affirmingly Both stages complete; the complete freehold

¶Open questions

  • The institution/induction predicate decomposition: This mapping from the two ecclesiastical stages to two predicates whose joint settlement defines full incumbency is motivated by the functional roles. Institution confers recognized meaning; induction confers forward-stable possession. Deriving this formally requires specifying what spiritual authorization and corporeal possession mean as predicates in the framework’s predication structure, and showing that the institution and induction acts settle those predicates as claimed. This derivation is the primary open technical task of this spec.

  • The agent-sheaf: The occupant aa is an Entity with a settled id. The proper type for agents in the framework is a section of an agent-sheaf A\mathcal{A} (Lambek–Scott 1986; Abramsky–Brandenburger 2011, New Journal of Physics 13), and the incumbency proposition ι\iota is a predication cell over aa. Whether A\mathcal{A} is the same sheaf as the Entity-sheaf — whether every Entity is a potential agent — or whether there is a distinct Agent type with a separate sheaf, is not yet derived.

  • The rule of recognition as covering structure: Hart’s rule of recognition specifies which acts count as law. Whether the framework’s covering structure on TT — what families of facts cover a history — is the formal counterpart to the rule of recognition, and whether the institution and induction predicates arise from this covering structure as derived settling conditions, is an open question. If the derivation goes through, Hart’s internal point of view (officials treat the rule as binding from the inside) corresponds to the framework’s commitment that settlement is achieved through the witnessings the inquiry has actually performed, not from a view from nowhere.
