Capital

Capital is a field-specific accumulated resource that generates further returns when deployed. Not raw quantity — structured, convertible asset whose value is constituted by the field in which it operates.

Four invariants

Something is capital if and only if it satisfies:

  1. Field-specificity. Capital has value because the field’s structure recognizes it. A university degree is capital in the academic field; it may be worthless in a street market. The field determines what counts as capital. Bourdieu: capital is “energy that only exists and only produces its effects in the field in which it is produced and reproduced.”

  2. Returns-generating. Deploying capital within a field generates further resources — capital is not consumed by a single use but produces yield. This distinguishes capital from an expendable resource. A commodity consumed in production is not capital; money invested that produces further money is capital.

  3. Accumulability. Capital can be built up over time through strategic action. Capital has history: it is the sedimented result of past investment. This is what makes inherited capital a genuine form and what makes capital unequally distributed.

  4. Convertibility. Capital in one form can be converted into capital in another form, though conversion is costly. Economic capital can be converted into cultural capital (paying for education) or social capital (entertaining influential contacts). The conversion rate is determined by the field’s structure.

Four forms

Economic capital: resources directly convertible into money and institutionalized as property rights. The most fungible form — money is the universal equivalent in the economic field. Crosses field boundaries with the least conversion loss.

Social capital: the aggregate of actual or potential resources linked to possession of a durable network of institutionalized relationships of mutual acquaintance and recognition (Bourdieu 1986). Held in common with the network — not held alone. The volume depends on the size of the network the agent can effectively mobilize and on the capital possessed by those connected to them. Requires maintenance: relationships not tended decay.

Cultural capital: appears in three states:

  • Embodied — durable dispositions of mind and body: tastes, skills, manners of speaking. Cannot be transmitted instantly; requires personal investment. Attached to the agent, lost at death.
  • Objectified — cultural goods (books, instruments, machines) that presuppose embodied cultural capital for their appropriation.
  • Institutionalized — academic qualifications, credentials, titles: given official recognition by a credentialing institution. Has a certified, socially guaranteed value with relative autonomy from its bearer.

Organizational capital (Penrose 1959): the productive capabilities, routines, and collective knowledge embedded in an enterprise — what makes the enterprise more than the sum of its members’ individual contributions. Includes competencies, routines, tacit coordination mechanisms, and collective learning held by the firm as a whole. Organizational capital generates new productive opportunities internally, making growth self-sustaining.

Symbolic capital: not a separate substance but the form any capital takes when it is perceived through categories that recognize its logic and fail to see it as capital. Prestige, reputation, honor — the misrecognized form. The most powerful because it operates without being recognized as power.

Capital and the field

Bourdieu’s key insight: the field and capital are mutually constitutive. The field is a structured space of positions; what determines position is the volume and composition of capital held. Capital without a field is not capital — without recognition, resources are mere things.

Capital is relational. It is not a property of isolated objects or agents but a relational property defined by the structure of the field. The same agent with the same resources has different capitals in different fields.

Capital and enterprise

Every enterprise operates within a field and draws on capital. The interaction is bidirectional:

  • Capital enables enterprise: sufficient capital is a precondition for the operative structure to function. An undercapitalized enterprise cannot sustain its cooperative structure.
  • Enterprise generates capital: successful pursuit of aims generates organizational capital (new capabilities, learned routines), social capital (relationships, reputation), and cultural capital (expertise, recognition). The enterprise is one of the primary mechanisms of capital accumulation.
