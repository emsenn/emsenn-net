Ship

2026-04-04 A chartered venture with a specific purpose, crew, captain, and command structure, commissioned by a higher authority and bounded by its charter.

A ship is a chartered venture — a bounded undertaking commissioned by a higher authority, given a specific purpose, crewed for that purpose, and placed under a captain’s command. The ship’s identity comes from its charter and its flag, not from its crew or its captain. Crews rotate, captains are relieved, but the ship persists as long as the charter is in force.

The metaphor is maritime but the structure is general. In admiralty law, a ship is a legal entity distinct from its owner, its master, and its crew. The ship itself can be sued (in rem jurisdiction), can hold liens, and has a legal personality that survives changes in ownership and command. William Tetley’s International Maritime and Admiralty Law (2002) traces this doctrine to medieval Mediterranean practice: the ship was the venture, and the venture had rights and obligations independent of the persons aboard. This legal personality is what makes the ship more than a vehicle — it is an institutional entity.

¶What constitutes a ship

A ship requires four things:

  1. A charter — the constitutive document that declares the ship’s purpose, establishes its command structure, and binds it to the commissioning authority’s directives. The charter is not a contract between equals; it is a grant of authority from above, bounded by the grantor’s directives. A ship without a charter is an unauthorized venture — it has no institutional standing.

  2. A flag — the identity conferred by the commissioning authority. In admiralty, the flag state is the sovereign entity that registers a ship and confers its legal identity. A ship without a flag is stateless: no port of registry, no authority backing its operations, no recognized identity. When a confederacy charters a ship, it confers identity — the ship sails under the confederacy’s flag and the confederacy’s directives are the highest authority the ship recognizes.

  3. A captain — the person invested with operational command. The captain commands the ship within the charter’s bounds. The captain’s authority is absolute aboard the ship — Chester Barnard’s The Functions of the Executive (1938) identifies this as the essential executive function: maintaining the system of cooperative effort by making operational decisions that the charter cannot anticipate. But the captain’s authority derives from the charter, not from personal power. The commissioning authority retains the power to relieve the captain without dissolving the ship.

  4. A crew — the persons assigned to operate the ship for the duration of the venture. Crew status is bounded by the voyage or mission — it has a start and an end. This distinguishes a crew from a staff, whose role-bindings persist through the institution’s history without a pre-specified terminal period. The same person may be staff of a company and crew of a ship the company charters.

¶Ship vs. company

A ship is bounded; a company is perpetual. A ship has a crew assembled for a venture; a company has a staff with ongoing role-bindings. A ship’s captain commands by delegated authority within the charter; a company’s governance produces collective decisions attributable to the company as an entity. The ship is the instrument of coordinated action; the company is the enduring fellowship that commissions it.

The East India Company (chartered 1600) illustrates the distinction. The Company was the perpetual entity — chartered by the Crown, governed by a Court of Directors, staffed by clerks and factors. Each trading voyage was a ship: a bounded venture with its own captain, crew, cargo, and destination. The Company persisted through hundreds of voyages; each ship’s venture began and ended. The Company’s charter was its constitution; each ship’s charter was its commission.

¶The directive-charter hierarchy

The confederacy’s directives occupy a higher position than any ship’s charter. The hierarchy is strict:

  1. Confederacy directives — the shared principles that bind all member groups and all chartered ships
  2. Ship charter — the constitutive document for a specific ship, issued under the confederacy’s authority, consistent with its directives
  3. Captain’s orders — operational decisions within the charter’s bounds

A conflict between a ship’s charter and the confederacy’s directives is resolved in favor of the directives. A conflict between the captain’s orders and the charter is resolved in favor of the charter. If the captain believes the directives are wrong, the captain can petition the confederacy for amendment. The captain cannot unilaterally override.

¶Commissioning and decommissioning

A ship comes into existence through commissioning — the act by which the authority issues a charter, confers a flag, and authorizes the venture. A ship ceases to exist through decommissioning — the revocation of the charter by the commissioning authority. The crew disperses, the captain is released from command, and the institutional entity dissolves. What remains is the record: the ship’s log, its decisions, its completed or abandoned purpose.

The commissioning authority retains three powers over every ship it charters: the power to relieve the captain, the power to amend or revoke the charter, and the power to decommission the ship. These powers are not delegated to the ship — they are retained by the commissioning authority. The captain commands the ship; the confederacy commands whether there is a ship.

¶Why ships matter for autonomous groups

The ship structure solves a specific problem: how autonomous groups produce coordinated action without becoming a hierarchy. A confederacy does not centralize — it commissions. Each ship it charters has hierarchical command (the captain commands), but the confederacy itself remains a covenant between equals. The ships answer to the confederacy’s directives, not to each other.

The Haudenosaunee confederacy coordinated sovereign nations through the Great Law of Peace. When coordinated action was needed — a diplomatic mission, a military campaign, a trade expedition — the relevant nations commissioned ventures with specific leaders and participants. The venture operated hierarchically under its commission; the confederacy that issued the commission did not. This is the ship pattern: hierarchy within the venture, equality among the venturers.
