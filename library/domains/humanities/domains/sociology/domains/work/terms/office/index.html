Office

An office is a normative position that exists and persists independently of any particular holder. The structural fact from which everything else follows: the authority of an office belongs to the office, not to the person who occupies it.

When a person is installed in an office, she acquires the office’s authority for the duration of her occupancy. When she vacates, the authority does not dissolve — it remains in the office, waiting for the next holder. This is the medieval legal doctrine dignitas non moritur — “the dignity does not die” (Kantorowicz, The King’s Two Bodies, 1957, p. 383).

What an office has

An office is defined by four components:

  1. A normative structure — the set of rules, constraints, and expectations that govern the office’s domain, independent of who holds it
  2. An action domain — the scope of acts and relations the office governs; “the constitutional rights of the place” (Madison, Federalist No. 51)
  3. A deontic profile — the powers, duties, privileges, and immunities attached to the office, specified in Hohfeldian terms; this transfers intact to each successive occupant
  4. An installation mechanism — the constitutive speech act (Searle 1995) that binds a natural person to the office’s deontic profile

Office vs. role

An office is categorically different from a role. Role-filling is capability-matching — demonstrating that you can do the work. Entering an office is a constitutive normative event — the installation creates a normative fact that was not there before, not by capability demonstration but by the speech act of installation. A surgeon fills a role by having surgical skill. A judge enters an office by being sworn in.

The body politic and body natural

Kantorowicz’s reconstruction of Tudor legal theory (from Plowden’s Commentaries, c.1571) provides the deepest grounding:

“The King has in him two Bodies, viz., a Body natural, and a Body politic… his Body politic is a Body that cannot be seen or handled, consisting of Policy and Government… utterly void of Infancy, and old Age, and other natural Defects.”

The body politic is the office itself — the normative structure that governs the domain, exists for all time, and cannot be seen or handled. The body natural is the current occupant — mortal, personal, indexed to a specific time. The body natural dies; the body politic continues. Succession is instantaneous because the incoming occupant submits to the same structure that governed the predecessor.

Vacancy

An office is vacant when its normative structure is defined but no person currently holds it. Vacancy is not dissolution — the office is not extinguished, just unoccupied. The Roman constitutional mechanism for vacancy was the interregnum: when both consuls died, the Senate appointed interreges for five-day periods, each charged with the single function of convening elections. The interrex held a partial grant — enough authority to perform the election, without the full consular dignity.

Structural opposition of offices

Madison’s constitutional theory extends office analysis from single offices to systems of offices in structural opposition. The mechanism: “ambition must be made to counteract ambition” — each office’s normative structure is designed so that no unilateral act of one officeholder can settle matters in another office’s domain. Separation of powers is the condition that each branch’s normative structure is closed under its own operations but not under another’s.

Installation

Installation is the normative act that binds a natural person to the office’s deontic profile. Before installation, the profile is abstract — the office exists, but no one is bound by it. After installation, the officer exists: the person has the powers, duties, privileges, and immunities of the office for the duration of occupancy.

The installation must satisfy felicity conditions (Austin, How to Do Things with Words, 1962): appropriate authority (the installer must hold the relevant power), appropriate procedure (the correct installation form), and completeness. An attempted installation by an entity lacking the power fails regardless of the words used.
