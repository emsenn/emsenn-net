Review

2026-04-03 The act of comparing actual outcomes against planned outcomes — the structured assessment that produces learning and feeds the feedback stage. As a record, a review is a container of findings.

A review is the act of comparing actual outcomes against planned outcomes. It takes what happened and what was supposed to happen and produces a judgment: what went right, what went wrong, and why. Review is where the operations loop learns.

A review is not a status check. Status checks observe the current state — “where are we?” A review evaluates the trajectory — “how did we get here, and was it the right path?” The distinction matters: tracking is continuous observation, review is periodic assessment.

The US Army’s After Action Review protocol (TC 25-20, 1993) distills review into four questions: What was supposed to happen? What actually happened? Why was there a difference? What do we do better next time? These four questions apply at every scale — session, plan, project, goal.

Argyris and Schön (1978) distinguish two depths of review. Single-loop review asks “did we execute the plan correctly?” and produces tactical adjustments. Double-loop review asks “was the plan the right plan?” and produces strategic adjustments. Both are necessary. A system that only does single-loop review optimizes for the current approach without questioning whether the approach is correct.

A review is a container of findings. Each finding compares an observation against an expectation and produces a judgment: what the variance is, why it exists, how much it matters. The review groups related findings and provides the scope — session, plan, project, goal — that determines what observations and expectations are relevant.

Findings feed the feedback stage, which turns them into adjustments — concrete changes to plans, processes, terms, policies. Without the feedback stage, findings accumulate and are ignored.

See Review and Retrospective Survey for the full theoretical grounding.
