Agastache cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing protocol for Agastache rugosa (Korean licorice mint), short-lived perennial with anise-scented leaves, hardy in zones 4-9, self-seeding readily, used fresh or dried in tea and Asian cuisine.

Agastache cultivation targets Agastache rugosa, a short-lived perennial herb hardy in USDA zones 4-9, typically persisting 3-4 seasons before declining due to aging root systems. The plant is easily grown from seed, self-seeds readily, and has minimal pest and disease problems. Its anise-scented leaves are used fresh or dried in teas and culinary applications across East Asian cuisines.

Starting from Seed

Agastache seeds are small but hardy and germinates easily at moderate temperature (65-75°F / 18-24°C). Surface-sow seeds on moist soil, pressing gently without covering; light aids germination. Germination occurs in 7-10 days. Start seeds indoors 6-8 weeks before transplanting, or direct-sow after last spring frost.

Seeds remain viable 3-4 years when stored cool and dry. Volunteer seedlings appear readily if plants are allowed to mature and drop seeds.

Planting and Spacing

Transplant seedlings at the 4-6 true leaf stage, spacing 18-24 inches apart. Agastache prefers full sun (6+ hours daily) and well-drained soil. The plant is drought-tolerant once established and does not require rich soil or regular fertilization. Good drainage is important for winter survival; wet soil in winter, particularly in colder zones, predisposes plants to root rot and death.

Growing Season Management

Agastache grows vigorously once established, reaching 2-3 feet tall by mid-summer. Pinching the apex in early summer (when plants are 12-18 inches tall) promotes bushier growth and delays flowering by 2-3 weeks, extending the leaf-harvest window.

The plant flowers from mid-summer through early fall (June-October in northern temperate climates), producing dense terminal spikes of purple-violet flowers that are highly attractive to hummingbirds, butterflies, and bees.

Leaf Harvesting

Harvest young, tender leaves from the top 6-8 inches of stems anytime during the growing season, pinching above a leaf node to encourage branching. Fresh leaves can be used immediately in teas, soups, and culinary applications. The anise-like flavor is most pronounced in young leaves and is retained in dried material.

Flower Harvesting

Flowers are edible and have an intensely aromatic, anise-scented profile. Harvest flowers by pinching off individual florets or the entire spike for use in fresh or dried arrangements, teas, or culinary garnishes. Harvesting flowers extends the blooming period and prevents seed set, but if self-seeding for next season is desired, allow at least one plant to flower fully and mature seeds.

Drying and Storage

Harvest leafy stems just before flowering (when essential oil content is highest), tie in small bundles, and hang-dry in a warm, airy location. Once dry and crackling, strip leaves from stems and store in airtight containers in a cool, dark location. Dried material retains aromatic quality for 1-2 years.

Overwintering and Short-Lived Perennial Habit

Agastache is cold-hardy in zones 4-9, surviving winter with mulch protection in zone 4. In colder locations or in zones 3-4, plants can be grown as annuals or overwintered in containers indoors. As a short-lived perennial, individual plants decline in vigor after 3-4 seasons; they should be replaced or divided every 3 years to maintain vigor. Alternatively, allow plants to self-seed, and remove old plants as vigor declines.

Self-Seeding and Perpetuation

Allow at least one plant to flower and mature seeds late in the season (September-October in northern regions). Seeds shatter and self-sow; seedlings germinate in spring and establish new plants. This strategy maintains a population of agastache with minimal labor.

Pest and Disease

Agastache is nearly pest-free in well-drained soil. Spider mites may infest plants in hot, dry conditions; misting or overhead watering deters them. Powdery mildew may occur in humid, poorly ventilated locations; ensure good air circulation.

Culinary and Medicinal Uses

Agastache leaves are used fresh or dried in teas throughout East Asia, particularly Korea and China, where the herb is traditionally associated with digestive and respiratory health. Fresh leaves are added to chueo-tang (loach soup) and maeun-tang (spicy soup) in Korean cuisine. The plant is one of the 50 fundamental herbs in East Asian traditional medicine.
