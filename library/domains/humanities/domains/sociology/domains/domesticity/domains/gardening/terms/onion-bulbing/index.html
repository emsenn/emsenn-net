Bulbing onion cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing protocol for common onion (Allium cepa var. cepa), matched to photoperiod categories (short/intermediate/long-day) based on latitude and harvest timing.
Defines short-day onion, long-day onion, intermediate-day onion, photoperiod-matched variety

Bulbing onion cultivation succeeds only when cultivar photoperiod requirement matches local latitude. Failure to match day-length type to region is the single most common reason onions bolt without bulbing or bulb late, yielding small, unsaleable crops. Select cultivars by latitude and intended harvest window.

Variety Selection by Latitude

Long-day (short-season types, 40°N and northward): Better for Canada, northern USA, and Europe. These cultivars initiate bulbs at 15-16+ hours daylight and mature quickly before fall daylengths shorten. Examples: Walla Walla, Ailsa Craig, Sturon, Red Wing, Cortland. Mature in 100-120 days.

Intermediate-day (35-40°N, mid-range regions): Suited to central and upper-middle US states. Initiate bulbs at 14-15 hours. Examples: Candy, Sweet Spanish, Maui Onion types. Mature in 120-130 days.

Short-day (25-35°N, southern regions): For warm climates and autumn planting. Initiate bulbs at 12-13 hours. Planted fall or very early spring for spring/early-summer harvest. Examples: Vidalia, Texas SuperSweet, 1015Y. Mature in 110-120 days after spring emergence.

Starting and Transplanting

Start seed indoors 6-8 weeks before last spring frost, in flats under lights, maintaining soil 65-70°F. Seedlings ready to transplant at 4-6 weeks (pencil-thin, 4-6 inches tall). Harden off over 1-2 weeks before transplanting outdoors after frost danger passes and soil is workable (spring soil temp 50°F+). Space transplants 4-6 inches apart in rows 12-18 inches apart for standard cultivars; closer spacing (3-4 inches) for small pickling onions.

Alternatively, direct-seed after last frost in regions with long growing seasons (120+ days), though transplants produce larger bulbs more reliably.

Growing and Nutrient Management

Onions require consistent moisture and moderate nitrogen; excessive nitrogen delays bulbing and reduces storage life. Apply balanced fertilizer (10-10-10) at planting and side-dress at mid-season with lower-nitrogen formula (5-10-10) to encourage bulb formation over leaf growth. Weed by hand or shallow cultivation to avoid damaging shallow root systems.

Bulbing and Harvest Timing

As daylength lengthens past the cultivar threshold, bulbing begins. Foliage yellows and falls over naturally 2-3 weeks before full maturity. Harvest when 50 percent of tops have fallen and dried; pull or dig carefully to avoid bruising. Lay bulbs on soil surface or in single layer to cure in warm, airy location for 2-3 weeks until outer tunics are papery and neck is completely dry.

Storage

Cured onions store 3-4 months at cool room temperature (50-60°F) in darkness with good air circulation. Store in mesh bags or shallow crates, never in plastic, to prevent mold. Check periodically and remove any sprouting or rotting bulbs.

Cultivar Notes

Yellow cultivars store longest (4-6 months). Red onions store shorter (2-3 months) due to thinner papery layers. Sweet onions (white/red types from warm regions) store poorly (1-2 months) and are best used fresh.
