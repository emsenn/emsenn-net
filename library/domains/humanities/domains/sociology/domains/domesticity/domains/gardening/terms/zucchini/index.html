Zucchini

2026-04-02 A fast-maturing summer squash cultivar of Cucurbita pepo ready in 50-60 days, best harvested young at 6-8 inches for tender flesh and high yield
Defines Zucchini

Zucchini is a cultivar group of Cucurbita pepo selected for rapid maturation (50-60 days seed-to-harvest) and harvest at immature fruit stage. The name derives from Italian zucchino (small squash). Modern zucchini cultivars are predominantly bush-type, parthenocarpic (seedless when young), and bred for consistent green color and compact plant habit suitable for small-space gardening.

Site Selection and Soil Preparation

Zucchini requires full sun (6-8 hours minimum), rich loam with pH 6.0-7.5, and excellent drainage. Work 3-4 inches of well-rotted compost or aged manure into the top 8-10 inches before planting. If soil drainage is marginal, plant in mounded beds 4-6 inches high. The species is nutrient-hungry; baseline soil NPK should be moderate to high (nitrogen 150-200 lbs/acre equivalent).

Spacing and Planting Schedule

Space bush cultivars 24-36 inches apart; vining types 36-48 inches. Direct-seed after soil temperatures reach 60°F and frost danger has passed. In short-season areas, start seeds indoors 3-4 weeks before transplant date. Succession-plant every 3 weeks until 8 weeks before the first fall frost to extend harvest. Zucchini sets flowers prolifically once plants are 4-6 weeks old; flowering signals imminent fruit production.

Water and Fertilizer Management

Consistent moisture is non-negotiable: irrigate 1.5-2 inches per week (combined rain and supplemental), maintaining soil moisture at 60-70% field capacity. Uneven watering causes misshapen, bitter fruit and invites powdery mildew. Mulch with 2-3 inches of straw to retain moisture and suppress weeds.

Side-dress with balanced fertilizer (5-10-10 or similar) at fruit initiation and every 3-4 weeks thereafter. Container-grown plants (5-gallon minimum) need liquid feeding weekly. Excessive nitrogen delays flowering and produces rank foliage at the expense of fruit.

Pest and Disease Management

Common pests include squash bugs, cucumber beetles (spotted and striped), and spider mites. Hand-pick egg clusters from squash bugs (orange, on leaf undersides). Use floating row covers until flowering (to protect early growth), then remove to allow pollination. Insecticidal soap or neem oil suppresses spider mites on drought-stressed plants.

Powdery mildew (Podosphaera xanthii) appears as white coating on leaves; improve air circulation (prune lower leaves once plants are large) and avoid overhead watering. Early blight is rare on zucchini; most “diseases” reflect poor drainage or nutrient deficiency (yellowing older leaves).

Harvest and Quality

Harvest at 6-8 inches length for peak tenderness (7-10 days post-anthesis). Larger fruits develop thick seeds and watery flesh. Frequent harvest (every 2-3 days) promotes continuous production by preventing fruit maturation signals. Use pruning shears to avoid plant damage. A single bush plant yields 5-10 lbs over 6-8 weeks of peak production.

Immediate post-harvest: store at 45-50°F for 1-2 weeks maximum; zucchini deteriorates rapidly after harvest, developing soft spots and mold.

Cultivar Recommendations

‘Black Beauty’ (60 days, productive): classic dark green, reliable. ‘Costata Romanesco’ (60 days, heirloom): ribbed, pale green, firmer flesh, superior flavor. ‘Ronde de Nice’ (60 days): round, pale green, excellent flavor, space-efficient. ‘Yellow Crookneck’ (55 days): yellow, unique flavor, prolific bush type.
