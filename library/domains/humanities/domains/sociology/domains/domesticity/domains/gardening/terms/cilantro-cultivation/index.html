Cilantro cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing protocol for Coriandrum sativum fresh leaf harvest (cilantro), cool-season annual with rapid bolting in heat, 40-50 day leaf harvest window, minimal flavor development, succession sowing for continuous harvest.

Cilantro cultivation targets Coriandrum sativum grown specifically for fresh leaf harvest (cilantro), not for coriander seed production. Cilantro is an extremely cool-season crop; heat above 25°C (77°F) triggers rapid bolting (transition to flowering and seed production), shortening the productive leaf-harvest window to 30-50 days. Success requires cool soil, regular moisture, and successive plantings throughout spring and autumn for continuous harvest.

Timing and Cool-Season Planting

Plant cilantro in early spring (as soon as soil can be worked, 4-6 weeks before last spring frost) for spring harvest, and again in mid-late summer (6-8 weeks before first fall frost) for autumn harvest. Summer plantings in hot climates will bolt rapidly and are not recommended.

In mild-winter climates (zones 8-10), cilantro can be grown as a winter crop, planted in autumn for winter and early-spring harvest. Cilantro thrives at temperatures of 15-20°C (59-68°F); growth slows above 25°C and essentially stops above 30°C (86°F).

Direct Seeding

Cilantro is best direct-seeded; transplanting disturbs the taproot and is rarely successful. Sow seeds directly into the garden 0.5 inches deep in rows or broadcast. Space seeds 2-3 inches apart initially; thin seedlings to 4-6 inches apart once they develop true leaves.

Germination occurs in 7-10 days at cool soil temperature. Seeds remain viable 3-5 years when stored cool and dry.

Soil and Water Management

Cilantro prefers rich, well-drained soil amended with compost. Consistent, regular moisture is essential; allow soil surface to dry between waterings but never let the plant experience prolonged drought stress. Inconsistent watering stresses plants and accelerates bolting.

No fertilization is needed if soil is amended with compost; excessive nitrogen actually accelerates bolting.

Extending Leaf Harvest Window

The cilantro leaf-harvest window is naturally 40-50 days before bolting occurs, but can be slightly extended by pinching out flower buds as they appear and providing afternoon shade in hot climates (shade cloth reducing light by 25-30 percent may extend harvest by 1-2 weeks). However, these strategies only marginally extend the window; heat is the primary trigger for bolting, and once bolting begins, pinching cannot prevent it.

Harvesting Technique

Begin harvesting cilantro leaves once plants reach 6-8 inches tall. Pinch or cut outer stems first, leaving inner growth to continue developing. Multiple harvests can be made from a single planting before bolting occurs. At first signs of bolting (flower buds appearing at the apex), harvest the entire remaining plant.

Fresh cilantro is most flavorful when used immediately; it does not dry well and loses its distinctive aroma profile when dried.

Succession Planting Strategy

To maintain a continuous cilantro supply throughout the season, make successive sowings every 2-3 weeks from early spring through early summer, and again from mid-late summer through early autumn. This strategy ensures that as one planting bolts, the next is reaching peak harvest.

Spring schedule (northern temperate): Sow early April, mid-April, late April, early May; harvest April-May, May-June, May-July, June-July Autumn schedule: Sow mid-July, early August, late August, early September; harvest August-September, September-October, October-November, October-November

Coriander Seed Production

If dual-harvest (both cilantro leaf and coriander seed) is desired from the same planting, allow some plants to bolt while harvesting others. Once bolting is initiated, the remaining plants will develop flowers and mature seeds in 40-50 days from sowing to seed ripeness. See coriander-seed-cultivation for full protocol.

Pests and Disease

Cilantro is relatively pest-free. Flea beetles may eat small holes in leaves early in the season; young seedlings are most susceptible. Row covers protect young plants from flea beetles. No serious diseases affect cilantro.

Storage and Preservation

Fresh cilantro can be stored in the refrigerator for 1-2 weeks in a plastic bag with a damp paper towel, or in a glass of water loosely covered with a plastic bag. For longer-term storage, cilantro can be blanched briefly and frozen in ice cube trays with water or olive oil, or dried (though dried cilantro loses most of its distinctive flavor).

Sensory Note

Approximately 10-20 percent of the population experience cilantro as tasting like soap due to olfactory receptor polymorphisms related to the aldehyde compounds in fresh cilantro. There is no remedy for this genetic variation; people with this trait typically avoid cilantro.
