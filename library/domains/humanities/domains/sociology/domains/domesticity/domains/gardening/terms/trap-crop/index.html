Trap Crop

2026-04-02 A plant grown specifically to attract pest insects away from the main crop, concentrating them where they can be monitored and managed
Defines Trap Crop

A trap crop is a plant grown deliberately to attract pest insects away from nearby valued crops. The trap crop concentrates pests in one location where they can be removed, killed, or treated, reducing pest pressure on the target crops. This is a specific application within integrated pest management — using the pest’s own behavior against it.

Mechanism

Trap crops work because specialist pests preferentially colonize plants they have evolved to feed on. A pest insect navigates its environment using volatile chemical signals released by its host plants. When a trap crop emits stronger signals than nearby target crops, or is chemically more attractive than the target, the pest preferentially lays eggs on the trap crop.

The trap crop must be managed actively. Once concentrated on the trap crop, pests must be removed or destroyed — either by hand-picking, spraying, or allowing natural enemies to hunt them. If left unmanaged, the trap crop becomes a pest breeding ground and serves no purpose.

Evidence-Based Examples

Nasturtium for Aphids: Yellow nasturtiums preferentially attract several aphid species, particularly the black bean aphid and melon aphid. The mechanism is strong chemical attraction; aphids will colonize nasturtiums growing 4–5 feet away from target crops (brassicas, legumes, cucurbits). However, management is critical: aphids must be monitored on the nasturtiums and removed regularly. Some gardeners report success; others find that unmaintained nasturtiums simply become aphid breeding grounds with overflow to target crops.

Blue Hubbard Squash for Cucurbits: Blue Hubbard squash is the most evidence-based trap crop for squash bugs and cucumber beetles. These beetles preferentially colonize Blue Hubbard over other squash varieties. When planted at borders around squash, melon, and cucumber beds, Blue Hubbard attracts pest populations away from susceptible varieties. This is more reliable than nasturtium-aphid combinations and is used in research settings.

Radish for Flea Beetles: Radish attracts flea beetles away from brassicas (cabbage, kale, broccoli). Planting radish as a border crop provides some protection to brassicas, though again, radish must be monitored and either harvested early or treated when heavily infested.

Distinction from Companion Planting

Companion planting encompasses several strategies: some plants repel pests, some attract beneficial insects, some provide shade or structural support. Trap cropping is a specific subset — a plant that attracts pests for the purpose of concentrating them. The trap crop is sacrificed to protect the main crop.

Push-Pull Strategy

A more sophisticated approach combines trap cropping (pull) with repellent plants (push). The target crop is surrounded by plants that repel the pest (push them away) and isolated with attractive trap crops that draw them into concentrations where they are managed (pull them into killable groups). This dual strategy is more effective than trap cropping alone.
