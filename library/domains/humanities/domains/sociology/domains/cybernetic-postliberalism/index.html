Cybernetic Postliberalism

Cybernetic postliberalism is a framework for analyzing how liberal subjectivity is reproduced inside people. Not through ideology or coercion, but through cybernetic feedback loops between their bodies, thinking, affect, technology, and economy. The system does not persuade its subjects. It formats them — producing subjects who self-regulate by routing structural crisis into mood, and whose self-regulation is the governance mechanism itself.

The central concept is californication: the process by which structural contradictions in liberalism are formatted as personally manageable affect. Liberalism promises both universalism and individualism. These commitments are structurally incompatible. Under stress, the contradiction becomes visible — but instead of resolving structurally, the system teaches its subjects to experience the contradiction as personal condition: as trauma, growth opportunity, lifestyle, vibe. The subject who meditates through a housing crisis is doing exactly what the system requires.

The framework synthesizes several traditions that each describe part of this circuit:

  • Michel Foucault’s governmentality and theory of confession — how power produces subjects and incites them to speak themselves into subjection
  • Lauren Berlant’s genre theory, cruel optimism, and crisis ordinariness — how people maintain attachment to conditions that harm them, and how ongoing crisis is absorbed into daily life rather than recognized as rupture
  • Arlie Russell Hochschild’s feeling rules and emotional labor — how institutions prescribe not just behavior but affect itself
  • Norbert Wiener, Stafford Beer, and W. Ross Ashby’s cybernetics — how systems maintain stability through distributed feedback without central control
  • Elizabeth Povinelli’s late liberalism and quasi-events — how governance demands endurance rather than resolution, and how disturbances are contained before they can become events

The contribution of cybernetic postliberalism is showing how platforms made affect governance operative at scale — closing a circuit that these theorists each described in parts. Foucault described how power produces subjects. Berlant described how subjects maintain attachment to what harms them. Hochschild described how institutions prescribe feeling. Cybernetics described how systems self-correct through feedback. Povinelli described how late liberalism governs through endurance. Californication names the specific process that connects them: structural contradiction is formatted as affect, affect is managed through self-regulation, self-regulation is the feedback mechanism that preserves system stability, and platforms industrialize this at planetary scale.

The framework is developed in the letters-to-the-web and working notes, where it is deployed against specific cases: the April 2025 tariff cycle, the narrativization of Stop Cop City, the American reception of Gramsci, the linguistic infrastructure of platform speech, and the collapse of care into risk management.

Central thesis

Cybernetic postliberalism’s central thesis is that liberal subjectivity is reproduced not through ideology or coercion but through cybernetic feedback loops between bodies, affect, technology, and economy — a process named californication, in which structural contradictions are formatted as personally manageable affect, and self-regulation becomes the governance mechanism that preserves system stability.
