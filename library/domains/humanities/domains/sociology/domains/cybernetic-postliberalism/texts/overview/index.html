Overview: What Cybernetic Postliberalism Analyzes

2026-03-06
Table of contents

  • The theoretical circuit
  • What this framework is not
  • Where to go from here

In April 2025, the US government announced a tariff. Markets dropped. Procurement systems paused within hours. Treasury auctions shifted over days. And then, last: thousands of commentators produced thousands of explanations — negotiating tactic, policy error, deliberate shock, market test, distraction. These explanations contradicted each other. Each felt plausible. The policy was partially suspended before most of the commentary was published. The commentary kept coming anyway.

The system did not need people to understand what happened. It needed them to keep trying to understand. Their interpretive labor — the takes, the threads, the analyses — was not a response to governance. It was governance. The confusion was the mechanism. The load was the system.

This is what cybernetic postliberalism analyzes: how liberal governance reproduces itself not through ideology or coercion but through cybernetic feedback loops between affect, platforms, economy, and the body. The system does not persuade its subjects. It formats them — producing subjects who self-regulate by routing structural crisis into mood, and whose self-regulation is the governance mechanism itself.

The framework

The central concept is californication: the process by which structural contradictions in liberalism are formatted as personally manageable affect. Liberalism promises both universalism and individualism — structurally incompatible commitments. Under stress, the contradiction becomes visible. Instead of resolving structurally, the system teaches its subjects to experience the contradiction as personal condition: as trauma, growth opportunity, lifestyle. The person who meditates through a housing crisis, journals through a pandemic, or microdoses through ecological collapse is doing exactly what the system requires.

Californication works through several interlocking mechanisms:

  • Genre calibration tunes expectations — formatting crisis as personal narrative rather than structural condition, so that job loss becomes a “pivot” and collapse becomes a “healing journey”
  • Zen fascism enforces which affects are legitimate — pathologizing distress and eroticizing detachment, so that the serene subject is authorized to speak and the distressed subject is offered therapy
  • Harm governance collapses care into risk management — so that people who have lost faith in the state reproduce its administrative logic with each other
  • Socially ontogenic media distributes inhabitable subject positions — modeling how to be inside contradiction without breaking

The subject this produces is the savior-slave subject: simultaneously responsible for managing global crises (savior) and structurally incapable of transforming the conditions that produce them (slave). Collapse is not hidden. It is narrativized, monetized, and aestheticized — sustained because it is wanted.

On platforms, this produces the neurotic platformal intellectual: a subject incited into performing expertise under conditions of anxiety, whose learning under crisis is formatted by platform feeling rules into authentic-seeming content.

The system-level condition is coherent confusion: subjects confused but functional, unable to resolve what is happening but still interpreting, still engaging, still producing the sensemaking labor that sustains the system.

And the rhetorical structure that persists across all of this — across fascism, liberalism, and postliberalism alike — is fascist grammar: a six-step sequence (crisis, pure community, corrupt enemy, suffering as sacrifice, destiny, inevitability) that recurs regardless of vocabulary. The framework does not argue that liberalism is fascism. It argues that liberalism inhabits fascist grammar as its dialectical twin — countering each step while remaining bound to the structure.

The theoretical circuit

The framework synthesizes several traditions that each describe part of the circuit:

  • Michel Foucault’s governmentality and theory of confession — how power produces subjects and incites them to speak themselves into subjection. Social media industrializes this confessional logic at scale.
  • Lauren Berlant’s genre theory, cruel optimism, and crisis ordinariness — how people maintain attachment to conditions that harm them, how genres structure what counts as crisis and resolution, and how ongoing instability is absorbed into daily life.
  • Arlie Russell Hochschild’s feeling rules and emotional labor — how institutions prescribe not just behavior but affect. Californication extends this from the workplace to the whole of liberal subjectivity.
  • Norbert Wiener, Stafford Beer, and W. Ross Ashby’s cybernetics — how systems maintain stability through distributed feedback without central control. Californication is, in cybernetic terms, a feedback mechanism: the subject detects crisis, processes it affectively, and returns to functional participation.
  • Elizabeth Povinelli’s late liberalism and quasi-events — how governance demands endurance rather than resolution, and how disturbances are contained before they can become events.

The contribution of cybernetic postliberalism is showing how platforms made affect governance operative at scale — closing a circuit that these theorists each described in parts.

What this framework is not

Cybernetic postliberalism is not a political program. It does not prescribe what should replace liberal governance. It is an analytical framework — a way of describing how the current system operates, with specific attention to the mechanisms that make it self-sustaining. It is developed in the letters-to-the-web, where it is deployed against specific cases: the tariff cycle (“Governing by confusion”), the narrativization of Stop Cop City (“Citing for containment”), the American reception of Gramsci (“The theory that survived the war”), the linguistic infrastructure of platform speech (“Marked speech, borrowed grammar”), and the collapse of care into risk management (“Between care and control”).

Where to go from here

The term definitions provide detailed treatments of each concept, grounded in the upstream theorists they synthesize. The primary sources — the letters-to-the-web and working notes — show the framework deployed against real cases. Start with whichever pulls you.
