The Ecclesial Recurrence

2026-04-27 Californication is recurrent across the modern pastoral apparatus; the medieval Christian church is its most thoroughly developed prior institutional form. What is recurrent: continuous calibration of inner life through pastoral relations and specific affective genres (acedia, spes, studiositas, curiositas). What is changed: the energetic substrate is inverted, latency has collapsed, protected interiors are gone, the contradictions metabolized differ.

Californication — the operation by which structural contradictions are formatted as personally manageable affect — is a recurrent operation of the modern pastoral apparatus. The contemporary form is not the original; the medieval Christian church carried a structurally analogous apparatus across earlier centuries, and the church-pastoral is californication’s most thoroughly developed prior institutional form.

What follows takes up the recurrence on its own terms. The aim is not historical-comparative scholarship of the church (extensive scholarship exists; cybernetic postliberalism draws on it rather than replacing it). The aim is to specify what is recurrent, what is changed, and why the relation is recurrence rather than continuity or analogy.

¶What the church-pastoral apparatus did

The medieval Christian church, in its institutional operation across roughly a millennium of European life, performed a comprehensive set of subject-formation operations. Foucault’s analysis of the Christian pastorate (1981, 2007), Talal Asad’s anthropological analysis of religious discipline (1993), Peter Brown’s historical analysis of the early-Christian formation of bodies and inner lives (1988), Giorgio Agamben’s analysis of monastic rules as form-of-life (2013), and Ivan Illich’s analysis of medieval reading-practice (1993) each develop one register of what the apparatus performed. Read together, they specify one continuing operation.

The apparatus’s operations included:

The continuous calibration of inner life. Through the practices of confession, examination of conscience, the daily and weekly liturgical cycle, and the elaborated devotional regimes of the laity, the church-pastoral apparatus performed continuous calibration of subjects’ inner states. Subjects were trained to attend to their own dispositions, to identify particular states (the seven capital vices and their daughters, the conditions of charity, hope, and faith), to bring those states to the pastoral apparatus for processing, and to receive the apparatus’s calibration in return. This is not metaphor for what the apparatus did; it is the apparatus’s actual operation.

The institutionalization of pastoral relations. The parish priest, the bishop, the confessor, the monastic rule-keeper, the spiritual director — these were not informal advisors. They were institutionalized roles within an apparatus that held the relations across generations. The seal of confession (the institutional capacity to keep what passed between confessor and penitent inside the apparatus) was not an ad hoc protection; it was the structural feature that allowed the apparatus to operate at scale. Subjects could bring their inner lives to the apparatus because the apparatus could hold them — exactly the protected interior that, as The Energetic Substrate shows, the contemporary platformal apparatus does not provide.

The specific calibration of affective genres. The medieval pastoral apparatus identified, named, and calibrated specific affective conditions. Acedia (spiritual sloth, the inability to rouse oneself to the good one knows one should pursue), spes (hope, the disposition oriented to a difficult-but-possible future good), studiositas (the disciplined love of knowledge), curiositas (the disordered cognitive appetite). These are not historical curiosities. They are the categories the apparatus used to format inner life, and the calibrations the apparatus performed were specific to these categories. A subject struggling with acedia was processed through a different pastoral protocol than a subject struggling with despair or with presumption; the apparatus had the discrimination to handle each.

The integration of practice across timescales. The pastoral apparatus operated at multiple timescales simultaneously. The daily liturgical office; the weekly mass; the seasonal cycle of feasts and fasts; the lifelong arc of catechesis, marriage, vocation, and last rites; the multi-generational continuity of the parish, the monastic foundation, the cathedral. The integration across timescales is what allowed the apparatus to format inner life as a sustained project rather than as episodic intervention.

The settlement of the constitutive contradictions. The apparatus’s metabolism was not infinite, but it had specific resources for handling contradictions internal to the medieval-Christian condition: the relation between the body and the soul, between the temporal and the eternal, between charity and merit, between freedom and grace. These were not idle theological puzzles; they were the contradictions the apparatus was structurally engineered to metabolize — the medieval-Christian counterpart to the contradictions liberalism’s apparatus metabolizes. The metabolism was performed through the pastoral relations, the sacraments, the affective genres, and the institutional infrastructures that held the practitioners together.

¶What is recurrent

What is structurally continuous across the church-pastoral and the contemporary platformal apparatuses:

The metabolism of structural contradiction through subject-formation. Both apparatuses operate by routing structural contradictions through subjects’ inner lives. The church-pastoral routed the medieval-Christian contradictions (body/soul, temporal/eternal, freedom/grace) through the inner life of the practitioner. The platformal-californicational routes the late-liberal contradictions (universalism/individualism, individualism/universalism, progress/exhaustion, equality/sovereignty — see Liberalism’s Constitutive Contradictions) through the inner life of the platformal subject. The metabolism is the apparatus’s principal function in both cases.

The calibration of specific affective genres. Both apparatuses identify, name, and process specific affective conditions. The medieval acedia and the contemporary burnout (the same exhaustion phenomenology that Han diagnoses in the Leistungssubjekt); the medieval spes and the contemporary discourse of hope-as-discipline; the medieval curiositas and the contemporary doomscrolling; the medieval studiositas and the contemporary effort to focus under conditions that systematically scatter attention. The names differ; the operations are recognizable across.

The pastoral relation as the apparatus’s principal interface. Both apparatuses operate through structured relations between practitioner and apparatus-mediator. The church-pastoral operated through priest-to-penitent, monastic-to-rule, layperson-to-spiritual-director. The platformal-californicational operates through user-to-algorithm, follower-to-influencer, client-to-therapist, member-to-coach. The relations are structurally analogous: the practitioner brings their inner life to the apparatus’s interface, the apparatus calibrates, the practitioner is reformatted by the calibration. Hochschild’s emotional labor names the contemporary specific form of this calibration.

The integration of practice into a comprehensive form of life. Both apparatuses do not just intervene episodically; they format an entire form of life. The medieval Christian’s daily round (matins, prime, terce, sext, none, vespers, compline; daily mass; confession; vocational labor) was a comprehensive structure within which the apparatus’s calibrations operated continuously. The contemporary subject’s daily round (notification, scroll, work-task, exercise, meal, content-consumption, bedtime-meditation), distributed across the platformal feedback architecture, is structurally analogous: a comprehensive cycle within which the apparatus’s calibrations operate continuously.

¶What is changed

The recurrence is not identity. The contemporary apparatus operates under conditions the medieval apparatus did not face, and the differences are consequential.

Energetic substrate. The medieval pastoral apparatus operated on a thin energetic substrate (animal-and-human-powered economy, candlelight, slow correspondence) and a rich institutional substrate (stone buildings that lasted six centuries, multi-generational vocational training, slow-cycling oral traditions). The contemporary platformal apparatus operates on a high-energy electrical-computational substrate and a thin institutional substrate (platforms with quarterly business cycles, rapid feature deprecation, no inter-generational continuity). The substrates are inverted: where the medieval had time and space and a sustainable energetic budget for slow institutional operation, the contemporary has tremendous energetic-computational power but no institutional memory longer than a venture-capital cycle. The Energetic Substrate develops the empirics.

Latency and pace. The medieval apparatus operated at the timescales of the daily round, the seasonal cycle, the lifetime arc, the multi-generational tradition. The contemporary apparatus operates at second-by-second timescales for its lowest-layer interactions, with the higher layers operating on hours-to-months — the nested feedback structure detailed in Platformal Feedback Architecture. The latency collapse is not just a quantitative change; it changes what kind of pastoral work is structurally possible. The slow integration of inner life across decades that the medieval apparatus could perform is not available to the contemporary apparatus; the rapid recalibration of affect across seconds that the contemporary apparatus performs was not available to the medieval one.

Institutional interiors. The medieval apparatus had genuinely protected institutional interiors. The seal of confession could be kept. The monastic enclosure could hold. The cathedral chapter could deliberate without market accountability. The contemporary platformal apparatus has structurally no such interiors. Every datum is in principle harvestable; every utterance in principle re-circulable; every conversation in principle archived. What the medieval apparatus could process inside its walls, the contemporary apparatus must process under continuous external visibility — the condition that produces the neurotic platformal intellectual and the coherent confusion that follows.

Subject-formations available. The medieval apparatus had a relatively narrow range of subject-formations it could recognize and process: the lay practitioner in their station, the cleric, the religious, the saint, the heretic, the unbeliever. The contemporary apparatus, by Ashby’s requisite-variety logic, must support a much wider range of subject-formations to match the variety of late-liberal subjects’ conditions. The contemporary apparatus’s market of affective genres is the requisite-variety provision the medieval apparatus did not need (and could not fund).

The constitutive contradictions metabolized. The medieval apparatus metabolized its own period’s contradictions; the contemporary apparatus metabolizes liberalism’s. The contradictions are not the same. Body-vs-soul is not universalism-vs-individualism. The operation is recurrent (the metabolism of structural contradiction through subject-formation) without the content being identical (the contradictions metabolized differ across periods).

¶Why recurrence rather than continuity

A reading that treats the contemporary apparatus as the historical continuation of the church-pastoral would be misleading. The two apparatuses are not connected by an unbroken institutional chain; the secularization documented by Charles Taylor (2007) is real, and the contemporary apparatus is not the church-pastoral with its Christian content stripped out. The relation is recurrence: the operational form recurs across institutional configurations that are not in direct historical succession.

The recurrence has structural-functional explanation rather than historical-causal explanation. Both apparatuses arose because subjects under specific structural conditions required the metabolism of contradictions through subject-formation, and the institutions that provided this metabolism were the institutions that endured. The medieval church endured for a millennium because it could perform this metabolism in the conditions of its time; the contemporary platformal apparatus has displaced earlier candidates (mass media, the welfare state, the broadcast religion of the twentieth century) because it can perform the metabolism in the conditions of its time. The continuity is functional, not lineal.

¶Why recurrence rather than analogy

A reading that treats the church-pastoral as merely analogous to the contemporary apparatus would underspecify the relation. Analogies admit dissimilarity at unspecified points; the recurrence claim is more specific. The two apparatuses share a structural form — the form Foucault analyzed as governmentality, with the addition of the cybernetic-feedback architecture the contemporary form has added — and the structural form is what makes the analysis of the contemporary apparatus methodologically supported by the well-developed analysis of the medieval one.

Foucault’s pastorate analyses (1981, 2007) are upstream: the pastorate is a transferable form, not a specifically Christian one, and the contemporary apparatus inherits the form even though it has displaced the Christian content. Asad’s anthropological analysis of religious discipline (1993) supplies the analytical resources for reading the contemporary apparatus’s disciplinary effects without smuggling Christian-theological content. Agamben’s analysis of monastic form-of-life (2013) supplies the analytical resources for reading the contemporary apparatus’s comprehensive-life formatting.

The specific extension is to apply these analytical resources to the contemporary apparatus while taking seriously what is changed by the energetic-substrate and platformal-architectural conditions. The recurrence is not analogy; it is the application of well-developed analytical apparatus to a new institutional configuration that exhibits the same structural form.

¶What this means for analysis

The recurrence has methodological consequences. The substantial analytical apparatus developed for the church-pastoral (Foucault, Asad, Brown, Agamben, Illich, Aquinas’s own categories) is available when analyzing the contemporary apparatus. The categories of acedia, spes, studiositas, and curiositas are not historical curiosities but live diagnostic categories under contemporary conditions, recoverable by reading the contemporary apparatus through the analytical resources the church-pastoral developed.

This is one of cybernetic postliberalism’s signature methodological moves: the contemporary apparatus is read with the analytical apparatus developed for prior institutional forms, not because the prior forms are the contemporary form’s parents in any direct sense, but because the analytical apparatus is structurally adequate to what is being analyzed. The recurrence makes the older analytical apparatus contemporarily useful.

¶What follows

With the ecclesial recurrence specified, the structural account is in place: the contemporary apparatus is the latest configuration of a recurrent pastoral form, operating under conditions (energetic-substrate, platformal architecture) that the prior configurations did not face. Cybernetic Postliberalism in Context positions the analysis relative to adjacent schools of postliberal critique. Methods of Cybernetic-Postliberal Analysis specifies how to perform the analysis on specific cases. The closure-claim (The Operational Closure of Late-Liberal Governance) sits structurally beside this text: the closure is what makes the recurrence’s contemporary form distinct, since the medieval pastoral apparatus was not operationally closed in the same sense — its institutional interiors were thicker and its couplings looser.
