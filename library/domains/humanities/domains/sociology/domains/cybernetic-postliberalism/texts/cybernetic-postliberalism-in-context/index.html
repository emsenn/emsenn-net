Cybernetic Postliberalism in Context

2026-04-27 How cybernetic postliberalism stands relative to right- and left-postliberalism, affect theory, governmentality, and settler-colonial studies.

Cybernetic postliberalism is positioned here relative to adjacent schools of postliberal critique. The aim is twofold. First, to specify what cybernetic postliberalism shares with each adjacent school, where it diverges, and what its specific contribution is. Second, to place its distinctive moves — the cybernetic-systems framing, the closure claim, the energetic-substrate presupposition, the recurrence-with-the-church-pastoral analysis — against the broader landscape, so that its contribution can be evaluated rather than treated as one critical idiom among many.

What follows is honest about disagreement. Cybernetic postliberalism is not equivalent to its adjacencies; if it were, it would not need to exist. The differences are stated, with arguments, in each section.

¶Right-postliberalism (Deneen, Vermeule, Ahmari)

A substantial school of right-postliberal thought has emerged in the last decade. Patrick Deneen’s Why Liberalism Failed (2018) is the canonical philosophical-historical statement; Adrian Vermeule’s Common Good Constitutionalism (2022) is the legal-theoretical program; Sohrab Ahmari’s journalism develops the political-cultural register. The school argues that liberalism’s anthropology — the propertied possessive individual, the contractual-consenting subject, the autonomous-rights-bearer — is a mistake about what humans are, and that the political-cultural pathologies of contemporary life follow from operating institutions on this mistaken anthropology. The proposed alternative is some configuration of the common good tradition (Catholic-natural-law, Aristotelian-virtue, integralist-political), with the family, the church, and the local community as the principal subject-formative institutions.

Cybernetic postliberalism agrees with the right-postliberal tendency that liberalism’s constitutive anthropology is a particular ethnoclass’s anthropology rather than a universal description of humans, and that contemporary pathologies cannot be explained without registering this. It also agrees that the common-good traditions have analytical resources contemporary liberal political thought has lost; this is why the analysis draws on Aquinas and the Catholic-natural-law tradition for diagnostic categories (acedia, curiositas, studiositas, spes).

Three structural disagreements. First, the common-good tradition is itself one of the prior institutional configurations of the pastoral apparatus — the medieval Christian church performed californication-functions in its own register (analyzed in The Ecclesial Recurrence), and a return to that institutional form would not exit the operation but would reconfigure it. Second, the right-postliberal alternative typically presupposes the existence of stable cultural traditions (the family, the church, the local community) whose stability is itself produced by the genring and genre-coherence operations cybernetic postliberalism analyzes; the right-postliberal program tends to treat these stabilities as natural rather than as products of the same apparatus it critiques. Third, the right-postliberal program does not register the energetic substrate; the institutional regimes the program proposes were possible under prior energetic conditions and may not be feasible under current ones.

¶Left-postliberalism (Brown, Streeck, Mouffe)

A parallel left-postliberal tendency has developed alongside the right one. Wendy Brown’s Undoing the Demos (2015) and In the Ruins of Neoliberalism (2019) are canonical; Wolfgang Streeck’s Buying Time (2014) supplies the structural-historical register; Chantal Mouffe’s agonistic political-theoretical program develops the political-philosophical alternative. The school argues that liberalism’s specifically neoliberal configuration has hollowed out the political domain in which structural contradictions could historically have been negotiated, that the resulting political-cultural condition produces the authoritarian formations the right-postliberals respond to, and that the response is not the right-postliberal turn to common-good integralism but the rebuilding of robust democratic-political life.

Cybernetic postliberalism agrees with much of the left-postliberal diagnosis. The dissolution of the political domain, the operation of neoliberal rationality, the structural-historical account of the post-1970s crisis-management regime — these are largely consistent. Brown, Streeck, and the broader left-postliberal literature supply institutional analysis used directly; the liberalism legitimacy crisis is the term that anchors this register.

The substantive extension is the cybernetic-systems framing and the platformal-architectural account that the left-postliberal tendency does not generally develop. The left-postliberal account tends to treat the apparatus’s operation as the result of policy choices and ideological-political formations; the structural-cybernetic and energetic dimensions show the contemporary configuration as more than a contingent policy outcome. The left-postliberal program also tends to treat democratic-political reconstruction as a structurally available option in a way the closure claim places under question. The substantive disagreement: the left-postliberal hope for democratic reconstruction may itself be metabolizable as a coherent subject-position the apparatus can absorb without changing its operation.

¶Affect-theoretical critique (Berlant, Hochschild, Cvetkovich)

The most direct upstream-adjacent school is affect theory, with Lauren Berlant, Arlie Hochschild, and Ann Cvetkovich as the principal figures cybernetic postliberalism draws on. The school analyzes how subjects’ affective lives are organized by structural conditions; how attachments to particular forms of life persist under conditions of structural disintegration (Berlant’s cruel optimism); how feeling is produced through institutional rules (Hochschild’s feeling rules and emotional labor); and how depression, anxiety, and burnout are political-cultural conditions rather than purely individual pathologies.

Affect theory’s central claims are constitutive resources rather than adjacencies. Berlant’s genre is the genre operative in genring and ontogenring; Hochschild’s feeling rules are part of the apparatus’s calibration mechanism; the affect-theoretical reading of late-liberal subjectivity is the closest immediate ancestor of cybernetic postliberalism’s reading.

Three specific extensions: the cybernetic-systems framing of the apparatus’s operations as feedback architecture; the closure claim that takes the affect-theoretical analysis to its structural conclusion (every coherent subject-position is metabolizable, including the affect-theoretical subject-position); and the energetic-substrate presupposition that grounds the affect-theoretical reading in material conditions affect theory tends not to address. These extensions do not contradict affect theory; they continue it in directions the school’s own resources do not always take.

¶Foucauldian governmentality studies (Rose, Lemke, Walters)

Substantial analytical apparatus is inherited from Foucauldian governmentality studies. Nikolas Rose’s Powers of Freedom (1999), Thomas Lemke’s work on biopolitics, William Walters’s analysis of governance — these supply the institutional-analytical apparatus used to read late-liberal subject-formation. The Foucauldian school’s analysis of how power operates through the production of self-governing subjects, of how confessional infrastructure incites self-disclosure, of how disciplinary regimes produce normalized populations — these are foundational.

The specific extension beyond Foucauldian governmentality studies is the cybernetic-systems register and the closure claim. Foucauldian governmentality studies, as developed by Rose and others, tends to be agnostic on whether the apparatus’s operation is structurally exhaustive or whether resistance, refusal, and counter-conduct produce structurally meaningful exteriors. The closure claim takes a stronger position: under contemporary platformal-energetic conditions, the apparatus is operationally closed in Luhmann’s specific sense, and the resistance, refusal, and counter-conduct registered by the Foucauldian apparatus are themselves metabolized as coherent subject-positions within the closure.

¶Settler-colonial studies and indigenous-critical thought (Wolfe, Coulthard, Simpson, Tuck and Yang)

Settler-colonial studies and the broader indigenous-critical tradition are constitutive resources. Patrick Wolfe’s analysis of settler colonialism as structure rather than event, Glen Coulthard’s analysis of the politics of recognition, Audra Simpson’s analysis of refusal, Tuck and Yang’s “Decolonization Is Not a Metaphor,” Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz’s Indigenous Peoples’ History, the broader work of Leanne Betasamosake Simpson, Nick Estes, and Vine Deloria Jr. — these supply analytical apparatus that is foundational rather than supplementary, developed in Settlerism and the Cybernetic Subject.

The relation is one of analytical-conceptual debt without claim to be doing the same work. Cybernetic postliberalism is a sociology written by a settler-position scholar; it cannot perform the cultural-political work that indigenous resurgence performs from inside indigenous traditions. What it can do is take the analytical apparatus indigenous-critical thought has developed and use it, with attribution and accountability, to read structural conditions the apparatus’s analytical resources illuminate. The temporal middle is the most explicit example: drawn directly from Wolfe, Simpson, Vizenor, and the broader literature, and treated as constitutive.

The specific contribution to this conversation is modest. It offers a reading of how the settler-colonial substrate, the platformal architecture, and the energetic conditions interact to produce the contemporary apparatus’s specific shape. It does not propose a politics; the politics indigenous-critical thought already develops.

¶Accelerationism (Land, Srnicek and Williams)

A different adjacency. Right-accelerationism, in Nick Land’s lineage, treats the apparatus’s operation as the unfolding of a teleological process toward a post-human destination and embraces the acceleration of platformal-energetic intensity as the way to that destination. Left-accelerationism, in Srnicek and Williams’s Inventing the Future (2015), treats the apparatus’s operation as available to repurposing for post-capitalist ends through political-technical interventions on the platform-economic substrate.

Cybernetic postliberalism disagrees with both accelerationist tendencies in a structural way. The right-accelerationist embrace of the platformal-energetic intensity ignores the energetic-substrate constraint; the contemporary apparatus’s operation is not an unfolding teleology but a configuration that the energetic substrate increasingly cannot fund. The left-accelerationist program of repurposing the platform-infrastructure for post-capitalist ends does not address the operational closure; the platform’s feedback architecture is not a neutral substrate available to alternative content but a structural form that metabolizes whatever content it carries.

¶Frankfurt-school inheritance (Adorno-Honneth-Habermas)

A more distant adjacency. The Frankfurt school’s inheritance — through Habermas’s communicative-action program, Honneth’s recognition-based critique, the broader critical-theory program — addresses many of the same structural conditions but with different analytical apparatus. The Frankfurt-school program tends to be normatively-philosophical (aiming at reconstructing the conditions of legitimate democratic life) where cybernetic postliberalism is structurally-descriptive.

The relation is selective inheritance. Habermas’s Legitimation Crisis is drawn on directly; Walter Benjamin’s adjacent work substantially, especially weak messianic power, which figures in the closure-text’s analysis of practices that may survive metabolization. The Habermasian or Honnethian normative-philosophical program is not generally taken up; it functions as a metabolizable subject-position the apparatus can absorb, and the closure claim places the recognition-based critique under the same structural condition as other coherent subject-positions.

¶What cybernetic postliberalism specifically contributes

Across these adjacencies, the specific contributions can be summarized:

The cybernetic-systems framing. No adjacent school applies the technical apparatus of Wiener, Beer, and Ashby to late-liberal governance with operational rigor. The cybernetic framing converts affect-theoretical and Foucauldian-governmentality observations into operational claims that can be checked against the engineering of actual systems — see Platformal Feedback Architecture and the cybernetics terms (viable system model, requisite variety, operational closure, ultrastability).

The closure claim. No adjacent school states the closure claim in Luhmann’s specific operational-closure sense. The closure claim is the load-bearing structural contribution; the other claims sharpen or follow from it.

The energetic-substrate presupposition. No adjacent school explicitly presupposes the energetic-substrate analysis. The presupposition gives the historicization (californication has intensified now) its specific anchor.

The recurrence with the church-pastoral. No adjacent school reads the contemporary apparatus as recurrent with the medieval Christian pastoral apparatus in the structurally specific sense developed here. Foucault’s pastorate analysis is the canonical upstream; the extension into operational specifics about californication is the original contribution.

The integration of multiple registers. Affect-theoretical, Foucauldian-governmentality, settler-colonial, ecclesial, cybernetic-systems, energetic-substrate — these are typically developed in separate scholarly conversations. The specific contribution is the integration: showing how these registers interact to produce the contemporary apparatus’s shape.

¶What follows

Methods of Cybernetic-Postliberal Analysis takes up how to perform the analysis on specific cases. With the methods specified, the monograph is complete, and the principal-cases in the school’s index become readable as cybernetic postliberalism worked against specific empirical material.
