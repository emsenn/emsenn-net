How to Locate the Temporal Middle Being Eliminated

2026-04-27 A working procedure for locating the temporal middle in a specific case where it is being eliminated: identify the binary the structure offers, map the temporal middle the binary forecloses, trace how the structure handles temporal-middle subjects (invisibility, reframing, recoding), and note what the elimination accomplishes.

This is a working procedure for locating the temporal middle in a specific case where it is being eliminated. The procedure analyzes how a structure offers subjects a binary (traditional vs. modern, authentic vs. assimilated, pure vs. corrupted) that systematically erases the actual position every living subject of the addressed people occupies — the position of inheriting and continuing a tradition without freezing or abandoning it.

This how-to assumes familiarity with temporal middle, genring, genre coherence, and Settlerism and the Cybernetic Subject.

¶What you’ll need

  • A specific case to analyze. Be concrete: a national-cultural narrative, a museum exhibit, a film, a policy document, a media discourse, a school curriculum’s treatment of a people.
  • Enough background knowledge of the specific people the case addresses to identify the actual living variety the case forecloses. If you do not have this background, consult sources from the people themselves first.
  • 60-90 minutes for a careful analysis.
  • A notebook for the binary, the middle, and the elimination operation.

¶Step 1: Identify the addressed people

Identify which specific people the case addresses or depicts. Be specific. Not “Indigenous peoples” but “the Lakota” or “the Pomo,” not “Asian people” but “the Hmong,” not “Middle Eastern people” but “the Kurds.”

If the case addresses multiple peoples, treat each separately. If the case deliberately blurs distinctions between peoples (treating “Native Americans” as a single category), this blurring is itself a finding — note it, but you may need to pick one specific people to do the analysis carefully.

¶Step 2: Identify the binary the case offers

Map the binary the case offers the addressed people. The structure typically presents two positions as exhaustive:

  • The traditional pole: the people as they (supposedly) were before contact, before modernity, before assimilation. The genre figure of the pre-contact, pre-modern, pre-disrupted form.
  • The assimilated pole: the people as they should become or have become — modernized, integrated, assimilated, indistinguishable from the dominant culture in the relevant respects.

Common binary forms:

  • The Noble Indian / the Assimilated Indian. The Lakota appear in the case either as pre-contact figures (traditional dress, traditional practice, sacred ceremony) or as fully Americanized figures (contemporary dress, contemporary practice, no visible indigenous markers).
  • The Mountain Kurd / the Persianized/Turkified Kurd. The Kurd appears either as the pastoral mountain figure or as the assimilated citizen of Turkey, Iraq, Iran, or Syria.
  • The Hearty Bavarian / the Modern German. The Bavarian appears either in the alpine-folk figure with lederhosen and folk festival, or as a generic German indistinguishable from any other German.
  • The Authentic [Religious Tradition] / the Modern Secular [Subject of That Tradition]. Members of the tradition appear either as traditional/orthodox figures or as fully secular subjects who have left the tradition.

Write down the specific binary the case offers and the genre figures that fill each pole.

¶Step 3: Map the temporal middle the binary forecloses

The temporal middle is the position of inheriting and continuing the tradition without freezing or abandoning it. Every living subject occupies this position; the case’s binary cannot accommodate it.

Map what the binary forecloses for the specific people. Three features mark the temporal middle:

  • Inheritance without freeze. The subject inherits the tradition (language, practices, kinship, place-relation) without treating the inheritance as a fixed cast to be reproduced unchanged. The tradition is alive precisely because it is continuing — and continuing means it changes.
  • Engagement without abandonment. The subject engages with material from outside the tradition (other intellectual traditions, technologies, languages, formal systems) without treating the engagement as exit from the inherited tradition.
  • Transformation as continuance. The subject transforms the tradition in the act of continuing it. There is no continuance that does not transform; there is no transformation that, if it remains within the tradition, is not also continuance.

For the specific people the case addresses, identify what each feature looks like. The Lakota mathematician inheriting Lakota intellectual traditions while engaging with European mathematics. The Bavarian Marxist organizing in alpine villages. The Kurdish anarchist filmmaker working in Kurmanji and contemporary international cinema. The Pennsylvania Dutch software engineer.

Write down the specific living subjects who occupy the temporal middle for the addressed people. The more specific you can be, the better.

¶Step 4: Trace how the case handles temporal-middle subjects

The case’s structure cannot directly accommodate temporal-middle subjects. Trace what the case does about them.

Common moves:

  • Invisibility. The case simply does not depict temporal-middle subjects. The depicted Lakota are pre-contact figures; the actual contemporary Lakota mathematicians, urban-cosmopolitan Lakota, Lakota filmmakers do not appear. Note the absence.
  • Reframing as not-really-members. The case acknowledges temporal-middle subjects but reframes them as not really members of the people. The Lakota mathematician is “really” an academic who happens to be Lakota, not a Lakota intellectual. The Bavarian Marxist is “really” a German socialist who happens to be from Bavaria.
  • Reframing as tragic loss. The case treats temporal-middle subjects as evidence of the tradition’s loss. The contemporary urban-cosmopolitan member is what happens when the tradition fails to maintain itself; the temporal-middle position is read as the absence of authenticity rather than as continuation.
  • Reframing as comic exception. The case treats temporal-middle subjects as quirky exceptions. The Lakota Cannes-winning filmmaker is an interesting curiosity, not a representative member of the people.
  • Reframing as confirmation. The case reframes temporal-middle subjects so that their existence confirms the binary. The Lakota mathematician “rediscovers their roots” through their academic work; the Bavarian Marxist’s organizing is read as expressing Bavarian-folk values; the temporal middle gets recoded as a route back to the traditional pole.

Write down which moves the case uses, with specific examples from the case.

¶Step 5: Identify what the elimination accomplishes

The elimination of the temporal middle accomplishes structural work for the case. Identify the work.

Common functions:

  • Authentication of the depicting culture’s project. The depicting culture (settler-colonial, national, imperial) needs the people to remain in the genre figure to anchor its claim to access authentic folk life. Temporal-middle subjects refuse this work; their elimination preserves the depicting culture’s authentication.
  • Justification of the depicting culture’s relation. The depicting culture’s relation to the people (settlement, governance, dispossession, integration) is structurally easier to justify if the people are presented as either pre-contact (and so eliminable as residue) or assimilated (and so eliminable as continuance). Temporal-middle subjects complicate the justification.
  • Maintenance of genre coherence. The depicting culture’s narrative integrity depends on the genre figure remaining stable. Temporal-middle subjects are narrative disruptions; their elimination restores the narrative’s coherence.
  • Reproduction of the structural settler-colonial / national / imperial apparatus. The elimination of the temporal middle is one of the operations through which settler colonialism, nationalism, or imperialism continues — not as an event but as a structure. See Settlerism and the Cybernetic Subject for the structural register.

Write down what the elimination does in the case’s broader political-cultural context.

¶Step 6: Trace the registers in which the elimination operates

The elimination of the temporal middle operates across multiple registers. For the case, identify which registers are active:

  • Legal-jurisdictional register. The legal status of the people (citizenship, sovereignty, treaty-rights, recognition) is structured to admit only the binary’s poles. Temporal-middle subjects find their legal claims inadmissible.
  • Epistemic register. The knowledge-system within which the people’s existence is registered (anthropology, history, political science, cultural studies) is structured to admit only the binary’s poles. Temporal-middle subjects produce knowledge that does not fit the discipline’s frame.
  • Linguistic register. The vocabulary available for talking about the people is structured to admit only the binary’s poles. Temporal-middle subjects find that the available vocabulary does not name them; new vocabularies have to be invented.
  • Architectural and physical register. The physical infrastructure (museums, monuments, cultural centers, public spaces) materially preserves only the binary’s poles. Temporal-middle subjects find no architectural form for their existence.
  • Affective register. The feelings authorized within the case’s discourse (admiration for the traditional, indifference toward the assimilated, discomfort or hostility toward the temporal-middle) sustain the binary at the affective level.

Write down which registers are operating in the specific case.

¶Step 7: Write the analysis

Compile your findings into a structured analytical statement:

  1. The case: what it is, when and where, who its audience is.
  2. The addressed people: specifically named.
  3. The binary: the two poles the case offers, with the genre figures that fill each.
  4. The temporal middle: the position the binary forecloses, with specific contemporary subjects who occupy it.
  5. The elimination operation: how the case handles temporal-middle subjects (invisibility, reframing, recoding).
  6. The structural function: what the elimination accomplishes for the case’s broader project.
  7. The registers: which registers the elimination operates in.

The analysis should be specific, evidenced from the case, and grounded in actual knowledge of the addressed people’s contemporary living variety.

¶What this analysis does not say

The analysis is structural; it does not say the addressed people are obligated to occupy any particular position. The temporal-middle position is a description of where actually-existing subjects of the people stand, not a prescription for what subjects should do. Subjects who do choose more traditional-pole or more assimilated-pole positions are not failing the temporal-middle analysis; the binary is the case’s structure, not theirs.

The analysis does not say the case’s authors are individually responsible for the elimination. Structural eliminations operate through individual artifacts, productions, and policies that their authors may not consciously intend. The structural reading is descriptive.

The analysis does not say what should replace the case. What political-cultural work would address the elimination is a different question that the structural analysis informs but does not answer.

¶Common pitfalls

Mistaking the case’s intent for its operation. The case’s authors may have intended to honor or include the people; the structural elimination of the temporal middle can occur regardless of intent. Track what the case does, not what it claims to do.

Mistaking the existence of one temporal-middle subject in the case for non-elimination. A case can include a token temporal-middle subject (the Lakota lawyer who appears in one scene) and still perform the elimination at the structural level. Look at the structural pattern, not the inclusion-or-exclusion of any single example.

Generalizing from settler-colonial cases to non-settler-colonial cases. The temporal-middle analysis was developed primarily in settler-colonial contexts (Wolfe, Simpson, Coulthard, Vizenor). The structural pattern recurs in non-settler-colonial cases (national-cultural narratives, religious-tradition narratives, ethnic-cultural narratives), but the registers and stakes differ. Be specific about which structural condition operates in the case.

Forgetting that the binary’s poles are themselves genre figures. The traditional pole and the assimilated pole are not the people in their actual variety; they are genre figures the case produces and circulates. The genring analysis applies to each pole separately.

Reading the analysis as moral indictment. The structural reading is descriptive sociology. It tells you what the case does at the structural-political level; it does not deliver a moral verdict on the case’s authors or audiences.

¶Where to take the analysis next

The temporal-middle analysis often opens into adjacent analyses:

  • The genring of the genre figures. For each pole of the binary, perform the genring analysis on the figure that fills it.
  • The settler-colonial register. If the case operates in settler-colonial conditions, Settlerism and the Cybernetic Subject develops the structural-historical context.
  • The fascist-grammar potential. If the case is part of a political-cultural movement, the binary may be deployed within a fascist grammar sequence; trace the deployment.
  • The genre-coherence pressure. The case sits within a broader genre coherence regime that maintains the binary against centrifugal pressure; trace what happens to subjects who refuse the regime.
  • The practices that sustain the temporal middle against the elimination. Audra Simpson’s refusal, Vizenor’s survivance, the broader Indigenous-resurgence tradition develop substantial answers; the school draws on these without claiming to systematize them.
