Genring and Ontogenring

2026-04-27 Genring is the reduction of a living people to a fixed literary type through selection, codification, and substitution. Ontogenring extends the same logic up to the individual subject, who is addressed in a genre and expected to feel and act inside its prescribed arc. Attribution is itself an ontogenic act.

The Energetic Substrate established the material substrate cybernetic postliberalism presupposes (declining net energy); Liberalism’s Constitutive Contradictions specified the input the apparatus metabolizes (universalism without individualism, individualism without universalism, progress without exhaustion, equality without sovereignty). What follows is the first of the specifically subject-forming operations. The apparatus does not metabolize contradictions through abstract subjects; it metabolizes them through subjects formatted in specific genres. The genre is not external to the subject — it is one of the conditions that constitutes who the subject is. Two operations — genring and ontogenring — produce the subjects on whom californication then performs its specific work.

¶Genring: the reduction of a people to a type

Genring is the reduction of a living people to a fixed literary type. Not a metaphor for stereotyping — a specific political operation with identifiable inputs, processes, and outputs.

The input is a living people — a community with multivocal practices, internal disagreements, ongoing transformations, members who depart from the community’s norms in various directions, traditions that adapt themselves to changing conditions. The process has three steps. Selection extracts visually striking, emotionally resonant features from the people’s life — dress, diet, habitat, ritual, occupational signature. Codification assembles those features into a stable character template, which is then circulated through novels, performances, images, festivals, and educational materials until it becomes self-reinforcing across the receiving culture’s institutions. Substitution completes the operation: the template replaces the people, so that encountering the people in any subsequent context means encountering the character template the discourse has produced.

The output is a genre figure: a character template complete with costume, setting, emotional register, and narrative arc. The Noble Indian, the Hearty Bavarian, the Mountain Kurd, the salt-of-the-earth rural American, the dignified African elder, the wise Asian sage — each is a genre figure produced by the same operation. Each serves a similar function: it guarantees that authentic folk life exists somewhere, so that the nation that consumes the figure can claim a relation to authenticity it has not actually maintained in its own ongoing life.

Genring is upstream of fascism, but not specific to fascism. The same operation is performed in liberal-multicultural framings, where the genre figure is admired rather than degraded but is no less a substitution for the actual people. Fascism’s specific contribution is not the production of genre figures (that is genring’s general work) but the insistence on their stability. When a member of a genred people deviates from the assigned genre — a Lakota mathematician, a Bavarian Marxist, a Kurdish anarchist filmmaker — the deviation is read as a problem for the genre rather than as the actual person. Genre coherence names the structural pressure that produces this reading; fascist grammar names the rhetorical structure within which genre coherence’s exclusions are legitimated. The position the operation eliminates — the temporal middle, of inheriting and continuing a tradition without either freezing it or abandoning it — is the position every actual living subject of a genred people occupies, and is precisely what the genre cannot accommodate.

The theoretical lineage is long. James Scott’s Seeing Like a State (1998) gives the broader analysis of how state apparatuses simplify legibly: the genre figure is the narrative-engine version of what the state does to populations through cadastral mapping, surname imposition, and standardized measurement. Eric Hobsbawm and Terence Ranger’s The Invention of Tradition (1983) traces the institutional infrastructures (folk museums, costume associations, festival calendars, marketing apparatuses) that maintain genre figures across generations. Edward Said’s Orientalism (1978) is the canonical analysis of a region’s reduction to a stable cast of types under colonial-imperial discourse. Mikhail Bakhtin’s heteroglossia (1981) names the multivocal condition genring collapses into monologue. Stuart Hall (1997) develops the cultural-studies apparatus for analyzing the production and circulation of types.

Genring’s specific work in cybernetic postliberalism is to produce the input subjects on whom californication will then operate. A subject who has been genred — reduced, in the discourse that addresses them, to a stable type — is a different kind of subject from one who has not. The genred subject is addressed in a genre, expected to feel and act in genre-appropriate ways, and the gap between their genre-formatting and their actual conditions is the gap the apparatus’s affective machinery is engineered to manage. The settler-colonial register develops this most thoroughly: the Californian projection is the historical site at which the operation has been most extensively iterated and exported, and settlerism is the structural condition under which genring’s specific outputs (the Noble Indian, the salt-of-the-earth American) have been produced.

¶Ontogenring: the extension to the individual

Genring as Scott, Hobsbawm, and Said describe it operates principally at the level of peoples: communities, ethnoclasses, regional populations. Ontogenring names the extension of the same logic up to the level of the individual subject — the constitution of subject-positions whose very being is generated by the genre they inhabit.

The term carries specific weight. Onto- (being) plus genre plus -ring (the operation): the operation by which the subject’s being is generated by genre. This is not interpellation in Althusser’s sense (where the subject is hailed and recognizes themselves in the hailing); it is closer to Hacking’s (2002) dynamic nominalism (where new kinds of person come into existence through classification) but specifically focused on how genre-classification operates on individuals. It is also adjacent to Butler’s (1990) performativity (where the subject is iteratively produced through citation), distinguished by emphasizing the expectation-and-arc dimension that genre supplies — not just citation but a structured affective-narrative form that the subject is formatted to inhabit.

The mechanism, in concentrated form: genres are selected, codified, and substituted at the level of subjects rather than peoples. A subject is addressed in a genre — the unhappy housewife, the working-class striver, the weekend-warrior dad, the wellness-girlfriend, the burnt-out activist, the resilient survivor, the woke ally, the based gentleman, the hustler, the digital nomad, the lonely bachelor, the empowered single mother. Each is a genre with a costume, a register, a narrative arc, and characteristic affects. The subject addressed in the genre is expected to feel and act in genre-appropriate ways; the gap between the subject’s actual conditions and the genre’s prescribed arc is, again, what the apparatus is engineered to metabolize.

The lineage runs through several traditions cybernetic postliberalism synthesizes. Lauren Berlant’s The Female Complaint (2008) and Cruel Optimism (2011) develop genre as a structure of expectation that calibrates affect: subjects maintain attachment to the arc the genre prescribes even when conditions make the arc’s promises unredeemable. This is the most direct upstream source. Althusser’s interpellation supplies the older mechanism that ontogenring extends. Butler’s performativity supplies the iterative production of the subject. Hacking’s dynamic nominalism supplies the historical-ontological frame in which new kinds of person come into being through classification. Goffman’s dramaturgical analysis supplies the staged-identity register. Foucault’s disciplinary regimes supply the institutional layer through which ontogenring proceeds.

What ontogenring contributes beyond these adjacent terms is the explicit framing of the subject-position as genre: a structured affective-narrative form, with an associated cast of supporting characters, a setting, a register, and an arc. The genre is not just a category the subject is filed under (Hacking’s nominalism). It is not just a citation the subject is iterating (Butler’s performativity). It is a story the subject is in, with all the structural features stories have, and the subject is expected to behave as a character in that story would behave. The principal subject-formations the apparatus rewards — the savior-slave subject, the neurotic platformal intellectual, the zen-fascist detached observer, the harm-governance administrator — are ontogenric productions that subsequent texts trace in their specific operations.

¶Attribution as ontogenic act

Attributing is itself an ontogenic act. To attribute a feeling, an action, or an intent to a subject is to position them within a genre that frames the attribution. The attribution is not neutral observation; it formats the subject toward the genre that makes the attribution intelligible.

This matters operationally. The platformal apparatus is engineered to attribute, at scale, continuously: the algorithmic feed makes inferences about what the user wants, will engage with, will buy, will vote for; the recommendation engine attributes preferences and serves content shaped by those attributions; the targeted-advertising apparatus attributes states (financial precarity, loneliness, political animus) and serves accordingly. Each attribution is an ontogenic act — it produces the subject as the kind of person to whom the next thing is appropriately served, and the subject’s response to that service further shapes the next attribution. This is the loop Platformal Feedback Architecture traces in its specific cybernetic structure.

¶How genring and ontogenring relate

Genring is the upstream operation: it produces the genre figures of peoples. Ontogenring is the downstream operation: it produces the genre-positions of individual subjects. The two operations are continuous rather than separate. The figure of the Noble Indian is produced by genring as a people-level type; the same figure becomes available, through ontogenring, as a subject-position that an individual person can be addressed within (whether or not they are Lakota, Cheyenne, or any other people). Conversely, the genre-positions produced by ontogenring (the wellness-girlfriend, the based gentleman, the resilient survivor) are routinely converted, by political-cultural movements, into people-level figures that the same operation then re-projects onto living populations.

The continuity matters because no clean line can be drawn between the operations. The genring of peoples and the ontogenring of subjects are dimensions of one larger apparatus that operates continuously. Wynter’s overrepresentation of Man is the constitutive condition of both: the genre figures and genre-positions are produced against the background of a particular ethnoclass’s universalism, and the operations are intelligible only against that background.

¶Adjacent operations to distinguish

Several operations are adjacent to genring and ontogenring without being identical to them.

Stereotyping is a cognitive simplification — a heuristic by which subjects organize information about social categories. Genring is a structural-political operation that includes stereotyping but is not exhausted by it. A community with no stereotypes circulating about it can still be subject to genring through the production of an admiring genre figure (Noble Indian, Wise African Elder); the figure does not have to be derogatory to be a genring product.

Interpellation, in Althusser’s sense, is the operation by which an ideological apparatus calls a subject into being — “Hey, you!” the policeman shouts, and the subject who turns is the subject the apparatus has produced. Ontogenring extends this: the genre is not just a hailing but a structured arc the subject is expected to inhabit. The hailing is the entry-point; the genre is the form of life the entry-point opens onto.

Performativity is the iterated production of the subject through citation: gender as the repetition of citational acts. Ontogenring is structurally adjacent but adds the genre-as-arc dimension: not just the iterated cite but the structured story the cite is part of. A performative reading of the unhappy-housewife genre would emphasize the iterated acts that produce the gendered subject; an ontogenring reading emphasizes the arc the iteration is moving the subject through and the gap between the arc and the actual conditions.

¶What follows

With genring and ontogenring specified, californication becomes legible: the operation by which the gap between the subject’s genre-formatting and their actual conditions is metabolized as personally manageable affect. The subject formatted in a genre whose arc cannot, under contemporary conditions, be fulfilled is the subject californication is engineered to manage. California as Laboratory traces the historical site at which the operation has been most thoroughly iterated and exported; Platformal Feedback Architecture specifies the technical infrastructure that makes the metabolism continuous at planetary scale; The Ecclesial Recurrence reads californication as recurrent with the medieval Christian pastoral apparatus, where genre-formatting was performed under different institutional conditions on a thinner energetic substrate.
