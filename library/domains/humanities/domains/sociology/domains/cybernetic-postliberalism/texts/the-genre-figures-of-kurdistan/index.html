The Genre Figures of Kurdistan: How Four States Produce the Mountain Warrior and Erase the Living Kurd

2026-04-02 Kurdish peoples are genred — reduced from a heteroglossic, multi-state, religiously diverse, politically sophisticated civilization to a set of consumable character templates — by four different states simultaneously. This paper traces how Iran, Turkey, Iraq, and the West each produce their own Kurdish genre figure, what those figures erase, and why the mechanism is uniquely unstable when applied to a people who keep contradicting the story.

Abstract

Genring — the reduction of a living people to a fixed literary type — is a political operation with identifiable inputs, processes, and outputs, described in the companion paper The Reduction of a People to a Type. This paper applies the framework to Kurdish peoples, who present a uniquely instructive case: they are genred not by one state but by four, each producing a different character template from the same heteroglossic civilization. Iran produces the Mountain Warrior — fierce, tribal, Aryan kin — while simultaneously destroying the language and dress the figure is costumed in. Turkey produces the Mountain Turk — denying Kurdish existence entirely, replacing the people not with a character but with a category error. Iraq produces the Jash and the Peshmerga — internal collaborator and heroic resistance fighter — splitting Kurdish identity into a binary the state can administer. The West, since 2014, produces the Feminist Ally — brave, secular, democratic, stripped of the anarchist politics that actually animate the movements it celebrates. Each genre figure erases the same thing: Kurdish heteroglossia — the coexistence within one civilization of Kurmanji and Sorani speakers, Sunni and Yezidi and Alevi believers, tribal conservatives and anarchist municipalists, dengbej storytellers and Marxist intellectuals, nomadic pastoralists and urban filmmakers. This paper traces each genre figure, names what it destroys, and argues that the Kurdish case reveals something the framework’s original cases obscure: genring is structurally unstable when applied to a people who refuse to stop being various.

1. A People, Not a Type

To begin with what the genre figures erase.

Kurdish peoples number between 30 and 45 million, dispersed across southeastern Turkey, northern Iraq, western Iran, and northern Syria, with significant diaspora communities in Germany, Sweden, the United Kingdom, and elsewhere. They speak not one language but a family: Kurmanji (the most widely spoken, written in Latin script in Turkey and Syria, Arabic script in Iraq), Sorani (dominant in Iraqi and Iranian Kurdistan, written in Arabic script), Hawrami, Zazaki, and others. The distance between Kurmanji and Sorani is roughly the distance between English and Dutch — mutual intelligibility is limited. A Kurmanji speaker from Diyarbakir and a Sorani speaker from Sulaymaniyah share a civilization, but not a grammar.

Religiously, approximately 75% of Kurds are Sunni Muslim — a fact that makes them a confessional minority in Shia Iran and positions them as permanent outsiders to the Islamic Republic’s theological self-understanding. But Kurdish religious life also includes Alevis (concentrated in central-eastern Turkey, whose syncretic practice blends Shia, Sufi, and pre-Islamic elements), Yezidis (whose monotheistic faith centers on Tawusi Melek, the Peacock Angel, and has been catastrophically mischaracterized as devil worship for centuries), Yarsanis (Ahl-e Haqq, concentrated in western Iran, with a reincarnation theology and initiatory structure), Christians, and secular communities. The genre figure is vaguely Muslim or vaguely pagan. The people contain theological traditions that predate Islam, survive Islam, and transform Islam from within.

Politically, Kurdish movements range from the tribal conservatism of the Barzani dynasty in Iraqi Kurdistan to the anarchist municipalism of Rojava’s democratic confederalism, from the Marxist-Leninist Komala in Iran to the Islamic Kurdish movements that align with various state projects. The PKK’s intellectual trajectory — from national liberation movement in the 1970s through Marxist-Leninist vanguardism to Abdullah Ocalan’s post-2000 turn toward Murray Bookchin’s libertarian municipalism (Jongerden and Akkaya 2012) — represents one of the most dramatic political-philosophical transformations of any modern movement. The genre figure has no politics. The people have more political diversity per capita than most European nations.

Economically, Kurds have been settled farmers, transhumant pastoralists, urban traders, industrial workers, and — when state violence destroyed all other options — smugglers, whose dangerous border crossings are genred as inherent criminality rather than economic adaptation to destroyed livelihoods. Intellectually, Kurdish scholars engage with every field from mathematics to film theory. Bahman Ghobadi’s films (A Time for Drunken Horses, Turtles Can Fly) brought Kurdish experience to Cannes. Amir Hassanpour’s scholarship on linguicide — the deliberate killing of a language — gave the field its conceptual vocabulary. None of this appears in any genre figure. The genre has no character for it.

This is what genring erases. Not ignorance — knowledge. Specific, detailed knowledge of a character template that functions precisely because it replaces the civilization it claims to depict.

2. Iran: The Aryan Cousin in the Mountains

Ferdowsi completed the Shahnameh around 1010 CE — a thousand years before the Brothers Grimm — giving Persian nationalism a literary codification of its folk spirit of extraordinary depth and prestige. The Shahnameh narrates the mythic and historical kings of Iran in pure Persian, deliberately purged of Arabic vocabulary. When Reza Shah Pahlavi seized power in 1925, this text was ready-made for nationalist deployment. The country was renamed from Persia to Iran — Land of the Aryans — in 1935. The Shahnameh became what Asgharzadeh (2007) calls the instrument of “Aryanist racism”: the proof that the Iranian Volk was ancient, racially distinct, linguistically pure, and continuous.

Within the Shahnameh’s mythology, the Kawa (Kaveh) story is the contested ground. Ferdowsi tells of Kawa the blacksmith who leads a rebellion against the tyrant Zahhak — but in the Persian text, the victory is completed by Prince Faridun, restoring the Kayani dynasty. Kawa is the catalyst; the dynasty is the resolution. Kurdish oral tradition tells the same story differently. Kawa is the sole agent. The youths he rescues become the ancestors of the Kurds. The mountains they flee to become Kurdistan. Newroz — the spring equinox — commemorates this liberation.

The contest over who owns Kawa is the contest over who gets to be the subject of the national story. Iranian nationalism claims Kawa as evidence of Iran’s unified ancient heritage, denying Kurdish ownership of the myth. Kurdish nationalism claims Kawa as evidence of Kurdish primordial independence, denying Persian subsumption of the story. Both readings genre the figure — selecting the features that serve the national narrative, codifying them into a stable template, circulating the template as truth. The difference is that Iran controls the state apparatus, the schools, the publishing houses, and the military.

Reza Shah’s regime produced its Kurdish genre figure through a specific contradiction. Kurds were acknowledged as Indo-European speakers — fellow Aryans, linguistically and racially kin to Persians. This incorporation served territorial claims: if Kurds are Aryans, then Kurdish lands are Iranian lands, and Kurdish autonomy is unnecessary because Kurds are already at home. At the same time, Kurdish language was classified as a “rustic and primitive dialect” unsuitable for education (Hassanpour 1992). Kurdish dress was banned. Kurdish music, dance, and literature were suppressed. Kurdish villages were renamed with Persian names.

The genre figure this produces is the Mountain Warrior: fierce, proud, untamed, close to land, simple, communal, timeless — and fundamentally part of Iran. The structural features map onto the standard genring template:

Feature The Mountain Kurd
Closeness to land Zagros mountains, flocks, highland pastures
Simplicity Tribal, pastoral, pre-modern
Communality Clan, tribal confederacy, collective identity
Timelessness “Descendants of the Medes,” “ancient mountain refugees”
Costume Sash (pshtwen), baggy trousers, turban, bright colors
Emotional register Fierce, proud, dangerous

But the emotional register is wrong for stable genring. The figure is not noble and doomed (which is safe) or hearty and enduring (which is comforting). The figure is dangerous. And it is dangerous because the living people keep doing things the genre cannot accommodate. The Mahabad Republic of 1946 — a modern Kurdish state with courts, schools, a Kurdish-language press, and a president who was a jurist — contradicted the tribal primitive. Komala’s Marxist-Leninist analysis contradicted the simple mountaineer. Every Kurdish intellectual, filmmaker, or political theorist who appears in public contradicts the genre by existing.

The result is not stable genring but oscillation. Incorporation: you are fellow Aryans, part of the family. Suppression: your language is banned, your dress is prohibited, your leaders are executed. Iran cannot sentimentalize Kurds because they live inside the borders Iran claims, and they keep interrupting the story. The genre requires aesthetic distance to stabilize. When the people are right there, refusing to be the character, the state must enforce the genre with violence rather than culture.

3. Turkey: The Erasure Without a Character

Turkey did not genre Kurds. It denied them.

From the founding of the republic through 1991, the official position was that Kurds did not exist. They were “Mountain Turks” — Turkish citizens who happened to live in mountains and speak a corrupted Turkish dialect. The words “Kurd” and “Kurdish” were legally prohibited. Kurdish-language publications, broadcasts, music, and education were banned. The “Citizen, Speak Turkish!” campaign embedded the hierarchy visually and linguistically: proper Turks wore modern dress and spoke Turkish; the mountain people were backward, bearded, and — the genre insisted — Turkish.

This is not genring in the strict sense. Genring produces a character that replaces the people. Turkey produced a negation that replaced the people. There was no Noble Kurd, no Kurdish folk figure in the Turkish national imagination. There was only the denial that Kurds existed at all, and the insistence that anyone claiming Kurdish identity was either confused or criminal.

The political cartoons of the Kemalist period (1925–1938) reveal what Turkey did produce: the Kurdish insurgent as savage. Bozarslan (2014) documents how Turkish political culture stereotyped Kurds as tribal, violent, and pre-modern — not as a romantic genre figure but as a threat to be eliminated. Where Iran produced a Mountain Warrior to be simultaneously admired and suppressed, Turkey produced a Mountain Savage to be civilized or destroyed.

The dengbej tradition suffered accordingly. Dengbej — Kurdish oral storytelling and singing — preserves what states cannot genre: multiple voices, contradictory histories, love stories alongside political epics, non-heroic suffering alongside martial narrative. Turkish suppression of the 1930s imposed fines on every word spoken in Kurdish. The 1980 military coup nearly extinguished the tradition. Dengbej were targeted not because of what they said but because of what they were: living archives of a heteroglossic culture that the state insisted did not exist. You cannot genre what you have already declared nonexistent. But you can destroy its carriers.

The 1990s counterinsurgency destroyed the economic base of southeastern Turkey’s Kurdish communities — agriculture, pastoralism, local trade — and produced a new figure: the Kurdish smuggler. Border communities turned to contraband to survive, and this survival was genred as inherent criminality. The “Kurdish mafia” genre figure is the economic counterpart to the Mountain Savage: not a romantic type but a criminal category, generated by the state’s own violence and then attributed to the people’s nature.

4. Iraq: The Binary Machine

Ba’athist Iraq genred Kurds through a binary: peshmerga or jash.

Peshmerga — “those who walk before death” — is the Kurdish word for armed resistance fighters. The Ba’ath regime recognized the genre and responded to it militarily. The Anfal campaign of 1986–1989, named from a Quranic term meaning “the spoils,” was designed to destroy the peshmerga not as a military force but as a viable genre figure. It did this by destroying the civilian base: killing entire families, razing villages, using chemical weapons at Halabja (approximately 5,000 dead, March 1988), and displacing hundreds of thousands. If the mountain warrior requires a mountain community to emerge from, destroying the community destroys the type.

Jash — “donkey’s foal” — was the regime’s term for Kurdish collaborators: men recruited as scouts, guides, and militia auxiliaries. The term is a genre figure imposed from above, designed to fracture Kurdish identity by creating an internal binary. You are either peshmerga (legitimate target) or jash (useful traitor). There is no position between resistance and collaboration, no complexity of motivation, no space for the ordinary person trying to survive.

The binary is itself genring. It reduces the Kurdish population to two characters — hero and traitor — and eliminates everything between them: the farmer who wants to be left alone, the teacher who navigates both sides, the merchant who trades with whoever controls the road, the mother who sends one son to the mountains and another to the government because she cannot afford to lose both. The temporal middle — the position of inheriting, continuing, and transforming — has no character in the Ba’athist script.

After 2003, the Kurdistan Regional Government in Iraq created a political space where Kurdish cultural and institutional life could develop. But the KRG itself produces genre figures: the Barzani peshmerga as professional soldier, the Kurdish flag as emblem of regional autonomy, the rhetoric of an independent Kurdish state. These are counter-genres — Kurdish self-representations against the Ba’athist erasure — but they are genres nonetheless, and they erase their own complexity. The KDP is patriarchal, dynastic, and pro-capitalist; its peshmerga include women’s units that “do not participate in fights,” serving symbolic rather than combat roles. The difference between this and Rojava’s gender revolution is immense. The genre “Kurdish fighter” contains both and distinguishes neither.

5. The West: The Feminist Ally

After 2014, when the Islamic State swept across Iraq and Syria and Kurdish forces became the ground troops of the American-led coalition, Western media produced a new genre figure: the Feminist Ally.

The template: brave, secular, democratic, gender-egalitarian fighters — especially women of the YPJ (Women’s Protection Units) — standing against medieval barbarism. The visual iconography was precise: young women in fatigues, hair visible, rifles in hand, smiling against desert backgrounds. The emotional register was admiration-plus-relief: here, in the heart of the Middle East, were people who shared “our values.”

The genre erased everything that made the movements it celebrated actually interesting.

The YPJ is the armed women’s wing of a political project derived from Abdullah Ocalan’s synthesis of PKK Marxism and Murray Bookchin’s anarchist municipalism. Its fighters undergo political education in democratic confederalism, jineoloji (the “science of women,” an indigenous feminist theory), and ecological communalism. The YPJ is not fighting for liberal democracy. It is fighting for the abolition of the nation-state as a political form. Western media stripped this away and presented “feminist warriors” legible to a liberal audience — brave women fighting bad men, interpretable without reference to anarchism, Bookchin, or the PKK’s four-decade revolutionary history.

The erasure operates on multiple axes:

The KDP’s peshmerga and Rojava’s YPG/YPJ were conflated under a single “Kurdish fighter” genre, despite being organizationally distinct, ideologically opposed (the KDP is pro-capitalist and pro-state; Rojava is anti-capitalist and anti-state), and intermittently hostile to each other. Iranian Kurdish movements — Komala, PJAK — disappeared from Western coverage entirely. The genre “our Kurdish allies” admitted no internal contradictions.

Kurdish patriarchy was erased. The YPJ’s gender revolution is a revolution against Kurdish tradition as much as against ISIS — against honor killing, forced marriage, and the tribal authority of men. Western media celebrated Kurdish feminism without noting that it was contested within Kurdish society, or that the KDP’s relationship to gender equality was closer to the societies the YPJ was fighting than to the YPJ itself.

The PKK’s designation as a terrorist organization by the United States and the European Union was managed by simply not mentioning it. The YPJ’s organizational and ideological descent from the PKK — which is also the YPJ’s greatest intellectual resource — was erased so that the genre figure could be consumed without political discomfort.

This is genring from a distance. The West genres Kurds in the same way it has always genred peripheral peoples who serve its geopolitical needs: by selecting the features that confirm Western values, codifying them into a consumable image, and substituting the image for the people. The Feminist Ally is the latest genre figure in a series that stretches back centuries. She is not the first. She will not be the last.

6. The Yezidi Case: Genring as Authorization of Genocide

Yezidis are Kurdish-speaking people whose distinct monotheistic religion centers on Tawusi Melek, the Peacock Angel — a figure of divine light tasked with the stewardship of the world. Beginning in the medieval period, Muslim clerics conflated Tawusi Melek with Iblis (the Islamic figure of Satan), producing what may be the most consequential genre figure in Kurdish history: the Yezidi as devil-worshipper.

This is not a stereotype in the colloquial sense. It is a genre figure — a stable character template, circulated across centuries, self-reinforcing through repetition, and capable of authorizing violence. Ottoman rulers and Kurdish tribal leaders used the devil-worship genre to justify massacres and enslavement for centuries. The genre persisted because it served the needs of every authority that deployed it: it made Yezidis legible as enemies of God, which made their dispossession legible as piety.

In August 2014, the Islamic State invaded Sinjar, the Yezidi heartland in northern Iraq. What followed — the killing of approximately 5,000 people, the enslavement of approximately 10,800 women and girls, the displacement of hundreds of thousands — was recognized by the United Nations as genocide. The Islamic State’s theology drew on the same medieval genre figure: Yezidis as devil-worshippers, whose women could be enslaved because their faith placed them outside the protections Islamic law extends to “People of the Book.”

The genre authorized the genocide. Not as its sole cause — the Islamic State’s territorial ambitions, military strategy, and apocalyptic theology all contributed. But the devil-worship character template provided the narrative infrastructure. It told the Islamic State’s fighters who Yezidis were, and what could be done to them, and why it was permissible. The template had been circulating for centuries. It was ready.

After the genocide, Western media sometimes corrected the devil-worship genre by explaining Yezidi theology. But the correction risks producing a new genre: the Oppressed Religious Minority. This figure is sympathetic rather than demonized, but it reduces Yezidis to victimhood — erasing their political agency, their internal diversity, their recovery strategies, and their own theological sophistication. The Yezidi intellectual tradition, which includes complex cosmological narratives, initiatory hierarchies, and a caste system, disappears behind the image of the suffering refugee. One genre replaces another. The people remain invisible behind both.

What Yezidi religious life actually contains — a theology of divine light and angelic stewardship, an initiatory structure, oral transmission of sacred texts (qewls and beyts), a caste system linking spiritual authority to lineage, annual pilgrimage to Lalish, and centuries of adaptation to hostile environments — is heteroglossic in the deepest sense. It contains internal debate, regional variation, generational transformation, and irreconcilable tensions between tradition and modernity. No genre figure can accommodate it. That is why every genre figure that has been applied to Yezidis — devil-worshipper, primitive pagan, suffering minority — has served someone else’s story.

7. The Dengbej and What Genring Cannot Contain

If genring is the reduction of heteroglossia to monologue, then the dengbej tradition is what refuses to be reduced.

Dengbej are Kurdish oral storytellers and singers who perform long, unaccompanied vocal narratives — epics, love stories, laments, historical accounts, and improvisations — drawn from a communal repertoire that spans centuries. The tradition is heteroglossic by structure: each dengbej performs the same material differently, audiences participate and contest, no single performance is authoritative, and the repertoire contains contradictory accounts of the same events. A dengbej epic about a battle may celebrate the warrior and mourn the widow in the same breath. A love story may honor the beloved and criticize the social order that separates the lovers. The form itself — unaccompanied voice, improvised within tradition, performed in community — refuses the boundaries of official literature.

This is why states target dengbej. Turkey’s suppression of Kurdish language in the 1930s imposed fines on every word of Kurdish spoken in public, and the 1980 military coup nearly destroyed the tradition entirely. Most dengbej ceased performing. The suppression targeted not the content but the form: a cultural practice that preserved multiple voices, multiple histories, and multiple ways of being Kurdish — exactly what genring cannot tolerate.

The recent revival of dengbej — through Kurdish-language television, diaspora festivals, and cultural institutions in Iraqi Kurdistan — is promising but carries its own risk. Institutionalization can turn living heteroglossia into heritage: a bounded, conservable genre rather than a contested, transforming tradition. When a dengbej performs at a cultural festival for an audience that includes UNESCO observers, the performance changes. It becomes a representation of Kurdish culture — a genre figure of Kurdish culture — rather than an enactment of it. The line between preservation and genring is thin, and it is precisely the line that matters.

8. Counter-Genres and Their Limits

Kurds are not only genred. They genre themselves.

The Medes origin narrative — “We are children of the Medes and Cyaxares” — positions Kurds as descendants of an imperial people who conquered Nineveh in 612 BCE. This counter-genre responds to the tribal-primitive template by asserting historical depth and political authority. If Kurds are Medes, then Kurdish sovereignty is not a modern aspiration but an ancient fact interrupted by conquest. The genre is useful. It is also, by scholarly consensus, historically unsubstantiated — there is no linguistic evidence connecting Median to any modern Kurdish variety. The counter-genre mirrors the structure it contests: selection, codification, substitution. Specific features of an ancient empire are extracted, assembled into a stable template, and circulated as identity.

The peshmerga figure operates similarly. “Those who walk before death” is a powerful self-genre: the mountain fighter who sacrifices for the nation. But the peshmerga genre, when it stabilizes, erases the same things state-imposed genres erase. The KDP’s peshmerga and the PKK’s guerrilla fighters and Rojava’s YPG/YPJ are ideologically incompatible movements — one fights for a conventional state, another fights for the abolition of the state form. The peshmerga genre contains both without distinguishing either.

Rojava’s political experiment — democratic confederalism, jineoloji, ecological communalism, multi-ethnic governance — is perhaps the most radical attempt at counter-genring in the Kurdish context. It does not produce a genre figure. It produces an institutional form: communes, councils, co-governance by gender, education in multiple languages. This is counter-genring not by substituting a new character but by refusing the character form altogether. The institution replaces the type.

But counter-genres are appropriable. The Medes narrative has been claimed by Azerbaijani nationalists. The peshmerga figure has been claimed by every state that has ever armed Kurdish fighters for its own purposes. Rojava’s feminism has been claimed by Western media and stripped of its anarchist content. Every counter-genre Kurds produce is immediately available for appropriation by the same powers that produced the original genre. The counter-genre does not escape the mechanism. It enters it.

What resists genring is not the counter-genre but the heteroglossia itself — the irreducible fact that Kurdish civilization contains Kurmanji and Sorani, Sunni and Yezidi, Barzani and Ocalan, dengbej and filmmaker, smuggler and scholar, and that no single image can contain them all. The resistance is not in the alternative character. It is in the refusal to be a character.

9. Four States, Four Genres, One People

The multiplication of genring states is what makes the Kurdish case structurally unique.

A Kurdish person crossing from Rojava into Iraqi Kurdistan into Iranian Kurdistan encounters three different genre expectations about who they are: feminist revolutionary, KRG citizen, Aryan tribesman. A Kurdish person in the Turkish diaspora in Germany encounters a fourth: the immigrant, the Gastarbeiter’s grandchild, the integration case. A Kurdish person in Sweden encounters a fifth: the refugee, the asylum seeker, the humanitarian object. Each context produces its own genre, selects its own features, circulates its own template. The heteroglossic reality — the same person, who speaks Kurmanji to her mother and Swedish to her children and follows the political debates in Rojava on her phone — is invisible behind every one of them.

State Genre Figure Mechanism What It Erases
Iran Mountain Warrior / Aryan Cousin Incorporation + suppression Kurdish linguistic independence, religious diversity, political modernity
Turkey Mountain Turk / Savage Denial + criminalization Kurdish existence as such; dengbej, language, name
Iraq (Ba’ath) Peshmerga / Jash Binary imposition The civilian, the uncommitted, the temporally middle
Iraq (KRG) Regional Patriot State-building incorporation Ideological diversity, gender politics, class
West (post-2014) Feminist Ally Selective celebration Anarchist politics, internal patriarchy, PKK lineage
Islamic State Devil-worshipper (Yezidi) Demonization Yezidi theology, humanity

No single genre figure governs the Kurdish case. The figures contradict each other — the Feminist Ally cannot coexist with the Mountain Savage; the Aryan Cousin cannot coexist with the Devil-Worshipper. But they all operate simultaneously, on the same people, producing a landscape of incompatible legibilities. A Kurdish person must navigate not one genre but six, performing different characters for different audiences, or — like the dengbej, like Rojava’s communes, like the Kahnawake Mohawks carrying their own passports — refusing to perform at all.

10. What the Instability Reveals

Genring is most visible when it is unstable. When a genre figure circulates without contradiction — when the people it replaces are too far away, too few, or too dispossessed to interrupt — the operation is invisible. The genre feels like knowledge. The character feels like the people.

Kurdish genring is permanently unstable because the people will not stop being various. They declare republics. They develop political philosophies. They make films. They carry arms for incompatible causes. They practice religions that predate the genre by millennia. They speak languages the genre insists are dialects. They cross borders the genre insists are permanent. Every genre figure applied to them — Mountain Warrior, Mountain Turk, Feminist Ally, Devil-Worshipper — is contradicted within a generation by the people doing something the genre cannot accommodate.

This instability reveals what stable cases conceal: that genring is always violent. When the genre holds, the violence is invisible — it happened earlier, or elsewhere, or it takes the form of illegibility rather than execution. When the genre fails, the state must enforce it openly. Iran bans Kurdish language. Turkey levels Kurdish cities. Iraq uses chemical weapons. The Islamic State commits genocide. The violence is not a consequence of the genre’s failure. It is the mechanism by which the genre is maintained when the people refuse to match it.

The Kurdish case, because it is unstable, because it is multiple, because it is ongoing, teaches the general lesson: a stable genre is one where the violence succeeded. An unstable genre is one where the people are still there.
