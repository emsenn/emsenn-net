The Operational Closure of Late-Liberal Governance

2026-04-27 The school's load-bearing claim: late-liberal governance is operationally closed in Luhmann's sense — no coherent subject-position produces an outside.

Cybernetic postliberalism’s load-bearing structural claim is the closure claim: late-liberal governance is operationally closed in Niklas Luhmann’s specific sense. No coherent subject-position produces an outside. Anarchist grammar, primitivist exit, accelerationist hyperengagement, abolitionist construction, melancholic critique, off-grid retreat, and spiritual transcendence are genres within the same metabolism, each producing the affective material the apparatus requires. The closure is descriptive sociology with a specific structural claim, not a political verdict — a discovery the analysis converges toward, not its conclusion.

What follows develops the technical content of the claim, examines what it does and does not entail, and explains why the closure functions as a convergence point rather than an endpoint.

¶What operational closure means

The phrase operational closure has technical content. Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela developed the concept in their biology of cognition (1980, 1987): a living system is operationally closed in the sense that its operations refer only to its own states and produce only its own further operations. The environment perturbs the system but does not directly determine its operations; meaning is generated within the system rather than received from outside. The closure is not isolation (the system is open to energy and matter exchange) but operational self-reference (the system’s operations are not interpretable except as the operations of that system).

Niklas Luhmann (1995, 2012) extends the concept into a general theory of social systems. A social system, on Luhmann’s account, consists of communications, and communications are the operations the system performs. The system is operationally closed in the sense that its communications refer only to its own prior communications and produce only its own further communications. What looks like external information (a fact, an event, a person, a problem) becomes legible within the system only as the system’s own communication about it. The legal system communicates legally; the economic system communicates economically; the scientific system communicates scientifically. Cross-system contact occurs through structural coupling: the systems are mutually responsive at their boundaries, but each system’s operations remain its own.

This is the technical apparatus the closure claim runs on. Late-liberal governance is operationally closed means: the apparatus’s operations refer only to its own prior operations and produce only its own further operations. What looks like an outside — a critique, a refusal, an alternative — becomes legible within the apparatus only as the apparatus’s own operation upon it. The critique is metabolized as a genre of critique; the refusal is metabolized as a genre of refusal; the alternative is metabolized as a genre of alternative. The metabolism does not require coordination; it follows from the operational structure of the apparatus, specified in Platformal Feedback Architecture.

¶Why “no-outside” is not the proper name

A coinage like the no-outside claim is misleading. Operational closure is the proper name for what is being analyzed; the lineage runs through Maturana and Varela, Luhmann, second-order cybernetics, and Beer’s viable-system model. To call the claim “the no-outside claim” would imply a novel philosophical observation. It is not. What is happening is the application of a well-established structural concept to a specific empirical-historical case (late-liberal governance under platformal-energetic conditions). The novelty is the application, not the structural concept itself.

This matters for how the claim is received. A novel philosophical observation invites philosophical contestation on the observation’s own terms. A specific application of a well-developed structural concept invites contestation on whether the application is correctly performed: are platforms structurally autopoietic in the relevant sense? Does the metabolism the analysis describes meet the formal criteria for operational closure? Is the empirical evidence consistent with what operational closure entails? These are the productive questions, and adopting the proper terminological lineage puts them in their productive form.

¶The closure claim’s specific content

The closure claim has three operative components.

Every coherent subject-position is metabolizable. A subject-position is coherent if it is articulable as a stable identity, a stable practice, or a stable politics within the apparatus’s discursive infrastructure. Anarchist grammar as a coherent subject-position (anarchist Twitter, anarchist publishing, anarchist movement-rhetoric) is metabolizable: it produces engagement, it has a genre, it can be served as content. Primitivist exit as a coherent subject-position (the back-to-the-land community, the homestead movement, the digital-detox retreat) is metabolizable: it has its genre, its content economy, its supporting industries. Accelerationist hyperengagement as a coherent subject-position (the techno-optimist, the doomer-accelerationist, the e/acc tendency) is metabolizable. The closure claim is that this metabolizability holds in general — not for any particular subject-position considered, but for any coherent subject-position the discursive infrastructure can register.

Metabolization does not require coordination. The metabolism is not performed by a central planner deciding which subject-positions to absorb. It is performed by the apparatus’s operational structure: the recursive readability of the platform, the algorithmic genre-routing, the requisite-variety provision, the cross-platform coherence. These features make metabolizability structural rather than contingent. No one is steering. The metabolism is the apparatus’s normal operation.

The closure does not preclude all action. Coherent subject-positions are distinct from practice in the strict sense. Practice — the unmanaged act, the care that does not wait for permission, the relation that does not lend itself to legibility within the apparatus — is something different from a subject-position. The closure claim is about coherent subject-positions; whether practice in this strict sense is also metabolized, or whether some practice survives metabolization, is the open question and is treated with care below.

¶Why the closure is structural, not pessimistic

The closure claim is sometimes received as defeatism: if there is no outside, what is the point? This reading is structurally wrong.

The closure is descriptive. It states what the apparatus is, not what subjects should do. A descriptive claim that water flows downhill is not a counsel against irrigation; it is information about the structural conditions any irrigation system must engage with. The closure claim is structurally analogous: it specifies the conditions any practice that wants to operate otherwise must engage with. Practices that ignore the closure tend to find themselves metabolized; practices that take the closure seriously may find ways to operate that the closure does not foreclose.

The closure is also historically specific. Operational closure is not the eternal condition of governance. The medieval pastoral apparatus (analyzed in The Ecclesial Recurrence) operated under different structural conditions — its institutional interiors were richer, its metabolism slower, its coupling to the energetic substrate different. The closure claim is about late-liberal governance under contracted energetic substrate and platformal feedback architecture. If the substrate or the architecture changes substantially, the closure may attenuate; the closure is not permanent.

The closure is also not totalizing. Luhmann’s social systems are operationally closed as systems, but subjects are not reducible to the social systems they participate in. A subject’s biological-organismic life, their dreams, their somatic states, their ancestor-relations, their kin-bonds — these are not coextensive with the social communications the apparatus metabolizes. The closure operates at the level of communication, not at the level of all of human life. What this opens for practice is the question taken up below.

¶What may survive metabolization

The question of what may survive metabolization is held open with care. There is no settled answer; what follows are candidates and the structural reasons they are candidates.

Practice that does not produce engagement. Practice that the platform’s algorithms cannot see — slow reading without commentary, sustained attention to one thing, conversation that does not lend itself to share, friendship maintained through presence rather than mediation, ancestral or ceremonial relations that do not generate digital exhaust — is structurally invisible to the apparatus. It does not produce the engagement signal the apparatus’s metabolism requires. Practices in this category are candidates for surviving metabolization, with the caveat that the boundary between “produces no engagement” and “is being read by analytics the practitioner does not see” is not always clean. Thomist studiositas — the disciplined love of knowledge that orders the cognitive appetite to its proper goods — names a virtue that this register can sustain.

Refusal in the strict sense. Audra Simpson’s refusal — the practice of declining the recognition-circuit by which settler states confer standing (developed in Settlerism and the Cybernetic Subject) — is not a coherent subject-position within the recognition-circuit; it is a relational-political practice that operates outside the circuit’s terms. Its persistence in actual indigenous political life suggests that the apparatus’s metabolism is not as comprehensive as a maximal closure claim would assert. Refusal is one of the principal candidates for surviving practice.

Weak messianic power and the dialectical image. Walter Benjamin’s weak messianic power names the structure of relation to the past’s claim on the present. The structure is asymmetric: the dead cannot reciprocate, the obligation cannot be discharged, but the recognition is possible. This is another candidate for surviving practice — not because it produces an outside in the apparatus’s discursive sense, but because its operation does not depend on the apparatus’s recognition or amplification. Theological spes — hope as the disposition oriented to a future good not yet possessed — operates structurally in the same register.

Care that does not wait for permission. Care-practice that operates outside the harm governance apparatus — friendship that holds without reporting, mutual aid that does not seek recognition, the sustained presence with the dying that produces no content — is another candidate. This is adjacent to refusal: it operates otherwise than the apparatus’s terms, not as a coherent subject-position the apparatus can serve.

Embodied and ceremonial relations. The cybernetic subject participates in a great deal of life that is not communications-in-Luhmann’s-sense: somatic states, ancestor-relations, ceremonial obligations, ecological relations. These are not metabolizable as communications because they are not communications. This register is held with care; cybernetic postliberalism does not claim the competence to systematize it, and explicitly draws on indigenous-critical and other traditions that develop it more fully.

The closure-text does not deliver a comprehensive list of practices that survive metabolization; it identifies candidates and the structural features that make them candidates. None is automatically protected; the boundary between “outside the metabolism” and “metabolized in a way the practitioner cannot see” is not always clean, and the apparatus’s operations include strategies for absorbing what looks initially like the outside.

¶What the closure claim is not

It is not the claim that critique is futile. Critique is one of the apparatus’s metabolizable subject-positions, but the metabolizing of critique-as-content is not the same as the futility of critique-as-action. Critique can be metabolized as content while still producing material consequences (regulatory action, institutional change, educational effect) at other registers. The closure claim is about subject-positions, not about all action.

It is not the claim that revolutionary politics are impossible. Revolutionary politics in any classical sense are not currently in operation. But the conditions of operational closure do not preclude their future emergence; the present configuration metabolizes what currently presents as revolutionary subject-positions. If conditions change — energetic substrate shifts, platformal architecture is restructured, institutional configurations regenerate — the metabolism’s specific character may change.

It is not the claim that liberation is impossible. Cybernetic postliberalism is not in the prescription business. It does not say what subjects should do; it says what the apparatus is. What can be done, and by whom, and on what conditions, exceeds the school’s competence and is deferred to other traditions (indigenous resurgence, abolitionist construction, ecological-relational practice, ancestral renewal) that develop these questions more fully.

¶What follows

With the closure claim stated and defended, the central structural claim is in place. The Ecclesial Recurrence reads californication as recurrent with the medieval Christian pastoral apparatus that performed structurally similar work under different institutional conditions. Cybernetic Postliberalism in Context positions the analysis relative to adjacent schools of postliberal critique. Methods of Cybernetic-Postliberal Analysis specifies how to perform the analysis on specific cases. After those, the monograph is complete; what follows in the school’s principal-cases is cybernetic postliberalism worked against specific empirical material.
