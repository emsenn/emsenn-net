Postliberal Grammar: The Shape Crisis Takes

2026-03-06

  • Harm governance as the liberal mode
  • How grammar and governance connect
  • Primary reading
  • Key terms

A politician declares that the nation has lost its way. They name a community of authentic values. They identify an enemy who has corrupted the community. They reframe present suffering as sacrifice. They promise destiny. They assert inevitability.

What vocabulary fills each slot — whether the community is the Volk or “the West,” the enemy is parasites or liberal elites, the destiny is national rebirth or civilizational restoration — changes across contexts. The sequence does not. This sequence is fascist grammar: a rhetorical structure that generates political speech regardless of its surface content.

This text examines two rhetorical structures that process crisis into manageable form. For the conceptual foundations, see the term definitions for fascist grammar (which traces the six steps through Goebbels, Miller, strategic forgetting, and the liberal dialectical twin) and harm governance (which traces the collapse of care into risk management).

Fascist grammar across vocabularies

In “A storm is a storm is a storm” (2025), Joseph Goebbels’s 1932 “storm” speech is placed alongside Stephen Miller’s 2025 eulogy for Charlie Kirk, showing the same grammar operating in both — despite 93 years and radically different vocabularies. The grammar survived 1945 through strategic forgetting: Carl Schmitt’s friend/enemy distinction became Cold War moral clarity, Oswald Spengler’s civilizational cycles became Huntington’s Clash of Civilizations, the Volk became “the West.”

The sharpest analytical move is showing that liberalism does not escape this grammar by opposing it. Liberalism inhabits fascist grammar in reverse — countering crisis with progress, community with universality, enemy with toleration, sacrifice with compromise, destiny with progress, inevitability with procedure — remaining bound to the structure it seeks to negate. Walter Benjamin targeted exactly this: the faith that history already leans toward justice is a mirror image of fascism’s fatalism.

Harm governance as the liberal mode

Where fascist grammar narrates crisis as caused by enemies and resolved through restoration, harm governance narrates crisis as manageable through proper administration. Both formats serve the same cybernetic function: they process structural contradiction into forms that sustain the system rather than challenging it.

In “Between care and control” (2025), harm governance operates in how people respond to direct action, masking, and mutual aid. The core observation: people who have lost faith in the state reproduce its administrative logic in their relationships with each other. Individual action is seen as dangerous because it lacks validation. Mass action is seen as safe because institutional form makes it feel legitimate. Care is collapsed into risk management.

How grammar and governance connect

Fascist grammar and harm governance are not separate phenomena. They are the two modes through which the same structural problem — liberalism’s constitutive contradictions becoming visible under stress — is processed into narrative. Fascist grammar processes it into a story of enemies, sacrifice, and destiny. Harm governance processes it into a story of responsible administration. Both keep the subject inside the loop. Both prevent the contradiction from being addressed as contradiction.

This is why they connect to californication: both formats provide the narrative templates that the affective mechanism requires. The subject needs a story for their confusion. Fascist grammar and harm governance provide two stories — one dramatic, one procedural — that both end in the same place: the system continues.
