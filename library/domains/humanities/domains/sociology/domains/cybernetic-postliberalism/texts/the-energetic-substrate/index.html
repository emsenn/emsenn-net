The Energetic Substrate

2026-04-27 The school presupposes declining net energy. Useful-stage global fossil-fuel EROEI is now ~3.5:1, below Hall's 5:1 floor for complex society.

Cybernetic postliberalism presupposes a material claim about declining net energy. The claim is not part of cybernetic postliberalism itself; it lives in the energy-systems literature, with its own methods and peer-review network. Cybernetic postliberalism’s relation to that literature is one of presupposition: if the claim is roughly right, the account of why californication has intensified now (rather than being one descriptive sociology among many) follows; if the claim turns out to be wrong, much of cybernetic postliberalism’s specific historicization would need to be rebuilt.

What follows establishes the energetic claim as it lives in the energy-systems literature, distinguishes it from cybernetic postliberalism proper, and explains why it matters for the analysis of late-liberal governance — particularly for understanding why the operations subsequent texts trace (genring, ontogenring, californication, platformal liberalism) have intensified under contemporary conditions in ways their prior institutional forms did not require.

¶What the claim is

Net energy names the energy a society has available for use after subtracting the energy required to produce it. The standard quantitative measure is EROEI — energy return on energy invested — the ratio of energy delivered to energy expended in delivering it. An EROEI of 20:1 means that for every unit of energy spent extracting, refining, and distributing energy, twenty units are delivered to the rest of the economy. EROEI of 10:1, ten units. EROEI of 1:1, none — the energy system is consuming all of its own output.

The claim has three components, each independently established in the literature and converging in the work of Charles Hall, Vaclav Smil, the Brockway group at Leeds, and others.

Fossil-fuel EROEI has declined substantially. Conventional oil at the wellhead in the 1930s-40s ran around 50:1; the wellhead figure today, across major fields, is in the range of 18-43:1 (Murphy, Hall, et al. 2022). But the wellhead figure overstates the energy actually available for societal use. By the time oil has been refined into finished fuels, EROEI drops; by the time the fuels have been delivered through the distribution infrastructure, it drops again. Brockway et al. (2019, 2024) measure these later stages and find that useful-stage global fossil-fuel EROEI is now approximately 3.5:1.

There is a net-energy cliff below which complex society is structurally unsustainable. The fraction of total energy available to society after subtracting energy reinvested in the energy system is given by (EROEI − 1) / EROEI. The function is hyperbolic. At 100:1, society receives 99% of the gross energy. At 20:1, 95%. At 10:1, 90%. At 5:1, 80%. At 3:1, 67%. Below 5:1, small declines in EROEI produce large losses in net energy — this is the cliff. Hall (2014) and others have argued that complex society probably cannot function below approximately 5:1 across its entire energy basis. Lambert and Hall (2014) find that quality-of-life indicators correlate with societal EROEI and that well-being thresholds appear in the 20-30:1 range. Global fossil fuels at useful stage are already at 3.5:1.

Renewable replacements do not, on current evidence, fund a return to the prior surplus. Solar PV, wind, and nuclear all return EROEIs above the cliff (Murphy 2022; Capellan-Perez et al. 2024). But the system-level question — whether a fully renewable transition produces societal-EROEI matching the historical fossil-fuel high — depends on storage, grid, intermittency-buffering, and the substantial reinvestment required to build the renewable infrastructure on a fossil-fuel-shrinking economy. The current literature is not unanimous; what is reasonably established is that the energetic-surplus regime of the twentieth century is not a baseline that can be straightforwardly restored.

The claim, then, is not that energy is running out in the simple-exhaustion sense. It is that the net energy available to society — the surplus that can fund activities other than running the energy system itself — has been contracting and is now below the levels at which the historical complexity of the contemporary apparatus was constructed.

¶Why the claim sits outside cybernetic postliberalism

The school’s _index links to the EROEI research under presupposes: rather than contains:. The relation matters. Cybernetic postliberalism is a sociology, not an energy-systems analysis. Its contribution does not depend on its competence in measuring fossil-fuel net-yield curves; the measurement is done by physicists, geologists, and energy economists working in their own institutions, with their own peer review. Cybernetic postliberalism takes the measurement as input.

This separation has methodological discipline. If the energy-systems literature converges on a different consensus — if useful-stage fossil EROEI turns out to be substantially higher than current estimates, or a renewable transition can in fact restore historical surplus — the specific historicization (californication has intensified now because the substrate has contracted now) would need to be rebuilt. Cybernetic postliberalism does not preempt the empirical question; it presupposes the empirical answer the energy-systems literature currently supports and is honest about what would change if that answer changes.

The separation also keeps the analysis from falling into the prophetic-collapse register that takes EROEI decline as the master fact and reads all other phenomena as its expressions. Cybernetic postliberalism rejects this move. EROEI is the substrate; what is built on the substrate is real and has its own dynamics. A society at EROEI 3.5:1 is not determined to operate as the contemporary apparatus does. It is constrained — there is a class of institutional forms that the energetic substrate can support and a class that it cannot — but within the constraints, multiple configurations remain possible. The configuration cybernetic postliberalism analyzes is the one that has actually emerged, not the one made inevitable by the energetic data alone.

¶Why the claim matters

Californication — the operation by which structural contradictions liberalism cannot resolve are formatted as personally manageable affect, with the resulting self-regulation functioning as the governance mechanism — is recurrent across the modern pastoral apparatus. Prior institutional forms, the medieval Christian church most prominently (developed in The Ecclesial Recurrence), performed structurally similar work in different institutional configurations. What is distinctive about the present is not that the operation exists but that it has intensified and redistributed onto bodies and platforms in a particular way. The energetic substrate is what makes the difference legible.

The medieval pastoral apparatus operated on a relatively low-energy base but did not require the institutional complexity the contemporary apparatus requires. The seal of confession could be carried by a parish priest reading by candlelight in a stone building that took two generations to build and lasted six hundred years. That apparatus was institutionally rich on a thin energetic substrate because its operations were structurally simple and slow.

The twentieth-century apparatus operated on the historical-high energetic substrate. The expansion of the modern state, the construction of mass-media broadcasting, the development of welfare-state institutional interiors, the cultivation of a large reflexive professional class — all of these were funded by a surplus that paid for what Illich (1974) analyzed as high-energy social forms. The institutional interiors grew in proportion to what the substrate could fund: think tanks, universities, regulatory agencies, public broadcasting, slow journalism, planning institutions, archives. These were the forms within which subjects could be governed slowly and at scale, with the energetic surplus paying for the institutional buffers between subjects and the structural contradictions the institutions managed.

The contemporary apparatus operates on a substrate that has contracted from that twentieth-century high. The institutional interiors have thinned. The slow public broadcasting, the staffed regulatory agency, the large research interior, the patient newsroom — these are not the dominant forms now. What replaces them is platformal infrastructure: feedback architectures (analyzed in Platformal Feedback Architecture) that operate at planetary scale, with low marginal energetic cost per interaction (a single tweet costs almost nothing in joules), but with structurally different institutional properties. The slow buffer is gone; the metabolism that used to run inside institutional walls now runs through bodies and feeds.

The metabolism has redistributed onto bodies and platforms because the energetic surplus that funded richer institutional interiors has contracted. The redistribution is not an accident, an ideological choice, or a Silicon-Valley conspiracy. It is a configuration the substrate can fund and the prior configuration cannot. Bodies metabolize the contradictions (liberalism’s constitutive contradictions, in detail) because there is no longer enough institutional surplus to metabolize them upstream.

¶What follows from the substrate’s contraction

The presupposition has three operative consequences for the contemporary apparatus’s specific shape.

Compression of timescales. The slow institutional interior allowed for analysis, deliberation, planning, and pastoral patience that took years to unfold. Platformal infrastructure operates at second-by-second timescales. When the apparatus loses the institutional buffer, the affective and cognitive timescales available for processing structural contradictions compress correspondingly. This is what subjects experience as the rate at which everything must be processed — the saturation condition that precedes destabilization.

Loss of protected interiors. The medieval seal of confession, the post-WWII journalist’s editorial independence, the regulatory agency’s deliberative chamber — these were institutionalized interiors within which decisions could be made and information could be held. The contemporary platformal apparatus has no equivalent: every datum is in principle harvestable, every utterance in principle re-circulable. Subjects’ inner lives are continuous with the apparatus rather than buffered from it. This is one of the structural conditions that produces the neurotic platformal intellectual: expertise performed under conditions where the protected interior that would once have held the work in private has dissolved.

Distributed metabolism. Because no central institution carries the metabolism, the metabolism runs through whatever can carry affect. Bodies become the metabolizing surface. Californication names exactly this redistribution: contradictions that were once buffered upstream now arrive directly at the level of personal affect, and the self-regulation by which subjects manage them is itself the governance the apparatus requires. The subject this produces is the savior-slave subject: personally responsible for managing structural crises, structurally incapable of transforming the conditions that produce them.

The empirical correlates of this redistribution match what the cognitive-reflexivity literature documents: the contraction of attention spans (Mark and colleagues, 2004-2020), the reverse Flynn effect in fluid reasoning (Bratsberg and Rogeberg 2018, Dworak et al. 2023), the rising prevalence of anxiety and depression measured at population scale, the steepening of these curves in younger cohorts. None of these were predicted by cybernetic postliberalism; they are consistent with it, and they suggest the substrate-level account has independent measurable correlates at the cognitive-affective level. Byung-Chul Han’s Leistungssubjekt names the affective form this condition takes from the inside; acedia and curiositas, recovered from the Thomist apparatus, name the spiritual conditions that the platformal substrate continuously regenerates.

¶What the substrate claim is not

It is not the claim that the energetic substrate determines all institutional outcomes. Many configurations are possible at any given EROEI; the substrate determines the class of feasible configurations, not which one is realized.

It is not the claim that contemporary subjects experience their conditions as an energetic phenomenon. The substrate is not legible to subjects as substrate; it shows up as the rate at which everything must be processed, the ambient affect of being-in-too-much, the impossibility of slowing down. Subjects experience the conditions; cybernetic postliberalism offers the structural account.

It is not the claim that the prior institutional forms (the church, the postwar welfare state, the bureaucratic interior) were good. Cybernetic postliberalism describes how the apparatus has reproduced itself across institutional regimes; it does not prescribe which regime to restore. The medieval pastoral apparatus performed californication-functions in its own register; this is data, not nostalgia.

¶What follows

With the substrate-claim established, the contradictions that the substrate’s prior surplus once buffered come into view: liberalism’s universalism without individualism, individualism without universalism, progress without exhaustion, equality without sovereignty, each detailed in Liberalism’s Constitutive Contradictions. The subject-forming operations that route these contradictions through the inner life of the subject — genring, ontogenring, californication itself — depend on the substrate-condition this text has specified, and produce the specific subject-formations that subsequent texts trace: the savior-slave subject, the neurotic platformal intellectual, zen fascism, harm governance, coherent confusion. The closure claim that follows from these — that no coherent subject-position produces an outside, because every “outside” is itself a genre running on the same affective infrastructure — is stated and defended in The Operational Closure of Late-Liberal Governance.
