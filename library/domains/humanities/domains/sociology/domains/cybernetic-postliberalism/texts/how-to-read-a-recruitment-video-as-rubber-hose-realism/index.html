How to Read a Recruitment Video as Rubber-Hose Realism

2026-04-30 A close-reading procedure for analyzing a specific recruitment or institutional-influence video as an instance of rubber-hose realism — the moral physics, the address mode, the temporal structure, the displacement, and the institutional cover.

This is a working procedure for reading a specific recruitment or institutional-influence video as an instance of rubber-hose realism. It is the methodological backbone the rubber-hose-realism paper itself does not give in procedural form. The procedure walks through the close-reading work scene by scene, tracking the constituent mechanisms of the regime in a single concrete artifact.

This how-to assumes familiarity with rubber-hose realism and its constituent terms: consequence-free violence, strategic infantilization, affective looping, moral displacement, soft surrealism. It also assumes familiarity with moral physics as an analytical layer.

If you are doing this kind of analysis for the first time, run your first rubber-hose reading on a smaller, more accessible artifact before attempting a recruitment video. The recruitment-video analysis is more demanding because the regime is in a more developed form.

¶What you’ll need

  • A specific recruitment or institutional-influence video. Be concrete: not “PSYOP recruitment generally” but, for example, the specific Ghosts in the Machine sequel released by the 4th Psychological Operations Group in 2024. Pick one artifact.
  • The capacity to view the artifact repeatedly. Frame-by-frame access if possible. Most of this procedure requires looking at moments more than once.
  • A way to take detailed notes. Plan to take more notes than you expect.
  • About 2–4 hours for a single short video; longer for a series.
  • Background context on the producing institution. You should know who made the video, under what institutional cover, for what declared purpose, and how it was distributed.

¶Step 1: Establish the institutional cover

Before watching, write down what the artifact’s institutional cover is:

  • Who is the producing institution?
  • What is the artifact’s declared purpose?
  • Where and how was it distributed?
  • What is the audience the institution names as the target audience?
  • What is the audience that is actually watching it (which is often different — for U.S. military recruitment in particular, the actual audience often includes ironic platform users, journalists, and adversary intelligence, not just potential recruits)?

This step matters because the institutional cover is what the formal reading will be compared against. Rubber-hose realism is most analytically interesting where the formal vocabulary is farther along the state-aesthetic continuum than the institutional cover acknowledges. To find this gap you need to have the cover written down explicitly.

¶Step 2: Establish the artifact’s moral physics

Watch the video once through, with the moral physics axes in mind. After the first watch, write down the artifact’s moral physics along the standard axes:

  • Persistence of harm. Does damage carry forward across scenes? When the video shows an act of violence (real or implied), what happens in the next scene? Is the violence registered, or has the world reset?
  • Locus of causation. Where do events come from in the video’s universe? Are events arising from structures (institutions, geopolitical conditions), from individuals (a specific actor’s choice), or from no traceable source at all (events simply happen, and the protagonist reacts)?
  • Reversibility of catastrophe. When the video shows large-scale harm (war, riot, disaster, ecological collapse), is the harm presented as reversible or as having permanently changed the world?
  • Weight of bodies. When bodies appear in the video, do they have mass, fragility, friction? Or are they elastic, mobile, infinitely rearrangeable? Pay particular attention to whose bodies are weighted and whose are not.
  • Memory of the world. Does the video present a world that carries forward what has happened, or a world that begins each scene anew?

Recruitment videos in the rubber-hose-realism regime typically have rapid persistence-decay, individual or unattributable locus of causation, high reversibility, light bodies, and short world-memory. If your artifact has those properties, you have located the substrate the rest of the procedure will read against.

¶Step 3: Catalogue consequence-free violence

Watch the video again, this time tracking moments of harm — explicit or implicit. Make a numbered list. For each moment of harm:

  • What is the harm? (Explosion, body shown injured, image of death, glimpse of conflict, mention of violence in voiceover.)
  • Whose harm is it? (Protagonist’s, an enemy’s, a civilian’s, an unspecified body’s.)
  • What persists from the harm into the next moment? (Visible injury, behavioral change, environmental damage.)
  • What does not persist?

The question to answer for each moment: is the harm consequence-free in the sense of the term — visually present, narratively undigested?

Most influence videos in this register operate with a great deal of consequence-free violence, often in stylized form: glimpsed bodies that are not addressed in the next cut, archival footage of conflict used as visual texture, references to operations whose human costs are not carried forward in the artifact. Track the cumulative pattern.

¶Step 4: Catalogue strategic infantilization

Watch the video again, tracking the address mode. List the formal markers of strategic infantilization the artifact deploys:

  • Iconic imagery. Are subjects rendered as instantly-readable icons rather than complex objects? (A globe, a silhouette, a single hand.)
  • Oversized affect cues. Does music, color shift, voiceover register, or rhythm tell the viewer what to feel and when? How explicitly?
  • Personification of complex systems. Are markets, institutions, militaries, ecosystems, or information environments rendered as agents with intentions and moods? Note specific instances.
  • Manipulable-control metaphors. Are systems represented with simple control surfaces — dials, levers, dashboards, switches?
  • Rhythmic repetition. Do phrases, images, and beats repeat across the video in ways that scaffold reception?
  • Reassuring resolutions. Even given unsettling content, does the video deliver an exit cue that returns the viewer to baseline? (A closing musical cadence, an institutional logo, a clean fade-to-black.)

Note which markers are present and where. The convergence of several is what establishes that strategic infantilization is operating; one marker on its own is rarely decisive.

This step often produces a finding particular to recruitment-video work: that adult, specifically intelligent adult viewers are being addressed in a register strongly inflected by children’s media. The address mode produces the impression that the institution trusts the viewer’s intelligence; the analytical reality is the opposite — the address mode is positioning the viewer below where their developed defenses operate.

¶Step 5: Track the affective looping

Watch the video again, this time attending to its temporal structure. Identify:

  • Repeated motifs. Which images, phrases, or symbols return across the video, with what variations?
  • Cyclical sequences. Does the video present material in cycles (intro–build–release–intro–build–release) rather than as a progressive arc?
  • Held affect. Identify the dominant affective register the video is sustaining (awe, dread, intrigue, fascination, mission-readiness). Track whether the video moves the viewer through this register toward something else, or holds them in it for the duration.
  • Content delivered during the hold. What content arrives during the sustained affective register? Recruitment videos often deliver their actual recruitment content — institutional names, capability claims, application steps — during a sustained affective hold, where it slips past the viewer’s evaluation.

Note the content that arrived during the hold. This is often the substantive payload of the recruitment video, and it is rarely the part the audience remembers most vividly.

¶Step 6: Identify the moral displacement

Now apply the moral-displacement procedure to the video. Briefly:

  • What structural condition does the video acknowledge? (Most recruitment videos in this register acknowledge surprisingly directly — the existence of psychological influence operations, the moral weight of the institution, the difficulty of the work.)
  • What personal question does it substitute? (Almost always: are you the kind of person who could thrive inside this institution?)
  • What is the unaskable question? (Almost always: should this institution exist in this form? What is the political relation between this institution and its audiences?)
  • What is the affective reward for accepting the substitution? (Often: a sense of being addressed as a sophisticated and morally serious adult, invited into a complex insider community.)

Note these explicitly. They are usually the most political part of the reading.

¶Step 7: Identify the soft-surrealist surface

Track the artifact’s soft-surrealist surface markers. List instances of:

  • Collage of historical and pop-cultural fragments.
  • Screens within screens, monitors filming monitors, footage of footage.
  • Retro-cartoon revival or vintage-medium texture.
  • Distorted voiceover (whispered, slowed, pitched, layered, subtitled).
  • Symbolic motif return (masks, eyes, hands, mirrors, threads).
  • Glitch as ornament.

For each marker, note where it appears and what content it accompanies. The soft-surrealist surface is usually the most visible feature of the artifact — it is what audiences mean when they describe these videos as “weird” or “creepy” or “unsettling.” Naming it as a structured aesthetic mode (rather than as miscellaneous strangeness) is part of what the analysis is for.

¶Step 8: Position the artifact on the state-aesthetic continuum

Now place the artifact on the state-aesthetic continuum. Ask:

  • Where does the institutional cover place the artifact? (Recruitment, public affairs, doctrinal communications.)
  • Where does the formal vocabulary place the artifact? (Often: deep into platform-native creator territory, sometimes into open-PSYOP territory openly named in doctrinal commentary.)
  • Is there a gap between the cover and the vocabulary? (For U.S. military recruitment in this register, almost always yes.)
  • How is the audience reading the gap? (Often, ironically — but the irony is itself an audience competency the apparatus has come to expect and design for.)

Note the gap explicitly. The gap is where the artifact’s most analytically-interesting work is happening.

¶Step 9: Compose the reading

Assemble your findings into a reading. The reading should name:

  • The artifact’s moral physics (along the standard axes).
  • The dominant constituent mechanisms of the regime that are operating (consequence-free violence, strategic infantilization, affective looping, moral displacement, soft surrealism — note which, with how much intensity).
  • The structural condition acknowledged and the personal question substituted.
  • The position(s) the artifact occupies on the state-aesthetic continuum.
  • The gap between institutional cover and formal vocabulary.
  • The audience’s likely relation to the gap.

If you are writing this up, the reading is the analytical core; descriptive cataloguing of moments goes into footnotes or appendix. The political claim is what the regime is doing through this artifact, given the cover and the gap.

¶Common difficulties

The video is too short to track everything. Recruitment videos often run two to four minutes. The procedure as written assumes you can watch repeatedly. If you cannot, prioritize Steps 2 (moral physics), 6 (moral displacement), and 8 (continuum positioning); these are the steps that produce the most analytical leverage per watch.

The video uses the regime’s surface but not its substrate. Some artifacts deploy soft-surrealist markers and strategic-infantilization markers without operating in a moral-physics regime of consequence-free violence. The diagnostic is Step 2: if the moral physics does not have the regime’s profile, the artifact is using the surface vocabulary without inhabiting the regime. This is a useful finding to note, since it identifies an artifact occupying a different position on the continuum than its surface suggests.

The video is sincere about the structural condition. Some recruitment material does engage the structural condition seriously rather than displacing it. The Step 6 diagnostic distinguishes these: if the artifact’s analysis of the structural condition would survive removal of the personal narrative, displacement is not operating. Such artifacts exist; they are rare in this register; finding one is itself a useful analytical event.

You feel implicated in the analysis. Recruitment videos in this register are often produced by labor drawn from the same aesthetic culture as their audiences. If you find that your own aesthetic preferences are recognizing themselves in the artifact, this is data, not bias. The continuum’s labor and audience pools are continuous; recognizing oneself is part of the regime’s infrastructure of trust. Note the recognition explicitly in your reading; it is part of how the artifact does its work.

¶What this procedure is not

This procedure does not produce a moral judgment of the institution that produced the artifact, of the workers who made it, or of the audiences that engage with it. It produces an analytical reading of the operation the artifact is performing at the level of audience formation. The political question of what to do with that reading — refuse the artifact, engage it ironically, produce counter-content, document the apparatus — is downstream of the reading. The reading is the precondition for any of those choices.
