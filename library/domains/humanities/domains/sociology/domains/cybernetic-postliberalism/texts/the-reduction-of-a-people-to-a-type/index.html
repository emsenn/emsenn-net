The Reduction of a People to a Type: Genring, Romantic Nationalism, and the Mechanics of Fascist Exclusion

2026-03-31 Genring — the reduction of a living people to a fixed literary type — is a political operation traceable from Herder's Volksgeist through the Brothers Grimm to Karl May and Oktoberfest. Fascist exclusion follows from genre coherence: once a nation accepts a fixed cast of folk types, anyone who deviates becomes a threat to the story.

¶Abstract

Genring is the reduction of a living people to a fixed literary type. This paper argues it is not a cultural curiosity but a political operation with a traceable philosophical genealogy and specific political consequences. The genealogy begins with Johann Gottfried Herder’s claim (1784) that each people has a unique spirit — a Volksgeist — expressed through its language, songs, and customs. This claim creates a demand: if the Volk has a spirit, the spirit must be made visible. The Brothers Grimm responded to this demand by producing the first systematically genred population in modern European history, editing and inventing a corpus of “German folk tales” (1812–1857) that confirmed the existence of a German Volksgeist by manufacturing its evidence (Zipes 2002). By the late 19th century, the operation had been deployed twice across German culture: outward as Karl May’s Noble Indian (1893) and inward as the Hearty Bavarian of the Trachtenverein movement (1880s–1890s). Both genre figures share identical structural features — closeness to land, simplicity, communality, timelessness — because both serve the same function: guaranteeing that authentic folk life exists somewhere. This paper uses Bakhtin’s concept of heteroglossia (1935/1981) to theorize what genring destroys, connects the mechanism to the elimination of the temporal middle under settler colonialism (Wolfe 2006), and argues that fascist exclusion follows from genre coherence: once a nation imagines itself as a cast of authentic folk types, anyone who deviates becomes a threat to the story.

¶1. Volksgeist: The Demand for a Character

In the 1780s, Johann Gottfried Herder proposed that humanity is not one but many. Each people — each Volk — possesses a unique spirit, a Volksgeist, expressed through its particular language, songs, customs, myths, and material practices. Against the Enlightenment universalism of his contemporaries, Herder argued that there is no single human nature but only particular peoples, each with its own way of being in the world. The Volk is not a political unit (that is the state) but a cultural one: the organic community that speaks, sings, and tells stories in a way no other community does (Herder 1784).

This was a philosophical claim with a political consequence. If each Volk has a spirit, then the spirit must be locatable. It must be expressed in specific cultural forms — songs that can be collected, tales that can be recorded, costumes that can be worn, festivals that can be attended. The Volksgeist must be made visible, because its visibility is the proof that the Volk exists as a coherent entity and not merely as a population residing in a territory.

Herder’s intention was pluralist: he argued for the equal dignity of all peoples and against the hierarchical ranking of cultures. But his framework created a demand that outlived his intentions. If the Volk has a spirit that must be made visible, then someone must do the making visible. Someone must decide which songs, which tales, which costumes, which practices count as expressions of the Volksgeist — and which do not. The framework requires curators, and curators require criteria. The criteria will necessarily select, simplify, and codify. This is where genring begins.

Johann Gottlieb Fichte radicalized Herder’s pluralism into political nationalism. His Addresses to the German Nation (1808), delivered during the Napoleonic occupation of Prussia, argued that the German Volk was not merely one culture among many but a culture with a special mission, and that the German language — unlike the Romance languages, which Fichte considered corrupted by Latin — preserved an original, living connection to the Volksgeist. Fichte transformed Herder’s cultural observation into a political imperative: the Volk must be defended, its spirit must be preserved, and its authenticity must be protected against foreign corruption.

Between Herder and Fichte, the demand for genre figures was established. A Volk needs characters that express its spirit. Those characters must be recognizable, reproducible, and emotionally resonant. They must embody closeness to land, simplicity, communality, and timelessness — the features that distinguish organic folk life from the alienated modernity that threatens it. The demand was philosophical. The response was editorial.

¶2. The Brothers Grimm: Producing the Folk

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm published the first edition of the Kinder- und Hausmärchen in 1812. The project was explicitly Herderian: they announced it as a collection of tales told by the German Volk, gathered from oral tradition, preserved as a record of the living Volksgeist. The preface presented the tales as ancient, authentic, and communally authored — the spontaneous expression of a people’s spirit.

The reality, documented by Jack Zipes (2002) among others, was different. The Grimms’ sources were largely middle-class, educated, and in several cases French-speaking. Dorothea Viehmann, celebrated as an archetypal German peasant storyteller, was of Huguenot descent. Many tales derived from French literary sources, including Charles Perrault’s 1697 collection. The first edition (1812) was rough, containing sexual content, morally ambiguous characters, and narrative structures that did not conform to any unified “German” sensibility.

Over seven editions spanning 45 years (1812–1857), the Grimms systematically transformed their material. Sexual content was removed. Violence against children was softened (though violence against villains was retained and intensified). Stepmothers replaced biological mothers as antagonists. Christian morality was woven into tales that originally carried none. Plots were tightened, characters were simplified, and narrative arcs were standardized. German-sounding settings, names, and customs were substituted for originally non-German elements.

By the seventh edition, the Kinder- und Hausmärchen had become what it claimed to have been from the start: a collection of distinctly German folk tales expressing the German Volksgeist. But the tales were not found. They were produced. The Grimms did not collect the folk. They genred a population — selecting specific narrative features, codifying them into a stable corpus, and circulating the corpus until it replaced the heterogeneous oral culture it claimed to represent.

Eric Hobsbawm’s concept of the “invention of tradition” (1983) names this operation precisely: practices that present themselves as ancient and organically continuous are in fact recent, deliberately constructed, and serving present political needs. The Grimms’ folk tales are the paradigmatic case. They invented a tradition of German folk narrative, presented it as immemorial, and thereby furnished the Volksgeist with the evidence of its own existence.

This is the template for all subsequent genring: a living, various, internally contradictory culture is curated into a stable set of characters, stories, and images that confirm the existence of the Volk. What does not fit the curation disappears — not because it is suppressed, but because it is not selected. The folk who remain are the folk who were produced.

¶3. The Operation

Genring operates through three steps:

Selection. Specific features of a people’s life are extracted: dress, diet, habitat, ritual, emotional register. The features are chosen not for representativeness but for narrative utility — they must be visually striking, emotionally resonant, and reproducible without the context from which they were taken. A Lakota sun dance becomes a feather. A Bavarian farming practice becomes a dirndl.

Codification. The selected features are assembled into a stable character template and circulated through novels, tales, images, performances, and festivals. The template becomes self-reinforcing: audiences recognize the genre, expect it, and reward its reproduction. Deviations are experienced not as inaccuracy but as inauthenticity — the character “doesn’t feel right.” The genre develops its own feeling rules.

Substitution. The genre replaces the people. To encounter the people is to encounter the character. Anything that does not fit the genre — internal disagreement, intellectual production, political complexity, modernity, grief, ambition — becomes invisible. Not suppressed: illegible. The living people disappears behind the literary type.

James C. Scott (1998) calls a related operation “legibility production”: the simplification of complex social reality into categories a state can see and administer. Genring is legibility with a narrative engine. The simplified image is not merely a category but a character, and the character inhabits a story. The story generates obligations: to protect the character, to maintain the genre, to exclude whatever threatens its coherence. Legibility makes populations administrable. Genring makes them narratively necessary — and narratively expendable.

¶4. The Outward Deployment: Karl May and the Noble Indian

Karl May began serializing his Winnetou stories in the 1870s and published the novels in book form in 1893. He never visited North America. His Apache protagonist routinely inhabited Lakota territory, dressed in Lakota-adjacent costume, and performed rituals drawn from May’s imagination rather than any nation’s practice. The inaccuracy is constitutive, not incidental: Winnetou is not a representation of any real people. He is a genre figure assembled from whatever features best served a narrative of noble, doomed authenticity (Penny 2013).

May remains one of the best-selling German-language authors in history. His novels were translated across Europe, establishing a continental template for “the Indian.” The template — feathered warrior, mystical medicine man, tipi-and-buffalo camp, noble bearing in the face of extinction — proved extraordinarily durable. It structured German encounters with Plains peoples for over a century and continues to structure them today (Lutz 2002).

The genre served a German need, not an Indigenous one. For German audiences facing industrialization, urbanization, and the dislocations of national unification, the Plains Indian became the fantasy of the natural, noble, unspoiled other: close to land, simple, communal, timeless. The genre guaranteed that authentic folk life existed somewhere beyond the factory and the city, and that the longing for roots had a referent in the world. Lakota and other Plains nations were not consulted about this role. They were cast in it.

Buffalo Bill’s Wild West Show, which toured Germany in the 1890s and early 1900s, reinforced the template through embodied performance. Live Lakota performers were presented as spectacle, confirming the literary genre through physical presence. The show taught audiences that the character had a body, not just a page (Deloria 1998). When the show crossed the Atlantic, it carried the same messages in both directions — teaching Americans and Germans the same genre figure through the same apparatus.

The author of this paper is Lakota. The genre figure Karl May invented to satisfy German Romantic longing remains the lens through which most non-Native people encounter Lakota identity. When the genre is not matched — when a Lakota person writes mathematics, designs protocol governance, argues about economic theory — they become illegible. The genre cannot accommodate them. This is not a personal grievance. It is the structural consequence of substitution: the character has replaced the people.

¶5. The Inward Deployment: The Invention of Bavaria

The same Romantic nationalism that produced the Noble Indian produced the Hearty Bavarian. The first Oktoberfest was held in Munich in 1810 — a horse race celebrating the marriage of Crown Prince Ludwig — but the beer-and-Tracht festival of modern recognition is a product of the late 19th century.

The Trachtenverein (traditional dress society) movement emerged in the 1880s and 1890s. Urban middle-class Germans, facing the same industrial dislocations that created the appetite for Karl May, organized to “preserve” Alpine peasant dress, music, and custom. The preservation was a production. The dirndl, the lederhosen, the felt hat, the oompah band — these were not drawn from living peasant practice but from an urban bourgeois imagination of what peasant practice should look like (Hobsbawm 1983). The peasant was curated: hearty, communal, close to the land, simple, timeless. Like the Grimms’ folk tales, the “traditional” Bavarian aesthetic was roughly as old as the national need it served.

The structural features are identical to the Noble Indian:

Feature Noble Indian (outward) Hearty Bavarian (inward)
Closeness to land Tipi, buffalo, open plains Alpine meadow, farm, forest
Simplicity Pre-industrial subsistence Rustic agrarian self-sufficiency
Communality Tribal bonds, campfire Village, beer hall, festival
Timelessness “Unchanged since ancient times” “Traditional since time immemorial”
Costume Feathers, buckskin, war paint Dirndl, lederhosen, felt hat
Emotional register Noble, stoic, doomed Hearty, warm, enduring

The identity of features is not coincidental. Both figures were produced in the same decades, by the same cultural class, responding to the same crisis, drawing on the same Romantic nationalist framework. The Noble Indian is the authentic other — proof that folk life exists outside modernity. The Hearty Bavarian is the authentic self — proof that folk life persists within the nation’s own past. Both guarantee what the Volksgeist requires: that the Volk is real, that its roots are deep, and that it can be represented by characters recognizable on sight.

¶6. What Genring Destroys

Mikhail Bakhtin’s concept of heteroglossia (1935, published in English in 1981) names the condition genring eliminates. Heteroglossia is the coexistence of multiple social languages within any living community — not just dialects but entire registers, worldviews, evaluative stances, and ways of being. A community contains farmers and poets, traditionalists and radicals, the pious and the profane, the ambitious and the content. These voices are not reconciled into a single perspective. They coexist in tension, answering and contesting one another. This multi-voicedness is the normal condition of any living culture.

Genring collapses heteroglossia into monologue. The multiple voices of a living people are replaced by a single authorized character. The Lakota community of the 1870s contained warriors and peace advocates, hunters and farmers, ceremonial specialists and political strategists, people who agreed about nothing except that they were Lakota. May’s Winnetou retains one voice: the Noble Savage. The Bavarian village contained radical Catholics and secular socialists, subsistence farmers and industrial workers, tradition-keepers and modernizers. Oktoberfest retains one voice: the Hearty Peasant.

Everything heteroglossia contains — internal debate, generational conflict, competing visions of the good life, the ordinary mess of people who share a territory and disagree about how to inhabit it — is silenced. Not by censorship. By illegibility. The genre does not suppress the other voices. It simply provides no character for them. A Lakota person who farms is not censored; they are invisible, because the genre has no role for a Lakota farmer. A Bavarian socialist is not suppressed; they are incomprehensible, because the genre cannot accommodate a Bavarian who is not hearty.

Genring also destroys what the companion paper The American Genocide Against the Temporal Middle calls the temporal middle: the position of inheriting, continuing, and transforming a tradition. Every living person occupies this position. No one is purely “traditional” (frozen in the past) or purely “modern” (severed from the past). People inherit what they inherit, transform what they can, and continue.

But genre does not accommodate the temporal middle. The Noble Indian is timeless — “unchanged since ancient times.” The Hearty Bavarian is timeless — “traditional since time immemorial.” Both are defined by stasis. A Lakota person who writes mathematics occupies the temporal middle: inheriting Lakota intellectual traditions, engaging with European mathematical traditions, continuing both. But the genre has no character for this person. It offers only two positions: the traditional Indian (who matches the genre) and the assimilated Indian (who has abandoned it). There is no position for someone who is doing what every living person does: continuing.

This is why genring is not merely offensive. It is eliminative. It eliminates the position from which a people can be recognized as what they actually are: living, various, temporally complex, and present.

¶7. Convergence in Fascism

The Nazi regime understood both genre figures as useful.

Karl May was one of Hitler’s favorite authors. Winnetou was actively promoted during the book-burning campaigns that destroyed most of Germany’s literary culture. The Karl-May-Festspiele were established in Bad Segeberg after the war but continuous with the wartime promotion; “Indianer” hobby clubs, where Germans dress in Plains-style regalia and perform invented rituals, persist across Germany today (Lutz 2002; Penny 2013). German scholarship from Lutz and Penny documents how, from the Kaiserreich through the Third Reich, Plains Indians served as stage and props for German debates about identity, nature, and empire — a surrogate for the authentic life the regime promised to restore.

Bavarian folk aesthetics were simultaneously incorporated into the regime’s visual language. The Volk was imagined through images of peasant community, agrarian self-sufficiency, and blood-and-soil belonging. Oktoberfest and its associated aesthetics were amplified, not suppressed.

This convergence follows from the logic of genring. Roger Griffin (1991) defines fascism as palingenetic ultranationalism: a mythic narrative of national rebirth from decadence. The narrative requires a cast. Someone must represent what the nation was before it declined, what it lost, and what it will become when it is reborn. Genring supplies this cast. The Noble Indian represents the natural life modernity destroyed. The Hearty Bavarian represents the folk community that must be reclaimed. Both are characters in the story of decline and rebirth — a story that cannot be told without genre figures to populate it.

The critical mechanism is not ideology. It is genre coherence. Once the nation imagines itself as a cast of authentic folk types, the integrity of the story depends on the characters remaining stable. Anyone who does not fit the genre — who is not noble, not hearty, not close to the land, not simple, not timeless — becomes a problem for the story. Not necessarily an enemy in the ideological sense, but a narrative disruption: a character who breaks the fourth wall.

Jews, Roma, queer people, disabled people, urban intellectuals, modern workers — none fit the genre of the authentic Volk. They are not characters in the story the regime tells about itself. Their presence threatens the coherence of the narrative. Exclusion follows from the logic of genre: the story cannot include them and remain the story. Whether the exclusion takes the form of social marginalization, legal disenfranchisement, or physical elimination depends on circumstances. The mechanism — genre coherence demanding the removal of what does not fit — is the same at every scale.

Genring is not a sufficient condition for fascism. Genred populations exist in many contexts that do not produce fascist regimes. But fascism requires genring, because it requires a mythic past populated by recognizable characters, and genring is the operation that produces them.

¶8. The Mechanism Persists

The dual deployment of the genre figure continues wherever national identity is stabilized through consumable images of folk authenticity.

In the contemporary United States, the Noble Indian persists as the Mystical Ecologist: Indigenous peoples genred as wisdom-keepers, ceremonial performers, and spiritual guides — roles that make them legible to settler audiences while erasing their political, intellectual, and economic lives. Coulthard (2014) analyzes this as the politics of recognition: the settler state offers inclusion in exchange for legibility, and legibility requires performing the genre (the authentic traditional Indian or the assimilated modern subject). Audra Simpson’s study of Kahnawà:ke Mohawks (2014) documents the alternative: refusal — declining to perform the genre, carrying Haudenosaunee passports, asserting sovereignty by withdrawing from the recognition framework entirely.

Simultaneously, rural white Americans are genred as salt-of-the-earth: gun-owning, church-going, truck-driving folk — a character template that erases the actual complexity of rural life while anchoring a populist narrative of “real America.” The genre figure is as recent and as constructed as Karl May’s Indians or the Grimms’ fairy tales: a product of political need, not an expression of living culture.

The mechanism operates wherever a living people is curated into a consumable image. Every feathered headdress at a music festival. Every “real America” political rally. Every “traditional Indigenous wisdom” printed on a T-shirt. Every Oktoberfest-themed bar in a city where no one has ever milked a cow. The character is present. The people are absent.

¶9. Closing

Genring does not produce stereotypes. Stereotypes are lazy generalizations that dissolve on contact with reality. Genring is precise: it selects specific features, codifies them into a stable character template, circulates the template through media and performance until it becomes self-reinforcing, and substitutes the character for the people. The result is not ignorance but a specific kind of knowledge — knowledge of the character — that functions as knowledge of the people. This false equation is what makes genring dangerous. It provides the feeling of understanding while destroying the capacity to encounter the actual person standing in front of you.

A living culture is heteroglossic: multi-voiced, internally contradictory, temporally complex, occupied by people who match no single type. Genring replaces this with a character who matches one type perfectly. The character is satisfying. The character is recognizable. The character confirms that the Volk is real. And because the character is satisfying, recognizable, and confirming, there is no felt need to look past it — to ask whether the Lakota person in front of you has an opinion about protocol governance, or whether the person in the dirndl has read Marx.

Genring makes a contradiction livable. An industrial society that has destroyed its folk roots produces genre figures that promise the roots still exist. A settler state that has displaced Indigenous peoples produces genre figures that promise the peoples still exist — as characters, if not as political agents. The figures manage the contradiction between what the nation has done and what it needs to believe about itself. This management is not resolution. It is formatting. And as long as the formatting holds, the contradiction persists — along with the exclusion that protects it.
