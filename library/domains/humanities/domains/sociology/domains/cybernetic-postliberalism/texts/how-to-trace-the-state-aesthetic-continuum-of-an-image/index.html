How to Trace the State-Aesthetic Continuum of an Image

2026-04-30 A procedure for taking a single image, clip, or short artifact and locating it across the state-aesthetic continuum: which positions it occupies, which conventions it has migrated, and which institutional cover it is presented under versus which it formally inhabits.

This is a working procedure for taking a specific image, short clip, or short artifact and locating it across the state-aesthetic continuum. The procedure is most useful when you encounter an artifact that puzzles you — an image whose institutional cover and formal register seem mismatched, a brand campaign that feels like a recruitment video, a recruitment video that feels like a music video, a wellness post that feels like influence content. The procedure walks through the work of pinning down what the artifact is and what it is doing.

This how-to assumes familiarity with the term state-aesthetic continuum, the long-form argument in the state-aesthetic continuum, and the constituent terms it draws on: rubber-hose realism, strategic infantilization, soft surrealism, memetic warfare, and the moral physics layer they all sit on.

¶What you’ll need

  • A specific artifact: a single image, a single short clip, or a single piece of platform-native content. The procedure can be applied to longer artifacts, but its precision is highest when the artifact is short enough to be held in mind at once.
  • The capacity to look up the artifact’s institutional context. You should be able to identify (or research) who produced it, under what cover, and how it was distributed.
  • About 30–90 minutes per artifact.
  • Optionally, a small comparison set: two or three other artifacts you suspect occupy adjacent positions on the continuum. Comparison sharpens the reading even when the analytical work is being done on a single object.

¶Step 1: Identify the institutional cover

Write down what the artifact’s institutional cover is:

  • Who produced it?
  • Under what declared purpose?
  • Where was it distributed?
  • What kind of artifact does the institution claim it is?

Be specific. “A 30-second video posted on Instagram by a brand” is too vague. “A 30-second video posted on Instagram by Brand X as part of their Year-Y campaign Z, with the declared purpose of celebrating their commitment to mental health” is specific enough to work with.

If the institutional cover is unclear (anonymous account, unattributed image, ambiguous origin), this itself is a finding. Note it. Many artifacts in the contemporary state-aesthetic continuum are deliberately distributed under ambiguous cover, as part of how the apparatus operates. The ambiguity is data.

¶Step 2: Identify the formal vocabulary

Now describe the artifact’s formal vocabulary. Use the constituent terms of the continuum’s analysis as a checklist:

  • Moral physics. What is the artifact’s regime along persistence, locus of causation, reversibility, body weight, and world memory? Is the artifact operating inside a rubber-hose realism regime or in a different moral physics?
  • Address mode. Is the artifact deploying strategic infantilization markers? Which?
  • Temporal structure. Is affective looping operating? At what scale?
  • Surface register. Is the artifact operating in soft surrealism? Which markers are present?
  • Doctrinal/strategic register. Does the artifact deploy vocabulary or formal moves associated with memetic warfare or other openly-doctrinal influence registers?

Write down the formal vocabulary with specific reference to the artifact. Be concrete. “Soft-surrealist with collage and motif-return” is more useful than “weird vibe.”

¶Step 3: Identify the artifact’s position(s)

Given the institutional cover (Step 1) and the formal vocabulary (Step 2), identify which position(s) on the continuum the artifact occupies. The major positions:

  • Commercial entertainment.
  • Corporate soft power.
  • Brand-justice marketing.
  • Platform-native creator content.
  • Recruitment and influence media.
  • Open psychological operations.

Do not assume the artifact occupies only one position. Many artifacts occupy two or three simultaneously — recruitment material distributed on platform-native channels under doctrinal influence is operating across recruitment, platform-native creator, and open-PSYOP positions at once.

For each position you identify, write down what specifically places the artifact at that position. Be precise about whether the placement is by cover (the institutional framing claims this position) or by vocabulary (the formal techniques are characteristic of this position).

¶Step 4: Locate the cover/vocabulary gap

Compare the institutional cover and the formal vocabulary. The diagnostic question:

Is the artifact’s formal vocabulary farther along the continuum than its institutional cover acknowledges?

Several patterns recur:

  • A brand campaign whose vocabulary is in the open-PSYOP register (collage, screens-within-screens, distorted voiceover, motif return). Cover: marketing. Vocabulary: influence operations.
  • A recruitment video whose vocabulary is in the platform-native creator register (meme-form, ironic juxtaposition, retro revival). Cover: institutional outreach. Vocabulary: viral creator content.
  • A wellness post whose vocabulary is in the recruitment-media register (oversized affect cues, manipulable-control metaphors, sustained loop). Cover: self-care content. Vocabulary: institutional address.

Note the gap. Where the cover and vocabulary align, the artifact is doing what its institution says it is doing. Where they do not align, the artifact is doing something its cover does not announce. The gap is almost always where the most analytically-interesting work is happening.

¶Step 5: Track what migrated

The artifact’s vocabulary did not arise from nowhere. The techniques it uses came from somewhere — from earlier positions on the continuum, from earlier artifacts, from earlier audiences’ competencies. Try to trace the migration.

Useful questions:

  • Where did this technique originate? (For rubber-hose moral physics, 1920s American animation; for soft-surrealist collage, late-twentieth-century music video; for meme-form structures, platform-native creator culture.)
  • Through what positions has it moved? (Animation → soft power → recruitment; music video → branding → influence operations; meme-form → creator economy → doctrinal use.)
  • What labor pool brought the technique to its current position? (Workers trained in earlier positions of the continuum, hired to produce content for current positions.)

You will not always be able to trace the migration in detail. The point of the step is not to produce an exhaustive history but to foreground that the technique is migrating, that the migration is part of what the artifact is, and that what looks novel about the artifact is often the recombination of techniques developed elsewhere on the continuum.

¶Step 6: Identify the audience and its mode

Recall that the same audience operates across the continuum in different modes. For your artifact:

  • Who is the artifact’s declared audience? (The institution’s named target audience.)
  • Who is the artifact’s actual audience? (Often broader, often including audiences the institution did not declare.)
  • What mode is the actual audience reading the artifact in? (Sincere reception, ironic reception, professional analysis, adversary intelligence, casual scroll.)

Multiple modes can be operating simultaneously. A recruitment video can be sincerely received by potential recruits, ironically received by online culture, and professionally analyzed by journalists, all at once. Note all the modes you can identify.

The mode the audience is in determines what the artifact is doing in its actual circulation, which is often different from what its institution intended. This is one of the principal payoffs of the continuum reading: it lets the audience’s positional fluency become an analytical object.

¶Step 7: Identify the openly named techniques

For artifacts in the open-PSYOP position or near it, check whether the doctrinal vocabulary names what the artifact is doing. Doctrinal discussions of memetic warfare, narrative-conflict capability, influence operations, and information environment shaping have become quite open in U.S. military and NATO publications. If the artifact’s techniques are openly named in this literature, note the names.

This step is useful because the open-PSYOP position serves as documentation for the rest of the continuum. Techniques the doctrinal register names are techniques the brand and entertainment positions deploy without acknowledgment. The doctrinal naming makes the unacknowledged uses readable.

¶Step 8: Compose the trace

Assemble your findings into a trace. The trace should name:

  • The artifact’s institutional cover.
  • The artifact’s formal vocabulary.
  • The position(s) on the continuum the artifact occupies (and the specific evidence for each).
  • The cover/vocabulary gap, if any.
  • The migrations the artifact’s techniques have made to arrive at this position.
  • The audience(s) and mode(s) actually receiving the artifact.
  • Any openly-named techniques the artifact deploys.

The trace is short — a few hundred words. It is not a critique of the artifact; it is a positioning. Critique can follow, but the trace’s job is to put the artifact accurately on the continuum so that subsequent analysis has the placement correct.

¶A worked example, in compressed form

Consider a single screen-shot from the Ghosts in the Machine PSYOP recruitment series: rapid-cut imagery of masks, archival footage of riots, a slow-pitched voiceover speaking of “shaping perception.”

  • Institutional cover. Produced by the U.S. Army’s 4th Psychological Operations Group; declared as a recruitment video; distributed through DVIDS and platform-native channels.
  • Formal vocabulary. Soft-surrealist collage with screen-within-screen and motif-return; distorted voiceover; rubber-hose moral physics with rapid persistence-decay and unattributable causation; strategic-infantilization markers (manipulable-control imagery, oversized affect cues); affective looping at the cut-rate level.
  • Positions occupied. Recruitment and influence media (by cover); platform-native creator content (by formal register and distribution); open psychological operations (by content thematized).
  • Gap. The institutional cover claims recruitment; the formal vocabulary is significantly into platform-native and open-PSYOP territory. The artifact is doing more than its cover names.
  • Migrations. Rubber-hose moral physics from 1920s–1930s animation; soft-surrealist collage from late-twentieth-century music video; meme-form structure from platform-native creator culture.
  • Audience and mode. Declared audience: potential recruits. Actual audience includes ironic platform users, journalists, and adversary intelligence services. Modes: sincere recruitment-target reception, ironic platform reception, professional journalistic analysis, and (presumably) adversary intelligence assessment, all simultaneously.
  • Openly-named techniques. Memetic warfare; narrative-conflict capability; perception-shaping operations.

The trace places the artifact: it sits at multiple positions, has a substantial cover/vocabulary gap, and operates inside an audience that is itself fluent in the continuum. Subsequent analysis can work from this placement.

¶Common difficulties

The institutional cover is opaque. Some artifacts circulate without clear institutional attribution. This is itself a feature of contemporary state-aesthetic operation; the opacity is part of the form. Note it as a finding rather than as a procedural failure.

The formal vocabulary is mixed. Most artifacts deploy techniques from multiple positions. The exercise is not to find a single position but to map the combination of positions the artifact occupies.

You cannot trace the migration. Migration tracking requires knowledge of the history of the techniques. If you do not know it, do what you can and note where your knowledge runs out. Migration tracking is an iterative skill that improves as you do more readings.

The audience modes are unstable. Audience reception of contemporary state-aesthetic content shifts faster than you can reliably observe. This is a feature of the continuum, not a bug of the procedure. A trace describes what is operating at a moment; the moments will move.

¶What this procedure is not

The trace is not a critique. It is a placement. Whether to be appalled, amused, fascinated, or instrumentally engaged with the artifact is a separate question; the trace is what makes that question well-posed.
