Liberalism's Constitutive Contradictions

2026-04-27 Liberalism carries four constitutive contradictions that no internal liberal move resolves — now metabolized through subjects rather than institutional buffers.

The Energetic Substrate established what kind of pastoral apparatus is structurally feasible under contemporary conditions: a thinner institutional interior than the twentieth-century predecessor, with the metabolism redistributed onto bodies and platforms. The next question follows directly: what is the apparatus actually metabolizing? What was the input the institutional buffer absorbed, and what is now being routed through subjects instead?

The answer cybernetic postliberalism gives is: liberalism’s constitutive contradictions. Liberalism has always carried tensions internal to its own operating logic. Under conditions of institutional surplus, these tensions were managed within institutional buffers — debated in legislatures, deferred to courts, routed through welfare-state compensations, absorbed by regulated markets, processed by the slow apparatuses that the energetic surplus funded. Under contracted conditions, the tensions must be metabolized somewhere. They are now metabolized as personally manageable affect: routed onto subjects who experience the contradictions as their own private problems and self-regulate in response. This is what californication names. The contradictions must be specified before the operation that metabolizes them can be read.

¶What “constitutive” means

The contradictions are constitutive, not residual. They are not flaws that liberalism can identify and correct; they are part of what liberalism is. A liberalism that resolved them would no longer be liberalism — it would be a different organizing logic, with a different distribution of who counts and how, and with different institutional commitments. Liberalism’s longevity has rested on its capacity to route the contradictions through successive institutional configurations, not on its capacity to dissolve them.

This claim has a long pedigree. C. B. Macpherson (1962) showed that liberalism’s universal subject — the individual whose rights are to be protected — is in fact the possessive individual, a particular configuration of personhood (proprietary, individuated, severed from prior relations) that emerges historically from a specific class context. Carole Pateman (1988) showed that the liberal social contract presupposes a prior sexual contract that gendered the subject of consent in ways the universalist vocabulary obscures. Sylvia Wynter (2003) showed that the universal subject of liberal thought has been Man in her overrepresentation sense — a particular ethnoclass occupying the universal position. Domenico Losurdo (2011) showed historically that liberalism’s universalist vocabulary has stably coexisted with chattel slavery, colonial expropriation, and gender exclusion across centuries. The point of these analyses is not that liberalism is hypocritical (which it sometimes is) but that the contradiction is structural: the universalism only works as universalism because some subjects occupy the position of the universal and others do not. This structural condition is what liberal ontologizing names — the operation by which liberalism produces its subjects as the kind of subject its categories presuppose.

¶The four contradictions

Cybernetic postliberalism names four constitutive contradictions. They are not the only ones identifiable; they are the four that bear on contemporary californication most directly.

¶Universalism without individualism

Liberalism is universalist: it claims that its categories (rights, dignity, consent, autonomy) apply to all humans. But its categories presuppose a particular kind of individual — propertied, autonomous, severed from kinship-and-place obligations, contracting from a position of formal equality, oriented to a future that can be planned. Where the actual subject does not match this presupposition, the universalism applies to them only insofar as they reshape themselves toward the presupposed individual. The universal coverage requires individual conformity to a particular form. Subjects who cannot or will not undergo the conformity are positioned as not-yet-fully-individual, and the universalism’s promise to them is conditional on a transformation they may have no interest in.

This is the contradiction Wynter analyzes: liberalism’s universalism presupposes Man as the standard against which other genres of the human are positioned as deviations. The contradiction cannot be resolved by extending Man’s categories to more subjects, because the categories themselves are Man’s categories. The reflex of inclusion does not address the contradiction; it intensifies the demand for transformation. The structural pressure that demands subjects fit the assigned position is what genre coherence names; subjects who deviate are read as narrative disruptions rather than as the actual people they are, and the temporal middle — the position of inheriting and continuing a tradition without either freezing it or abandoning it — is structurally eliminated.

¶Individualism without universalism

The complementary contradiction. Liberalism is individualist: it treats the rights-bearing individual as the foundational unit of political life. But individuality, as a lived condition, is sustained by relations — kinship, community, shared practice, place — that liberalism’s vocabulary does not register and whose loss produces the isolated individual that liberalism’s institutions cannot hold. The universalist promise was supposed to compensate for the loss of relation by providing rights that the relation was no longer available to provide. But the rights presuppose a state, a market, and a civil society capable of recognizing the individual; when these institutional supports thin, the individual is left holding rights without conditions for their exercise.

This is the contradiction Polanyi (1944) traces in The Great Transformation: the disembedding of the market from social relations produces a society in which individual subjects have universal market-rights and no relational fabric to exercise them within. Polanyi’s “self-protection of society” — the cycle of disembedding and re-embedding — is the historical signature of this contradiction.

¶Progress without exhaustion

Liberalism is developmental: it organizes itself around the promise that conditions improve over time, that growth resolves what redistribution cannot, that the future will be richer (in possibility, in welfare, in capacity) than the past. But the developmental promise presupposes inputs — energetic, material, ecological, demographic — that are not unlimited. The contradiction is between liberalism’s organizing temporality (linear progress) and the actual conditions of its operation (cyclical, finite, exhaustible).

The Energetic Substrate established the contraction’s empirical specifics. The contradiction here is the temporal-political form of that contraction: liberalism’s institutional vocabulary cannot register exhaustion, because exhaustion contradicts the developmental promise. Subjects are addressed with the language of progress (your future will be richer than your past) under conditions in which progress can no longer be funded. The address persists; the conditions for its fulfillment do not. Mark Fisher’s capitalist realism names the cultural-imaginative correlate: alternatives have been rendered structurally unthinkable, and the slow cancellation of the future is the temporal phenomenology of liberalism’s unfundable promise.

¶Equality without sovereignty

Liberalism is egalitarian in a specific sense: it organizes itself around the formal equality of subjects before the law, the market, and the political process. But the formal equality presupposes a polity that can guarantee it — a sovereign that can enforce contracts, redistribute resources, regulate markets, secure rights. Liberalism’s economic vocabulary, in its dominant late-twentieth-century form, has dissolved that sovereign capacity: capital flows beyond national jurisdiction, markets coordinate across political boundaries, supranational arbitration overrides domestic deliberation. The formal equality persists; the sovereign that could give it material content has been hollowed out.

Wendy Brown (2015) traces this hollowing in Undoing the Demos. Wolfgang Streeck (2014) traces the post-1970s management of the contradiction through inflation, debt, and austerity in Buying Time. The contradiction is not new — liberalism’s universal subject and its national-political enforcement have always been in tension — but the contemporary form is sharper because the sovereign capacity has thinned in ways the formal-egalitarian vocabulary continues to assume. The named structural moment in which the contradictions become experientially insoluble for subjects rather than locally manageable is the liberalism legitimacy crisis, under which the apparatus shifts from promising resolution to promising regulation.

¶Why liberalism cannot resolve them internally

The contradictions are constitutive, which means that no internal liberal move resolves them. Several familiar moves attempt to:

Inclusion-extension. Extend the universal categories to more subjects: women, the colonized, the racialized, the disabled, queer subjects. This is morally important and, often, materially consequential. But it does not resolve the universalism-individualism contradiction, because the categories being extended are still Man’s categories. The newly-included subject is admitted on the terms the existing standard provides, not on terms that change the standard. Wynter’s overrepresentation of Man persists.

Welfare-state compensation. Buffer the individualism-universalism contradiction by providing conditions (income, healthcare, education) within which the isolated individual can exercise their rights. This historically worked, on a contracting scale, where the institutional surplus permitted it. But the welfare-state buffer presupposes the energetic and economic surplus that funded it. As the surplus contracts, the buffer thins, and the contradiction the buffer was managing returns to the level of the subject’s own metabolism.

Reformist proceduralism. Manage the contradictions through better procedures: transparent governance, expert oversight, deliberative democracy, evidence-based policy. The procedures are real and they sometimes work. But they manage the symptoms of the contradictions (perceived illegitimacy, dysfunctional outcomes) rather than the contradictions themselves. They presuppose, again, the institutional surplus that allows procedures to be slow, deliberate, and binding. Expert naivete names the structural condition under which these procedures continue to operate as if their conditions of possibility had not changed.

Identitarian rearticulation. Make the contradictions visible through the perspective of the excluded, demanding recognition and redistribution. This is structurally important; Wynter, Pateman, the black radical tradition, and feminist political thought are foundational sources. But within the apparatus’s metabolism, the rearticulation often becomes content the system absorbs rather than transformation the system undergoes. The operational closure of late-liberal governance is the structural reason why: the rearticulation as a coherent subject-position is metabolizable.

These moves are not useless. They are often the most that can be done within liberal institutions, and the people who do them are not failing; the institutions they operate within are constrained. The point is that they do not resolve the contradictions, because the contradictions are constitutive, and the apparatus is now operating past the conditions under which these moves could have produced sufficient internal management.

¶How the contradictions become the input californication metabolizes

Under contracted institutional surplus, the metabolism of structural contradiction has redistributed onto bodies and platforms. The four contradictions specified above are what the apparatus is now metabolizing through subjects directly rather than upstream.

The connection is not abstract. Take the universalism-individualism contradiction concretely: a person who is not the propertied possessive individual is offered the universal vocabulary (you have rights, you matter, you can succeed) under conditions where their actual situation (precarious labor, debt, social isolation, racialized vulnerability) does not match the conditions under which the universal vocabulary’s promises could be redeemed. The institutional buffer that historically would have managed this — through public provision, regulated labor, redistributive measures — has thinned. The contradiction arrives at the subject as their personal experience: the universal promises and the particular conditions, in their face, every day. They are required to manage the tension. The mechanism by which they manage it is the apparatus’s governance.

Take the progress-exhaustion contradiction: subjects are addressed with the language of personal-development progress (your career, your relationship, your wellness, your output) under conditions of structural exhaustion. The gap between address and condition arrives as exhaustion-without-explanation: you should be progressing, you cannot identify the cause of your failure to progress, you must be the cause. The self-regulation that follows — the wellness-tracker, the productivity tool, the optimization regime — is californication at work. Han’s Leistungssubjekt names the affective shape of this condition: the achievement-subject who exploits themselves in the name of self-realization, whose burnout is not a failure of the form but the form itself. The structural condition has been formatted as a personal problem, and the subject’s labor of managing the problem is what the apparatus runs on.

Take the equality-sovereignty contradiction: subjects are addressed as formally equal under conditions where the sovereign capacities that could give equality material substance have been thinned. The gap arrives as the experience that “the system” is unaccountable: complaint cycles to no resolution, claims to standing produce no traction, the actual decisions affecting one’s life are made elsewhere by entities that do not respond to the formal-equal demand. The interpretive labor of trying to understand what is happening, who is responsible, what could be done — this is the labor the apparatus extracts. Coherent confusion names the system-level condition that results: subjects are confused but functional, producing interpretive labor that sustains the system precisely because it never resolves.

In concentrated form: the contradictions are not new, they are not failures of liberalism’s promise, they are constitutive of liberalism’s promise; what is new is the institutional condition under which the contradictions are metabolized through the subject rather than upstream. The texts that follow trace the specific mechanisms by which this metabolism is performed: genring and ontogenring (the subject-formation operations on which californication operates), californication and california as laboratory (the central operation and its historical site of iteration), platformal feedback architecture (the technical infrastructure that operates the metabolism at planetary scale), and the specific subject-formations the apparatus produces (the savior-slave subject, zen fascism, harm governance, the neurotic platformal intellectual, socially ontogenic media).
