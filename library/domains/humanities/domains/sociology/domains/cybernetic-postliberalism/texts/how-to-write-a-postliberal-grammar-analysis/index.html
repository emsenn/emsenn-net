How to Write a Postliberal-Grammar Analysis of a Public Discourse

2026-04-27 A step-by-step procedure for analyzing a public discourse for the rhetorical grammar it deploys: fascist, anarchist, or liberal-inverted.

This is a working procedure for analyzing the grammar of a public discourse — the structural rhetorical pattern that generates the discourse’s statements, distinct from the ideology the discourse expresses. Cybernetic postliberalism distinguishes fascist grammar and anarchist grammar as two principal generative structures, with liberal grammar reproducing fascist grammar in inverted form. The procedure walks through how to recognize which grammar operates in a specific discourse.

This how-to assumes familiarity with fascist grammar, anarchist grammar, genring, genre coherence, and the broader methods.

¶What you’ll need

  • A specific public discourse to analyze. Be concrete: a transcript of a speech, the text of a manifesto or column, a recorded media segment, a policy document. The discourse should be self-contained enough that you can read or listen to the whole of it.
  • 60-120 minutes for a careful analysis.
  • A notebook for tracking the grammar’s moves.

¶Step 1: Map the discourse’s main moves

Read or listen through the discourse and identify its main rhetorical moves in sequence. Don’t yet try to fit them into a grammar; just track what the discourse does.

Common moves to look for:

  • A diagnostic claim about the present (we are in crisis, we are in decline, we are in transition, we are in a moment).
  • A naming of the subject of the claim (we, the people, the West, the working class, the movement, the community).
  • A characterization of opponents (the parasites, the elites, the corrupt, the captured, the indifferent).
  • A reframing of suffering (sacrifice, cost, struggle, burden, evidence, witness).
  • A future-orientation (destiny, restoration, renewal, transformation, hope, possibility).
  • A modal claim about action (we must, we will, we are, we can, we may).

Write down the moves in the order the discourse makes them. Note any moves that don’t fit the categories above.

¶Step 2: Test against fascist grammar’s six steps

Fascist grammar has six structural moves:

  1. Declare crisis. Assert existential threat — not merely imperfect, fundamentally broken.
  2. Name a pure community. Identify the group whose survival is at stake.
  3. Denounce enemies as sterile or corrupt. Characterize opponents as agents of decay, parasites on the community’s generativity.
  4. Reframe suffering as sacrifice. Convert pain into evidence of moral standing; humiliation into proof of chosen status.
  5. Promise destiny. Assert that the community’s future greatness is assured if it acts now.
  6. Assert inevitability. Collapse the distinction between what should happen and what will happen.

For each step, ask: does the discourse perform this move? Where? What language does it use?

If the discourse performs all six (or most of six), it is operating in fascist grammar. If it performs the moves in laundered form (the postliberal-Catholic, the Cold War conservative, the contemporary right-postliberal), the laundering is itself diagnostic — note which moves are laundered and which appear in their cleaner-fascist form.

¶Step 3: Test against anarchist grammar’s six moves

Anarchist grammar has a different sequence:

  1. Condition (replaces crisis): catastrophe is not an event demanding salvation but the ongoing condition.
  2. Commons (replaces pure community): shared, negotiated relations rather than purified identity.
  3. Relation (replaces enemy): domination is relational, not metaphysical.
  4. Solidarity (replaces sacrifice): the refusal to abandon one another, not the glorification of suffering.
  5. Desire (replaces destiny): aspiration in the present, not fantasy projected onto history.
  6. Immanence (replaces inevitability): nothing is guaranteed, everything depends on what is done here and now.

Test the discourse against this sequence as well. Does it perform any of these moves? Does it perform some of fascist grammar and some of anarchist grammar (a mixed grammar)? Does it perform a different grammar entirely?

¶Step 4: Test against liberal-inverted grammar

Liberal grammar reproduces fascist grammar in inverted form. Each move is countered:

  • Crisis → progressive stability (Dewey’s managerial optimism).
  • Pure community → universal citizenship (Kelsen’s procedural Rechtsstaat).
  • Enemies → toleration (Popper’s open society).
  • Sacrifice → compromise (Rawls’s veil of ignorance).
  • Destiny → progress (incremental justice).
  • Inevitability → procedure (value pluralism).

If the discourse performs the liberal-inverted moves, it is operating in liberal grammar — which is not an exit from fascist grammar but its dialectical twin (Benjamin’s analysis, developed in the fascist grammar term).

Note: many discourses produced by self-identifying liberals operate in liberal-inverted grammar without recognizing the structural relation. The relation is what to identify, not the speakers’ self-identification.

¶Step 5: Identify the genre figures

The grammar’s moves typically deploy genre figures. Identify them.

Common deployments:

  • Pure community as genre figure. The “real American,” the “true patriot,” the “authentic working class,” the “ordinary citizen.” Each is a genre figure produced by genring, and the grammar deploys it to fill the second move’s content.
  • Enemy as genre figure. The “globalist elite,” the “woke mob,” the “deep state,” the “cosmopolitan intellectual.” Each is a genre figure deployed in the third move.
  • Sacred-past as genre figure. The “founding fathers,” the “ancient tradition,” the “civilizational heritage.” Deployed in the fifth move (destiny) to anchor the future-orientation in a constructed past.

Note which genre figures the discourse uses. Cross-reference with the genring how-to for the structural analysis of each.

¶Step 6: Identify the laundering

Strategic forgetting (developed in fascist grammar) launders fascist categories into post-1945 conservative and centrist discourse. Identify the laundering.

Common laundered moves:

  • “Western civilization” — Spengler’s Volk-civilization laundered into Cold War cultural-political vocabulary.
  • “Common good” — Schmitt’s friend-enemy distinction routed through Catholic-natural-law vocabulary.
  • “Makers vs. takers” — Jünger’s productive Worker vs. parasitic Other, laundered through Reaganite economic vocabulary.
  • “Realism” or “common sense” — fascism’s anti-deliberative posture laundered into procedural-political vocabulary.
  • “The forgotten man” — interwar populist vocabulary laundered into contemporary right-populist vocabulary.

Note where the discourse uses laundered vocabulary. The vocabulary’s history is part of its current function.

¶Step 7: Identify the structural function

The grammar serves a function in the broader apparatus. Identify it.

Ask: what does the grammar accomplish in this discourse? What political-cultural work is the rhetorical pattern performing?

Common functions:

  • Mobilization for political action. The grammar produces the affective conditions for collective political action under the speaker’s banner.
  • Justification for exclusion. The grammar provides the rhetorical structure within which exclusion (of the enemy, of the impure, of the deviating) is legitimated.
  • Stabilization under contradiction. The grammar provides a coherent narrative for subjects experiencing structural contradiction; the genre coherence the grammar enforces makes the narrative livable.
  • Recruitment of opposing subjects. The grammar’s specific moves are engineered to recruit subjects who initially identify against the speaker — the move’s affective work is what does the recruitment.

If the discourse operates in liberal-inverted grammar, the function is structurally adjacent: the grammar reproduces the structure while opposing the content, and its function is to keep the structure intact even as the speaker opposes its current incumbent.

¶Step 8: Write the analysis

Compile your findings into a short analytical statement. The structure:

  1. The discourse: what it is, when and where it appeared, who its audience is.
  2. The grammar: which generative structure operates (fascist, anarchist, liberal-inverted, mixed, other).
  3. The moves: how each move appears in the discourse, with specific language quoted.
  4. The genre figures: the figures the grammar deploys.
  5. The laundering: where laundered vocabulary appears and what it inherits.
  6. The structural function: what political-cultural work the grammar performs.

The analytical statement is specific, evidenced from the discourse, and structural rather than ideological. The structural reading does not depend on whether the speaker is, in their political-ideological self-understanding, a fascist, a liberal, an anarchist, or something else.

¶What this analysis does not say

The analysis is structural; it does not say the speaker is a fascist (or a liberal, or an anarchist). The structural-grammar identification is independent of the speaker’s political-ideological self-understanding. A speaker can operate in fascist grammar without being a member of a fascist political organization; the grammar is a generative structure that produces statements with fascist-form regardless of the speaker’s affiliations.

The analysis does not say the speaker should be denounced. The structural reading is descriptive sociology, not a moral verdict. What the speaker should be denounced for, supported for, organized against, or organized with is a different question that the structural reading does not answer.

The analysis does not say the discourse cannot be read in other ways. Other analytical frames — ideology-criticism, rhetorical-stylistic analysis, intentional-political analysis — produce other readings. The grammar reading is one analytical move that sits alongside, not above, these other readings.

¶Common pitfalls

Mistaking the speaker’s self-identification for the discourse’s grammar. Many speakers operating in fascist grammar self-identify as liberals, conservatives, populists, or progressives. The grammar is what the discourse does; the speaker’s self-identification is a separate datum.

Mistaking the discourse’s content for its grammar. A speaker can argue against fascism in fascist grammar (declaring the crisis of fascism, naming the pure anti-fascist community, denouncing the fascist enemies, reframing democratic suffering as sacrifice, promising democratic destiny, asserting the inevitability of justice). This is the dialectical-twin pattern Benjamin identified. Note both the content and the grammar.

Forgetting the genre-figure analysis. The grammar’s moves are typically empty without the genre figures that fill them. Skipping the genre-figure step makes the grammar analysis abstract and harder to evidence.

Treating “anarchist grammar” as automatically virtuous. Anarchist grammar names a different generative structure, not an automatically better politics. Speakers can deploy anarchist grammar in service of practices that the school’s broader analysis would criticize. The structural identification of which grammar operates is one analytical move; whether the politics produced by the grammar are the politics one wants is a different question.

Generalizing from one discourse to the speaker’s whole oeuvre. A single discourse may operate in one grammar; the speaker’s broader work may operate in another, or in a mix. Conclusions about the broader oeuvre require analyzing more discourses.

¶Where to take the analysis next

The grammar analysis often opens into adjacent analyses:

  • The genring of the genre figures. For each genre figure the grammar deploys, perform the genring analysis on its production.
  • The genre coherence pressure. The grammar typically operates within a genre coherence regime; trace what happens to subjects who deviate from the genre figures the grammar deploys.
  • The platformal amplification. If the discourse circulates on platforms, the platformal feedback architecture shapes which moves get amplified and which dampened.
  • The historical-laundering trace. Trace the laundered vocabulary back to its prior form. The trace is part of the discourse’s current rhetorical force.
  • The audience-formation effect. The grammar produces a particular kind of audience-subject. Trace what kind of subject the grammar’s audience is being formatted into. See Genring and Ontogenring for the structural register.
