Rubber-Hose Realism in Context

2026-04-30 How rubber-hose realism sits among the existing terms of cybernetic postliberalism — what it adds that genring, californication, and socially ontogenic media do not already cover, and where the analytical seams are.

¶What this text is for

This text situates rubber-hose realism inside the existing analytical vocabulary of cybernetic postliberalism. The term is recent in the domain and overlaps in suggestive ways with several older terms — californication, genring, socially ontogenic media, feeling rules, genre calibration. The overlaps are not problems; they are seams. This text walks the seams. It is for readers who have read the rubber-hose-realism term and want to know where the term adds something that the existing vocabulary did not already cover, and where it is doing similar work to existing terms from a different angle.

¶What rubber-hose realism is, in compressed form

Rubber-hose realism is a moral-physics regime: a coherent set of implicit rules an artifact establishes about how harm, consequence, persistence, and weight operate in the world it depicts. Its constituent mechanisms are consequence-free violence, strategic infantilization, affective looping, moral displacement, and soft surrealism. Its origin is the rubber-hose animation style of the 1920s and 1930s; its current scope is a much wider regime that operates across the state-aesthetic continuum — commercial entertainment, brand soft power, recruitment media, overt psychological operations, and platformal creator content.

The regime’s signature is the reset: the world begins each scene approximately fresh, harm does not propagate forward, and the moral question has been routed off the structure that produced the harm onto the individual who navigates it.

¶What rubber-hose realism is not

Rubber-hose realism is not a synonym for any of the existing terms. It is sometimes mistaken for them. The clarifications:

Not the same as californication. Californication is a cybernetic operation: structural contradiction is formatted as personally manageable affect, and the formatting is itself the governance. Rubber-hose realism is a moral-physics regime that runs underneath: a world of resettable harm makes californication’s formatting feel natural, because in such a world the structural cannot exert force on the affective. The two are not the same; one is the operation, the other is the substrate that supports it.

Not the same as genring. Genring reduces a living people to a fixed type. Rubber-hose realism shapes what happens to the type once it has been produced. A type rendered inside rubber-hose realism cannot accumulate grievance; the moral physics has already decided injuries do not persist. Genring produces the figure; rubber-hose realism is the universe the figure operates in.

Not the same as socially ontogenic media. Socially ontogenic media produces inhabitable subject positions. Rubber-hose realism is one of the moral-physics regimes those positions are built inside. The regime tells you what kind of universe the subject position is supposed to operate in.

Not the same as feeling rules. Feeling rules prescribe what audiences should feel in response to events. Rubber-hose realism determines which events are legible-as-events at all. Feeling rules require something to feel about; rubber-hose realism shapes what counts as an occasion for feeling.

These distinctions matter because they tell the analyst where to look. A californication analysis tracks how a structural problem becomes an affective one. A genring analysis tracks how a people becomes a type. A rubber-hose realism analysis tracks the moral physics of the world inside which the californication is performed and the type is inhabited. The three operate at different layers.

¶What rubber-hose realism adds

Three things the existing vocabulary did not cover well, and that motivated naming the regime separately.

A unit of analysis below genre. The existing vocabulary works at the level of genre, register, and discourse. Rubber-hose realism works at the level of moral physics, beneath genre. A war film, a wellness app, a recruitment video, and a prestige drama can occupy completely different genres while all operating inside rubber-hose realism. Without the term, an analyst noticing the family resemblance had no way to name what the resemblance was. With the term, the resemblance is locatable: they share a moral-physics regime even when their genres differ.

A frame for tracking migrations across the state-aesthetic continuum. When a Disney convention shows up in a recruitment video, or a meme-form structure shows up in a Pentagon press release, or a wellness-app affect cue shows up in a brand-justice campaign, the existing terms can describe each artifact in isolation but cannot well describe the transfer. Rubber-hose realism, as a moral-physics regime that travels between institutional positions on the state-aesthetic continuum, names what is moving. The transfer is not of a particular technique; it is of the underlying moral physics that hosts many techniques.

A way of holding the cartoon and the war together. The empirical observation that motivated the rubber-hose realism paper is that the same moral physics is operating in a 1928 Mickey Mouse cartoon and a 2024 4th PSYOP recruitment video. This observation cannot be made cleanly using genring, californication, or socially ontogenic media. The cartoon and the PSYOP video do not share a genre, do not share a recruitment subject, do not share a register. What they share is a moral physics. The term lets the observation be stated.

¶Where the seams are

There are several places where rubber-hose realism overlaps with neighboring terms in ways that are productively complicated rather than fully resolved.

Rubber-hose realism and californication. Both names are doing some of the same work, viewed from different angles. A world of resettable harm (rubber-hose) is the same world in which structural problems become private feelings (californication). The conceptual question is whether one is prior to the other. The current working answer: rubber-hose realism is the moral-physics regime that audiences are trained in over many years, and californication is the cybernetic operation that becomes naturalized inside that training. The seam is real, and analysts may want to use one term or the other depending on whether they are tracking the audience-training (rubber-hose) or the operation-on-the-subject (californication).

Rubber-hose realism and genre calibration. Rubber-hose realism is, in part, a particular pattern of genre calibrations across many artifacts. The seam: at what point do enough convergent calibrations constitute a moral-physics regime rather than a collection of genre conventions? The current working answer: when the calibrations converge across genres and across positions on the state-aesthetic continuum, a moral-physics regime is operating; when they remain confined to a single genre, they are calibrations within that genre. This is a judgment call, and the term should not be deployed as if the line were sharper than it is.

Rubber-hose realism and harm governance. Both names operate at a register where structural harm is acknowledged but defanged. Harm governance is the institutional move (collapsing care into risk management). Rubber-hose realism is the cultural training that prepares a population to accept the institutional move. The seam: when an artifact stages harm-governance dynamics inside its diegesis (a recruitment video that openly discusses the moral weight of its institution), it is both performing rubber-hose realism (the moral physics in which the discussion is held) and modeling harm governance (the institutional posture being recommended). Analysts can usefully read for both.

Rubber-hose realism and socially ontogenic media. Socially ontogenic media is the broader category — media that produces inhabitable subject positions. Rubber-hose realism is one of the moral-physics regimes those positions are produced inside. A full analysis of an artifact will often want to track both: what subject position is being produced (socially ontogenic), and what moral-physics regime is the position embedded in (rubber-hose, or some other regime).

The seams indicate that the terms are doing related work at different levels and that good analysis often uses several together rather than trying to force a single term to carry the whole reading.

¶What rubber-hose realism is best at

The term is most useful for:

Reading across institutional positions. When an artifact’s institutional cover (recruiter, brand, entertainment) does not predict what its formal vocabulary will be — because the same vocabulary is in use across covers — rubber-hose realism gives a name for what the vocabulary is doing.

Tracking what was learned from earlier artifacts. When an analyst wants to ask not just what does this artifact do but what did the artifacts that taught its audience how to read it do, rubber-hose realism gives a frame for the long-running training the audience has received.

Reading retroactively. Older artifacts (mid-century cartoons, postwar action cinema, brand mascots) can be re-read for the moral-physics regime they were teaching, with the present-day uses of the regime in mind. The retroactive reading is one of the term’s principal payoffs.

Reading between genres. When an analysis needs to hold together artifacts from several genres that share something an audience-formation analysis should track, rubber-hose realism is the term for what they share.

The term is less useful for:

Single-artifact close reading at the level of dialogue or scene. Rubber-hose realism is a substrate-level term; close-reading work at the level of language is usually better served by other tools.

Analysis of artifacts outside the state-aesthetic continuum. The term is calibrated to a particular range of artifacts. Applied to artifacts outside that range — academic prose, religious texts, much of pre-twentieth-century literature — the term can be stretched, but the stretching is often a sign that another framework would be more appropriate.

Distinguishing very fine variations within the regime. Rubber-hose realism is a regime. Subdistinctions inside it (the difference between the moral physics of a Pixar film and the moral physics of a Marvel film) require finer-grained analysis than the term itself supplies.
