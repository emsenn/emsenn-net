Tutorial: Your First Rubber-Hose Reading

2026-04-30 A hands-on first analysis. We'll watch Steamboat Willie (1928, 7 minutes, public domain) together and walk through reading it for rubber-hose realism end-to-end. By the end, you'll have done one complete moral-physics reading.

This is your first reading. By the end of this tutorial you will have done one complete moral-physics reading of one specific cartoon. You will not have read the whole regime; you will have read one short artifact end-to-end, which is what gives you the analytical muscle the rest of the work requires.

The artifact is Steamboat Willie (1928, directed by Walt Disney and Ub Iwerks, 7 minutes 23 seconds). It is in the U.S. public domain as of 2024 and freely available on YouTube and the Internet Archive. Pull it up before you start. You will watch it three times. Each watch has a different job.

¶Before you watch

Read the term moral physics. The rest of this tutorial assumes you know what its five axes are: persistence of harm, locus of causation, reversibility of catastrophe, weight of bodies, memory of the world. If you do not know these, the watches will not deliver what they are supposed to.

Read the term consequence-free violence. The first watch is going to track this directly.

Pull out a piece of paper or open a document. You are going to take notes. Plan to take more than you expect.

The video is short. Three watches will take you about 30–40 minutes total, plus another 30 minutes to write up the reading. Set aside an uninterrupted hour.

¶First watch: just watch it

Play Steamboat Willie once through, without notes. Do not pause. Watch it the way an audience in 1928 watched it.

Notice what you feel. Most contemporary viewers of Steamboat Willie feel some combination of:

  • Cheerful, mildly bemused affection for an obviously very old cartoon.
  • Mild discomfort at moments where the cartoon’s casual cruelty is more visible than they expected.
  • A flat sense that “nothing really happens” — there is no plot to speak of.

Note what you felt. Especially note where you felt the mild discomfort, if you felt it. Those moments are the seams in the moral physics; the analysis below is going to walk you back to them.

Welcome back. Put away the affective notes. Now watch it the way the analysis watches it.

¶Second watch: catalogue the harm

Play it again. This time, you have a job: list every moment of harm.

Pause when something happens. Each of these counts as a moment of harm — full stop, even if it is “just a cartoon”:

  • A character is hit, stretched, crushed, flattened, struck, or thrown.
  • A character is grabbed, restrained, or coerced.
  • An animal is used as an instrument, tool, or surface (the goat, the cow, the piglets, the cat).
  • A character is verbally cued to fear, pain, or distress (Minnie’s scream, the parrot’s cries).

Number them. Write down what happens at each one. Be specific. Not “Mickey hits something” but “Mickey grabs the cat by the tail and uses the cat’s tail as a rope to swing it across the deck while it howls.”

You should produce a list of somewhere between fifteen and thirty numbered harm-moments. If you are getting fewer than ten, you are missing things; the cartoon is denser with harm than it feels. Watch again until you have at least fifteen.

Take a break. Get some water.

¶What you have just demonstrated

Look at your list. Several things should be visible.

The cartoon is approximately constant low-grade violence. The harm moments are not isolated incidents. They are the cartoon’s texture. Stripped of music, color, and the rubber-hose drawing style, what is happening on screen is a continuous sequence of cruelties to bodies — Mickey’s, Minnie’s, the animals'.

Most of the harm is to non-Mickey bodies. Mickey is rarely on the receiving end. The harm flows outward from him: he hits the cat, plays the cow’s teeth, swings the goat by the horns, pulls the cat’s tail, strikes the piglets. He is, narratively, the perpetrator. The bodies receiving the harm are uniformly subordinate to him in the cartoon’s hierarchy: the captain (who threatens him at the start) gets harm back; everything else absorbs.

Each harm is concluded inside its scene. The cat is howling; cut. The goat is bleating; cut. The next scene begins fresh. The harm does not propagate forward.

You have just identified the moral physics’ first axis: persistence of harm — none. Write that down.

¶Third watch: the four other axes

Play it once more. You are going to track the other four moral-physics axes. Do not start over your harm list; just attend to these as you go.

Locus of causation. Where do events come from in this cartoon? Mostly from Mickey’s whim. He sees the cow’s teeth; he plays them. He sees the goat; he turns it into a music box. He sees Minnie; he pulls her aboard. There is a brief structural element (the captain’s authority) at the beginning, but it is dispatched almost immediately and the rest of the cartoon operates on Mickey’s improvisation. Locus of causation: individual whim. Write that down.

Reversibility of catastrophe. Watch what happens to bodies after harm. The cat that Mickey swings by the tail is fine in the next shot. The goat that Mickey plays as a barrel-organ is fine when next seen. The piglets that Mickey strikes are fine. Bodies do not stay damaged. The cartoon’s catastrophes — and a goat being played as an instrument is, on its face, a catastrophe for the goat — are fully reversed by the cut to the next scene. Reversibility of catastrophe: total. Write that down.

Weight of bodies. Watch the limbs. Mickey’s are jointless rubber tubes. So are Minnie’s. The cat’s tail is a rope. The cow’s neck is whatever Mickey needs it to be. Bodies have no mass that resists; they reshape to fit whatever Mickey is doing with them. The original technical sense of “rubber-hose” is right here on screen. Weight of bodies: light, elastic, infinitely rearrangeable. Write that down.

Memory of the world. Does the cartoon’s world remember? At the end of Steamboat Willie, the captain returns and Mickey is punished — sent to peel potatoes. This looks, briefly, like the cartoon’s world remembering: the captain’s earlier authority comes back. But notice what is not remembered. The cat does not remember being swung by the tail. The goat does not remember being played. Minnie does not remember being yanked aboard. The cartoon’s world remembers what is structurally convenient (the captain’s authority returns to provide the closing punchline) and forgets everything else. Memory of the world: short, selective, and only retains what closes the cartoon’s structure. Write that down.

¶What you have just produced

Look at your five axes:

  • Persistence of harm: none.
  • Locus of causation: individual whim.
  • Reversibility of catastrophe: total.
  • Weight of bodies: light, elastic, infinitely rearrangeable.
  • Memory of the world: short and selective.

This is the moral physics of Steamboat Willie. Take a moment with this. You have just produced a moral-physics reading of a 1928 cartoon, and the reading is precise. You did not have to know anything about Disney’s biography, the studio’s labor history, or the Production Code to produce it. The moral physics is on the screen; the cartoon teaches it explicitly across seven minutes.

¶What the moral physics is teaching

Now sit with the reading for a moment. Imagine an audience — a child, an adult, both — watching this cartoon, and watching it again, and watching dozens like it across the 1920s and 1930s. What is the audience being trained to expect about how worlds work?

A world in which:

  • Harm to subordinate bodies happens, is funny, and leaves no residue.
  • Events arise from individual whim rather than from structures.
  • Catastrophe is reset by the next scene.
  • Bodies do not have the kind of mass that would let them refuse what is being done to them.
  • The world remembers what closes a structure neatly and forgets what was done to subordinate bodies along the way.

This is rubber-hose realism in its origin form. You have just read it directly. You did not have to take the term on faith. You watched the cartoon teach it across seven minutes and you wrote down what it taught.

¶Why this matters for the larger argument

The rubber-hose realism paper makes the claim that this same moral physics is operating, eight decades later, in U.S. military influence content. To evaluate that claim, you need to be able to recognize the moral physics directly. You now can. When you watch a recruitment video and ask whether it operates in this regime, you have the analytical muscle to answer.

The next tutorial, your first PSYOP-aesthetic reading, takes you through one specific recent recruitment video and runs the same kind of analysis on it. The analytical question to bring forward: do the five axes of Steamboat Willie’s moral physics also describe what the recruitment video is doing? You will find that the answer is largely yes, but with revealing differences, and the differences are where the contemporary analysis becomes interesting.

¶What you have completed

You have now done one complete moral-physics reading. You watched the artifact three times, catalogued its moments of harm, and identified the rules its world operates by along five axes. You produced a reading that names those rules concretely.

This is the foundational analytical operation. Every reading in this domain — of a Pixar film, a wellness app, a recruitment video, a brand campaign, a political speech — proceeds by something like this procedure, scaled up. You have done it once. You can do it again on harder material.

¶If you want to extend the exercise

Some natural next steps, in increasing difficulty:

  • Watch a 1930s Mickey Mouse short that is not Steamboat Willie (e.g., The Band Concert, 1935). Run the same procedure. Note the differences in the moral physics — Disney was already moving past pure rubber-hose technique by this point, and the moral physics is changing along with the technique.
  • Watch a Looney Tunes short from the 1940s (e.g., Falling Hare, 1943, with Bugs Bunny vs. a gremlin). Run the procedure. The technique is different; ask whether the moral physics has the same profile.
  • Read the introduction to moral physics explainer, now that you have the analytical operation in your hands. The conceptual frame will land differently than it would have before the watching.
  • Move to the next tutorial: your first PSYOP-aesthetic reading.

¶What this tutorial has not done

This tutorial has not produced a moral judgment of Steamboat Willie, of Walt Disney, of his audiences, or of the readers who enjoy this cartoon. It has produced an analytical reading of what the cartoon teaches at the level of moral physics. The cartoon is not condemned by being read this way; nor is it absolved. It is seen. Seeing what an artifact teaches is the precondition for thinking about what to do with the teaching. That thinking is downstream of this tutorial. The reading is what makes the thinking possible.
