The Threat That Does Not Pass: Structural Crisis and the Physiology of Chronic Activation

2026-03-31 When the source of threat is structural, the nervous system's defensive response initiates but cannot complete, producing chronic activation that becomes invisible through sensory-motor amnesia. Five traditions describe this single pathway from structural crisis to managed bodily activation.

Abstract

When a gazelle escapes a lion, its body completes a discharge sequence — trembling, shaking, deep breathing — that returns the nervous system to baseline (Levine 1997). When a human loses a job in a labor market organized against stable employment, no discharge is possible, because the threat has not passed. This paper traces what happens to the defensive activation that cannot complete: it persists as chronic sympathetic mobilization or dorsal vagal shutdown (Porges 2011), becomes invisible to the subject through sensory-motor amnesia (Hanna 1988), degrades the interoceptive signals that autonomic regulation depends on (Craig 2009; Friston 2010), and manifests at population scale as what Berlant (2007) calls slow death — wearing out through the ordinary operations of living. Five research traditions — somatic psychology, polyvagal theory, computational neuroscience, affect theory, and the sociology of emotion — describe a single pathway: structural crisis enters the body through autonomic activation, persists through failed discharge, becomes invisible through adaptation, and is managed through affective infrastructure (Hochschild 1983; Berlant 2011) that sustains the activation rather than resolving its source.

1. The Discharge Cycle

In the 1970s, Peter Levine began observing something ethologists had documented but clinicians had not explained: prey animals that survive predation do not carry the encounter forward as chronic disturbance. A gazelle chased by a lion, upon reaching safety, enters an involuntary sequence — trembling, shaking, rapid breathing, sometimes brief convulsions — after which it stands, shakes itself, and resumes grazing. The sympathetic nervous system, which mobilized the animal for flight, discharges its activation through the body and returns to baseline (Levine 1997).

Levine’s central observation was that humans routinely interrupt this sequence. Social conditioning (“pull yourself together”), medical intervention (sedation after accidents), immobilization (car crashes, surgical restraint), and cognitive override (“I should be over this”) prevent the discharge phase from completing. The sympathetic activation remains bound in the body — not as a memory, but as a physiological state. The person may not consciously recall the event, but their body continues to produce the defensive response: chronic muscular tension, elevated startle, constricted breathing, autonomic instability.

This is not a theory of memory. It is a theory of physiology. The defensive cycle has a beginning (threat detection), a middle (mobilization), and a necessary end (discharge). When the end is prevented, the middle persists.

2. Three States and a Hierarchy

Stephen Porges’ polyvagal theory (2011) identifies three phylogenetically ordered response states of the autonomic nervous system:

Ventral vagal. The newest system, unique to mammals. Governs social engagement: facial expression, vocalization, listening, the capacity to signal and detect safety. Active when the organism’s nervous system assesses the environment as safe. Supports flexible, adaptive behavior.

Sympathetic. The mobilization system. Governs fight and flight: increased heart rate, muscular tension, redirected blood flow, suppressed digestion. Active when the organism detects threat that can be addressed through action.

Dorsal vagal. The oldest system, shared with reptiles. Governs immobilization: reduced heart rate, dissociation, collapse, feigned death. Active when the organism detects threat that cannot be addressed through action — when neither fight nor flight is viable.

The hierarchy is not a menu. It is a cascade. When ventral vagal engagement fails (the environment is not safe), the system drops to sympathetic. When sympathetic mobilization fails (the threat cannot be outrun or outfought), the system drops to dorsal vagal. Recovery requires climbing back up — from shutdown, through mobilization, to social engagement. Each transition requires specific physiological conditions.

Porges calls the detection mechanism neuroception: the nervous system’s below-conscious assessment of safety or danger. A person cannot decide to feel safe. The nervous system evaluates the environment through sensory channels — vocal prosody, facial expression, ambient sound, postural cues — and sets the autonomic state accordingly (Porges 2011). Thayer and Lane (2000) showed that this regulation operates through measurable neurovisceral integration: vagal tone indexes the flexibility of the system’s response, with higher vagal tone corresponding to greater capacity to shift between states as conditions change.

3. The Threat That Does Not Pass

Levine’s model assumes a temporal structure: threat appears, defensive response activates, threat passes or is survived, discharge completes, baseline returns. Porges’ hierarchy assumes the same structure: the system responds to changing conditions, cascading down under threat and climbing back up when safety is restored.

What happens when the threat does not pass?

Economic precarity is not an event. It is a condition. A worker in contingent employment faces threat — to shelter, to food, to continuity — not as an episode but as the structure of daily life. The sympathetic system mobilizes. There is no predator to outrun, no fight to win, no moment after which the body can discharge. The next morning, the same threat is there.

Discrimination is not an event. A Black patient whose pain is systematically undertreated in clinical settings (Hoffman et al. 2016) faces a medical system organized against accurate diagnosis of their suffering. The nervous system detects danger — correctly — every time they enter a clinic. No discharge is available because the structure generating the threat has not changed.

Institutional abandonment is not an event. A community whose water is contaminated, whose infrastructure is defunded, whose governance is organized around extraction rather than maintenance, lives inside a chronic threat that no individual defensive action can resolve.

In each case, the nervous system does exactly what it is designed to do: detect threat and mobilize. The mobilization cannot complete because the threat is not an episode within a life. It is the condition of the life. The defensive cycle initiates, again, each day. The discharge that would return the system to baseline cannot occur because the conditions that triggered the activation have not changed.

The result, in Porges’ terms: the nervous system lodges in sympathetic mobilization or dorsal vagal shutdown. It cannot return to ventral vagal because ventral vagal requires the detection of safety, and the environment is not safe. The person is not traumatized by an event. They are held in a state of chronic defensive activation by a structure.

4. How Chronic Activation Becomes Invisible

Wilhelm Reich (1945) described character armor: chronic muscular tension patterns that physically embody psychological defenses. A person who learned to suppress anger in a household where anger was punished holds tension in the jaw and shoulders. A person who learned to defend against vulnerability holds the chest rigid and the breath shallow. These patterns develop as responses to environments where certain expressions are dangerous. Over time, the muscular contraction becomes tonic — constant, automatic, and eventually unfelt.

Thomas Hanna (1988) described the mechanism by which this happens: sensory-motor amnesia. When a muscular contraction persists long enough, the sensory cortex stops registering it. The contraction does not relax. The brain stops monitoring it. Hanna identified three reflexive patterns that accumulate through a life:

  • The green-light reflex: chronic posterior extension — back extensors, hip flexors engaged, the body bracing for action. Produced by sustained arousal demands.
  • The red-light reflex: chronic anterior flexion — abdominals, chest, and neck flexors engaged, the body curling inward. Produced by sustained threat or startle.
  • The trauma reflex: chronic lateral contraction — the body guarding an injury or compensating for asymmetric strain.

A person who has accumulated these reflexes does not feel tense. They feel normal. The tension has dropped below the threshold of awareness while remaining physiologically active — consuming metabolic resources, restricting range of motion, altering breathing patterns, and maintaining the body in a posture of defense that the person cannot perceive.

This is the critical transition. Chronic activation produced by structural threat does not remain a felt experience of danger. It becomes the body’s baseline. The person adapts — not in the sense of resolving the threat, but in the sense of ceasing to consciously register the defensive response their nervous system is still producing.

5. The Precision Problem

A. D. Craig (2009) established that interoception — the sense of internal physiological state — is the sensory basis of subjective feeling. Signals from small-diameter afferent fibers (C and Aδ fibers) travel from tissues throughout the body through the lamina I spinothalamocortical pathway, converging in the posterior and mid-insular cortex. Vagal afferents carry visceral signals through the nucleus of the solitary tract to the same region. The anterior insula integrates these inputs into what Craig calls the “sentient self”: the felt sense of how the body is doing right now.

Karl Friston’s free-energy principle (2010) formalizes what this integration accomplishes: the nervous system minimizes the divergence between predicted and actual physiological states. Prediction errors — discrepancies between what the system expects and what interoceptive signals report — drive corrective autonomic responses. When the model improves (prediction errors decrease), the system registers what Schultz (1998) identified as a dopaminergic reward signal. Positive affect corresponds to increasing predictive accuracy; negative affect corresponds to decreasing accuracy. Affect, in this framework, is the nervous system’s running index of how well its model matches the world.

Chronic activation degrades this process. When the body is persistently mobilized and the subject has ceased to register the mobilization (sensory-motor amnesia), the interoceptive signals are still present but the cortical model has adapted to treat defensive activation as the baseline. The system develops what the clinical literature describes as high interoceptive sensibility — hypervigilance to bodily signals — coupled with low interoceptive accuracy — inability to correctly interpret those signals.

The consequence is a precision problem. Autonomic regulation depends on accurate error signals. When the baseline includes chronic defensive activation that the subject cannot feel, every subsequent perturbation is interpreted against a distorted reference. A stressful conversation registers with the same autonomic intensity as a housing crisis, because the nervous system is already operating near the edge of its tolerance and cannot identify why. The system reacts to everything as if it were the structural threat, because the structural threat has become the substrate on which all other experience is processed.

This is why cognitive intervention alone cannot resolve it. The dysregulation operates at the level of autonomic feedback, interoceptive signal precision, and embodied defensive patterns — all below the threshold that conscious reasoning can reach.

6. From Body to Population

The pathway described so far — structural threat, defensive activation, failed discharge, chronic mobilization, sensory-motor amnesia, degraded interoceptive precision — operates in individual nervous systems. But the threats are structural, which means they affect populations.

The Adverse Childhood Experiences study (Felitti et al. 1998) demonstrated dose-response relationships between childhood adversity and adult disease: cardiovascular disease, diabetes, autoimmune disorders, chronic pain, depression, substance dependence. The mechanism is not narrowly psychological. Repeated developmental trauma alters the stress-response architecture of the developing nervous system itself — hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis calibration, vagal tone set points, inflammatory baselines. A body that develops under chronic threat is organized differently from a body that develops in safety. The differences are measurable across a lifespan.

Bruce McEwen (1998) named the cumulative cost: allostatic load. Allostasis — the body’s ongoing adjustment to sustained demands — is not free. Each adjustment (elevated cortisol, suppressed immune function, altered pain thresholds) trades long-term resilience for short-term stability. The load accumulates. It is not a metaphor. It is measurable in cortisol curves, inflammatory markers, and cardiovascular risk.

The biopsychosocial model of pain (Engel 1977; Woolf 2011) provides the clearest clinical demonstration. Identical lumbar disc herniations produce different pain experiences depending on psychological state and social context. Job loss increases chronic back pain without any change in tissue. Economic precarity produces allostatic load that alters pain processing at every level — peripheral sensitization, central sensitization (Woolf 2011), descending facilitation. Structural discrimination — being systematically disbelieved about one’s pain (Hoffman et al. 2016) — increases threat perception and amplifies cortical pain processing directly.

These are not confounding variables. They are mechanisms. Social conditions enter the body through the same autonomic and interoceptive pathways that process any threat, and they alter the biological systems that all subsequent experience depends on.

Lauren Berlant (2007) named the population-level pattern: slow death. Not crisis as rupture — a catastrophic moment that divides before from after — but crisis as the ordinary texture of living. Populations wearing out through the continuous operation of conditions that erode health, agency, and futurity. Obesity, environmental toxicity, debt, chronic illness, pain: not events that befall individuals but conditions that grind down populations across the span of ordinary time.

Slow death is what chronic undischarged activation looks like at population scale. The physiological mechanism is the same — threat that does not pass, defensive mobilization that cannot complete, adaptation that renders the mobilization invisible while it continues to consume the body. The scale changes. The process does not.

7. Managed, Not Resolved

If chronic activation were left unaddressed, it would eventually produce breakdown visible enough to threaten the structures generating it. It is not left unaddressed. It is managed.

Arlie Hochschild (1983) described feeling rules: social prescriptions dictating which emotions count as appropriate responses to given situations. A wedding prescribes joy. A job interview prescribes confident enthusiasm. A layoff prescribes resilient optimism. These are not suggestions. They carry normative force: violating them produces guilt, confusion, or social sanction.

Feeling rules manage chronic activation by prescribing what the activation should feel like. The person whose nervous system is mobilized by economic precarity is not told the precarity is fine. They are told what to feel about it: not rage (which would be disruptive) but determination (which is productive), not grief (which would be incapacitating) but growth (which is aspirational). The activation remains. Its affective character is reformatted.

Hochschild called the result the managed heart: the condition in which the boundary between authentic feeling and institutional demand becomes impossible to locate. When the management succeeds, the person can no longer distinguish between what they feel and what they are required to feel. The chronic activation continues — the body is still mobilized, the nervous system still at its tolerance edge — but the conscious experience has been formatted to match the feeling rules of the situation.

Berlant (2011) extended this into infrastructure. Affective infrastructure names the emotional and procedural frameworks through which people absorb ongoing instability: genres of public feeling, modes of narrative resolution, interpretive labor, institutional practices of acknowledgment-without-change. Affective infrastructure does not resolve the structural conditions producing the activation. It metabolizes the activation into continuity. The rush of interpretation after a crisis — commentary, analysis, takes — does not explain the crisis. It provides the substrate through which the system absorbs the disturbance and continues operating.

The person who burns out in the effort to understand and respond is not failing the system. They are doing what the system requires: converting disturbance into circulation, chronic activation into interpretive labor, unresolvable threat into manageable affect.

8. The Pathway

Five research traditions describe a single process:

Tradition Contribution
Somatic psychology (Levine 1997) Defensive activation that cannot discharge persists as chronic physiological state
Autonomic neuroscience (Porges 2011) The nervous system lodges in sympathetic or dorsal vagal states when safety cannot be established
Somatic education (Hanna 1988; Reich 1945) Chronic activation drops below conscious awareness through sensory-motor amnesia
Computational neuroscience (Friston 2010; Craig 2009) Degraded interoceptive precision prevents discrimination between structural threat and personal mood
Critical theory and sociology of emotion (Berlant 2007, 2011; Hochschild 1983) Affective infrastructure manages the activation without resolving its source

Read separately, each tradition addresses its own object: trauma, autonomic regulation, embodied awareness, neural prediction, social affect. Read together, they trace a continuous process: structural crisis enters the body through autonomic activation, persists through failed discharge, becomes invisible through sensory-motor adaptation, degrades the precision of the signals the body uses to regulate itself, and is managed through affective infrastructure that formats the activation into livable experience while leaving its source in place.

The neuroscience is not a metaphor for the politics. The politics is not a metaphor for the neuroscience. They are the same process described at different scales. The body does not distinguish between a predator and a labor market. What it distinguishes is whether the threat has passed. Structural threats, by definition, have not. The clinical question — how do we treat this person’s chronic pain, anxiety, autonomic dysregulation — and the structural question — how do we change the conditions producing the threat — are the same question, asked at different levels of the same pathway.
