California as Laboratory

2026-04-27 California is the historical laboratory in which californication iterates: Misiones, Gold Rush, Hollywood, Esalen, Silicon Valley, the wellness industry.

Californication is named after California. The choice is not metaphorical. California — the actual coastal territory and the imaginative screen of the same name — has been the historical laboratory in which the apparatus has iterated, and the apparatus has been continuously exported from that laboratory to the rest of the system. To take the name seriously is to read California’s specific history not as a region with idiosyncratic features but as the place where the operation has been most thoroughly worked out and most thoroughly exported.

What follows is not a complete California history; many such histories exist. The aim is to show how the major phases of California’s development — Mission, Gold Rush, agriculture, Hollywood, counterculture, technology, wellness — fit into a single continuous apparatus rather than being separate phenomena that happened to occupy the same coastline. Californian projection is the formal name for this iterating-and-exporting structure; settlerism is the structural condition that makes the substrate ignorable enough for rapid iteration to be possible.

¶What “projection” means

The word projection operates in two senses simultaneously. In the cartographic sense, a projection is a mapping of one surface onto another with systematic distortion. In the psychoanalytic sense, projection is the attribution of one’s own contents to an external screen so that they can be encountered as if they were external. Californian projection is both. The actual coastal territory has been continuously mapped onto by the imaginative screen, and the screen has been used by the broader apparatus to externalize contents — anxieties, desires, contradictions — that could not be encountered as internal.

The doubleness matters. The territory is not California. California is what the apparatus has made of it. The Indigenous peoples who lived along that coast for at least ten thousand years did not live in California; they lived in their own territories, which the projection has overwritten. The projection’s first move, in every phase, is the substitution of its image for whatever was there. Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz (2014) and the Indigenous-historical literature she stands within are the foundational corrective: the territory’s own peoples — Ohlone, Tongva, Chumash, Miwok, Pomo, Yurok, and many others — were not erased; they were made invisible by the projection’s success in occupying the public imagination.

Joan Didion’s literary work, especially Slouching Towards Bethlehem (1968) and The White Album (1979), is the most sustained literary diagnosis of the projection from inside. Carey McWilliams’s Southern California: An Island on the Land (1946) made the systematic argument that Southern California operates as a distinct civilizational laboratory rather than as a region of the United States. Kevin Starr’s multi-volume California Dream series excavates the imaginative across its successive nineteenth- and twentieth-century forms. Mike Davis’s City of Quartz (1990) is the canonical critical history of Los Angeles as the metropolitan engine of American futurity. Greg Grandin’s The End of the Myth (2019) places California at the terminal coast of the American frontier-projection.

¶The phases of the laboratory

California’s development reads as a sequence of phases, each performing its own version of the apparatus’s work and exporting its techniques to the rest of the system.

¶The Misiones (1769-1834)

The first phase. The Spanish Franciscans established a string of twenty-one missions along the California coast, each operating as a labor-and-belief regime: convert the Indigenous population, format their daily practice (work schedule, devotional cycle, dietary regime, language, dress), and produce a population legible to the colonial administration. The mission is the first Californian instance of a pastoral apparatus in the operative sense — an institution whose work is the formatting of inner life through structured practice, with the institutional capacity to hold the practice over time and at scale.

The mission’s specific California feature was its catastrophe. The combination of forced labor, disease, and the coerced compression of distinct peoples into a single regime killed perhaps two-thirds of the coastal Indigenous population over the mission period. The pastoral apparatus’s structural ability to hold and format inner life was, in this case, performed atop demographic collapse. The mission is californication’s prior institutional form on this coast specifically; not historical curiosity but the apparatus’s first iteration in this place. The seal-of-confession infrastructure operated; what is distinctive is what it did under California conditions. The broader recurrence of the pastoral form across institutional configurations is the subject of The Ecclesial Recurrence.

¶The Gold Rush (1848-1855)

The second phase. With the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848) and the discovery of gold at Sutter’s Mill, California was abruptly converted from a marginal Mexican province into the destination of the largest mass migration in American history. Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz, Andrew Isenberg (2005), and others trace what the Gold Rush actually did: a systematic genocide of California Indigenous peoples (the population of Indigenous Californians dropped from approximately 150,000 in 1845 to perhaps 30,000 by 1870), an environmental reorganization of the territory through hydraulic mining and water diversion, and the production of a new kind of subject — the prospector, the figure who has staked everything on the chance of strike, who is structurally available to extreme labor under conditions of equally extreme uncertainty.

The Gold Rush is the apparatus’s second phase: the formatting of extraction-as-opportunity. The contradiction metabolized was the contradiction between the universalist promise of opportunity (anyone can strike it rich) and the structural reality (most prospectors lost everything; the gold was disproportionately captured by capital that arrived after the prospectors did, in the form of merchants, transport, and bank infrastructure). The metabolism was performed through the prospector’s affective regime: the willingness to absorb loss as personal misfortune rather than as structural inevitability. This is californication in an early industrial form, working on the universalism-without-individualism contradiction laid out in Liberalism’s Constitutive Contradictions.

¶Agribusiness and the migrant labor regime (1870s-1940s)

The third phase. With the railroad’s arrival and the conversion of the Central Valley to industrial-scale agriculture, California developed what McWilliams (in Factories in the Field, 1939) analyzed as the largest-scale capitalist agricultural regime in the world. The labor was performed by successive waves of migrant populations — Chinese, Japanese, Filipino, Mexican, Dust Bowl refugees, contemporary undocumented workers — each subject to specific subject-formatting that legitimated their position within the labor regime. The genres of the migrant worker (the diligent industrious laborer, the foreign threat, the helpful imported hand) were continuously revised; the structural relation persisted.

This phase is consequential because it shows the apparatus’s capacity to maintain its operation across genre-substitutions. The genre is what changes; the labor regime is what persists. The genring operations and the genre coherence that enforces them are continuous across the substitutions; the structural pressure does not depend on any particular genre. This is a feature the contemporary platformal phase reproduces at digital scale.

¶Hollywood (1910s-1960s)

The fourth phase. The film industry, consolidated in Los Angeles in the early twentieth century, became the apparatus’s exporter of inhabitable fantasy at planetary scale. Hollywood’s work is the production of genres in the genring/ontogenring sense: structured narrative-affective forms that subjects can inhabit. The Hollywood film is engineered to format the viewer’s affect: this is how to feel about love, this is how to feel about loss, this is how to feel about war, this is what the resolution looks like, this is what counts as a happy ending.

What Hollywood specifically exports is the infrastructure of affective formatting. The genre figures (the cowboy, the gangster, the noir detective, the romantic ingenue) and the genre-arcs (the redemption story, the romance, the heist, the war film) become available, through the film’s worldwide distribution, to subjects who never set foot in California. The formatting work that the missions performed on a population in physical proximity, Hollywood performs on a population at planetary scale through media. The continuity is not metaphorical; it is the same operation specified in Genring and Ontogenring, now operating through cinema rather than mission practice.

¶The counterculture and Esalen (1960s-1970s)

The fifth phase. The 1960s counterculture, centered in the Bay Area and at Big Sur, produced a critical contribution to the apparatus: the transmutation of political contradiction into spiritual journey. The contradictions the political-cultural movements of the period addressed (Vietnam, racial-political violence, gender-political emergence, ecological-political emergence) were partly managed by political action; partly, and structurally, they were redirected into personal-spiritual development.

Esalen Institute, founded in 1962, is the canonical site. The Human Potential Movement, which Esalen helped consolidate, took the political claim that something is wrong with how subjects are organized and reformatted it as the developmental claim that something is wrong with how each subject relates to themselves. The work of transformation was relocated from political contestation to inner work: encounter groups, gestalt therapy, meditation retreats, psychedelic experimentation. The pattern is californication in concentrated form: structural contradiction routed into personally manageable affective work. The genre this produces — the detached observer who treats their own non-reaction as ethical credential — is what zen fascism names; its inheritors are the savior-slave subjects who will, by the platformal phase, manage global crises through personal practice.

The counterculture does not admit a simple verdict. The political work the counterculture also performed was real and consequential, and the personal-developmental work it produced was also genuinely useful for many of its participants. The structural reading is: regardless of what was intended, the formation became one of the apparatus’s principal export channels for the operation of routing political-structural contradiction onto personal affective management.

¶Silicon Valley (1970s-present)

The sixth phase. The technology industry, consolidating in the Santa Clara Valley from the 1970s onward, built the platform infrastructure that made the apparatus operative at planetary scale. Silicon Valley is the infrastructural correlate of the apparatus’s other operations, not a separate phenomenon. The platforms produce affect-engagement-attention loops that operate at second-by-second timescales and at planetary scale, providing the technical substrate within which the other phases’ work is now performed continuously rather than episodically.

What Silicon Valley specifically contributes is recursive readability: where Hollywood’s films were produced and released and then operated, the platform reads the user’s response to each piece of content and shapes the next piece accordingly. Platformal Feedback Architecture traces this in cybernetic detail (Wiener’s feedback, Beer’s Viable System Model, Ashby’s requisite variety); platformal liberalism names the broader operating mode in which platforms have become the primary medium of subject-formation. The recursive-loop structure is what makes contemporary californication structurally distinct from its prior forms. The apparatus has not just new techniques; it has acquired a continuous-loop recursive structure that earlier institutional forms did not have.

¶The wellness industry (1990s-present)

The seventh phase. The contemporary wellness industry — yoga, meditation, mindfulness apps, supplement protocols, biohacking, lifecoaching, manifestation practices, the broad self-optimization apparatus — is the apparatus’s most direct surface in subjects’ actual lives. It packages self-regulation as lifestyle, offering subjects a continuous menu of practices through which they can metabolize their conditions as personal-developmental work.

The reading is structural. The practices the industry sells are sometimes useful, often harmless, occasionally damaging; the question of which is which is not what cybernetic postliberalism takes up. The structural claim is that the industry is one of the apparatus’s principal genre-providers: it supplies the ontogenric genres in which subjects format their conditions, and the formatting it supplies is consistently in the direction of personal-affective management rather than structural contestation. Calm, Headspace, the wellness Instagram, the neuroscientific self-help podcast, the mindful productivity app — these are the platforms within which subjects perform the metabolism the apparatus requires. Socially ontogenic media names the artifacts that distribute the survivable templates of selfhood; Han’s Leistungssubjekt names the subject the templates produce.

¶How the phases relate

Reading the phases as one apparatus rather than seven turns on three structural features that connect them.

Continuous export. Each phase has exported its formatting techniques to the rest of the system. The mission exported pastoral practice. The Gold Rush exported the prospector-genre. Hollywood exported affective genres. Esalen exported personal-developmental work as the form of political response. Silicon Valley exported the platform. The wellness industry exports the self-management practice. The phases do not stay in California; they become available to subjects globally, and through the platform they are now distributed continuously and recursively.

Recombination back to the laboratory. Each phase is recombined back into the next phase’s work. Hollywood drew on the prospector-genre and the Gold Rush mythology. Esalen drew on Hollywood’s affective vocabulary. Silicon Valley drew on Esalen’s developmental framing (the “personal computer,” the user-centric design philosophy, the wellness-aesthetic of the contemporary tech firm). The wellness industry runs on Silicon Valley’s platforms. The laboratory is iterative; each phase is partly a recombination of what the prior phase produced.

Continuous demographic-environmental substrate. The phases run on a continuous substrate: the displacement and ongoing dispossession of Indigenous peoples; the labor of successive migrant populations; the environmental reorganization of the territory (water diversion, agricultural conversion, suburban sprawl). The substrate does not change between phases; the apparatus draws on it across all of them. Reading the apparatus’s exports without reading its substrate produces a partial picture that Settlerism and the Cybernetic Subject corrects in its specific structural register.

¶Why the laboratory has worked there

California was not uniquely destined to play this role. Many laboratories could have, in principle, performed similar functions. The historical-material conditions provided a particular combination: a peripheral region that could be reorganized rapidly, a continuous influx of labor and capital, a settler-colonial substrate that produced (and then erased the production of) the conditions for further iteration, and — particularly in the twentieth century — proximity to the federal-military funding networks that funded the technology base.

California is a place where things have been built rapidly on a substrate that the rest of the apparatus could afford to ignore. The rapidity has allowed the apparatus to test its operations in conditions that would be politically difficult elsewhere; the ignorability of the substrate has allowed the apparatus to externalize the costs of testing. Crisis settlerism names the structural condition that intensifies the apparatus’s reliance on continued frontier-projection when its prior settlements destabilize.

¶What follows

Platformal Feedback Architecture takes up the platform infrastructure that Silicon Valley built and that the apparatus now runs on. With californication located historically (here) and with the technical-feedback architecture specified there, the account of how the contemporary apparatus operates at planetary scale comes into focus. The specific subject-formations the apparatus produces — the savior-slave subject, the neurotic platformal intellectual, zen fascism, harm governance — operate within that infrastructure; The Ecclesial Recurrence reads californication as recurrent with the medieval Christian pastoral apparatus, of which the Misiones are the California-specific instance; The Operational Closure of Late-Liberal Governance states the structural claim about why no coherent subject-position produces an outside under contemporary conditions.
