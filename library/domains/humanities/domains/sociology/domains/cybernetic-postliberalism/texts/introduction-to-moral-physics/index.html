Introduction to Moral Physics

2026-04-30 An orientation to moral physics as an analytical layer beneath genre — the implicit rules an artifact establishes for how harm, consequence, and persistence work — and why media studies routinely misses it.

¶What this text is for

This is an orientation to moral physics as an analytical layer that media analysis tends to skip. It explains what moral physics is, what it is not, why genre and representation are not adequate substitutes for it, and how to start reading for it once you have the term. It does not give a procedure (the procedure is in how to identify moral displacement in a media artifact) or a hands-on first analysis (the tutorial is your first rubber-hose reading). It gives the conceptual map that makes those procedures legible.

If you have already read the moral physics term and want practical work, skip to the how-to. If the term felt clear but the placement of moral physics inside the wider analytical landscape felt hazy — what work it is doing that genre, representation, and ideology critique are not already doing — this is the text that situates it.

¶The layer media analysis tends to skip

Most media analysis operates on three layers, and skips a fourth.

Representation. What is shown, who is shown, who is left out, how the shown are characterized. The layer where most cultural-studies work has historically lived. A representation analysis of a war film identifies whose deaths are visible, whose are not, which bodies are dignified and which are background, what the film says about its subjects.

Genre. What kind of artifact this is, what conventions it observes, what audience expectations it tunes (genre calibration) and what populations it reduces to types (genring). A genre analysis of the same war film identifies its arc structure, its cues, its placement in a tradition of war films, and the figures it produces or reproduces.

Ideology. What political-economic interests the artifact serves, what naturalizations it performs, what social order it reproduces. Ideology critique of the war film identifies whose interests its narrative quietly endorses and whose it makes harder to articulate.

These three layers interlock and have been worked over thoroughly by media and cultural studies. The fourth layer is the one underneath all three: what kind of universe the artifact is constructing, what the rules of cause, consequence, persistence, and weight are inside that universe. This is moral physics. It is rarely named directly. It is usually treated as an artifact’s background — the world the representation occurs in, the substrate the genre operates on, the grain the ideology rides through. Treating it as background is exactly how it does its work.

Moral physics is the layer where an artifact teaches its audience how worlds behave. The teaching is not propositional — the artifact never says “in this universe, harm does not propagate” — but it is constant. Every scene in which a character takes damage and is fine in the next scene is teaching the rule. After enough scenes, the audience has been trained in a moral physics. The training transfers. Audiences carry the moral physics they have been trained in into their reading of further media, and into their reading of the world the media is about.

¶Why this layer keeps being missed

Moral physics is rarely named because the conditions for noticing it are unusual. A few of the structural reasons:

It is the substrate, not the surface. Analyses tend to focus on what is foregrounded: representation, genre conventions, declared themes. The substrate by definition is what is not foregrounded. Spotting it requires reading across an artifact’s surface choices to the rules they imply.

It is taught by repetition. A single scene rarely establishes a moral physics. The physics is established by the cumulative pattern across an artifact (or a genre, or a body of work, or a corpus an audience has consumed across years). Single-artifact analysis tends to look at a single artifact. Cumulative-pattern analysis is rarer.

It is invisible when shared. When the analyst and the artifact share the same moral physics, the analyst does not see the physics — they see the artifact’s surface, against the background of the physics they have already absorbed. Noticing the moral physics requires the analyst to be at some distance from it. This is why analyses of artifacts from radically different traditions sometimes spot moral physics more clearly than analyses of artifacts from the analyst’s own tradition.

It is mistaken for genre. Many of the markers of moral physics — body weight, persistence of injury, locus of causation — line up loosely with genre conventions, and analyses sometimes attribute moral-physics work to genre and stop there. But genre is plural and surface; moral physics is singular and underlying. A war film, a romance, a comedy, and a horror film inside the same moral physics are doing different genre work on a shared substrate.

It is not in the dialogue. Most close-reading techniques foreground language, dialogue, and explicit statement. Moral physics is rarely in the language. It is in the rules of the world the language occurs inside. An artifact can preach justice in its dialogue while operating in a moral physics where injustice is reversible by the next scene; the analysis that stays at dialogue cannot see this contradiction.

These reasons compound. Moral physics ends up as the unmarked frame inside which the marked work happens, and unmarked frames are precisely what is hardest to see.

¶What moral physics covers

The full description is in the moral physics term. Briefly, the layer can be specified along several axes:

  • Persistence of harm. Does damage carry forward, or does the next scene reset?
  • Locus of causation. Are events arising from structures, from individuals, or from no traceable source?
  • Reversibility of catastrophe. Can the worst be undone, or does it leave a permanent mark?
  • Weight of bodies. Do bodies have mass, friction, fragility, or are they elastic and rearrangeable?
  • Memory of the world. Does the world carry forward what has happened, or does it begin each scene anew?

These axes are descriptive. The analytic question is not whether an artifact’s moral physics is good or bad along each axis, but what the combination is, and what the combination makes thinkable or hard to think for the audience trained in it.

¶A worked illustration

Consider two artifacts that occupy the same genre but operate in radically different moral physics.

A standard mid-budget action film: protagonist takes multiple gunshots across the runtime, has visible-but-quickly-healing wounds, drives through three exploded buildings, witnesses bystander deaths, and arrives at the climax in essentially full physical and psychological condition. By the third act, the city is in ruins; by the credits, the city has been resolved. The genre is action. The moral physics has rapid persistence-decay, individual locus of causation (one villain, one hero), high reversibility of catastrophe, light bodies, and short world-memory.

A late Tarkovsky film: a character receives a small physical injury. The injury is in every subsequent scene. Other characters’ faces register the injury. The injury accumulates with other small persistent details — a damaged room, a dead animal, a slow-burning building — until the cumulative weight of accumulated unreversed events constitutes the texture of the film. The genre may be drama or art cinema. The moral physics has high persistence, distributed locus of causation (events arise from a thick mesh of conditions), low reversibility, heavy bodies, and very long world-memory.

The genre framing tells you these are different kinds of film. The moral physics framing tells you they are constructing different kinds of universe — and that audiences trained primarily on the first will read the world they live in differently from audiences trained primarily on the second. The political consequence of that difference is the substantive thing moral-physics analysis is for.

¶Where moral physics fits with the rest of cybernetic postliberalism

Moral physics is the substrate beneath several operations that cybernetic postliberalism studies.

Genring reduces a living people to a fixed type. The reduction is performed inside a moral physics that determines what can happen to the type once produced. A type rendered inside a moral physics of consequence-free violence cannot be the subject of a permanent grievance, because the moral physics has already decided that grievances do not accumulate.

Californication formats structural contradiction as personally manageable affect. The formatting is naturalized by a moral physics that treats structural causation as illegible — a world with diffuse, unattributable causation makes the rerouting of moral attention onto the self feel like the only available kind of moral attention.

Genre calibration tunes audience expectations about how situations unfold. The expectations rest on the moral physics: a calibration only works if the audience has been trained in the underlying physics the calibration assumes.

Harm governance collapses care into risk management. The collapse is supported at the cultural level by a moral physics that treats harm as registerable-but-not-persistent, manageable through institutional process rather than as an event that has changed the world.

Rubber-hose realism is the specific moral-physics regime — elastic bodies, reversible catastrophe, world-without-memory — that has become the dominant substrate of late-liberal entertainment, brand, and state-aesthetic content. It is where moral-physics analysis most directly leads in this domain.

¶Where to go from here

If you want to start reading artifacts for moral physics:

  • The hands-on tutorial your first rubber-hose reading walks through one specific concrete artifact end-to-end.
  • The procedure how to identify moral displacement in a media artifact gives a step-by-step diagnostic for one of the most common moral-physics operations.
  • The longer-form text rubber-hose realism in context situates the dominant late-liberal moral-physics regime against the existing analytical vocabulary of the domain.

If you want to extend the analytic to artifacts the rubber-hose paper does not address — wellness apps, prestige television, journalism, video games — the moral-physics axes (persistence, locus of causation, reversibility, body weight, world-memory) work as well in those registers as they do in animation. The work is the same; the materials change.

¶What this text has not done

This text has not made a moral judgment about any artifact. It has named a layer at which artifacts can be analyzed. The judgments belong to the analyses, not to the analytical layer. A moral-physics analysis is not, by itself, a critique of the artifact it is performed on. It is a precondition for one — the substrate has to be visible before the work the substrate is doing can be addressed.
