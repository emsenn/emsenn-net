Cybernetic Governance: The Load Is the System

2026-03-06
Learning objectives
  • Cybernetic Feedback in Governance
Prerequisites
  • cybernetic-postliberalism-overview

Classical models of governance describe a sovereign who decides, a bureaucracy that implements, and subjects who comply or resist. Cybernetic postliberalism replaces this with distributed feedback loops. No one commands the system. It self-regulates — and the regulation includes you, interpreting what just happened.

This text examines the cybernetic mechanism at the heart of the framework. For the conceptual foundations, see californication (which explains the full circuit) and coherent confusion (which explains “the load is the system”). This text shows how those concepts operate against a specific case.

The tariff cycle

In “Governing by confusion” (2025), the April 2025 tariff announcement is traced through its full operation. The key observation is that different systems responded on different timescales:

  • Procurement adjusted within hours — pausing orders, repricing, rerouting supply chains
  • Financial markets corrected in real time — futures spiking, spreads widening, algorithms rebalancing
  • Treasury auctions shifted over days — softened bid coverage, upward pressure on yields
  • Narrative interpretation arrived last, on a weekly cadence — commentary, analysis, punditry

By the time commentators began explaining what had happened, the system had already completed its behavioral adjustment. The explanations were not causal. They were the final stabilizing behavior — what Niklas Luhmann would recognize as communication systems generating recursive readability rather than conveying truth.

This is what Norbert Wiener’s cybernetics and Stafford Beer’s Viable System Model describe at the organizational level: stability through distributed correction across nested feedback layers, each operating on its own timebase, without central control. The tariff cycle is not a plan executed by a strategist. It is a viable system self-regulating.

Why the interpretive layer matters

The distinctive claim of cybernetic postliberalism is that the interpretive layer — the commentary, the takes, the engagement — is not separate from this process. It is the final feedback layer. Each explanation absorbs interpretive energy. Each take feeds data back to platforms. Each analysis produces the narrative closure that allows the system to proceed as though coherence was maintained.

The more an event resists easy explanation, the more interpretive cycles it produces. The more cycles, the more legibility the system has to route into behavioral analytics. The confusion is not a failure of governance. The confusion is governance operating through the feedback mechanism that confusion creates.

As “Governing by confusion” puts it: “this piece didn’t escape the loop, it executed it.” The act of explaining cybernetic governance is itself an instance of the interpretive labor the system requires. Writing this sentence is performing it. Reading it is performing it too.
