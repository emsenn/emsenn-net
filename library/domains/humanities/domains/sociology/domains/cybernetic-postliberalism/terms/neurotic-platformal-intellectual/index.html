Neurotic platformal intellectual

2026-03-03 A structural subject position produced when platforms industrialize confession, turning every user into a performer of expertise whose intellectual labor is governed by anxiety, feeling rules, and platform logics of visibility.

Neurotic platformal intellectual

Someone posts a detailed analysis of monetary policy on a social media platform. Within hours, people with no economics training are debating the analysis in replies — not asking questions but offering corrections, alternative theories, and confident disagreements. No one does this with surgery. When a surgeon posts about a procedure, non-surgeons do not show up to debate incision technique. But policy analysis on a platform is discourse, and discourse is everyone’s medium. The platform makes no structural distinction between someone who has spent years studying monetary systems and someone who read a thread about inflation that morning. What it registers is engagement.

This asymmetry is the neurotic platformal intellectual in miniature. The platform tells every user they are qualified to engage with any intellectual content, because the platform’s mode of intellectual labor does not distinguish between expertise and the performance of expertise. What matters is that engagement happens — that discourse is produced, circulated, and consumed. The platform’s metrics reward the fact of participation, not its depth.

The neurotic platformal intellectual is the subject position this produces. It is not a type of person but a structural position that anyone participating in platform discourse occupies to some degree. The term names the intersection of three conditions that have been documented independently but whose convergence under platform capitalism produces something distinct.

Why “neurotic”

Vik Loveday, the British sociologist of higher education, described what she called the “neurotic academic”: the condition of academics in neoliberal universities who experience anxiety not as personal failing but as structural requirement. The academic is governed by metrics — citations, h-index, grant income — that determine professional worth but remain permanently insufficient. There is no amount of publication that resolves the anxiety, because the anxiety is what the institution runs on. Self-entrepreneurialism is mandatory. Credibility is performed through the mechanics of citation, where citations function not as claims about the world but as signals of genre membership.

Mari Lehto, the Finnish media scholar, extended this to social media with the “neurotic influencer.” Influencers operate under parallel pressures: maintain authenticity for followers, maintain professionalism for brands, produce content at a rate that demands the confession of lived experience as raw material. Arlie Russell Hochschild’s feeling rules — her concept for the social expectations governing which emotions are appropriate — determine which affects count as authentic. The neurotic influencer does not hide anxiety. Anxiety performed is the content.

The neurotic platformal intellectual inherits both conditions. The anxiety is structural — produced by the requirement to continuously demonstrate coherence under conditions of incoherence — and the anxiety is productive — it generates the confessional content the platform rewards.

Why “platformal”

The term is platformal rather than platformed deliberately. A platformed intellectual is an intellectual who uses a platform — the platform is a tool. A platformal intellectual is an intellectual whose mode of intellectuality is produced by the platform’s structural logics. They think in threads. They evaluate ideas by engagement. They produce analysis in platform-native formats. The platform does not just distribute their work — it formats how they think.

Michel Foucault, the French historian and philosopher of power, described how modern power operates through the incitement to discourse. In The History of Sexuality, he showed that confession is not a liberation of truth but a technique of subjection: power does not prohibit speech but compels it. The subject is incited to speak about themselves, their desires, their beliefs, and this speech binds the speaker to the ideological system they confess within. Social media platforms industrialize this confessional logic. The platform is a confession machine: it incites discourse at scale, processes it as data, and sells the data back as engagement metrics. Every post is a confession. Every like is an absolution. The loop is cybernetic.

Grant Bollmer and Katherine Guinness, writing on digital labor and corporate personhood, describe the result: social media users function as “corporate persons” — simultaneously owner (of their brand), worker (producing content through self-commodification), and product (whose public image circulates as capital). The neurotic platformal intellectual is this corporate person performing intellectual labor. Their life is the raw material. The platform’s industrial process is neurotic confession. The commodity is authentic-seeming expertise.

Why “intellectual”

Antonio Gramsci, writing from prison under Mussolini, distinguished traditional intellectuals — embedded in established institutions — from organic intellectuals — emerging from a class to articulate its interests. Social media platforms produce a mode of organic intellectualism: any user can be incited into performing expertise by speaking from lived experience. But the organic intellectual has been formatted for survivability. What Gramsci described as embedded, insurgent knowledge-production has become a role that is “valuable, fundable, format-compatible” (emsenn, “The theory that survived the war,” 2025). The platform completes this formatting: the platformal organic intellectual articulates not class interest but platform interest. The platform’s logics of visibility and engagement determine what is worth saying.

The neurotic platformal intellectual is what every social media user is to some degree — because every liberal subject is a “naive scientist” (emsenn, “Californication and affect,” 2025), and platformization converts this naive scientism into the platformal mode of intellectual labor. Some users are additionally incited into performing expertise: those with followers, those with credentials, those whose confessions are particularly authentic-seeming. These occupy the position most fully. But the position is general.

Under crisis

The concept becomes sharpest when the neurotic platformal intellectual encounters systemic crisis. Crisis destabilizes — it induces novelty, exploration, learning. The neurotic platformal intellectual encounters ideas outside their established expertise. But the platform’s dual-audience structure — Loes van Driel and Delia Dumitrica showed that followers want authenticity while brands want professionalism — demands continued performance of expertise. The result is a specific paradox:

The neurotic platformal intellectual educates audiences on ideas about which they are naive, from a position of expertise. The audience takes it as authoritative because that is how the platform has formatted the relationship. This is not deception — the intellectual may genuinely believe they understand. The platform’s feeling rules and the audience’s expectations converge so that partial understanding is formatted as complete understanding. What gets circulated is not the learning itself but the affect surrounding it — the feeling of having learned, packaged as expertise.

This is californication operating at the level of intellectual labor. The learning is real. The anxiety is real. The curiosity is real. What is formatted is the claim to have understood — and the formatting is what the platform rewards.
