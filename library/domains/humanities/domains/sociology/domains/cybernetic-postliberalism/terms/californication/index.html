Californication

Californication

Californication is the process by which liberal governance sustains itself under structural contradiction by formatting crisis as personally manageable affect. The term names what happens when a political system cannot resolve its own contradictions but can teach its subjects to experience those contradictions as personal conditions – as mood, trauma, growth opportunity, or lifestyle. The subject who meditates through a housing crisis, journals through a pandemic, or microdoses through ecological collapse is not failing to respond politically. They are doing exactly what the system requires: converting structural incoherence into self-regulatory behavior.

No conspiracy is needed. No ideology department coordinates this. The process is cybernetic: feedback loops between bodies, platforms, economy, and affect produce and sustain it without anyone steering.

The problem: liberalism’s constitutive contradictions

Liberalism promises both universalism and individualism – that all people are equal and that each person is sovereign. These commitments are structurally incompatible. Universalism requires shared standards; individualism requires their refusal. Under stable conditions, this tension is manageable. Under stress – economic crisis, pandemic, ecological collapse, institutional delegitimization – the contradiction becomes visible. The system cannot deliver on both promises simultaneously, and the gap between what it promises and what it produces becomes experientially available to its subjects.

At this point, the system faces a legitimacy crisis. It can no longer produce the resolutions it promises. It shifts from promising resolution to promising regulation – from solving problems to managing them. This shift is where californication begins.

Producing subjects who self-regulate

Michel Foucault showed that modern governance does not operate primarily through prohibition or repression. It operates through what he called governmentality: the production of subjects who govern themselves. The state does not merely tell you what you cannot do; it shapes who you are, what you desire, and how you understand yourself. Foucault’s concept of confession is central to the mechanism. Modern power incites people to speak about themselves – their desires, traumas, beliefs. This speech is not free expression. It is an act of subjection that binds the speaker to the ideological system they confess within.

Social media industrializes this confessional logic at scale. But confession alone does not explain how people stay attached to a system that visibly fails to deliver what it promises. Lauren Berlant named this persistence cruel optimism: a condition in which the thing you desire is itself an obstacle to your flourishing. The fantasy of the good life – stable job, upward mobility, political representation – persists even after the structural conditions that once made it plausible have disintegrated. People maintain attachment to the fantasy not out of ignorance but because the attachment itself provides a livable structure of feeling.

What Berlant adds to Foucault is the role of genre. In her use, a genre is not a literary category but a set of expectations about how situations unfold – what counts as crisis, what counts as resolution, what feelings are appropriate at each stage. Genre calibrates affect. The self-help genre formats job loss as an opportunity for reinvention; the therapeutic genre formats it as a wound; the activist genre formats it as evidence of exploitation. The material event is the same. The genre determines what it means. And the genre, crucially, determines what the subject does next: not challenge the conditions that produced the event, but metabolize the event through the appropriate affective arc.

Berlant also names crisis ordinariness: ongoing instability absorbed into daily life rather than recognized as crisis. Crisis does not arrive as rupture. It persists as condition. This persistence is not failure – it is the system working as designed. Californication is the process that makes crisis ordinariness sustainable at the level of individual bodies.

Feeling rules as governance infrastructure

If Foucault describes how power produces self-governing subjects and Berlant describes how those subjects stay attached to conditions that harm them, Arlie Russell Hochschild identifies the specific mechanism of compliance: institutions prescribe not just behavior but feeling. Her concept of feeling rules names the social expectations governing which emotions are appropriate in which contexts. Her study of flight attendants demonstrated that workers are required not merely to perform warmth but to cultivate it internally – this is emotional labor: the management of feeling to produce a publicly observable display.

Californication extends this insight from the workplace to the whole of liberal subjectivity. The system prescribes which affects count as legitimate responses to crisis. Serenity, resilience, growth-mindedness – these are not personal choices. They are the feeling rules of a system that requires its subjects to self-regulate rather than demand structural change. The subject who responds to ecological collapse with grief rather than growth is violating the feeling rules. The correction is immediate and distributed: social discomfort, therapeutic redirection, algorithmic demotion.

Hochschild’s insight converges with Berlant’s genre theory at a decisive point. Genre determines what feelings are appropriate; feeling rules enforce the determination. Together they explain how a subject can experience structural violence and genuinely feel that the appropriate response is a breathing exercise. This is not false consciousness. The feeling is real. The rules that produced it are governance.

The cybernetic circuit

The theorists above describe how the process works on individual subjects. Cybernetics explains how it sustains itself without coordination.

Norbert Wiener defined cybernetics in 1948 as the study of control and communication in animals and machines. His central insight was that systems maintain stability through feedback: they detect deviation from a desired state and correct for it. No central controller is needed. The correction is distributed and automatic. Stafford Beer extended this into organizational theory with the Viable System Model, showing how organizations maintain viability by distributing correction across multiple feedback layers operating on different timescales. W. Ross Ashby formulated the Law of Requisite Variety: a system remains stable only if its internal complexity matches the complexity of its environment.

Californication is a feedback mechanism in exactly this sense. The subject detects crisis (deviation), processes it affectively (correction), and returns to functional participation (stability). The correction does not require understanding. It requires self-regulation. The subject who routes crisis into mood – through therapy, wellness practice, content consumption, or confessional posting – is performing a cybernetic function: absorbing system-level deviation as personal adjustment. Platforms accelerate this by operating as feedback infrastructure: engagement metrics reward genre-compliant affect and dampen deviation in real time. Beer’s nested feedback layers map precisely onto the structure: the wellness industry calibrates on one timescale (years), platform algorithms on another (seconds), interpersonal feeling rules on another (the immediate social cost of being a killjoy). These layers do not coordinate. They converge.

Ashby’s requisite variety explains why the system proliferates affective options rather than enforcing a single response. A single prescribed feeling would be too brittle – the variety of crises would exceed the variety of responses, and the system would destabilize. Instead, californication produces a market of affective genres: therapy, mindfulness, activism-as-self-care, spiritual emergence, hustle culture. Each provides a different format for metabolizing crisis. The variety of formats matches the variety of contradictions. The system’s stability is guaranteed not by one correct feeling but by the availability of enough feelings to absorb whatever comes.

Endurance without resolution

Elizabeth Povinelli describes a governance regime – what she calls late liberalism – that no longer promises resolution. What it demands instead is endurance: the ongoing performance of survival under conditions that do not change. Her concept of the quasi-event names a disturbance that appears to open possibility but is already embedded in the logic of its own containment. The event never actually happens because the system has already processed its potential disruption before it arrives.

Californication is the affective mechanism of Povinelli’s endurance. The subject endures not through stoic resignation but through active self-management – routing crisis into affect, affect into content, content into participation. The quasi-event is what crisis looks like under californication: volatile on the surface, already contained at the structural level. And the containment is not imposed from above. It is performed by the subject, through the feeling rules, within the genres, along the feedback loops. Povinelli’s insight that late liberalism tolerates harm as long as the actor can be shown to have been constrained dovetails with Hochschild’s feeling rules: the constraint is affective. You were not prevented from responding. You were trained to feel that your response was adequate.

California as projection zone

The term references California not as geography but as historical laboratory. The Franciscan missions (Misiones Californianas) established the template: convert populations through the management of labor, belief, and daily practice. The Gold Rush formatted extraction as opportunity. Hollywood industrialized the production of inhabitable fantasy. The counterculture – Esalen Institute, psychedelic experimentation, the Summer of Love – transmuted political contradiction into spiritual journey. Silicon Valley built the platform infrastructure that made affect governance operative at planetary scale. The wellness industry – the Calm app, Burning Man, lifehacking, microdosing – commodified self-regulation as lifestyle.

Each of these formations performs the same operation: structural contradiction is reprocessed as personal experience, and that reprocessing is sold back to the subject as freedom. California did not invent this logic, but it has been the site where each iteration was prototyped, refined, and exported.

How californication operates

The process works through several interlocking mechanisms:

  • Genre calibration tunes expectations, formatting crisis as personal narrative rather than structural condition
  • Zen fascism enforces which affects are legitimate, pathologizing distress and eroticizing detachment – making crisis desirable as a source of healing and growth
  • Feeling rules prescribe the appropriate emotional responses – resilience, not rage; growth, not grief
  • Socially ontogenic media distributes inhabitable subject positions, modeling how to be inside contradiction without breaking
  • Harm governance collapses care into risk management, so that even resistance is administered

The subject produced by this process is the savior-slave subject: simultaneously responsible for managing global crises (savior) and governed by systems they cannot transform (slave). Collapse is not hidden. It is narrativized, monetized, and aestheticized. It becomes sustained because it is wanted – the subject has been incited to desire crisis as source of healing, spiritual growth, and personal betterment. Wanting to resolve collapse becomes structurally incommensurable with the systems and affects that sustain it.

On platforms specifically, californication produces the neurotic platformal intellectual: a subject incited into performing expertise under conditions of anxiety, whose learning under crisis is formatted by platform feeling rules into authentic-seeming content. What circulates is not understanding but the affect of understanding.

The full circuit: structural contradiction produces crisis. Crisis is formatted by genre into personal narrative. Feeling rules prescribe the appropriate affect. The subject self-regulates. Platforms distribute the self-regulation as content. The content calibrates other subjects’ genres. The system absorbs the deviation and continues. No one is in charge. The feedback is the governance.
