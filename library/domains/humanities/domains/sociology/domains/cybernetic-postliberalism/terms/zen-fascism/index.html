Zen fascism

2026-03-03 A formation within californication where detachment from crisis is enforced as an ethical credential and distress is pathologized — where the affective register of contemplative practice becomes a normative hierarchy with real consequences.

In 1979, Jello Biafra of the Dead Kennedys wrote “California Uber Alles,” a song that identified something no political theorist had yet named: a mode of governance in which serenity is compulsory. The target was Jerry Brown’s California — a political culture that had fused counterculture spirituality with administrative power, producing a regime where the well-governed subject was the calm subject. Not compliant through fear, but compliant through inner peace. The song’s title made the connection explicit: this was authoritarianism, but it smelled like yoga.

Zen fascism is the formation within californication where that compulsory serenity becomes enforceable. Where californication names the general process by which structural contradiction is formatted as personally manageable affect, zen fascism names what happens when the formatting becomes normative — when detachment from crisis functions as an ethical credential, and distress registers as pathology.

Crisis becomes desirable

The logic runs deeper than “stay calm.” It begins with the constitutive contradictions of liberalism — between universalism and individualism, between the promises the system makes and the conditions it produces. When those contradictions become visible under stress, the system faces a legitimacy problem. It can no longer deliver the resolutions it promises.

Michel Foucault showed how modern governance responds: it shifts from promising resolution to promising regulation. The system no longer solves problems — it manages them. And it manages them by producing subjects who manage themselves.

Zen fascism takes this one step further. Under its logic, crisis is not merely tolerable — it is desirable. Crisis becomes a source of healing, spiritual growth, and personal betterment. The subject learns to want collapse: as material for self-work, as occasion for depth, as proof of seriousness. Ayahuasca entrepreneurism, survivalist retreats, trauma aesthetics, vagus nerve exercises — all formats for experiencing systemic breakdown as personal enrichment.

This is the move that distinguishes zen fascism from ordinary coping. The system does not merely ask you to endure crisis. It teaches you to eroticize your own detachment from it. Nonreactivity becomes attractive. The person who stays centered while everything falls apart is not just functional — they are admirable, desirable, trustworthy. The person who is visibly distressed is dismissed. Not as weak, exactly, but as unfinished. They haven’t done the work.

The enforcement

This is where it becomes fascism. Arlie Russell Hochschild’s feeling rules — social expectations governing which emotions are appropriate in which situations — become, under zen fascism, a normative hierarchy with real consequences. Distress is pathologized. Anger at structural conditions is recoded as personal dysregulation. Grief that refuses therapeutic formatting is treated as evidence of the griever’s failure to process. The serene subject is authorized to speak. The distressed subject is offered therapy.

Those most affected by structural violence are least authorized to name it as structural, because naming it structurally is itself a violation of the feeling rules — it refuses the genre of personal growth. It insists on a cause outside the self, and the whole apparatus depends on there being no such outside.

Lauren Berlant’s concept of crisis ordinariness — ongoing instability absorbed into daily life as a background condition rather than an event — describes the substrate. Under zen fascism, crisis ordinariness is not just a condition but a requirement. You must absorb the crisis. You must make it ordinary. If you cannot, the problem is you.

The result is a legitimacy inversion. Liberalism becomes legitimate because it produces crisis — because the crisis provides the raw material for the self-work that the system’s feeling rules prescribe. The subject who manages crisis with grace demonstrates the system’s viability. The subject who does not is evidence only of their own inadequacy.

The two halves of the term

The “zen” names the affective register: detachment, nonreactivity, presence, acceptance. These are drawn from contemplative traditions but stripped of their contexts. The contemplative traditions from which “mindfulness” and “presence” are extracted had specific relational, ethical, and monastic commitments. What survives into the wellness industry is the affective posture without the obligations — serenity as lifestyle product.

The “fascism” names the enforcement. This is not optional detachment. It is a normative framework with a hierarchy of legitimate and illegitimate affect, with credentials attached to the right feelings and penalties attached to the wrong ones. The hierarchy is not incidental. It is the mechanism by which the system reproduces itself: those who perform the correct affect gain authority; those who do not are managed, medicated, or dismissed.

The M*A*S*H trajectory

The television series M*A*S*H (1972–1983) tracks this formation emerging in real time. The early seasons center Trapper John McIntyre, whose response to the absurdity of war is East Coast irony — satirical detachment, refusal to take the system seriously on its own terms. When Trapper is replaced by B.J. Hunnicutt, the register shifts. B.J.’s response to the same absurdity is sincerity under pressure: managed grief, regulated compassion, emotional depth that never becomes unregulated. The show moves from critiquing the system to modeling how to feel within it. The viewer learns not to resist war’s contradictions but to inhabit them with therapeutic grace.

Satire gives way to therapeutic sincerity. The earlier posture said: this system is absurd and I refuse its terms. The later posture says: this system is painful, and I will metabolize that pain into personal depth. The first is a political stance. The second is a survival template — and the template it distributes is zen fascism. This is socially ontogenic media in action: media that does not explain how to think about a system but models how to be inside it.
