Liberal ontologizing

2026-04-27 The foundational operation of liberal thought that treats things as discrete substances with inherent properties — pre-relational, pre-political, sovereign over themselves — and so makes the world into a collection of independently-existing entities that can subsequently 'enter into' relations.

Liberal ontologizing is the foundational operation of liberal thought. It treats the things of the world — persons, peoples, properties, values — as substances with inherent attributes, existing prior to and independently of the relations they participate in. The world that liberal ontologizing produces is a world of pre-political individuals, pre-relational entities, sovereign units that then enter into the social, the political, and the economic.

The operation is not a description of how things actually are; it is a metaphysical posture that has the appearance of being a neutral default. To denaturalize it is the first move of any analysis that does not take its products for granted.

¶Etymology and lineage

The word ontologize combines Greek ontos (being) with the verb-suffix -ize (to make or render into). To ontologize is to render something as a being — to fix it in the order of what is. The qualifier liberal situates the operation in the political-philosophical tradition that takes the individual as the unit of analysis and rights as the property of those individuals. Liberal ontologizing is what that tradition does first, before any of its other claims become coherent.

The historical operators are well-known. Hobbes (1651) imagines individuals in a state of nature prior to the social contract; political society arises because there were individuals first. Locke (1690) imagines property as a fact about persons that political society exists to defend. Each of these moves makes individuals ontologically prior to the relations they participate in. C. B. Macpherson (1962) traced the form of self-conception this lineage produces — possessive individualism: the person owns themselves and their capacities the way one owns property, and political life flows from that ownership.

A separate Western critical line — Whitehead’s process metaphysics (1929) chief among it — argued that substance ontology is itself a category mistake. Sylvia Wynter (2003) showed how liberal ontologizing accomplishes its work as a racial-colonial operation: Man — a particular Western, secular, bourgeois figure — becomes the overrepresented stand-in for the human, with everyone outside its template registering as defective instances rather than alternative ontologies. Karen Barad (2007) argues, from the side of physics, that “things” are intra-actively constituted rather than pre-existent. Indigenous relational epistemologies (Cajete 2000 among many) hold that the substance posture is a particular cultural-historical move rather than a metaphysical default.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a discourse as performing liberal ontologizing.

Substance-priority. The discourse treats what something is as fixed prior to and independent of how it relates. The voter is a voter before voting; the citizen is a citizen before citizenship; the property-owner is a property-owner before any specific transaction. Relations are subsequent; entities are first.

Inherent attribution. The discourse treats attributes as inhering in the substance. Rights, identities, capacities, dignities are properties of the individual, not features of the relations the individual participates in. The discourse can speak coherently of the rights of an isolated person.

Discrete countability. The discourse takes the world to be made of discrete, countable things. Populations are sums of individuals; markets are aggregates of participants; legal subjects are enumerable. Relational fields, gradients, and continuities are reduced to the entities that can be counted off against them.

A discourse that performs all three is doing the work of liberal ontologizing whether or not it announces itself in those terms.

¶Examples

Contract law treats parties as pre-existing sovereign individuals who choose to bind themselves; the contract is made between discrete agents. The relational fact that the parties’ standing-to-contract arises from the legal-economic order that the contract presupposes is invisible within the discourse.

Voter as autonomous individual. Electoral discourse treats the voter as a pre-political unit whose preferences exist prior to the campaign and whose vote expresses those preferences. The relational facts of campaign-formed preferences, group decision-making, and the campaign’s own role in constituting the electorate fall outside the frame.

Standardized testing treats educational achievement as a property of the individual student, comparable across contexts. The relational facts of class formation, classroom dynamics, and institutional resource allocation are reduced to noise around an inherent capacity that the test is supposed to measure.

¶Adjacent concepts

Liberal ontologizing is not the same as substance metaphysics (which is a broader family of philosophical positions, including pre-modern variants); liberal ontologizing is specifically the political-philosophical version that takes the individual as the substance.

Liberal ontologizing is not the same as essentialism (which posits unchanging natural kinds with shared essences); liberal ontologizing requires only that individuals be discrete and prior, not that they share an essence with others.

Liberal ontologizing is not the same as social construction in its weak form (which holds that some specific categories — race, gender, nationality — are produced by social processes). Strong social construction approaches the inverse position; weak social construction is often compatible with liberal ontologizing as long as the constructed categories are then treated as substantial properties of individuals.

¶Open questions

How thoroughgoing is liberal ontologizing in any actual liberal political philosophy? Communitarian liberals (Charles Taylor, Michael Sandel), republican liberals (Philip Pettit), and capabilities theorists (Amartya Sen, Martha Nussbaum) each modify the operation without abandoning it. The framework’s interest is in the basic operation rather than in mapping every variant.

Does the operation admit exceptions — minor entities, relational beings, persons-in-relation — without losing coherence? The historical record suggests it incorporates such exceptions without revising the basic posture; the exceptions are treated as derived rather than as foundational.

What does it cost a discourse to not perform liberal ontologizing? Indigenous and process-philosophical traditions show alternative practices, but these have so far not produced legal-political infrastructures of comparable scale. Whether that is a constraint of the alternatives or of the legal-political infrastructures themselves is contested.
