Crisis-settlerism

2026-04-27 Settlerism intensified under structural saturation: the apparatus accelerates its frontier-projection when prior settlements destabilize.

Crisis-settlerism is the intensification of settlerism under conditions of structural saturation and destabilization. When the apparatus’s contradictions become visible, settler subject-positions are reproduced more aggressively, more aestheticized, and more violent. The legitimating erasures that operate quietly in ordinary settlerism — terra nullius, the Doctrine of Discovery, the frontier myth — shift toward overt assertion of the right to displace. The formation does not invent itself in crisis; it is settlerism’s pre-existing structure operating under conditions where its work is no longer concealable.

¶Lineage

The contemporary form of the formation is documented by Kathleen Belew (2018), whose study of post-Vietnam white-power organizing traces the consolidation of paramilitary settler movements as the racial-colonial settlement of the U.S. interior reached its post-frontier configuration. Greg Grandin (2019) provides the broader frame: the closing of the geographic frontier in the late nineteenth century and its replacement, in the late twentieth and early twenty-first, by the border wall as the new site at which settler subject-positions are reproduced. Alexander Reid Ross (2017) maps the contemporary intersections of fascism, ecology, and settler ideology — the eco-fascist current that imagines settler restoration as planetary stewardship.

The framework’s term crisis-settlerism draws these lineages together by naming the specific mode in which settlerism operates when its routine displacement-and-replacement is no longer absorbed by ordinary legal-cultural infrastructure. Eve Tuck and K. Wayne Yang’s settler moves to innocence (2012) is the structural antecedent: under decolonial pressure, settlers do not abandon the structure but adopt postures (mock-Indigenous spirituality, settler-victim narratives, settler-saviorism) that preserve the structure’s operation while appearing to address it. Crisis-settlerism is the more aggressive cousin of those moves — the postures that appear when the displacement work itself, not merely the discourse around it, is what is destabilizing.

¶Definitional structure

Three features distinguish crisis-settlerism from ordinary settlerism.

Overt assertion replaces tacit erasure. Where ordinary settlerism operates through legal-cultural infrastructures that erase indigenous prior presence (the founding myth, the doctrine of discovery, the legal fiction of terra nullius), crisis-settlerism asserts the right to displace as a political program. The frontier’s closing has not ended the displacement work; it has shifted the legitimating discourse from concealment to assertion.

Aesthetic intensification. Crisis-settlerism produces aestheticized subject-positions — the homesteader, the prepper, the rural-traditionalist, the eco-fascist, the paramilitary — that are explicitly settler-flavored where ordinary settler subject-positions are merely settler-by-default. The aesthetic intensification is doing legitimating work the disappearing infrastructure can no longer do.

Violence approaches the surface. Ordinary settlerism’s displacement is mediated by legal and economic mechanisms whose violence is dispersed across institutions. Crisis-settlerism’s displacement is more directly violent: paramilitary organizing, vigilante border action, infrastructural violence (pipelines, mining concessions on Indigenous land, urban-renewal acceleration), and the explicit cultivation of populations willing to perform displacement directly.

¶Examples

The post-1990s U.S. white-power movement. Belew (2018) traces the consolidation of paramilitary, militia, and movement organizing in the United States from the post-Vietnam period forward. The formation operates under conditions in which the geographic frontier has closed and the routine displacement work of settlerism is increasingly visible as such; it responds with aestheticized settler subject-positions (the militia member, the prepper, the patriot) and overt assertions of the right to defend the settler population against perceived encroachment.

Border wall infrastructure. Grandin (2019) treats the U.S.-Mexico border wall as the post-frontier site at which crisis-settlerism is enacted. Where the historical frontier had been a continuously-extending site of displacement, the border wall is a terminal site of displacement performed against populations now framed as encroaching from outside the settler territory. The infrastructure is the legitimation; its existence is what asserts the right to displace.

The eco-fascist current. Ross (2017) documents the merger of settler ideology with environmental politics in contemporary far-right organizing. The aestheticization of settler relation-to-land (homesteading, traditionalist farming, blood-and-soil agrarianism) is mobilized under planetary-emergency framing — settler restoration is recoded as ecological responsibility, with the displaced indigenous and racialized populations recoded as demographic-environmental threats.

Standing Rock and infrastructural violence. Nick Estes (2019) reads the 2016–2017 NoDAPL standoff as a contemporary site at which crisis-settlerism’s infrastructural violence (pipelines crossing Indigenous-treaty land, militarized response to Indigenous resistance) operated openly, as routine settler infrastructure could no longer absorb the contradiction.

¶Adjacent concepts

Crisis-settlerism is not the same as fascism, though the two formations overlap. Fascism is a broader political form with its own genealogy and structural features. Crisis-settlerism is settler-formation-under-stress, which can take fascist organizational form (in the U.S. white-power movement, eco-fascist organizing, contemporary nativist nationalism) but is analytically distinct.

Crisis-settlerism is not the same as eco-fascism, though the two formations intersect. Eco-fascism specifically merges settler-restoration ideology with environmental politics under planetary-emergency framing; crisis-settlerism is the broader category within which eco-fascism is one current.

Crisis-settlerism is not the same as settler-victimhood-discourse, though such discourse is one of the formation’s features. Settler-victimhood (the narrative that settlers are under threat, displaced, persecuted) is a rhetoric that crisis-settlerism mobilizes; the formation itself is the structural condition under which the rhetoric becomes operational.

¶Open questions

How is crisis-settlerism related to ordinary settlerism — is it a phase, a mode, or a successor? The framework treats it as the same structure under different operating conditions, but a sharper specification of the conditions under which ordinary settlerism intensifies into crisis-settlerism is needed.

What relations does crisis-settlerism bear to climate-and-energy transitions? The eco-fascist current and the homestead-traditionalist current both engage planetary-emergency framings; whether settlerism intensifies or rather dissolves under conditions of severe energetic decline is contested.

How does Indigenous resurgence respond to crisis-settlerism? The work of Coulthard, Simpson, Estes, and others develops alternative practices — relational governance, land-based education, refusal — that operate outside crisis-settlerism’s intensified frame; the conditions under which those practices can be sustained against intensifying pressure is a substantial open question.
