Socially ontogenic media

2025-04-13 Media artifacts that model survivable identity postures within contradictory or collapsing systems, distributing templates for how to be a coherent actor when inherited identity models no longer function. Unlike media that entertains, explains, or persuades, socially ontogenic media provides its audience with a usable template for how to *be* inside a system that does not offer clear guidance on who to be. The mechanism is the modulation loop: a rhythmic, repeatable pattern of affective response that the audience can emulate and internalize. The category recurs across wildly different genres (Kipling's 'If—,' M*A*S*H, Phineas and Ferb) because the function — modeling survivable being under contradiction — is independent of tone.

Socially ontogenic media is any media artifact that models a survivable identity posture within a contradictory, unstable, or collapsing system. Unlike media that entertains, explains, or persuades, socially ontogenic media provides its audience with a usable template for how to be a coherent actor inside a system that does not offer clear guidance on who to be. It distributes survivable selfhood.

A surgeon in a war zone cracks a joke while amputating a leg. The audience laughs — not because it is funny, but because M*A*S*H has spent seasons teaching them that laughter is how you survive a system that processes bodies without pause. The show does not explain how to cope with war. It does not argue a position on war. It models a way of being inside war’s contradictions: ironic, compassionate, rhythmically attuned to crisis that never resolves. The viewer does not learn a lesson. They internalize a posture.

¶Etymology and lineage

The compound parses directly. Socially: the identity template is shared and transmissible — it circulates between people, not just within them. Ontogenic: it generates ways of being, not just ways of knowing or feeling. Media: it operates through cultural artifacts that circulate. The concept names a specific function that certain artifacts serve: not representation, not ideology critique, not entertainment, but the production of inhabitable subject positions under conditions where inherited identity templates have broken down. The term is the ontogenring operation specifically as it operates through media artifacts at scale.

The conceptual lineage runs through several traditions. From Raymond Williams (1977), structures of feeling — the affective register that precedes articulated ideology and is transmitted through cultural form. From Lauren Berlant (2008), the analysis of intimate publics and the genres of attachment they distribute, providing templates for survivable inhabitation under conditions where political-collective registers have thinned. From Stuart Hall (1980) and the broader cultural-studies tradition, the methodological apparatus for reading how media’s coded structures are received and reproduced by audiences. From Fredric Jameson (1981), the analysis of narrative form as the symbolic resolution of structural contradictions — the closest theoretical antecedent for socially ontogenic media’s specific function. From Sianne Ngai (2005), the analysis of minor affects as politically diagnostic.

What socially ontogenic media adds beyond these adjacent terms is the explicit framing of the artifact’s function as being-template rather than as belief-formation, ideological-vehicle, or aesthetic experience. The artifact is not a message; it is a rehearsal of inhabitation.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a media artifact as socially ontogenic.

Distribution of being-templates rather than content. Propaganda tells you what to believe. Didactic media tells you what to do. Entertainment offers temporary escape. Socially ontogenic media offers something more fundamental: a template for how to be when the available templates have failed. Each of the others addresses the content of experience — what to think, what to act on, what to forget. Socially ontogenic media addresses the structure of the subject who has the experience. The artifact’s effect is not propositional; it is dispositional.

Modulation loops. The mechanism is the rhythmic, repeatable pattern of affective response that the audience can emulate and internalize. The pattern does not resolve contradiction; it teaches the audience how to move through contradiction without breaking. M*A*S*H’s ironic-compassionate register is a modulation loop. Kipling’s stoic-imperial composure is a modulation loop. Phineas and Ferb’s anarchic-creation-into-system-reset is a modulation loop. The audience does not consciously decide to inhabit the loop; they absorb it through repeated exposure.

Emergence under conditions of inherited-template breakdown. Socially ontogenic media typically emerges during or following periods of ideological fragmentation — moments when inherited identity templates no longer produce functional coherence. Kipling at the height of imperial overextension. M*A*S*H during Vietnam’s erosion of Cold War consensus. Phineas and Ferb in the post-2008 landscape where meritocratic success has become visibly incoherent. The pattern is consistent: inherited ways of being break down, and media artifacts emerge that provide replacement postures — not replacement ideologies, not replacement beliefs, but replacement rhythms of selfhood.

¶Examples

Rudyard Kipling’s “If—” does not argue for stoicism. It distributes a template for being stoic. The poem is a modulation loop: a rhythmic rehearsal of composure under conditions of violence, absurdity, and imperial overreach. The colonial subject who internalizes Kipling has a way to be inside an empire whose contradictions would otherwise produce incoherence. The poem does not explain the empire. It does not critique it. It provides the posture that makes inhabiting it possible.

M*A*S*H functions as a simulation lab for affect under institutional absurdity. Characters do not develop — they adjust. Hawkeye modulates between clown, surgeon, and moral-reflex arc. The show trains viewers to track change without transformation: processing war, bureaucracy, and moral ambiguity as signals to be managed rather than problems to be solved. Even the laugh track participates — a rhythmic reminder that the audience’s affective response is part of the loop, not separate from it. The trajectory from Trapper John (East Coast irony, refusal of the system’s terms) to B.J. Hunnicutt (sincerity-under-pressure, managed grief, regulated compassion) tracks the formation of zen fascism emerging in real time within a single show.

Phineas and Ferb converts anarchic creation into a repeatable rhythm through its episodic reset structure. Each episode models a way of being inside corporate capitalism: creative, joyful, and ultimately absorbed. The children build impossible machines; the system erases them; the next episode begins. What looks like rebellion is the system training itself to metabolize rebellion. The show’s apparent freedom is real at the level of affect, but null at the level of reproduction. It teaches children how to inhabit a system that converts anarchy into product.

The wellness influencer’s daily-routine post. A short video showing the influencer’s morning ritual: the protein shake, the meditation, the journal, the workout. The video does not argue that this is the right way to live; it does not explain the practices. It distributes the modulation loop — the rhythm of self-management that signals competent contemporary inhabitation. Viewers internalize the rhythm whether or not they adopt the specific practices.

¶Adjacent concepts

Socially ontogenic media is not the same as propaganda in the conventional political-communication sense. Propaganda’s function is belief-formation; socially ontogenic media’s function is being-formation. The two can overlap — some artifacts perform both — but their structural functions are distinct, and an artifact can perform either without the other.

Socially ontogenic media is not the same as lifestyle media in the marketing-cultural sense. Lifestyle media is one configuration in which socially ontogenic media operates (the wellness influencer, the home-improvement YouTube channel), but socially ontogenic media is broader: it includes artistic-literary artifacts (Kipling’s poetry, M*A*S*H) whose marketing-cultural-positioning is incidental to their being-template function.

Socially ontogenic media is not the same as ideological state apparatus in Althusser’s sense. The ideological state apparatus operates through institutions (school, church, family) that produce subjects through interpellation; socially ontogenic media operates through artifacts whose circulation does not require institutional sponsorship. The two can interact (institutions distribute artifacts) but they are distinct mechanisms.

Socially ontogenic media is not the same as identity formation in the developmental-psychological sense. Identity formation names an intra-psychic developmental process; socially ontogenic media names a cultural-distributional artifact-class. The same individual subject’s identity can be formed through socially ontogenic media (among many other channels), but the categories operate at different scales.

¶Open questions

How does socially ontogenic media operate under platformal conditions specifically? Pre-platform socially ontogenic media (Kipling’s poetry, M*A*S*H) circulated through fixed forms (book publication, broadcast schedule). Platformal socially ontogenic media (the influencer’s daily-routine post, the recurring meme) circulates through algorithmic recommendation, with rapid genre-iteration. Whether the platformal conditions produce a structurally distinct category or an intensification of the existing one is open.

What practices alter or refuse the modulation loop without simply substituting another? Critique of a socially ontogenic media artifact often distributes the artifact’s modulation loop more broadly while ostensibly opposing it. Whether any practice exits the loop structurally, rather than producing a counter-loop within the same metabolism, is the question the school’s closure analysis holds open.

What is the relation between socially ontogenic media and the savior-slave subject? The artifacts circulate primarily among subjects already positioned in the savior-slave structure; the modulation loops are calibrated to the structure’s affective requirements. Whether the artifacts produce the position or only refine it is empirical.
