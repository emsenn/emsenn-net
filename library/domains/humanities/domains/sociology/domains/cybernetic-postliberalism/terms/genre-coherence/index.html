Genre coherence

2026-04-02 The structural demand that a nation's cast of folk types remain stable, so that anyone who deviates from the assigned genre figure becomes a narrative disruption requiring exclusion. The mechanism connecting genring to fascist violence: not the production of the genre figures themselves, but the pressure to keep them in their assigned positions.

Genre coherence is the structural demand that a nation’s cast of folk types remain stable. Once a nation imagines itself as a collection of authentic genre figures — the Noble Indian, the Hearty Bavarian, the Mountain Kurd, the salt-of-the-earth farmer — the integrity of the national story depends on the characters remaining as assigned. Anyone who does not fit the genre becomes a problem for the story. Not necessarily an ideological enemy, but a narrative disruption: a character who breaks the fourth wall.

¶Distinction from genring

The framework distinguishes genre coherence from genring. Genring is the operation that produces the genre figures in the first place — the selection, codification, and substitution by which a living people is reduced to a literary type. Genre coherence is the structural pressure to maintain the produced figures in their assigned positions. The two are sequenced: genring constitutes the cast; genre coherence enforces it. A nation can perform genring without yet having developed a strong coherence demand; under stress, the coherence demand intensifies. The intensification is what connects genring to fascist violence.

¶Etymology and lineage

The phrase genre coherence combines genre (the literary or expectational category) with coherence (from Latin cohaerere, to stick together). The combination foregrounds the structural force that holds the cast together against the centrifugal pressures of actual living variety. Where genring is content-producing, genre coherence is structure-maintaining.

The concept draws on three lineages. From fascist-studies, Roger Griffin’s (1991) account of fascism as palingenetic ultranationalism identifies the mythic-rebirth narrative that requires its cast of authentic folk figures to remain recognizable across the rebirth. Robert Paxton’s Anatomy of Fascism (2004) shows how the demand operates across the stages of fascist development. Hannah Arendt (1951) traces how stable national-mythological figures are manufactured and maintained, and what kind of violence the maintenance requires.

From dialogic literary theory, Mikhail Bakhtin’s heteroglossia (1981) names the polyphonic condition that genre coherence works against — the actual multivocality of any living community against the monologue the genre demands. From the Caribbean critical tradition, Édouard Glissant’s creolization (1997) names a counter-formation in which mixture and relation are not threats to coherence but the substance of cultural life.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a structural condition as enacting genre coherence.

Cast-stability requirement. The discourse’s narrative integrity depends on a particular cast of folk figures remaining recognizable. The figures are not just acknowledged as one possibility among many; they are required as the recognizable substance of the national story. Other configurations (multivocal, creole, plural) are not merely absent but unacceptable.

Disruption-as-narrative-problem. A subject who does not fit the assigned genre is read not as a personal exception but as a problem for the story. The discourse cannot accommodate the disruption without compromising itself. This produces social-discomfort registers (the Lakota mathematician registers as wrong; the Bavarian Marxist registers as wrong; the Kurdish anarchist woman registers as wrong) that operate at the level of feeling-rule rather than ideology.

Exclusion as structural rather than ideological. The exclusion of disrupting subjects is structural — it follows from the genre’s coherence demand — rather than ideological in the conventional sense. The excluder may not consciously believe the excluded subject is bad or wrong; the excluder feels the narrative-disruption and acts on the feeling without articulated ideology. The structural character is what makes the operation difficult to address through anti-ideological argument.

¶Examples

The “real America” trope. U.S. nationalist discourse imagines a cast of authentic American folk figures (the small-town farmer, the working-class manufacturer, the patriotic veteran). The cast is partial — it excludes urban intellectuals, queer subjects, multiethnic figures, displaced indigenous populations — and the partiality is enforced through the structural pressure of genre coherence. Whoever does not fit the cast registers as not-real-America rather than as a different kind of American.

The Bavarian Heimat. Hobsbawm and Ranger trace how the stable Bavarian folk-figure (Hearty Bavarian, in lederhosen, in the alpine context, with appropriate festivals and food) was historically assembled in the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries and the institutional infrastructures (folk museums, Trachtenvereine, marketing) that maintain it. The Bavarian socialist, the Bavarian feminist, the Bavarian Turkish-German register as not-Bavarian under genre coherence’s pressure.

The Mountain Kurd genre. The Kurdish-as-mountain-tribesman genre — frozen-in-time, agrarian, pre-modern — is the genre into which the Kurdish people have been formatted across multiple national projects (Turkish, Iraqi, Syrian, Iranian). The Kurdish anarchist-feminist movement, the Kurdish urban-cosmopolitan, the Kurdish Cannes-winning filmmaker register as disruption to the genre — and the structural pressure to exclude them is genre coherence at work.

¶Adjacent concepts

Genre coherence is not the same as stereotyping in the conventional sense. Stereotyping is a cognitive simplification; genre coherence is a structural-narrative demand. The two can coincide and frequently do, but they operate at different levels: stereotype is a feature of perception; genre coherence is a feature of the social-narrative infrastructure.

Genre coherence is not the same as identity politics in either its dominant or its critical sense. Identity politics involves claims-making by particular subject-positions; genre coherence is the structural pressure that determines which subject-positions are admissible as recognizable. A given identity-politics formation may operate within genre coherence (claiming recognition within the genre) or against it (refusing the genre’s pressure).

Genre coherence is not the same as cultural conservatism in the ordinary political-philosophical sense. Cultural conservatism is a normative position about what should be preserved; genre coherence is a structural pressure that may operate even in formations that do not consider themselves conservative.

¶Open questions

What conditions intensify the genre coherence demand? Stress on the system (saturation, destabilization), threats to legitimacy, contact with creolizing alternatives — each appears to intensify the demand. The framework’s working hypothesis is that intensification follows from the system’s broader operating conditions rather than from any single trigger.

How does genre coherence operate at scales smaller than the nation (workplace cultures, professional fields, subcultures)? The same structural pressure appears to operate at smaller scales (the “real” software engineer, the “authentic” artist, the “true” academic) — whether the same analytical apparatus applies straightforwardly is open.

What practices erode genre coherence rather than merely defying it? Defiance of the genre often re-stabilizes it (the disrupter becomes the recognizable figure of the disrupter). Erosion would require something different — practices that make the cast itself unstable rather than positioning oneself outside it. The framework gestures at heteroglossia and creolization as candidates without claiming to map them systematically.
