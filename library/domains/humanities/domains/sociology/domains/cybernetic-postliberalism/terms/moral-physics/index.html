Moral Physics

2026-04-30 The implicit rules a media artifact establishes for how harm, responsibility, and consequence work in its world.

Moral physics is the implicit rules a media artifact establishes for how harm, responsibility, and consequence work inside the world it depicts. Where genre tells the audience what kind of story they are watching, moral physics tells the audience what kind of universe the story occurs in: whether bodies break, whether choices accumulate, whether catastrophe leaves residue, whether cause links to effect. These rules are rarely declared. They are taught through repetition — the way a body bounces back, the way a death is or is not registered, the way the next scene does or does not remember the last.

Moral physics is the load-bearing layer that sits beneath genre. A film can declare itself a war film and still operate in a moral physics where soldiers reset between scenes; a wellness app can declare itself therapeutic and still operate in a moral physics where structural conditions cannot exert force on individual feelings. The genre announces the surface; moral physics governs the substrate.

¶Why the term

The vocabulary already available to media analysis tends to cover what is shown (representation), how it is framed (genre, genring), or what audiences are induced to feel (affect, feeling rules). What it tends to miss is the artifact’s underlying physics: the rules its world quietly enforces about consequence, persistence, and causal weight. These rules are not propositional. They are not stated as claims. They are felt as the texture of the world.

Cartoon animation makes the layer especially visible because its physics are openly fictive: a character is hit by a piano, flattens, peels off the floor, walks away. No one mistakes this for a claim about real bodies. But the regularity of the reset is a claim about how harm works inside this world, and a viewer trained on these resets imports the expectation. Live-action media inherit the same problem in subtler form. A war film in which protagonists face danger but never lose limbs, in which civilian deaths occur off-screen and cease to matter by the next scene, is enforcing a moral physics no less specific for being unstated.

Moral physics names this layer so that analysis can address it directly.

¶What it covers

A media artifact’s moral physics can be specified along several axes. None is exhaustive, but together they sketch the shape of the world the artifact insists upon.

Persistence of harm. Does damage stay? When a character is injured, is the injury present in the next scene, the next episode, the next season? A moral physics in which harm persists treats consequence as cumulative. A moral physics in which harm dissolves treats each scene as fresh. The reset is a primary diagnostic.

Locus of causation. Where do events come from? In some moral physics, events arise from structures (institutions, economies, ecologies). In others, they arise from individual choices (the hero’s pluck, the villain’s malice). In others still, they arise from no traceable source at all — events simply happen, and characters react. The locus of causation determines what kind of action makes sense to take in response.

Reversibility of catastrophe. Can the worst be undone? A moral physics with reversible catastrophe — disasters reset, bodies reassemble, regimes can be voted out — invites a different ethical posture than one in which catastrophe leaves a permanent mark on the world.

Weight of bodies. Do bodies have mass, friction, fragility? Or are they elastic, mobile, infinitely rearrangeable? This is not just a question of physical realism. A body that cannot be permanently broken cannot be the subject of a permanent injustice; the moral physics of weightless bodies makes some kinds of moral claim literally illegible.

Memory of the world. Does the world remember? A moral physics with memory carries forward what has happened. A moral physics without memory begins each scene anew. The latter approaches the structure of dream or gag; the former approaches the structure of history.

These axes are descriptive, not prescriptive. The point is not that artifacts should have heavy bodies and persistent harm. The point is that whatever combination they have, that combination is doing political work — quietly, continuously, beneath the level at which audiences feel they are being addressed.

¶Moral physics is not realism

Moral physics is sometimes confused with realism, but the two are orthogonal. A surrealist film can have a heavy, persistent moral physics in which every catastrophe leaves residue across the entire diegesis. A photorealistic action film can have a featherlight moral physics in which protagonists fall from buildings, walk it off, and crack jokes by the next reel. Moral physics is about the rules of the world the artifact constructs, regardless of how stylized or naturalistic its surface is.

This distinction matters because the most powerful moral physics often runs underneath the most realistic-looking surfaces. The fact that a war film looks like documentary does not tell you whether it operates in a moral physics that registers civilian death as consequential. The look is genre. The rule is physics.

¶Relation to other terms in the domain

Moral physics interlocks with several existing terms in cybernetic postliberalism.

Genring reduces a living people to a literary type. Moral physics determines what can happen to the type once it has been produced. A genre figure rendered inside a moral physics of consequence-free violence cannot be the subject of a real grievance, because the moral physics has already decided that grievances do not accumulate.

Genre calibration tunes audience expectation about how a situation will unfold. Moral physics is the substrate that makes genre calibration possible at all: the audience can only know that a setup will resolve into a punchline because they have been trained in a moral physics where setups always do.

Feeling rules prescribe what a subject is allowed to feel in response to events. Moral physics determines which events are legible as events in the first place. A harm that the moral physics does not register cannot be the object of a feeling, because there is nothing to feel about.

Harm governance collapses care into risk management. Moral physics is the cultural training that prepares subjects to accept this collapse: a population accustomed to consequence-free violence on screen is a population that has already been given the affective grammar for harm-without-accountability off screen.

¶How to read for it

Reading an artifact for its moral physics requires resisting the artifact’s surface. The procedure, in compressed form:

  1. Identify a moment of harm, choice, or rupture in the artifact.
  2. Track what happens in the next scene, episode, or sequence. What persists from the moment? What dissolves?
  3. Repeat for several moments across the artifact. The pattern of persistence and dissolution is the artifact’s moral physics.
  4. Compare against the genre the artifact declares. The gap between declared genre and operating physics is where ideological work is done.

A more complete walk-through is given in how to identify moral displacement in a media artifact, which uses the moral-physics analytic to track one specific kind of displacement: the rerouting of attention from structural cause to individual character.

¶What moral physics does not name

Moral physics is not a moral judgment. The claim is not that some moral physics are good and others bad. The claim is that every artifact has one, that the moral physics of an artifact is doing political work whether or not its makers intended it to, and that analysis that stops at genre and representation has stopped one layer too soon.

Moral physics also does not name the moral content of an artifact. An artifact can preach justice in its dialogue while operating in a moral physics where injustice is reversible by the end of the next scene. The dialogue is the message. The physics is the lesson.
