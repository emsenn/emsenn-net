Collapse as narrativization

2026-04-27 The ontogenric formatting of saturation-induced destabilization as 'collapse' — a narrative structure with phases, affects, and resolutions that allows the structural condition to be experienced as a story-arc the subject is entitled to and anticipates, rather than as a structural condition the subject inhabits.

Collapse as narrativization is the result of ontogenring acting on destabilization. The structural condition produced by saturation does not arrive at subjects as a structural condition; it arrives genre-formatted, with a name (collapse), an affective register (dread, mourning, resolve), a temporal shape (decline, fall, possibility-of-renewal), and a set of subject-positions ready to occupy each phase (the prepper, the doomer, the chronicler, the mourner, the rebuilder).

¶Etymology and discursive history

The word collapse derives from Latin collabi — col- (together) + labi (to slip, fall) — to fall together, to fall in one body. The medieval and early-modern uses are physical and mechanical: a structure collapses; a cave collapses; a body collapses. The civilizational sense — collapse as the structural breakdown of a society — emerges in the eighteenth century with Gibbon’s account of Rome, but does not become a popular discursive formation until the twentieth.

The contemporary discursive formation is post-1970s. It coincides with the publication of The Limits to Growth (Meadows et al. 1972), the oil crisis, and the early articulation of ecological-systems thought. By the early 2000s a stable genre had emerged: collapse-discourse with recognizable subject-positions (the doomer, the prepper, the eco-realist), recognizable arcs (decline, awareness, preparation, descent), and recognizable affective rules. By the 2010s the genre had multiplied into sub-genres — collapse-now (Greer 2008), capitalist realism (Fisher 2009), catastrophic times (Stengers 2015), the future-cancelled (Berardi 2011), the doomerism of online discourse — each of which inherits the underlying genre while differing in affective tone and prescribed resolution.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark this as a narrativization rather than a structural designation.

It is genre-shaped, not condition-shaped. Collapse in contemporary discourse arrives with phases, affects, and resolutions. A reader recognizes the genre and slots themselves into a phase. A condition-designation has none of these — it would be a flat description with no affective arc, and most usage of collapse clearly carries the arc.

Subject-positions are pre-specified. The genre supplies a set of positions a participant can occupy: the prepper (preparing materially for the descent), the doomer (registering the descent affectively without preparation), the chronicler (documenting the descent), the mourner (grieving what is being lost), the rebuilder (preparing for what comes after). Each position has its own affective register and behavioral norms; the subject installs themselves into one (or migrates among them) and the genre’s rules apply.

The structural condition becomes peripheral. Once the genre is engaged, the underlying destabilization recedes from explicit attention. The subject’s attention goes to the genre — to performing the appropriate affect for their phase, to consuming and producing genre-content, to occupying the subject-position correctly — rather than to the underlying dynamics the genre is supposed to be about.

¶Examples of the genre’s forms

Doomerism is one form. Affective register: dread, fatalism, dark humor. Resolution-arc: preparedness, isolation, witness. Recognizable in online forums, certain podcasts, and a substantial subset of contemporary literary fiction.

Hopepunk is another. Affective register: determined cheerfulness, deliberate optimism, communal warmth. Resolution-arc: rebuilding, mutual aid, post-collapse community. Recognizable in YA fiction, certain solarpunk subgenres, and post-2016 progressive media.

Apocalyptic-religious is another. Affective register: awe, dread, anticipation of judgment. Resolution-arc: divine intervention, sanctified survival, the return. Recognizable in evangelical eschatology, certain New Religious Movements, and revival of millenarian traditions.

Tradlife / homesteading is another. Affective register: grounded calm, productive labor, traditional gendered virtue. Resolution-arc: retreat to the land, recovery of pre-industrial competence, generational continuity. Recognizable in Instagram aesthetics, certain rural-relocation movements, and a portion of the homeschooling community.

Each is a genre-position within the same narrativization. Choosing among them is choosing one’s relation to the same underlying destabilization, not choosing whether to engage it.

¶Adjacent concepts

Collapse-as-narrativization is not the same as decline. Decline is gradual and gradient-shaped (a slow lowering); collapse is catastrophic and threshold-shaped (a sudden falling-together). The two genres have different temporal registers and different prescribed responses.

Collapse-as-narrativization is not the same as catastrophe in the event-sense. Catastrophe is event-shaped (something happens); collapse-as-narrativization is condition-shaped (we are in collapse). Stengers (2015) develops a catastrophic-times register that is adjacent to collapse-as-narrativization but operates with different affective rules.

Collapse-as-narrativization is not the same as structural collapse in Tainter’s (1988) sense. Tainter analyzes societies whose marginal returns on complexity have turned negative and whose institutional structures cease to be reproducible; this is a structural designation, not a genre. The framework’s term distinguishes the structural condition from its narrativized form. The structural condition may obtain or not; the narrativization is operative regardless.

¶Open questions

What is the relationship between recognizing a real destabilization and entering the collapse genre? The framework does not deny that real destabilization is underway; it argues that engaging the destabilization through the collapse genre tends to convert structural attention into genre-performance. Whether non-genre engagement with destabilization is possible — and what it would look like — is open.

Are there protective uses of the genre? Some readings of doomerism, hopepunk, or homesteading argue these genres do useful preparatory work even if they also have the metabolic effect the framework describes. The framework is skeptical but does not yet specify the conditions under which the genre’s preparatory aspects exceed its metabolic costs.

Is the genre exiting its current form? Successive genre forms (doomerism in 2010, hopepunk in 2015, tradlife in 2020) suggest the formation is mutable; whether the underlying narrativization is mutable, and whether it can be exited entirely, is contested.
