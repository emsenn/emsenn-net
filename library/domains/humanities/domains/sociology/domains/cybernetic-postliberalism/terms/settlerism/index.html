Settlerism

2026-04-27 The structural formation of governance organized around continuous displacement-and-replacement of indigenous relations, the production of stable settler subject-positions on a continuously-renewed colonial frontier, and the legal-cultural infrastructures that legitimate the displacement by erasing indigenous prior presence.

Settlerism is the structural formation of governance organized around continuous displacement-and-replacement of indigenous relations, the production of stable settler subject-positions on a continuously-renewed colonial frontier, and the legal-cultural infrastructures that legitimate the displacement by erasing indigenous prior presence. It is a structure rather than an event, a regime rather than an episode — settler arrival is not the founding moment of the formation but its first iteration, with each subsequent generation continuing the displacement under different vocabularies.

¶Etymology and lineage

The term combines settler — one who establishes a residence in a territory previously inhabited by others — with -ism in its sense of an organizing structure or doctrine. The construction emphasizes that settlement, on this account, is not a private fact about individual residence but a formation that organizes governance, economy, and subject-position around the displacement that residence depends on.

The conceptual lineage is the field of settler colonial studies, formalized by Patrick Wolfe (2006) and Lorenzo Veracini (2010), which distinguishes settler colonialism from other colonial forms by its logic of elimination: settler colonies do not extract labor and resources to send back to the metropole; they replace the indigenous population in place. This makes settler colonialism a structure that does not end when “decolonization” occurs in the post-1945 sense, because the structure was never one of distant rule but of substitution.

The Indigenous critical tradition has insisted, against settler colonial studies’ theoretical tendency, that the structure remains operative in present-day settler nations and that decolonial discourse that imagines reconciliation without return-of-land is itself a settler-colonial mechanism. Eve Tuck and K. Wayne Yang’s “Decolonization Is Not a Metaphor” (2012) is the canonical statement; Glen Coulthard’s Red Skin, White Masks (2014) and Leanne Betasamosake Simpson’s As We Have Always Done (2017) develop the alternative — Indigenous resurgence, relational governance, the rebuilding of nations whose continuity settlerism has tried to interrupt.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a governance formation as settlerist.

Continuous displacement. Indigenous people, lands, and relations are displaced not in a single founding act but continuously. Each generation faces a new round of land-loss, treaty-violation, jurisdictional encroachment, or cultural-erasure. The displacement is the structure’s ongoing operation, not a fact about its origin.

Replacement-in-place. The settler population does not extract resources back to a metropole; it replaces the indigenous population in the territory itself. The settler becomes the local — sometimes called the native — and the actually-indigenous become the displaced foreign element within their own territory. This inversion is the structure’s signature.

Legitimation through erasure. Legal and cultural infrastructures legitimate the displacement by erasing indigenous prior presence. Terra nullius in Australian law, the Doctrine of Discovery in U.S. and Canadian law, the founding-myths of frontier nations, the cartographies that empty the map before re-populating it — each is an instance of the legitimating erasure. The legal-cultural work is what makes the displacement appear as something other than what it is.

¶Examples

Westward expansion of the United States. The continuous nineteenth-century displacement of Indigenous nations across the territories that became the contiguous United States — through treaty-violation, military campaign, residential schooling, land-allotment, and termination policy — is the canonical instance from which Wolfe and Dunbar-Ortiz draw. The structure persists in the contemporary regime of federal-tribal relations, the Bureau of Indian Affairs, and the ongoing struggles over jurisdiction, land restitution, and resource extraction.

Israeli settlement of Palestinian territories. The post-1948 and post-1967 establishment of Israeli settlements in Palestinian territories is widely analyzed as a contemporary settler-colonial formation. The legal-cultural infrastructure (security-doctrine, archaeological priority, biblical legitimation), the continuous displacement (settlement expansion, evictions, separation barriers), and the replacement-in-place (settler populations becoming the local civic majority) are the formation’s three features in operation.

Australian frontier and continuing dispossession. Australian settler colonialism is foundational for the field’s theoretical literature. The terra nullius doctrine, the displacement of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples, the Stolen Generations, and the contemporary politics of recognition (which Coulthard’s analysis treats as a continuation rather than rectification of the structure) are studied extensively in Veracini’s and others’ comparative work.

Urban renewal as internal settlement. Within already-settled territory, urban renewal, gentrification, and infrastructure-driven displacement reproduce the formation at a smaller scale. The legal-cultural infrastructures (eminent domain, zoning, “blight” designation), the continuous displacement (rolling waves of redevelopment), and the replacement-in-place (new resident populations becoming the local) are the formation’s three features at the urban scale.

¶Adjacent concepts

Settlerism is not the same as settler colonialism in the academic-disciplinary sense. Settler colonialism is the field of study; settlerism is the framework’s term for the formation that field describes. The choice of -ism is intentional: it names a structure of organization rather than a historical episode.

Settlerism is not the same as multiculturalism, even when multicultural discourse is operative within a settler state. Multiculturalism organizes the relation among populations within the settler state; settlerism names the structure within which those populations are configured. A multicultural settler state remains a settler state.

Settlerism is not the same as post-colonialism in the post-1945 sense. Post-colonial discourse describes the aftermath of distant-rule colonialism; settler colonialism is what remains operative in places where distant rule was never the structure. Decolonization in the post-1945 sense ended distant-rule colonialism; it did not end settlerism, and the conflation has been criticized extensively in the Indigenous critical tradition.

¶Open questions

How does settlerism interact with capitalism? The two formations are entangled — settler economies are typically capitalist economies, and capitalism’s expansion has historically operated through settler frontiers — but the formations are analytically distinct. The framework’s claim is that settlerism is not reducible to capitalism, but the conditions under which the two formations diverge or reinforce each other are open.

How does settlerism operate in places where indigenous populations have been almost entirely eliminated? The structure’s continuous-displacement feature requires displacement to continue; what continues to happen in places where displacement has approached completion is studied by Veracini and others under the heading of “settler society” rather than “settler colonialism.”

How does settlerism operate on differently-positioned subjects within the settler population? Black populations in the United States, for instance, occupy a position within the settler-colonial structure that is neither indigenous nor settler in the classical sense; the position has been theorized by Frank Wilderson, Saidiya Hartman, and others under the heading of Afropessimism and adjacent traditions. The framework treats this as a substantial open area at the intersection of settlerism and the broader racial-colonial register.
