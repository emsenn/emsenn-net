Memetic Warfare

2026-04-30 The doctrinal use of meme-form content as tactical influence — the operation, in cybernetic-postliberalism, by which state and adjacent actors weaponize the affective grammars of platformal feedback architectures.

Memetic warfare is the use of meme-form content — short, recombinable, emotionally-loaded units circulated through algorithmic platforms — as tactical influence. The phrase originates in late-2010s U.S. and NATO doctrinal discussion, where it names a recognized capability area within information operations: the production and distribution of low-cost, high-virality content designed to shape perception, recruit attention, harass adversaries, or shift the affective register of an information environment.

Within cybernetic postliberalism, memetic warfare is treated less as a novel doctrine than as the open avowal of a longer state-aesthetic capacity. Memes did not invent recombinable, emotionally-loaded, low-cost content; advertising, propaganda, and entertainment had been producing it for a century. What memes added was the platformal substrate: an algorithmic distribution system that made meme-form content the dominant unit of public attention. Memetic warfare names what happens when state actors operate inside that substrate using the same forms its native users have developed.

¶Why the term enters the domain

The cybernetic-postliberalism domain studies how late-liberal governance reproduces itself through formatting rather than persuasion. Memetic warfare is the explicit, doctrinal name for a piece of that formatting work — and one of the few cases in which the apparatus names itself. Most of the operations the domain analyzes (genring, californication, harm governance) are unspoken; the system does not advertise that it is doing them. Memetic warfare, by contrast, has been openly discussed in U.S. military journals and NATO publications since the mid-2010s. The doctrinal openness is itself a finding.

Including the term in the domain serves three purposes: it gives other texts a place to link to when they touch the doctrinal register; it positions the doctrinal concept inside the domain’s analytical vocabulary rather than treating it as exterior; and it allows analyses of artifacts produced under doctrinal influence (recruitment videos, influence-op campaigns) to name the doctrinal context without having to import vocabulary from outside the domain.

¶What the doctrinal concept covers

Doctrinal discussions of memetic warfare typically include several elements. None is unique to memes; their convergence in platformal substrates is what makes the doctrinal category coherent.

Low-cost content production. Meme-form content can be produced rapidly, in large quantities, by small teams or individuals. The economics differ from those of traditional broadcast propaganda, which required centralized production capacity and expensive distribution.

High-virality distribution. Algorithmic platforms amplify content that performs well by their engagement metrics. Meme-form content is engineered (or selected) to perform well by these metrics, which means distribution is largely free.

Recombinant payloads. Memes carry their content in a format that invites variation. Adversaries, supporters, and bystanders all become potential redistributors and modifiers. The content propagates through a population of remixers rather than being broadcast from a single source.

Affective rather than propositional address. Meme-form content typically delivers a feeling, a stance, or an in-group cue rather than a propositional claim. The doctrinal interest in memes is precisely that they bypass propositional argument and operate at the level of affect and identity.

Plausible deniability. A meme that spreads through a population cannot easily be attributed to its origin. Doctrinal frameworks recognize this as a feature rather than a problem: the influence operates without a return address.

These features make meme-form content particularly suited to the open use case of contemporary state-aesthetic operations: not the secret manipulation of a population that is unaware of being addressed, but the acknowledged shaping of an information environment in which audiences know that someone is shaping it and consume the content anyway, often appreciatively.

¶The cybernetic-postliberal reframing

Standard doctrinal discussion treats memetic warfare as a tool. Cybernetic postliberalism reframes it in three ways.

Memetic warfare is the doctrinal capture of a pre-existing operation. Long before the doctrine, the state-aesthetic continuum was producing recombinable, emotionally-loaded, low-cost content through commercial entertainment, advertising, and brand culture. Memetic warfare is the state’s open arrival inside a substrate the state had already been operating in by other means. The novelty is doctrinal rather than operational.

The audience is not principally a target. Doctrinal framing presents adversary populations as targets of memetic operations. In cybernetic-postliberal analysis, the more important audience is often the domestic one. Recruitment videos that adopt memetic registers (such as the Ghosts in the Machine series produced by the U.S. Army’s 4th Psychological Operations Group) are addressed to potential recruits and to the broader online culture that ironizes them, not principally to foreign adversaries. The audience is the audience that already lives inside the substrate.

Memetic warfare exposes the moral-physics of the substrate. The doctrinal discussion treats memes as a delivery vehicle for content. Cybernetic postliberalism notes that the meme-form itself enforces a moral physics: short duration, no narrative arc, consequence-free violence of statement (you can post and walk away), strategic infantilization of address, affective looping as the temporal structure of the feed. The form is the message, in the McLuhan sense; the content delivered through the form is shaped by the form’s moral physics regardless of what the content claims.

These three reframings allow the term to do useful analytical work inside the domain rather than merely importing a doctrinal label.

¶Relation to other terms

Memetic warfare is one register of the state-aesthetic continuum. It is the register where the state operates openly inside the platformal substrate using meme-form content. Other registers of the continuum (commercial entertainment, brand culture, soft-power broadcast) deliver similar content under different institutional cover.

It depends on platformal feedback architecture, which provides the substrate without which meme-form distribution would not function. Memes were possible before algorithmic feeds, but memetic warfare as a doctrinal category requires the algorithmic substrate.

It interacts with rubber-hose realism: memetic warfare’s preferred aesthetic forms (collage, glitch, retro-cartoon revival, ironic juxtaposition) are descendants of the moral-physics regime rubber-hose realism names. The doctrinal use of these forms is one of the most explicit cases of the regime’s migration from entertainment into state operations.

¶What the term does not name

Memetic warfare is not the same as propaganda, though doctrinal discussions sometimes elide the difference. Classical propaganda operates by broadcast from a centralized source toward audiences understood as passive recipients. Memetic warfare operates inside a platformal substrate that audiences are themselves operating, using forms audiences themselves are producing. The relation between operator and audience is structurally different.

It is also not the same as disinformation. Disinformation is content that is false. Memetic warfare is a form, not a truth-value: a memetic warfare campaign can deliver true content, false content, or content for which the truth-value is irrelevant (in-group cues, affective stances, identity markers). Treating memetic warfare as primarily about disinformation misses what makes it distinct from earlier influence operations.

The narrower claim of the term is that meme-form influence operations are now a recognized doctrinal capability area, that they ride on a substrate the state did not build but did not need to, and that the operations they conduct can be analyzed using the same vocabulary the rest of cybernetic postliberalism uses for adjacent regions of the state-aesthetic continuum.
