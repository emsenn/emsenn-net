Temporal Middle

2026-04-02 The position of inheriting, continuing, and transforming a tradition without either renewing it wholesale or abandoning it. Every living person occupies it.

The temporal middle is the position of inheriting, continuing, and transforming a tradition without either renewing it wholesale or betraying it. Every living person occupies this position. No one is purely “traditional” (frozen in the past) or purely “modern” (severed from the past). People inherit what they inherit, transform what they can, and continue.

The concept names what settler colonialism, genring, and binary nationalist frameworks systematically eliminate. The elimination operates by offering only two positions: the traditional subject (who matches the assigned genre — the Noble Indian, the Hearty Bavarian, the Mountain Kurd) and the assimilated subject (who has abandoned the genre and joined the dominant culture). There is no position for someone who is doing what every living culture does: inheriting, engaging, adapting, continuing.

A Lakota mathematician occupies the temporal middle: inheriting Lakota intellectual traditions, engaging with European mathematical traditions, continuing both. A Mohawk steelworker who carries a Haudenosaunee passport occupies it. A Kurdish filmmaker who makes Cannes-winning cinema in Kurmanji occupies it. But genre figures cannot accommodate these people because genre requires stasis — “unchanged since ancient times.” The genre offers the traditional Indian or the assimilated Indian, the mountain tribesman or the Persianized citizen. The middle position, where the living person actually stands, is the one the genre eliminates.

Patrick Wolfe (2006) established that settler colonialism is a structure, not an event — invasion continues as long as Indigenous peoples exist on land the settler state claims. The companion paper The American Genocide Against the Temporal Middle extends this argument: the elimination of the temporal middle operates at five scales simultaneously — physical (land seizure), legal (blood quantum), epistemic (boarding schools), linguistic (prefix capture), and architectural (platform visibility). Each scale reinforces the others.

Audra Simpson’s study of Kahnawake Mohawks (2014) documents the practice of the temporal middle through refusal — declining to perform the genre, carrying Haudenosaunee passports, asserting sovereignty by withdrawing from the recognition framework. Refusal is the temporal middle practiced in a world built to eliminate it.
