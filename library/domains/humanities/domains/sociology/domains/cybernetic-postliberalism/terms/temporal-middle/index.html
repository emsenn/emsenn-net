Temporal middle

2026-04-02 The position of inheriting, continuing, and transforming a tradition without either renewing it wholesale or abandoning it — occupied by every living person but systematically eliminated by settler colonialism, genring, and binary nationalist frameworks that offer only the 'traditional' or the 'assimilated' position.

The temporal middle is the position of inheriting, continuing, and transforming a tradition without either renewing it wholesale or abandoning it. Every living person occupies this position. No one is purely “traditional” (frozen in the past) or purely “modern” (severed from it). People inherit what they inherit, transform what they can, and continue. The framework names the position because the structures it analyzes systematically eliminate it.

¶Etymology and conceptual sources

The expression temporal middle foregrounds the temporal dimension that purity-binaries collapse. Tradition and modernity, as ordinarily deployed, are time-coded: tradition is the past inhabited in the present; modernity is the present that has separated from the past. The middle position — present that is continuing the past — is invisible within the binary, because the binary requires that one moment in time be cleanly distinguished from another.

The concept draws on three lineages. From settler-colonial studies, Patrick Wolfe (2006) and the field’s subsequent literature establish that settler structures require the binary in order to operate — the indigenous population must appear as either fixed-traditional (and so eliminable as residue) or assimilated (and so eliminable as continuance). From Indigenous critical thought, Audra Simpson (2014), Glen Coulthard (2014), and Gerald Vizenor (1999) develop the practice that inhabits the position the binary erases: refusal, survivance, the everyday continuance that settler structures cannot register. From the Caribbean tradition, Édouard Glissant (1997) develops relation and creolization as alternatives to the purity-binary, with the same effect of opening a position that traditional/modern cannot hold.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a position as occupying the temporal middle.

Inheritance without freeze. The subject inherits a tradition — language, practices, kinship, place-relation — without treating the inheritance as a fixed cast to be reproduced unchanged. The tradition is alive precisely because it is continuing, and continuing means it changes.

Engagement without abandonment. The subject engages with material from outside the tradition (other intellectual traditions, technologies, languages, formal systems) without treating that engagement as exit from the inherited tradition. The Lakota mathematician engages European mathematics; this does not make them any less Lakota.

Transformation as continuance. The subject transforms the tradition in the act of continuing it. There is no continuance that does not transform; there is no transformation that, if it remains within the tradition, is not also continuance. The two are not separable practices but two descriptions of the same act.

¶Examples

A Lakota mathematician. Inheriting Lakota intellectual traditions (epistemology, relational ontology, oral-narrative practices) and engaging with European mathematical traditions (algebraic structures, category theory, set theory). The mathematician occupies the temporal middle. The settler-genre offers only “traditional Indian” (whose intellectual life is what it was 200 years ago) or “assimilated Indian” (who has abandoned Lakota tradition for the dominant culture); neither position fits the actual person.

A Mohawk steelworker carrying a Haudenosaunee passport. Inheriting the long Haudenosaunee tradition of urban industrial work (the high-steel labor history is multi-generational), refusing to recognize the Canadian or U.S. state’s jurisdiction over the body, asserting Haudenosaunee sovereignty in the act of crossing borders. The position is the temporal middle. (Simpson 2014.)

A Kurdish filmmaker making Cannes-winning cinema in Kurmanji. Inheriting Kurdish narrative traditions and cinematic forms developed by earlier Kurdish filmmakers, engaging with contemporary international cinema’s vocabulary, continuing both. The genre offers “traditional mountain Kurd” (frozen in agrarian-pastoral time) or “Persianized/Turkified Kurd” (abandoned the tradition). The actual filmmaker is neither.

A second-generation immigrant who speaks two languages at home. The temporal middle on a smaller scale: inheriting the language and practices of an origin culture, engaging with the host culture’s resources, continuing both — without committing to the binary that would require choosing one as authentic and the other as inauthentic.

¶Adjacent concepts

The temporal middle is not the same as hybridity (Bhabha and the post-colonial tradition), though the two concepts are adjacent. Hybridity emphasizes the mixture of cultural elements; the temporal middle emphasizes the temporal continuity across the engagement. A subject inhabiting the temporal middle may or may not be hybrid in form; what they share is the refusal of the temporal binary.

The temporal middle is not the same as cosmopolitanism (Appiah and others). Cosmopolitanism is a normative-philosophical position about how to relate to multiple traditions; the temporal middle is a structural-positional analysis of where actually-existing subjects stand. A cosmopolitan may or may not occupy the temporal middle; the cosmopolitan posture sometimes performs the very binary the temporal middle refuses.

The temporal middle is not the same as assimilation in either its enthusiastic or its critical sense. Assimilation is the binary’s other half: the subject who has abandoned the inherited tradition for the dominant culture. The temporal middle is what neither traditional-fidelity nor assimilation describes.

¶Open questions

Is the temporal middle a position that all subjects occupy, or is it characteristic of subjects under certain structural pressures? The framework’s working position is that all living subjects occupy it, but that the elimination operates differently for differently-positioned subjects — the colonized, the indigenous, and the racialized face structural pressures that the dominant population does not face on the same terms.

What makes the elimination of the temporal middle differ across the registers in which it operates (legal, epistemic, linguistic, architectural, physical)? Each register has its own mechanisms; whether they amount to one operation or to several distinct operations that converge is open.

What practices sustain the temporal middle against the structural pressures that eliminate it? Simpson’s refusal, Vizenor’s survivance, and the Indigenous-resurgence tradition more broadly develop substantial answers; the framework draws on these without claiming to systematize them.
