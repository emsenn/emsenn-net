Fascist grammar

2026-03-03 The rhetorical sequence that fascist political speech follows regardless of its surface content: declare crisis, name a pure community, denounce enemies as sterile or corrupt, reframe suffering as sacrifice, promise destiny, assert inevitability. The concept distinguishes grammar from ideology — an ideology is a set of beliefs, but a grammar is a structure that generates statements regardless of their specific content. Fascist grammar can generate fascist politics, but it can also generate postliberal Catholic integralism, left-wing populism, or liberal crisis rhetoric. The grammar is the deep structure; the politics is the surface. Liberalism does not escape fascist grammar by opposing it — it inhabits the grammar in reverse, preserving the structure step-for-step while negating each move. Anarchist grammar names a different sequence entirely (condition, commons, relation, solidarity, desire, immanence) that does not preserve the same structure.

Fascist grammar is the rhetorical sequence that fascist political speech follows regardless of its surface content. The concept distinguishes grammar from ideology: an ideology is a set of beliefs, but a grammar is a structure that generates statements regardless of their specific content. Fascist grammar can generate fascist politics, but it can also generate postliberal Catholic integralism, left-wing populism, or liberal crisis rhetoric. The grammar is the deep structure. The politics is the surface.

In July 1932, Joseph Goebbels told a crowd of Germans that their nation was collapsing, that the Weimar system was in ruins, that parasites and traitors had corrupted the community, that years of humiliation would be redeemed through struggle, that the German people’s destiny was assured, and that the storm would come and sweep away the old order.

In September 2025, Stephen Miller stood before tens of thousands in Arizona and declared that enemies had tried to kill Charlie Kirk and made him immortal, that “we are the ones who build” while the enemies “create nothing,” that the movement’s lineage stretched from Athens to Rome to Monticello, that “the light will defeat the dark,” and that “we are the storm.”

The vocabulary is different. The grammar is the same.

¶Etymology and lineage

The compound fascist grammar foregrounds the structural-linguistic level at which the operation runs. Where conventional fascism studies tend to focus on ideology (the content of fascist beliefs) or political movement (the institutional history of fascist organizations), fascist grammar identifies the recurrent rhetorical sequence that survives across ideological and movement-level differences. The methodological precedent is Victor Klemperer’s LTI (1957), a philologist’s diary-analysis of how Nazi German worked as a language form rather than a doctrine; the cybernetic-postliberal extension treats fascist speech under the same analytical apparatus across periods, including periods after 1945 when the explicit fascist content has been laundered out of the surface.

The conceptual lineage runs through several traditions. From Roger Griffin (1991), fascism as palingenetic ultranationalism — the mythic-rebirth narrative that requires its specific cast of authentic folk figures and its specific arc from decline to restoration. From Carl Schmitt (1932), the friend-enemy distinction as the fundamental category of politics — the load-bearing concept fascist grammar reproduces in step three (denounce enemies as sterile or corrupt). From Oswald Spengler (1922), civilizational decline as the structural temporal frame. From Robert Paxton (2004), the stages of fascist development, which let the grammar be tracked across the lifecycle of the formation. From Hannah Arendt (1951) and Walter Benjamin (1940), the analytical resources for reading liberalism as fascist grammar’s dialectical twin rather than its opposite.

Fascist grammar is closely connected to other operations in cybernetic postliberalism. Genring produces the genre figures (the Volk, the True American, the Authentic Catholic) the grammar requires; genre coherence is the structural pressure that maintains those figures against the centrifugal forces of actual living variety. The grammar is the rhetorical structure within which genre coherence’s exclusions are legitimated.

¶The six steps

  1. Declare crisis. Assert that the present moment is one of existential threat. Not merely imperfect — fundamentally broken.
  2. Name a pure community. Identify the group whose survival is at stake: the Volk, “the West,” the working class, the faithful, the True People.
  3. Denounce enemies as sterile or corrupt. Characterize opponents as agents of decay. The enemy creates nothing, produces nothing, builds nothing. They are parasites on the community’s generativity.
  4. Reframe suffering as sacrifice. Convert the community’s pain into evidence of its moral standing. Humiliation becomes proof of chosen status. Martyrdom becomes political sacrament.
  5. Promise destiny. Assert that the community’s future greatness is assured if it acts now. History, God, or nature guarantees restoration.
  6. Assert inevitability. Collapse the distinction between what should happen and what will happen. The storm is coming. Or: we are the storm.

The steps need not appear in this order, and not all six need be present in every instance. The grammar is recognized by its pattern, not by a checklist.

¶Strategic forgetting

The grammar survived 1945 through strategic forgetting: the laundering of fascist categories into post-war conservative discourse, stripped of their explicit associations while retaining their structural function. The process was systematic. Spengler’s civilizational cycles reemerged as Cold War declinism — Allan Bloom’s Closing of the American Mind (1987) reprised Spengler’s fears in academic idiom. Schmitt’s friend/enemy distinction returned as Reagan’s “Evil Empire” moral clarity. Arthur Moeller van den Bruck’s yearning for unity beyond parliamentary division reappeared as “Western heritage” education and Samuel Huntington’s Clash of Civilizations (1996). Ernst Jünger’s exaltation of the productive Worker resurfaced as Reaganomics’ “makers versus takers.”

The Volk became “Western civilization.” Humiliation became martyrdom. Schmitt’s friend/enemy was baptized into evangelical moral dualism. The vocabulary changed. The grammar held.

This laundering prepared the ground for the postliberal authors (Patrick Deneen, Adrian Vermeule, Sohrab Ahmari) who argue that liberal neutrality must be replaced by civilizational inheritance. They rarely cite Schmitt or Goebbels. But their categories — civilization over nation, moral truth over procedure, sacrifice over compromise — belong to the same family tree.

¶Liberalism as dialectical twin

Liberalism does not escape fascist grammar by opposing it. Liberalism inhabits fascist grammar in reverse:

  • Fascism declares crisis; liberalism promises progressive stability (Dewey’s managerial optimism).
  • Fascism exalts organic community; liberalism offers universal citizenship (Kelsen’s procedural Rechtsstaat).
  • Fascism names enemies; liberalism answers with toleration (Popper’s Open Society and Its Enemies).
  • Fascism glorifies sacrifice; liberalism valorizes compromise (Rawls’s veil of ignorance).
  • Fascism promises destiny; liberalism replies with progress (Rawls’s incremental justice).
  • Fascism asserts inevitability; liberalism defends procedure (Berlin’s value pluralism).

Walter Benjamin (1940) targeted exactly this: the faith that history already leans toward justice is a mirror image of fascism’s fatalism. Arendt (1951) showed how liberal proceduralism generates the isolation that fascism exploits. Adorno and Horkheimer saw how liberal optimism, by aestheticizing progress, prepared subjects for authoritarian aesthetics. Liberalism is not fascism’s opposite — it is its dialectical twin, preserving the grammar by countering it step-for-step, remaining bound to the structure it seeks to negate.

¶Anarchist grammar as alternative

Fascist grammar can be contrasted with anarchist grammar, which follows a different sequence entirely (condition, commons, relation, solidarity, desire, immanence) rather than reproducing fascism’s structure in inverted form. Where fascist grammar moves from crisis to inevitability, anarchist grammar moves from present conditions to present possibility:

  • Condition replaces crisis: catastrophe is not an event demanding salvation but the ongoing condition of living under capitalism, empire, and the state.
  • Commons replaces pure community: shared, negotiated relations rather than purified identity.
  • Relation replaces enemy: domination is relational, not metaphysical — relations can shift, solidarities can emerge.
  • Solidarity replaces sacrifice: the refusal to abandon one another, not the glorification of suffering.
  • Desire replaces destiny: aspiration in the present, not fantasy projected onto history.
  • Immanence replaces inevitability: nothing is guaranteed, everything depends on what is done here and now.

Fascism glorifies builders but builds nothing. It feeds parasitically on the commons, on solidarity and care, on the practices it denounces as weakness. Anarchist grammar actually produces futures. The storm survives only by consuming the worlds that anarchism generates.

¶Adjacent concepts

Fascist grammar is not the same as fascist ideology in the conventional history-of-ideas sense. Ideologies are the contents of belief — racial doctrine, national mission, integralist program. Grammars are the structural patterns that generate statements regardless of their belief-content. The same grammar can express different ideologies; the same ideology can be expressed through different grammars.

Fascist grammar is not the same as authoritarian rhetoric in the political-science sense. Authoritarian rhetoric names a broader category that includes any speech-form sustaining authoritarian governance; fascist grammar is the more specific structural pattern that generates fascist-form statements. Some authoritarian rhetoric is fascist-grammatical; some is not.

Fascist grammar is not the same as propaganda technique in the communication-studies sense. Propaganda technique catalogs specific persuasive moves (testimonial, bandwagon, plain folks); fascist grammar is the deep-syntactic structure that organizes whole speeches and movements regardless of which surface techniques are deployed. The two operate at different levels.

¶Open questions

Can fascist grammar be inhabited without producing fascist political effects? The dialectical-twin analysis suggests that liberal anti-fascism, by inhabiting the grammar in reverse, sustains the structure even while opposing fascist content. Whether any anti-fascist political form can avoid this — and what such a form would look like — is the question anarchist grammar attempts to answer at the structural level.

How does fascist grammar interact with platformal feedback architecture? The platformal apparatus rewards engagement, and fascist grammar’s six-step sequence is engineered for engagement (crisis, threat, enemy, sacrifice, destiny, inevitability are each strong engagement-producers). The platform’s metabolism may amplify fascist grammar more efficiently than other grammars; this is empirical and contested.

What practices counter fascist grammar without simply substituting another grammar that the apparatus can metabolize? Anarchist grammar is one candidate; whether it can sustain itself under platformal conditions, or whether it gets metabolized as a different content within the same operational closure, is the question the school holds open.
