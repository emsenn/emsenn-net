Anarchist grammar

2026-04-27 The rhetorical sequence of anarchist political speech: condition, commons, relation, solidarity, desire, immanence — the structural alternative to fascist grammar.

Anarchist grammar is the rhetorical sequence that anarchist political speech follows regardless of its surface content — a six-step structure that mirror-inverts the corresponding steps of fascist grammar. Where fascist grammar declares crisis, names a pure community, denounces enemies, reframes suffering as sacrifice, promises destiny, and asserts inevitability, anarchist grammar refuses each move with a structurally specific countermove. The framework’s analysis is that the inversion does not constitute an exit from the operational closure within which fascist grammar operates; anarchist grammar inhabits the same metabolism as another available genre.

¶The mirror inversion

The six steps of fascist grammar (declare crisis, name pure community, denounce enemies as sterile or corrupt, reframe suffering as sacrifice, promise destiny, assert inevitability) are inverted by anarchist grammar in structurally specific ways:

  1. Refuse crisis as urgency. Where fascist grammar declares crisis to authorize emergency measures, anarchist grammar refuses the genre of crisis-urgency, insisting that the conditions named as crisis are continuous structural features that emergency measures do not address. The crisis has always been here; nothing about its current visibility licenses concentrated action.

  2. Refuse pure community as identity. Where fascist grammar names a pure community whose survival is at stake, anarchist grammar insists on multiplicity — the irreducible plurality of communities, the heteroglossia of voices, the refusal of any single identity-claim to organize political action.

  3. Refuse the enemy as a population. Where fascist grammar identifies enemies as agents of decay and locates them in a particular population, anarchist grammar locates the antagonist in the system — capital, state, hierarchy, settler-formation — rather than in any group of people. Enemies, when named at all, are structural rather than personal.

  4. Refuse suffering as sacrifice. Where fascist grammar reframes the community’s pain as evidence of its moral standing and sanctifies its martyrs, anarchist grammar refuses the sacrificial calculus: suffering is not redemptive, martyrdom is not currency, and the political work is not legitimated by anyone’s pain.

  5. Refuse destiny as inevitability. Where fascist grammar promises destiny and asserts that history’s arc guarantees the community’s restoration, anarchist grammar refuses destiny — there is no preordained arc, only the contingent practice of acting otherwise.

  6. Refuse inevitability as agency. Where fascist grammar asserts that “we are the storm” — that the community’s action is itself the inevitable arc made visible — anarchist grammar refuses the conflation of action with inevitability. Action is what does not arrive on its own; the storm metaphor is precisely what anarchism rejects.

¶Etymology and lineage

The construction anarchist grammar parallels the corpus’s fascist grammar and inherits the analytical move: a grammar is a deep structural pattern that generates statements regardless of their surface content. Anarchist grammar names what anarchist political speech sounds like at the structural level, abstracted from particular vocabularies, periods, and tendencies.

The lineage is the long anarchist tradition. Peter Kropotkin’s Mutual Aid (1902) supplies the anti-Hobbesian foundation: cooperation rather than competition is what species do, and the move that refuses-the-enemy-as-population has roots in Kropotkin’s evolutionary anti-pessimism. Murray Bookchin’s libertarian-municipalist writings articulate the grammar in its most systematic mid-twentieth-century form. David Graeber’s “The New Anarchists” (2002) names the contemporary register; Cindy Milstein’s Anarchism and Its Aspirations (2010) articulates the prefigurative-political register; the CrimethInc. collective’s From Democracy to Freedom (2017) is a recent platform-era exemplar.

Saul Newman’s post-anarchist work (2005) provides the theoretical resources for understanding the limits of pure-negation as a political mode — the post-anarchist insight that anti-state politics organized only through negation tends to reproduce the very structures it negates. The framework’s claim that anarchist grammar is itself a genre within the same metabolism as fascist grammar is structurally adjacent to the post-anarchist critique.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a piece of political speech as performing anarchist grammar.

Mirror-structural inversion. The speech inverts the corresponding fascist-grammatical move at each of the six steps. The inversion is not arbitrary; it is structurally specific — each anarchist-grammatical move is the negation of the corresponding fascist-grammatical move along the dimension that fascist grammar makes operative.

Negation as the principal mode. Anarchist grammar tends to operate through negation rather than positive construction. Where fascist grammar asserts (we are the community, they are the enemy, the storm is coming), anarchist grammar refuses (we are not a single community, there is no enemy-as-population, no storm is coming on its own). The negation-mode is structurally consequential.

Performance is genre-shaped. Anarchist grammar produces a recognizable genre with its own affective register (refusal, irony, dark humor, prefigurative care) and its own subject-positions (the comrade, the affinity-group, the autonomous individual, the prefigurative practitioner). The genre is reproducible and recognizable across different historical conditions and political movements.

¶Examples

The classical-period exemplars. Bakunin’s letters, Kropotkin’s pamphlets, Emma Goldman’s speeches, Errico Malatesta’s writings — each operates the grammar with different vocabulary but the same structural inversion of the fascist-grammatical moves emerging in their respective periods.

The 1968 generation. May 1968 in France, the Italian operaismo movement, the Provos, the Situationist International — each produced anarchist-grammatical formations responding to the post-war order’s fascist-grammatical registers (state nationalism, Cold War destiny, sacrifice for industrial-development).

The post-Seattle generation. The 1999 anti-WTO protests, the 2008–2011 anti-austerity movements, the Occupy formation, contemporary mutual-aid networks — each produces anarchist-grammatical content responding to neoliberal-era fascist-grammatical registers (security-state declension, market-civilizational destiny, austerity as sacrifice).

Contemporary platform-era expressions. Anarchist-grammatical content on Twitter, in the abolitionist-organizing register, in solarpunk fiction, in CrimethInc. publications — the grammar continues to operate at platform velocity, producing the same six-step inversions in compressed forms suited to platform feeds.

¶Adjacent concepts

Anarchist grammar is not the same as anarchism as a political tradition. Anarchism is a political tradition with theoretical, organizational, and tactical dimensions. Anarchist grammar is the rhetorical-structural register that anarchist speech tends to exhibit — a feature of how anarchism is spoken rather than what anarchism is.

Anarchist grammar is not the same as liberal critique of fascism, though they share certain content. Liberal critique typically operates through procedural counter-claims (toleration against enmity, due process against extra-legal action, deliberation against destiny). Anarchist grammar’s countermoves are structural-systemic (refuse the system that produces enemies, refuse the order that requires deliberation) rather than procedural.

Anarchist grammar is not the same as left-populist grammar. Left-populist political speech follows a different structure (a popular community against an oligarchic enemy, a redistributive destiny, a programmatic inevitability) that resembles fascist grammar more than anarchist grammar in its formal features. The framework treats anarchist grammar as structurally distinct from left-populist grammar despite their shared political tradition.

¶Open questions

Does the framework’s claim that anarchist grammar inhabits the same metabolism as fascist grammar undermine the political force of anarchist refusal? The framework’s analysis is descriptive: anarchist grammar operates within the operational closure that fascist grammar also operates within. Whether the description forecloses a politics or merely names what such a politics has to reckon with is contested within the school.

Are there genuinely non-grammatical political modes — modes of action that do not produce a recognizable rhetorical-structural pattern? The framework’s working position is that recognizable speech tends to operate through grammar, but practices that proceed through doing-rather-than-speaking (the unannounced act, the care that does not wait for permission) may operate at a different layer. The relation of practice to grammar is open.

Can anarchist grammar be re-tooled in conditions where fascist grammar is not the principal antagonist? Most articulations of anarchist grammar develop in opposition to a particular fascist-grammatical formation. Whether the structural moves can be detached from that opposition — adapted to other antagonists, or to the absence of an antagonist altogether — is open.
