Harm governance is the institutional-affective regime in which care has been collapsed into risk management. Political action must be administered through proper procedural channels in order to count as legitimate, and people who have lost faith in the state reproduce its administrative logic with each other. The signature feature is an asymmetry: individual direct action registers as inherently risky because it lacks institutional validation, while mass action registers as safe because institutional form makes it feel legitimate. The framework cannot recognize that institutional form itself introduces risks — of co-optation, of slowness, of dependence on the structures being challenged.

Care that does not wait for consensus is what harm governance captures and reformats.

¶Etymology and lineage

The compound harm governance foregrounds the operation: the governance is performed through the management of harm, with the management itself becoming the legitimate political activity. Where conventional political analysis would treat harm as the object of political response, harm governance treats harm as the occasion for procedural management — and the procedural management as the substance of legitimate action. The shift parallels what Foucault diagnosed as the broader move of late-modern governance from prohibition to administration, but specifies a particular contemporary configuration in which the administrative apparatus has migrated beyond the state into the everyday relations between subjects.

The lineage runs through several traditions. From Foucauldian governmentality (1978), the structural account of how subjects internalize the categories of state administration so thoroughly that external enforcement is unnecessary; the well-governed subject is one who monitors, assesses, and corrects without being told to. From Povinelli (2011), the analysis of late-liberal tolerance: harm is not denied but absorbed into a calculus of constraint, with the relevant question shifted from “what happened?” to “could the person who caused it reasonably have done otherwise?” Once behavior can be framed as non-choice (economically rational, emotionally rational, structurally constrained), harm is reclassified from injustice to unfortunate outcome. From the radical-organizing literature (INCITE! 2007, Spade 2020), the concrete institutional pattern: nonprofit-administrative logics capturing mutual-aid practices, grant-metric requirements reshaping organizing, intake forms appearing in care networks. From feminist-queer affect theory (Ahmed 2010, Brown 1995), the analysis of how the politicization of injury can itself become a governance technology.

Harm governance is where these operations meet: Foucault’s self-governing subject encounters Povinelli’s tolerance regime, and the result is a population that administers its own political life through the same risk frameworks the state uses — even after the state has lost credibility. People do not stop being governed when they stop trusting the government. They become the government of each other.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a regime as harm governance.

Care collapsed into risk management. To care for someone becomes synonymous with managing the risks they face or pose. Management is only legitimate when it resembles institutional or state practice — when it has protocols, oversight, measurable outcomes. Individual care that falls outside these frameworks is treated as naive at best, dangerous at worst. The structural inversion is that care, in the sense that matters — feeding people, masking in a pandemic, acting without waiting to be told — is precisely what harm governance does not recognize as care.

Asymmetric legitimation of individual vs. institutional action. Individual action registers as inherently risky because it lacks validation; mass action registers as safe because institutional form makes it feel legitimate. The framework cannot see that institutional form itself introduces risks. A general strike with no support infrastructure registers as legitimate because of its scale; a small direct-action that addresses immediate need registers as suspect because of its individuality. The asymmetry is not analytic — it is the regime’s structural signature.

Migration of administrative logic into post-trust spaces. When subjects lose faith in the state, the administrative logic does not disappear; it migrates. People who no longer trust the state begin to reproduce its role in how they interact with each other. They warn, contextualize, preempt, and correct — not always because they want to, but because they have been trained to understand responsibility as managing the behavior of others. Community organizations adopt grant metrics. Mutual aid groups develop intake forms. Care networks build hierarchies. The administrative apparatus of the state migrates into the spaces that were supposed to be its alternatives.

¶Examples

The masking discourse, late COVID period. When someone says “you can evaluate a person’s political consistency by whether they’re still masking,” the pushback does not contest the claim that abandoning masking caused real harm. It does not deny that immunocompromised and disabled people have been excluded. What it does is reframe the conversation: away from harm and toward whether the judgment of the unmasked has been ethically justified. Povinelli’s late liberalism operates at the level of everyday discourse. Harm is not denied but tolerated — absorbed into a framework where the relevant question is not whether people were hurt but whether the person who stopped masking can be understood as having acted within rational constraints (economic pressure, information fatigue, social cost). Once behavior is framed as constrained, the harm is reclassified from injustice to unfortunate outcome.

The supervision response to direct action. Someone shares a small act of direct action online — stealing banner materials, organizing unpermitted aid, refusing to comply with public-health abandonment. The immediate response is not about the target of the action or its consequences. It is about the terms under which it was shared: was it safe to post, will someone misinterpret it, did the actor properly disclose the risk. The pushback is not strategic critique from within an organizing context. It is supervision — the internalized logic of an administrative state, reproduced between people who no longer trust the state itself.

The mutual-aid-network-with-intake-form. A group forms around getting food to neighbors during a crisis. Within months, the group has an intake form, an eligibility framework, a confidentiality policy, and a board structure. The administrative apparatus of the state has migrated into the practice. The food still gets delivered, but the form of life within which it gets delivered has been calibrated to administrative legibility — and the people whose practice of care had previously been faster and less mediated find themselves filling out forms.

The contrast case: care that does not wait for consensus. Direct mask-distribution to immunocompromised neighbors without an organizational sponsor. Food brought to an encampment without intake. Standing with someone in a difficult moment without a hotline-protocol. These practices, structurally adjacent to what Audra Simpson calls refusal, register within harm governance as suspicious because they lack the institutional form harm governance requires for legitimacy. They are care, in the sense that matters; they are not what harm governance recognizes as care.

¶Adjacent concepts

Harm governance is not the same as harm reduction in the public-health-policy sense. Harm reduction is a set of practical-political strategies developed in drug-policy and sex-work-policy contexts to reduce the harms associated with criminalization. The contemporary harm-reduction tradition has its own internal critiques of harm governance; the linguistic similarity is misleading. Harm reduction is one of the practices harm governance can capture, but it is not equivalent to harm governance.

Harm governance is not the same as risk management in the institutional sense. Risk management is a specific organizational practice; harm governance is the regime that has extended risk-management logic into care, organizing, and personal-political life beyond the institutional contexts where it originated. The two operate at different scales and different registers.

Harm governance is not the same as carceral logic in the abolitionist sense, though they are structurally adjacent. Carceral logic names the broad pattern by which punishment, surveillance, and containment become the dominant modes of response to social harm. Harm governance is one specific configuration in which carceral logic operates affectively and procedurally, including in ostensibly anti-carceral spaces.

Harm governance is not the same as emotional regulation as a personal practice. Emotional regulation is a personal-affective skill; harm governance is a structural-political regime. The two can interact (subjects who have internalized harm governance may experience their internalization as personal emotional regulation) but they are distinct.

¶Open questions

What practices exit harm governance without simply moving to a different metabolizable subject-position? Refusal of harm governance often re-stabilizes as the genre of the unaccountable actor or the irresponsible individualist. Whether any practice exits harm governance structurally — rather than producing a new genre within it — is the question the school’s closure analysis holds open.

How does harm governance interact with structural conditions that genuinely require coordination? Some material situations (pandemics, infrastructure systems, climate response) genuinely benefit from large-scale coordinated action that necessarily has procedural form. Distinguishing the legitimate-coordination case from the harm-governance case is empirical and contested; the school’s analytical apparatus does not provide a clean criterion.

What are the conditions under which post-trust spaces avoid the migration? Some communities (long-standing kinship networks, ceremonial-ritual collectives, certain religious communities) appear to avoid harm governance’s migration even after they have lost trust in state institutions. Whether their resistance is conditional on specific historical-cultural conditions or transferable to broader settings is open.
