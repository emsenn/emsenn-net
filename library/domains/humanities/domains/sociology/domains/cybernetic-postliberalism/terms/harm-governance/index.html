Harm governance

2026-03-03 The logic by which care is collapsed into risk management — political action must be administered through proper channels in order to count as legitimate, and people who have lost faith in the state reproduce its administrative logic with each other.

Harm governance

Someone shares a small act of direct action online — stealing banner materials, organizing unpermitted aid, refusing to comply with public health abandonment. The immediate response is not about the target of the action or its consequences. It is about the terms under which it was shared. Was it safe to post? Will someone misinterpret it? Did the actor properly disclose the risk? The pushback is not strategic critique from within an organizing context. It is supervision — the internalized logic of an administrative state, reproduced between people who no longer trust the state itself.

At the same time, calls emerge for a general strike — an action historically known to trigger state repression, with no infrastructure in place to support it. The same people who warned against the small direct action now defend the strike on the grounds that its scale makes it safe. Enough people doing it together will make it legitimate.

This asymmetry is not arbitrary. It is the signature of harm governance: the logic that requires political action to be managed — administered through risk frameworks — in order to count as legitimate (emsenn, “Between care and control,” 2025).

The theoretical architecture

Michel Foucault showed that modern power does not govern primarily through prohibition. It governs through the production of self-governing subjects — through what he called governmentality. The state does not simply tell people what they cannot do; it shapes how they understand responsibility, risk, and legitimate conduct. The well-governed subject is the one who has internalized the state’s categories so thoroughly that external enforcement becomes unnecessary. The subject monitors, assesses, and corrects — not because they are ordered to, but because they have come to understand this as what responsibility means.

Elizabeth Povinelli, working from within Indigenous Australian contexts, describes the governance regime that follows: late liberalism does not deny harm. It tolerates harm, as long as the actor can be shown to have been constrained. If a choice can be framed as non-choice — if behavior can be understood as economically or emotionally rational — then harm becomes acceptable. The question shifts from “what happened?” to “could the person who caused it have reasonably done otherwise?” Harm is absorbed into a calculus of constraint.

Harm governance is where these two operations meet. Foucault’s self-governing subject encounters Povinelli’s tolerance regime, and the result is a population that administers its own political life through the same risk frameworks the state uses — even after the state has lost credibility. People do not stop being governed when they stop trusting the government. They become the government of each other.

Care collapsed into risk management

The central distortion is the equation of care with risk management. To care for someone becomes synonymous with managing the risks they face or pose. And management is only seen as legitimate when it resembles institutional or state practice — when it has protocols, oversight, measurable outcomes. Individual care that falls outside these frameworks is treated as naive at best, dangerous at worst.

This produces a specific inversion: individual action is seen as inherently risky because it lacks validation, while mass action is seen as safe because institutional form makes it feel legitimate. The framework cannot recognize that institutional form itself introduces risks — of co-optation, of slowness, of dependence on the structures being challenged.

What makes this dynamic durable is what happens when people lose faith in the state. The administrative logic does not disappear. It migrates. People who no longer trust the state begin to reproduce its role in how they interact with each other. They become administrators of the discourse. They warn, contextualize, preempt, and correct — not always because they want to, but because they have been trained to understand responsibility as managing the behavior of others.

Community organizations adopt grant metrics. Mutual aid groups develop intake forms. Care networks build hierarchies. The administrative apparatus of the state migrates into the spaces that were supposed to be its alternatives — not because anyone chose this, but because harm governance has become the only available grammar for legitimating action.

The masking case study

The COVID-era masking debate is a concentrated example. When someone says “you can evaluate a person’s political consistency by whether they’re still masking,” the pushback does not contest the claim that abandoning masking caused real harm. It does not deny that immunocompromised and disabled people have been excluded. What it does is reframe the conversation: away from harm and toward whether the judgment of the unmasked has been ethically justified.

This is Povinelli’s late liberalism operating at the level of everyday discourse. Harm is not denied but tolerated — absorbed into a framework where the relevant question is not whether people were hurt but whether the person who stopped masking can be understood as having acted within rational constraints. Economic pressure, information fatigue, social cost — any of these suffice to render the decision legible as non-choice. Once behavior is framed as constrained, the harm it produces is reclassified from injustice to unfortunate outcome.

The conversation shifts from epidemiology and solidarity to the governance of judgment itself. Not: “what does continued masking accomplish?” but “who are you to evaluate someone else’s behavior?” The political claim — that abandoning collective protection while a pandemic continues is a form of harm — is neutralized by routing it through administrative questions about who has standing to make such claims and under what conditions.

Care is not governance

Care does not wait for consensus. Care does not require everyone to understand. Care is not afraid of smallness, or inconsistency, or discomfort. Care is practice.

The people who continue to mask are not morally pure. They are acting in alignment with an analysis they already held. They did not stop when the mandates ended because they were never masking out of obedience. They were masking because they understood the system had already abandoned them — and others. And they understood that action did not require consensus to be valid.

The people still stealing supplies, organizing unapproved aid, refusing to perform normalcy — they are not reckless. They are no longer oriented around permission.

What harm governance reveals is how many others still are. People want mass action because it feels legitimate. They reject individual initiative because it feels ungoverned. They describe both impulses as “care,” but the framework is governance all the way down.

Care, in the sense that matters — the sense that feeds people, that masks in a pandemic, that acts without waiting to be told — is not governance. It is the thing governance captures and reformats. Recognizing the difference is the first condition of doing anything useful.
