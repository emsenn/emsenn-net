Liberalism legitimacy crisis

2026-04-27 When liberalism's contradictions become experientially insoluble, the system shifts from promising resolution to promising regulation of the symptoms.

The Liberalism Legitimacy Crisis is the named structural moment in which liberalism’s constitutive contradictions become experientially insoluble for its subjects rather than locally manageable, and the system shifts from promising resolution of those contradictions to promising regulation of the symptoms. The system loses legitimacy as an ideological system in the specific sense that it can no longer produce the resolutions it has historically promised; what it can still produce is administrative management.

The formation is structural rather than episodic — it is the condition the system enters, not a particular failure-event — and naming it explicitly is the first move of any analysis that does not take its products as anomalies.

¶Lineage

The closest theoretical antecedent is Jürgen Habermas’s Legitimation Crisis (1973), which gave a formal account of how late-capitalist polities encounter legitimacy crises arising from the unmet imperatives of their economic and political subsystems. Habermas’s account is structural — legitimacy crises are not produced by particular policy failures but by the inability of the system to fulfill the conditions of its own reproduction — and the framework’s use of Liberalism Legitimacy Crisis draws on this analytical heritage.

Karl Polanyi’s The Great Transformation (1944) supplies the long-arc historical precedent: liberalism has cycled through prior crises in which its constitutive contradictions became visible (the late-nineteenth-century self-protection of society, the inter-war breakdown of the gold standard and parliamentary liberalism, the post-1945 reconstruction). Each prior crisis has been managed by reconfiguring the operating-mode of liberalism rather than by abandoning the structure; the contemporary form is the latest cycle.

Foucault’s Birth of Biopolitics lectures (1979) trace liberalism’s shift from promising resolutions to administering populations. The shift is what the system shifts from promising resolution to promising regulation names in the term’s definition; Foucault’s lectures provide the analytical resources for understanding how the shift happens institutionally and what it does to subject-formation.

Wendy Brown’s In the Ruins of Neoliberalism (2019) gives the contemporary account: neoliberalism’s specific form (market-rationality applied to all spheres, the displacement of the political by the economic, the dismantling of public provision) has produced a particular legitimacy crisis whose response has been not the restoration of liberal institutions but the rise of antidemocratic politics. Mark Fisher’s Capitalist Realism (2009) supplies the affective register: the crisis is one in which alternatives have become unimaginable, and the unimaginability itself is part of how the crisis sustains itself. Wolfgang Streeck’s Buying Time (2014) provides the structural-historical reading of the post-1970s crisis-management: how successive financial-political instruments (inflation, debt, austerity) have postponed the resolution.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a moment as a Liberalism Legitimacy Crisis.

Constitutive contradictions become experientially insoluble. The contradictions internal to liberalism (universalism without individualism; individualism without universalism; progress without exhaustion; equality without sovereignty) have always been in tension. Under non-crisis conditions, the tensions are routed into negotiation, deferral, or local resolution. Under the crisis condition, the routings stop converging and the tensions become available to the system’s subjects as continuous experience rather than as occasional anomalies.

Resolution-promising shifts to regulation-promising. The system stops promising that the contradictions can be resolved (full employment will be achieved, inequality will be addressed, prosperity will be restored, public health will be assured) and shifts to promising that their symptoms can be regulated (employment volatility will be smoothed, inequality will be measured and tracked, growth will be optimized, public-health risks will be managed). The promise itself has changed.

Legitimacy itself becomes the manageable variable. The system’s response to its own legitimacy crisis is to manage perceptions of legitimacy — through communication strategies, expert-managed framing, platform-mediated narrative-management — rather than to address the conditions producing the crisis. Legitimacy thus becomes one more thing the regulatory apparatus regulates.

¶Examples

The 1970s rupture and its aftermath. The end of post-war Bretton Woods, the 1973 oil shocks, and the breakdown of the welfare-state consensus mark the conventional historical onset of the contemporary legitimacy crisis. Streeck (2014) traces how the response — inflation, then debt, then austerity — was structured as a series of crisis-management instruments rather than a resolution of the underlying contradictions. The crisis has been continuous since the 1970s rather than discrete events.

The 2008 financial collapse and its non-resolution. The 2008 crisis exposed the structural unsustainability of the post-1970s management regime. The institutional response (bank rescue, sovereign debt management, quantitative easing, austerity for the public, return to financial-sector profitability) addressed symptoms without resolving contradictions. The post-2008 decade has been characterized by the continuation of the crisis condition under successive management instruments.

The post-2016 political reorganization. The widespread emergence of nationalist, far-right, and crisis-populist political formations across liberal polities is, in Brown’s reading, the response to the now-decades-long legitimacy crisis: the system’s inability to deliver liberal resolutions has produced political formations that abandon the liberal framework rather than seek to repair it. The framework reads these formations as effects of the legitimacy crisis rather than as its causes.

¶Adjacent concepts

The Liberalism Legitimacy Crisis is not the same as a state legitimacy crisis in the narrower sense. State legitimacy crises (popular delegitimation of a particular government, regime, or constitution) are episodic and can be resolved by institutional change. The Liberalism Legitimacy Crisis is structural — it concerns the operating mode of liberalism itself rather than any particular state’s standing.

The Liberalism Legitimacy Crisis is not the same as regime collapse. Regime collapse is the failure of a political-institutional order; the Liberalism Legitimacy Crisis is the condition under which the liberal organizing logic of regimes has become unable to deliver its resolutions, but the institutional regimes typically continue to operate, with different content, under that condition.

The Liberalism Legitimacy Crisis is not the same as legitimacy crisis in Habermas’s full theoretical sense. Habermas’s legitimacy crisis is broader — it covers any late-capitalist legitimacy formation. The framework’s term specifies the version that pertains to liberalism’s particular constitutive contradictions and to the contemporary period; it inherits Habermas’s analytical structure but limits the empirical scope.

¶Open questions

Is the contemporary Liberalism Legitimacy Crisis terminal, or another cycle in liberalism’s long-arc reconfiguration? Polanyi’s history suggests liberalism has reconfigured through prior crises. Streeck and Brown are skeptical that the contemporary form admits a similar reconfiguration; the framework treats the question as substantively open.

What is the relationship between the Liberalism Legitimacy Crisis and the underlying material conditions (energetic substrate, ecological limits) that the framework presupposes? The framework’s working position is that the legitimacy crisis is intensified by — though not reducible to — the material conditions; how exactly the two interact is open.

How does the crisis interact with the post-2016 political formations that have arisen during it? The framework reads those formations as effects of the crisis rather than as its causes; whether the framework’s reading has the explanatory weight it claims, against alternatives that treat the political formations as autonomous developments, is contested.
