Industrial intellectualism

2026-03-03 The structural condition in which intellectual labor is organized as a production process — thought converted into narrative products, evaluated by genre satisfaction rather than material correspondence.

Industrial intellectualism

A scholar writes about an abolitionist movement. The movement failed — the facility it opposed was built and is operating. But the paper succeeds. It moves through the correct arc: death-world (thesis), life-worlding (antithesis), building-and-fighting (synthesis). The citations confirm genre membership. The analysis produces the satisfaction the genre demands. The paper circulates, gets cited, gets taught. The facility continues operating. The paper does not need the facility to have been stopped. It needs the narrative to resolve correctly.

Industrial intellectualism is the structural condition in which intellectual labor is organized as a production process. Thought is converted into narrative products — threads, essays, papers, podcasts, newsletters — packaged for recognizability within a genre and evaluated by whether the analysis feels right, not by whether it corresponds to material outcomes. The term names not a type of person or a moral failing but a condition: the condition in which intellectual work, regardless of the worker’s intentions, is formatted by its market of circulation.

From feeling rules to intellectual production

The concept begins with Arlie Russell Hochschild’s framework of feeling rules and emotional labor. Hochschild, the American sociologist, demonstrated that flight attendants are required not merely to perform cheerfulness but to cultivate genuine warmth as a condition of employment. The institution prescribes not just behavior but affect — and the worker who cannot produce the prescribed feeling is failing at their job.

The same mechanism operates in intellectual labor. Scholars, journalists, and public intellectuals are required to produce analysis that satisfies the affective expectations of their genre. A scholar writing within the abolitionist genre must produce grief-turning-into-solidarity. A journalist writing within the liberal genre must produce concern-turning-into-procedural-hope. A public intellectual writing within the critical genre must produce complexity-turning-into-insight. The analysis succeeds when it satisfies the genre’s feeling rules — not when it corresponds to outcomes.

This is “industrial” in a specific sense. Thought is organized as a production process with inputs (events, data, citations) and outputs (narratives, takes, analyses) optimized for market circulation. The dialectical form — thesis, antithesis, synthesis — is one of the most efficient production templates because it generates resolution at the level of narrative without requiring resolution at the level of fact.

Citation as containment

The production process operates through citation itself. The Stop Cop City movement provides a case study. An academic account of the movement uses the theorists — Berlant, Glissant, Gilmore, Moten and Harney, Mbembe — who were already part of how people on the ground were thinking and acting. But in the academic text, these theorists do not appear as companions in contradiction. They appear as citations that justify choices already made.

Berlant’s Cruel Optimism was used on the ground to navigate hard decisions — when does hope become a trap? when does staying become stuck? — in ways that slowed things down and sometimes prevented harm. In the academic text, Berlant’s work does not introduce tension. It resolves it. The arrest becomes transformation. The forest becomes a “life-world.” The carceral becomes fertile ground. The citations function less as tools than as signals, marking each step in a narrative arc that was already set (emsenn, “Citing for containment,” 2025).

This is citing for containment: citation as genre performance rather than analytical claim. The citation does not make a claim about the world. It signals that the writer belongs to a genre. It confirms that the narrative arc has the right shape. It produces the affective satisfaction the market of circulation demands.

Extraction and formatting

Industrial intellectualism converts embedded knowledge into circulable product. The conversion follows a pattern.

Practices that were fragile by design — intentionally quiet, dependent on trust, not meant to be made legible — are extracted from the contexts that made them possible and turned into narrative material. Leanne Betasamosake Simpson and Eve Tuck have warned that not all knowledge is meant to be extracted, cited, or scaled. Some knowledge is held in place — geographically, relationally, politically — because that is where it works. When it is lifted out and circulated as insight, it does not just lose context. It causes harm. It changes what others expect to find in those places. It turns embedded practice into transferable content.

What survives under industrial intellectualism is what can be turned into narrative. What cannot be separated from its conditions falls away. Sylvia Wynter’s work exposes the deeper operation: much of what registers as knowledge in the Western academy is legible only because it has already been reformatted — stripped of land, specificity, and obligation — into forms compatible with the genres that govern circulation. The most vital knowledge is not the most portable. But portability is what the industry selects for.

The formatting loop

The loop is cybernetic. The genre calibrates what counts as a satisfying analysis. The analysis is produced to satisfy. The satisfaction generates circulation. The circulation generates the metrics — engagement, citation, attention — that determine the intellectual’s standing. The standing determines what they can say next. Each output feeds back as input for the next cycle. This connects industrial intellectualism to californication: both are feedback mechanisms through which structural contradiction is converted into personally manageable, genre-conforming output.

Antonio Gramsci’s concept of the organic intellectual illustrates what happens on the other side of this loop. Gramsci developed the concept to name intellectuals produced by and accountable to a class — thinkers whose authority rests on their structural position within a political struggle, not on institutional credentials. The organic intellectual’s value is strategic: they articulate the interests of a class that is forming itself as a political force.

What survived into the academic archive is not this strategic function. It is the concept, formatted for survivability — made usable by the very systems that destroyed its referents. The organic intellectual circulates in curricula because the concept is valuable, fundable, format-compatible. Its ability to circulate without resistance is itself evidence of its formatting (emsenn, “The theory that survived the war,” 2025). A concept designed to name intellectuals embedded in material struggle now circulates most freely in institutions that select for portability over embeddedness. The survival of a theory is not evidence of its strategic value. It is evidence that the theory has been formatted for the production process — which is the condition industrial intellectualism names.
