Coherent confusion

2025-04-15 The governance condition in which subjects are confused but functional — unable to resolve what is happening, yet still interpreting, engaging, and producing the sensemaking labor that sustains the system.

After the April 2025 tariff announcement, commentators produced thousands of explanations: it was a negotiating tactic, a policy error, a deliberate shock, a market test, a distraction. These explanations contradicted each other, yet each felt plausible. People remained confused — but they kept interpreting, kept producing takes, kept engaging. The system did not need them to understand. It needed them to keep trying.

Coherent confusion names this governance condition: subjects confused but functional, unable to form a stable interpretation of events yet still participating in the systems that produce the confusion. The confusion is not a failure of governance. The confusion is governance. The interpretive labor of trying to make sense of contradictory signals — the takes, the threads, the analyses, the commentary — is itself what sustains system coherence.

¶The load is the system

The April 2025 tariff cycle reveals the mechanism in full operation (emsenn, “Governing by confusion,” 2025). Different systems responded on different timescales: procurement adjusted within hours, repricing and pausing orders. Futures markets corrected in real time. Treasury auction behavior shifted over days. Narrative interpretation — media analysis, punditry, commentary — arrived last, on a weekly cadence. Each layer operated on its own timescale, self-correcting without central coordination.

The interpretive layer is where coherent confusion operates. By the time commentators began explaining what had happened, the system had already completed its behavioral adjustment. The explanations were not causal — they did not produce the correction. They were the final stabilizing behavior: the narrative closure that rendered the correction legible and allowed the system to proceed as though coherence had been maintained throughout.

This is what “the load is the system” means. Norbert Wiener’s cybernetics describes how systems maintain stability through feedback rather than understanding. Stafford Beer’s Viable System Model extends this to organizations, where correction distributes across nested subsystems operating on independent timebases. The tariff cycle follows exactly this structure — and the interpretive layer is its outermost feedback loop. The cognitive and affective burden people carry under conditions of incoherence is not a byproduct of governance. It is the governing mechanism itself. Curiosity, sensemaking, and the felt pressure to understand are system resources. The more people work to interpret what is happening, the more they participate in the feedback loops that stabilize what is happening.

Niklas Luhmann’s systems theory clarifies why: communication systems do not convey truth — they reproduce structure. Media does not report reality. It generates recursive readability. Each explanation adds interpretive density that stabilizes perception, allowing financial, political, and platform systems to proceed. The more an event resists easy explanation, the more interpretive cycles it produces — and the more cycles, the more legibility the system has to route into behavioral analytics.

¶Coherent confusion vs. mere confusion

The distinction matters. Mere confusion halts participation — people disengage, withdraw, stop processing. Coherent confusion sustains participation by keeping the signal ambiguous enough that interpretation never resolves but never fully fails either. There is always one more explanation, one more thread, one more angle. The subject remains in a state of active processing: confused about the situation, but coherent in how they narrate their confusion.

Lauren Berlant’s crisis ordinariness describes the affective register this inhabits: ongoing instability absorbed into the texture of daily life rather than recognized as rupture. This is the condition under which coherent confusion becomes sustainable — when crisis stops feeling like crisis and starts feeling like weather. Wendy Chun shows the temporal mechanism in Updating to Remain the Same: modern systems preserve coherence by requiring subjects to update themselves continuously in order to remain visible and functional within systems whose logic is opaque to them. The update is the confusion made productive. The subject does not understand the system but continues to operate within it — and that continued operation is precisely what the system requires.

¶Interpretation as metabolic load

Interpretation does not just stabilize the system — it costs something. Franco Berardi’s analysis of cognitive capitalism identifies attention as both commodity and exhaust: the currency of platform economies and the waste product of their operation. Explanatory behavior — threads, op-eds, briefings, critiques — is not friction to the system. It is a thermodynamic feature: a way of converting signal instability into engagement heat, which can then be measured, traded, and predicted against.

Georges Bataille’s concept of the accursed share — surplus energy that must be expended — completes the picture. In cybernetic capitalism, what must be burned is sensemaking itself. The interpretive strain is not accidental. It is constitutive. The system needs it. The subject who endures interpretive pressure becomes more signal-rich, more visible to governance. Their burnout is not dysfunction — it is yield.

This is why “Governing by confusion” concludes: “this piece didn’t escape the loop, it executed it.” The act of analyzing coherent confusion is itself an instance of coherent confusion. The writer who explains the mechanism is performing the interpretive labor the mechanism requires. Reading the explanation is performing it too. The loop closes when you feel it.
