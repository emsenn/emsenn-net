Municipal Decisions Follow Capital, Not Ideology

2026-03-31 Cities do not decide based on what they believe. They decide based on what they can afford — where 'afford' means fiscal, political, administrative, and discursive capital. Jackson, Flint, and Duluth demonstrate the theorem.

Abstract

On April 12, 2021, Duluth’s city council declared climate change an emergency. The city’s stormwater system — 411 miles of pipe, 36 miles of it over 100 years old, some segments dating to 1880 — needs 4.6millionannuallytomaintain.Thecitybudgets4.6 million annually to maintain. The city budgets 1.2 million. The declaration cost nothing. The infrastructure costs everything the city does not have. Duluth is not hypocritical. It is capital-constrained. The declaration is what a city does when discursive capital is the only capital left.

This paper develops a framework for reading municipal decisions as expressions of capital constraint rather than ideology. Municipalities operate through five intervention forms (rules, administration, infrastructure, programs, symbols) selected according to four forms of capital (fiscal, political, administrative, discursive). The capital configuration at any moment produces a readable preference regime — orders over money, projects over rules, or voluntarism — that predicts which interventions the city will choose regardless of what it says it values. 1. Five Intervention Forms

Municipal action takes five forms. The forms are defined not by policy domain but by institutional shape — the kind of capacity each requires and the kind of cost each imposes.

Rules change what is legally allowed, required, or prohibited: zoning amendments, ordinances, performance standards. Low fiscal cost. High political cost. Shifts compliance burden onto residents and businesses. Jackson’s city council can pass water-quality ordinances without money — but cannot enforce them without staff.

Administration changes how existing rules are interpreted and enforced: modified permit conditions, adjusted enforcement intensity, revised workflows. Invisible to the public. High leverage. In Flint, the decision to not add corrosion inhibitors when switching water sources was an administrative decision — no ordinance was passed, no council voted, no public process occurred. A single administrative judgment, made under fiscal pressure by a state-appointed emergency manager, contaminated 100,000 people’s drinking water.

Infrastructure builds, upgrades, or decommissions physical assets: roads, drainage, water treatment. High fiscal cost. High visibility. Generates political capital through ribbon-cutting. Duluth’s 2012 flood caused over 100millionindamages.Thestormwatersystemthatfailedwasbuiltin1880.Replacingitcosts100 million in damages. The stormwater system that failed was built in 1880. Replacing it costs 907,000 per mile. The city has 36 miles past their 100-year lifespan. The arithmetic is $32.7 million the city does not have.

Programs use incentives rather than mandates: grants, cost-sharing, tax abatements, outreach. Ongoing fiscal cost. Dependent on uptake and trust. In Jackson, 50% of water supplied yielded no revenue due to faulty metering and system loss — a programmatic failure compounding a fiscal one.

Symbols operate at the level of narrative: resolutions, strategic plans, pledges, advisory boards. Low fiscal cost. Framing function. Over 2,100 local governments in 39 countries have declared climate emergencies. Critics describe these as “performative gestures lacking concrete policy plans or funding.” Global emissions have continued to rise. The declarations are not failures of will. They are what cities produce when discursive capital is the only capital they can spend.

2. Four Capitals

Every potential intervention is evaluated against implicit thresholds. The thresholds are not ideological. They are capital constraints.

Fiscal capital is money: revenue, borrowing capacity, reserves, grant access. Jackson’s water system needs 2 billion in repairs. The city's median wage declined 7.6% from 2009 to 2019 — the worst performance nationally. Its job growth ranked 46th of 57 large metro areas. The tax base that would fund repairs left with white flight: whites went from nearly half Jackson's population in 1980 to roughly a quarter by 1990. The infrastructure aged as the revenue base contracted. By 2022, the system averaged 55 pipe breaks per 100 miles annually — nearly four times the safe rate. Mayor Lumumba estimated 2 billion in needed repairs while presiding over a city where a quarter of residents live below the poverty line. Fiscal capital was not scarce. It was absent.

Political capital is tolerance for conflict. In Flint, the state’s emergency manager law granted appointed managers authority to override elected local government. The decision to switch water sources was made to save $5 million over two years while a new pipeline was built. The political capital required to impose cost savings on a majority-Black city was supplied by the state, not the city — the emergency manager answered to the governor, not to Flint’s residents. When the water turned brown and residents complained, the institutional response was denial, because acknowledging the problem would have cost political capital the state apparatus was not willing to spend.

Administrative capital is capacity: staff, expertise, institutional memory. Jackson’s Department of Public Works raised alarms for years about persistent budget deficits and staffing shortages that made water system maintenance impossible. A 2022 EPA report documented understaffing, high turnover, and malfunctioning meters across the system. Duluth maintains 148 structures averaging over 40 years of age with a stormwater maintenance gap of 3.4 million annually — the difference between the 4.6 million needed and the $1.2 million budgeted. Administrative capital is not just about having staff; it is about having enough staff to do the work the infrastructure requires.

Discursive capital is narrative credibility. Duluth declared a climate emergency, published a Climate Action Work Plan, and joined national resilience networks. These actions cost discursive capital and generated more of it — the city can now position itself as climate-forward, attracting grants and aligning with federal priorities. But the narratives that justify investment in stormwater infrastructure for Lincoln Park — a low-income neighborhood hit hard by the 2012 flood — compete with narratives about downtown development, tourism infrastructure, and the “climate haven” branding that markets Duluth to relocating professionals. When discursive capital flows toward the haven narrative, the neighborhoods that need flood infrastructure lose the narrative support their projects require.

3. Preference Regimes

The capital configuration produces readable patterns.

Orders over money. Fiscal capital scarce, political capital adequate. The city governs by regulation: ordinances, codes, enforcement. Jackson before the crisis operated here — passing water-quality rules it could not fund, imposing compliance requirements it could not administer. The city was not choosing rules over infrastructure. It was governing with the only capital available: the authority to issue orders.

Projects over rules. Fiscal capital available through external sources, political capital for regulation limited. After the 2022 crisis, Jackson received 600millionfromCongressand600 million from Congress and 74.9 million from the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act. The city immediately shifted into project mode — capital improvements, pipe replacement, treatment plant repair. The regime transition was not ideological. It was fiscal. New money opened the infrastructure window that had been structurally closed for decades. When the money runs out, the regime will revert.

Voluntarism. Political capital fragile, sensitivity to backlash acute. Duluth’s climate response operates substantially in this regime: voluntary programs, strategic plans, advisory committees, pilot projects. The stormwater utility fee — 6.75permonthperhousehold,proposedtoincreaseto6.75 per month per household, proposed to increase to 12.80 by 2026 — is the kind of incremental, opt-in-feeling intervention a voluntarism regime can tolerate. It is not enough. The fee increase, even if fully implemented, does not close the $3.4 million annual maintenance gap. But it is what the political capital configuration permits.

Regime collapse. When all four capitals are simultaneously scarce, no intervention form is affordable. This is infrastructural abandonment. It is not a decision. No one voted to let Jackson’s pipes break 55 times per 100 miles per year. No one voted to leave Duluth’s 1880 stormwater tunnels in place beneath Lincoln Park. The capital configuration produced the outcome. The outcome looks like neglect because there is no visible actor making a visible choice. That is the point. Abandonment operates through the absence of capacity, not the presence of malice.

4. The Racial Architecture of Capital Constraints

The four-capital framework is not race-neutral, and pretending otherwise would make the analysis useless. Capital constraints are produced. They have histories. In Jackson, the fiscal constraint was produced by white flight: five decades of racialized population loss that eroded the tax base while the infrastructure aged on its original timeline. In Flint, the administrative constraint was produced by Michigan’s emergency manager law — a mechanism that disproportionately placed majority-Black cities under state control, replacing local democratic governance with appointed fiscal managers whose mandate was cost reduction.

Ruth Wilson Gilmore defines racism as “the state-sanctioned or extralegal production and exploitation of group-differentiated vulnerability to premature death.” Municipal capital constraints are one mechanism through which that vulnerability is produced. The pipe breaks in Jackson do not kill people the way a bullet does. They kill people the way Klinenberg’s heat wave killed people in Chicago: through the absence of infrastructure that would have kept them alive, in neighborhoods where that infrastructure was withdrawn because the capital configuration made maintenance structurally impossible, in cities where the capital configuration was itself a product of racialized disinvestment.

The framework does not name racism as a separate variable. It names the capital constraints that racism produces. The analytical value is precision: instead of saying “Jackson’s water crisis is caused by racism” — true but strategically vague — the framework says “Jackson’s fiscal capital was destroyed by white flight, its administrative capital was destroyed by chronic understaffing, its political capital was captured by a hostile state government, and its discursive capital was exhausted by decades of crisis narrative. The capital configuration made maintenance structurally impossible.” Each constraint is specific. Each specificity implies a different intervention.

5. Strategic Implications

For communities facing abandonment, the framework replaces moral indictment with structural analysis.

Stop reading decisions as ideology. Duluth’s climate declaration coexisting with deferred stormwater maintenance is not hypocrisy. It is discursive capital being spent because fiscal capital is constrained. Accusing the city of hypocrisy is strategically useless. Identifying the binding constraint tells you what to change.

Identify which capital is actually scarce. If the city avoids infrastructure everywhere, the constraint is fiscal — the intervention is external funding that changes the fiscal profile. If the city builds infrastructure in other neighborhoods but not yours, the constraint is political — your neighborhood does not generate the political capital that justifies expenditure. Jackson’s crisis was fiscal. Flint’s was administrative (imposed by the state). Different constraints require different strategies.

Exploit regime transitions. Jackson’s $600 million in federal funding opened a project window that had been closed for decades. That window is the moment for capital-intensive proposals. When it closes — when the federal money runs out — the regime will revert to orders-over-money, and infrastructure proposals will be rejected again regardless of need. Timing matters more than merit.

Know when to stop engaging the city. When the capital configuration makes your proposal structurally impossible — when no form of capital is available to fund, authorize, staff, or justify what your neighborhood needs — engagement with the municipal process is not just futile. It is costly. The time, trust, and relational energy spent navigating a capital filter that will reject your proposal is time, trust, and energy not spent on relational adaptation that bypasses the filter entirely. The framework tells you not just when to push but when to stop pushing.

6. Conclusion

Cities decide what they can afford. The four capitals — fiscal, political, administrative, discursive — determine which interventions pass through the decision filter. The preference regimes — orders over money, projects over rules, voluntarism — are structural responses to capital configurations, not ideological commitments. Infrastructural abandonment is what happens when all four capitals are simultaneously insufficient.

Jackson’s 100-year-old water system, Flint’s poisoned pipes, Duluth’s 1880 stormwater tunnels — these are not stories about cities that didn’t care. They are stories about capital configurations that made care structurally impossible, in cities where the configurations were themselves products of racialized disinvestment extending back decades. Understanding the machine does not make it just. But it tells you which constraints are binding, which regime the city is in, and whether pushing the municipal process or bypassing it entirely is the better use of your community’s limited relational capital.
