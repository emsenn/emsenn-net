Invention of Tradition

2026-04-02 Hobsbawm's concept: practices that present themselves as ancient and organically continuous are in fact recent, deliberately constructed, and serving present political needs. The mechanism by which genred folk culture is produced.

The invention of tradition is Eric Hobsbawm’s concept (1983) for practices that present themselves as ancient and organically continuous but are in fact recent, deliberately constructed, and serving present political needs. The Scottish Highland kilt, the British coronation ceremony, the Grimms’ “German folk tales,” Oktoberfest — all are invented traditions that claim immemorial origins while being products of the 18th and 19th centuries.

Hobsbawm identifies three overlapping types: traditions establishing or symbolizing social cohesion and group membership, traditions establishing or legitimizing institutions and authority, and traditions whose main purpose is socialization — the inculcation of beliefs, values, and conventions. All three share the feature of claiming continuity with a suitable historic past while being substantially discontinuous with it. The past they claim is largely factitious.

The concept is analytically central to genring because it names the production mechanism. The Brothers Grimm did not collect German folk tales — they produced them, editing a heterogeneous body of stories (many from French-speaking, middle-class sources) into a corpus that confirmed the existence of a German Volksgeist. The Trachtenverein movement of the 1880s did not preserve Bavarian peasant dress — it produced the dirndl and lederhosen as consumable images of folk authenticity. The Shahnameh was not discovered by the Pahlavi regime — it was deployed as proof of an Aryanist national spirit that had existed for millennia.

Invented traditions are not lies in the simple sense. They draw on real materials — actual songs, actual garments, actual stories — but curate, edit, and codify them until the result confirms what the nation needs to believe about itself. The invention is in the selection and the claim of continuity, not in the material. This is what makes invented traditions powerful: they feel authentic because they contain authentic elements, rearranged to serve a purpose the original elements never served.
