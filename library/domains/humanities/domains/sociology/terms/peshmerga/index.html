Peshmerga

2026-04-02 Kurdish armed resistance fighters — 'those who walk before death' — a term spanning ideologically incompatible movements that has itself become a contested genre figure, both imposed and self-adopted.
Defines peshmerga

Peshmerga (Kurmanji: pêşmerge, from pêş “before” + merg “death”) means “those who walk before death” — Kurdish armed resistance fighters willing to die for Kurdish liberation. The term carries the weight of a century of struggle against four states (Turkey, Iran, Iraq, Syria) and the dignity of a people who have maintained armed self-defense in the face of genocide, chemical weapons, and systematic cultural erasure.

The term is also a genre figure — and this is where its complexity begins. “Peshmerga” applies to the KDP’s (Kurdistan Democratic Party) forces in Iraqi Kurdistan, the PUK’s (Patriotic Union of Kurdistan) fighters, the PKK’s guerrillas in Turkey, the PJAK’s fighters in Iran, and the YPG/YPJ in Rojava. These are not the same movement. The KDP is conservative, dynastic, pro-capitalist, and seeks a conventional Kurdish nation-state. The PKK and its affiliates fight for democratic confederalism — the abolition of the nation-state as a political form. The KDP and PKK have fought each other. The YPJ’s gender revolution is a revolution against traditions the KDP upholds. Yet “peshmerga” contains them all, and Western media uses the term as if it named a single force with a single politics.

Under Ba’athist Iraq, the peshmerga genre figure had a deliberate counterpart: the jash (“donkey’s foal”) — Kurdish men recruited as collaborators, scouts, and militia auxiliaries for the Iraqi state. The binary was a state-imposed genring operation: you are either peshmerga (heroic resistance) or jash (useful traitor). There is no position between — no character for the farmer trying to survive, the teacher navigating both sides, the mother hedging against total loss. The binary eliminates the temporal middle by reducing Kurdish life to two characters in a script written by the occupying state.

As a self-adopted genre figure, the peshmerga carries genuine meaning — the willingness to die for a people’s survival is not a fiction. But when the genre stabilizes, it erases the same things state-imposed genres erase: political disagreement, ideological complexity, the ordinary life that armed struggle is supposed to protect.
