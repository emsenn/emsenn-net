Tribe

2026-04-02 A kinship-based political unit organized through lineage, alliance, and reciprocal obligation — analytically useful for specific social structures but politically dangerous when applied as a genre category that reduces complex societies to 'tribal peoples.'

A tribe is a kinship-based political unit organized through lineage, alliance, and reciprocal obligation — typically governed by councils of elders, chiefs, or aghas whose authority derives from kinship position and personal prestige rather than from bureaucratic office. Tribal structures are real social forms with specific logics: they distribute resources through kinship networks, resolve disputes through mediation and customary law, and reproduce themselves through marriage alliances and genealogical reckoning.

The analytical problem is that “tribe” is also a genre category — a word that colonial and nationalist states apply to peoples they wish to classify as pre-modern, pre-political, and pre-civilized. When the British classified African polities as “tribes,” they reduced complex kingdoms, confederacies, and commercial networks to a single category that justified colonial administration. When Turkish and Iranian states call Kurds “tribal,” they reduce a civilization with multiple political forms — democratic confederalism, parliamentary parties, Marxist movements, religious orders — to a single pre-modern category that justifies tutelage.

Martin van Bruinessen (1992) documented Kurdish tribal structures with care: the agha (tribal chief), the shaikh (religious authority), and their negotiation with the state. Kurdish tribal organization is real — it structures land tenure, dispute resolution, marriage, and political alliance in specific regions. But it coexists with every other form of Kurdish political life: urban intellectualism, leftist movements, feminist revolution, diaspora organizing. The word “tribal” becomes a genring operation when it replaces this heterogeneity with a single image — the fierce, simple, mountain tribesman — and uses that image to explain everything about Kurdish life.

“Tribalism” — the pejorative use of the concept to mean irrational group loyalty, sectarianism, or inability to participate in modern politics — is a genre judgment, not an analysis. It attributes to the people what belongs to the word: the simplification is in the label, not in the life.
