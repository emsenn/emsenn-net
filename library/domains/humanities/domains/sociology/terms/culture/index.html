Culture

2026-04-02 The totality of a people's practices, meanings, institutions, and ways of being — not a possession or a product but a living process of collective sense-making that is always heteroglossic, always contested, always in transformation.

Culture is the totality of a people’s practices, meanings, institutions, symbols, and ways of being — their language, religion, art, law, kinship, food, architecture, humor, grief, and the implicit rules governing all of these. The word has carried different weights across its history. Raymond Williams (1958) called it “one of the two or three most complicated words in the English language,” tracing its shift from cultivation (of soil, then of mind) to a whole way of life.

Two insights matter for the concepts that depend on this one.

First: culture is not a thing but a process. Clifford Geertz (1973) defined it as “webs of significance” that human beings spin and within which they are suspended — not a set of objects (songs, costumes, rituals) but the ongoing activity of making and interpreting meaning. This matters because genring treats culture as a collection of extractable features: a dress, a dance, a tale. The genring operation works by converting process into product — freezing living practice into a consumable image. The dirndl is not Bavarian culture. Kurdish dengbej performance is not Kurdish culture. Each is one artifact extracted from an ongoing process of collective meaning-making, codified and circulated until it replaces the process it was taken from.

Second: culture is always heteroglossic. Any living culture contains multiple voices, internal disagreements, competing visions of the good life, generational conflicts, and irreconcilable tensions. A culture that has been reduced to a coherent set of features — “Kurdish culture is mountain warrior plus tribal honor plus Newroz” — is no longer a culture but a genre. The reduction from culture (multi-voiced, contested, living) to genre (single-voiced, stable, consumable) is the central operation of the genring papers.

The concept of ethnocide — the destruction of a culture while leaving the population alive — depends on understanding culture as this living process. What is killed is not a set of objects but a people’s capacity to make meaning on their own terms.
