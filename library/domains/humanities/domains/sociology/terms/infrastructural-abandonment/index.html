Infrastructural Abandonment

2026-03-30 The withdrawal of institutional maintenance from communities, spaces, or systems that remain inhabited — not destruction but the managed cessation of upkeep, producing slow degradation rather than acute crisis.

Infrastructural abandonment is the withdrawal of institutional maintenance from communities, spaces, or systems that remain inhabited. It is not demolition — nothing is destroyed. It is the cessation of upkeep: the road is not repaired, the water treatment plant is not upgraded, the levee inspection is deferred, the school is not staffed, the clinic loses its funding. The infrastructure remains physically present but degrades on a timeline set by neglect rather than by catastrophe.

The distinction from destruction matters. Destruction is legible — a hurricane, an airstrike, an eviction produces a clear before-and-after that can be named, mourned, and responded to. Abandonment is slow, diffuse, and deniable. The pipe didn’t burst because someone decided to harm you; the pipe burst because no one decided to replace it. Responsibility dissolves into institutional absence. The harm is real but the agent is nowhere.

Eric Klinenberg’s Heat Wave (2002) documents how infrastructural abandonment killed people in Chicago in 1995: the neighborhoods with the highest death tolls were those where parks had closed, senior centers had lost funding, commercial strips had emptied, and the social infrastructure that would have connected isolated elders to help had been withdrawn over decades. The heat was the proximate cause. The abandonment was the structural one.

Abandonment is not random. It follows the contours of race, class, and political disposability. Indigenous communities on reservations, Black neighborhoods in deindustrialized cities, rural communities whose extractive industries have departed — these are not accidentally neglected. They are deprioritized through institutional logics that allocate maintenance resources toward populations and spaces that generate revenue, political support, or strategic value. The communities left behind are not destroyed; they are released from the institutional care that would have prevented their degradation.

For communities facing abandonment, the adaptive implications are specific. The institutional scaffolding that proceduralist frameworks assume — functioning government, responsive emergency services, accessible courts, maintained infrastructure — does not exist. Frameworks that depend on this scaffolding cannot operate. What persists is relational infrastructure: kinship networks, place-based knowledge, informal mutual support, and the opacity that protects adaptive structures from the same institutions that withdrew maintenance in the first place.

Infrastructural abandonment is the condition that makes mutualist proceduralism fail and relational adaptation necessary. It is the background against which the paper on collapse adaptation operates.
