Collective Memory

2026-04-02 The shared representations of the past that a group maintains through institutions, rituals, narratives, and material culture — not individual recall but the social frameworks through which a people remembers who it is.

Collective memory is the shared representations of the past that a group maintains through institutions, rituals, narratives, monuments, and material culture. Maurice Halbwachs (1925) established the foundational insight: memory is not a private psychological event but a social process. We remember within “social frameworks” — the family, the religious community, the nation — and these frameworks determine what is remembered, how it is remembered, and what is forgotten. An individual who leaves all social groups loses the frameworks that sustain memory. Memory is collective before it is individual.

Jan Assmann (2011) distinguishes two forms. Communicative memory is the living memory transmitted through everyday interaction — family stories, personal recollections, witness accounts — limited to about three generations (80–100 years) before it fades. Cultural memory is the institutionalized memory maintained through texts, rituals, monuments, festivals, and specialized carriers (priests, bards, archivists, dengbej) — capable of spanning millennia.

The genring framework depends on this distinction. Genring operates on cultural memory: it selects specific features from a people’s institutionalized past, codifies them into a stable template, and circulates the template as the authoritative memory of who that people is. The Shahnameh is a vehicle of cultural memory — but when the Pahlavi regime deploys it as proof of Aryanist national identity, it becomes a vehicle of genring. Newroz is cultural memory — but when states contest who owns the Kawa myth, the memory becomes a battlefield.

What linguicide and ethnocide destroy is not just language and custom but the social frameworks through which a people remembers itself. Destroy the dengbej, ban the ceremonies, rename the villages, forbid the language — and the collective memory loses its carriers. The people survive biologically but lose the institutional means of knowing who they are.
