Preference Regime

2026-03-31 The characteristic pattern of intervention selection that a municipality exhibits given its current capital configuration — predictable not from ideology but from which forms of municipal capital are scarce and which are available.

A preference regime is the characteristic pattern of intervention forms a municipality selects, given its current configuration of municipal capital. The regime is not an ideological identity (“pro-business,” “progressive,” “green”). It is a structural response to capital constraints that can be identified by tracking which intervention forms the city consistently chooses and which it consistently avoids.

Three stylized regimes appear repeatedly:

Orders over money. Fiscal capital is scarce; political capital is adequate. The city governs by regulation — ordinances, codes, enforcement — because rules cost authority, not money. Compliance burdens fall on residents and businesses. This is the regime of cities with structural deficits, state-imposed tax caps, or no access to grant pipelines. The city appears “regulatory” but it is actually broke.

Projects over rules. Fiscal capital is available (grants, bonds, windfalls); political capital for regulation is limited. The city builds — infrastructure, capital improvements, visible public works — and avoids imposing new mandates. It appears “development-oriented” but it is actually politically fragile: it can spend money it was given but cannot impose rules that generate opposition.

Voluntarism. Political capital is fragile; the city is acutely sensitive to backlash. It governs through opt-in programs, pilot projects, advisory boards, resolutions, and strategic plans. Nothing is binding. Staff may privately acknowledge that stronger measures would be more effective. The city appears “collaborative” but it is actually afraid.

Real cities move between regimes as capital configurations shift: a new grant opens the projects window, a scandal pushes toward voluntarism, a fiscal crisis entrenches orders-over-money. The regimes are not permanent identities; they are readable structural states. Understanding which regime a city is in tells advocates what proposals can pass, what will be killed, and when transitions create openings that would not otherwise exist.
