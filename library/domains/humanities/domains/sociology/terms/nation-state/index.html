Nation-State

2026-04-02 The political form that fuses nation (a people imagined as culturally unified) with state (a sovereign territorial administration) — claiming that each nation should govern itself and each state should contain one nation. The form that genring serves and that democratic confederalism rejects.

The nation-state is the political form that fuses two distinct concepts: the nation (a people imagined as sharing language, culture, history, and destiny) and the state (a sovereign administration exercising authority over a bounded territory). The fusion claims that these two must coincide — that each nation should have its own state, and each state should contain one nation. This claim, which became the organizing principle of global politics after the Treaty of Westphalia (1648) and accelerated after the French Revolution, is one of the most consequential fictions in modern history.

Ernest Gellner (1983) argues that nationalism is not the awakening of dormant nations but the production of nations by states that need culturally homogeneous populations to administer. States need citizens who speak the same language, obey the same laws, and identify with the same symbols — because modern industrial economies require mobile, interchangeable labor and centralized education. The nation is not prior to the state; it is a product of the state’s administrative needs. Benedict Anderson (1983) adds that the nation is an “imagined community”: not a fiction, but a community constituted through shared imagination — print capitalism, maps, museums, censuses — rather than face-to-face contact.

The nation-state form creates a structural problem for peoples who do not coincide with state boundaries. Kurdish peoples span four states (Turkey, Iran, Iraq, Syria) and constitute a majority in none. The nation-state framework offers them only two positions: create your own state (which four existing states resist with violence) or assimilate into the state you’re in (which destroys your identity). Democratic confederalism — the political philosophy practiced in Rojava — rejects both options by rejecting the nation-state form itself, proposing governance through nested communes that do not require territorial sovereignty.

Genring serves the nation-state by producing the cultural content that makes the nation feel real — the folk tales, the costumes, the festivals, the genre figures that prove the Volk exists. Without genring, the nation-state has territory and administration but no story. With genring, it has characters, a plot, and a cast of authentic folk types whose coherence justifies exclusion of whoever does not fit.
