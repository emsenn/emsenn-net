Palingenetic Ultranationalism

2026-03-31 Griffin's definitional core of fascism: a myth of national rebirth after a period of decline, fusing nostalgia with utopian futurity to mobilize populations through promises of purification and regeneration.

Palingenetic ultranationalism is Roger Griffin’s term for the ideological sine qua non of fascism: the myth that a sacred nation has fallen into decadence and will be reborn through struggle, purification, and collective will. Without this myth, fascism has no ideological engine. With it, every other fascist domain — the exceptional state, corporatist economy, biopolitical sorting, civic religion — has its organizing center.

The myth operates on a dual temporal axis. Backward: there was a time when the nation was pure, whole, and powerful. Forward: that purity will be restored through the heroic action of the present generation. The present is framed as the hinge — the moment of decision between continued decline and redemptive transformation. This temporal structure is what makes palingenetic ultranationalism a teleological formation: it organizes all action around a projected future state (the reborn nation) and evaluates the present solely by its progress toward that state.

The “ultra” in ultranationalism marks the distinction from ordinary nationalism. Fascist nationalism is not civic patriotism or cultural pride. It is the assertion that the nation is an organic, sacred body whose existence is prior to and more real than the individuals composing it. The individual has value insofar as they serve the national body. Those who contaminate it — through racial impurity, political dissent, cultural deviance, or biological unfitness — are pathological elements to be expelled or destroyed. This is where palingenetic ultranationalism connects to biopolitics: the rebirth myth specifies who belongs to the future and who must be eliminated so the future can arrive.

Emsenn’s concept of fascist grammar describes the rhetorical sequence through which palingenetic ultranationalism is performed: declare crisis, name the pure community, denounce enemies as sterile, reframe suffering as sacrifice, promise destiny, assert inevitability. The grammar can be spoken in different vocabularies — Volk, Western civilization, the common good — but the structure is the same. What changes is the surface; what persists is the myth.

Griffin’s contribution is making the myth falsifiable. A political formation that does not invoke national rebirth after decline — that does not posit a sacred people, a fall from grace, and a redemptive future — is not fascism. It may be authoritarian, populist, theocratic, or otherwise dangerous. But it is not fascism, because it lacks the ideological nucleus around which all other fascist structures crystallize.
