Labor

¶What This Is

Labor is purposeful human activity that transforms the world. When you cook a meal, build a shelter, tend a garden, teach a child, or write a song, you are laboring — shaping materials, relationships, or knowledge into something they were not before. Labor is broader than a “job” or “work” in the narrow sense of paid employment; it is everything people do to sustain, reproduce, and enrich collective life.

The category is not innocent. Whether a particular activity counts as labor — and whether it counts as labor that gets paid — is a political question with a long history. Three traditions structure that history. The Marxist tradition opens the question by distinguishing labor from labor-power and reading capital’s profit as the gap between them. Feminist political economy extends the question by recovering the reproductive labor that capitalism depends on but does not pay. Indigenous traditions refuse the separation between labor and the relational life that grounds the question in the first place.

¶Formal Distinction: Labor and Labor-Power

Karl Marx, in Capital Volume 1 (1867), draws the analytical distinction at the heart of the modern theory of labor. Two terms must be separated:

  • Labor (LL) is the activity itself — the actual work of sewing clothes, harvesting crops, or caring for the sick. It is the expenditure of human capacity in concrete form, producing a determinate product or service.
  • Labor-power (Λ\Lambda) is the capacity to labor — the worker’s potential to expend LL over a given period, considered as a thing the worker possesses and can transfer.

Under capitalism, Λ\Lambda becomes a commodity. Workers, dispossessed of means of production, must sell their labor-power for a wage; the employer then directs how that capacity is used. The wage’s value is set by the cost of reproducing Λ\Lambda — the worker’s subsistence. The value the worker actually produces during the contracted period is set by what their labor LL creates.

The fundamental claim follows: LL’s value-creation exceeds Λ\Lambda’s wage. The gap is surplus-value, the basis of capitalist profit:

surplus-value=value created by L−value of Λ\text{surplus-value} = \text{value created by } L - \text{value of } \Lambda

The general circuit of capital is then M ⁣− ⁣C ⁣− ⁣M′M\!-\!C\!-\!M': money invested in commodities (including Λ\Lambda), consumed in production, and exchanged back as more money. The increment M′−MM' - M is the surplus extracted from the unique commodity whose use produces more value than its purchase cost.

This is not theft in any individual sense. The wage exchange is “free” — no individual coercion is required for any particular sale. The structural condition is the dispossession that makes the sale necessary: workers must sell their labor-power on whatever terms the labor market sets, because the alternative is starvation. The structural feature, not any single transaction, is what the analysis tracks.

¶Marx: Alienation and Living Labor

In the Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844, Marx develops the labor analysis at the level of the worker’s experience. Under capitalism the worker is alienated in four registers:

  1. From the product of labor, which belongs to capital, not the worker.
  2. From the activity of labor, which is directed by capital and stripped of the worker’s purposive control.
  3. From species-being, the human capacity for self-realizing creative activity that defines the species and that wage-labor systematically forecloses.
  4. From other workers, who become competitors for limited employment rather than collaborators in shared activity.

Labor under capitalism is thus labor against the laborer. The activity that should constitute the worker’s species-being instead reduces them to means for capital’s self-expansion. The four-register account of alienation is the affective-existential register of the political-economic claim about surplus-value.

A second analytical move from the same period: dead labor vs. living labor. Dead labor is past labor congealed in the means of production (machines, infrastructure, accumulated capital). Living labor is present labor expending itself now. Capital, on Marx’s reading, is the relation in which dead labor commands living labor — the worker animates machinery whose value was set by prior workers’ expended labor, while their own expended labor in turn becomes dead labor commanding the next worker.

¶Federici and the Wages-for-Housework Tradition: Reproductive Labor

Feminist political economy — Silvia Federici, Mariarosa Dalla Costa, Selma James, Maria Mies — shows that conventional accounts of labor, including Marx’s, render invisible the labor that reproduces the workforce itself.

Cooking, cleaning, childcare, emotional support, eldercare, community maintenance: these are forms of social reproduction that capitalism depends on but does not compensate. They fall disproportionately on women, and the invisibility is structural — capital depends on this labor remaining unwaged. If reproductive labor were waged, the economy as currently structured could not afford the workforce.

Dalla Costa and James (The Power of Women and the Subversion of the Community, 1972) make the founding move of the Wages for Housework tradition. The political demand is not “pay us for housework” as a literal monetary claim — it is the analytical move of refusing the gendered separation that exempts reproductive labor from the wage relation. The demand exposes what the separation does and forces the question of who benefits from its persistence.

Federici’s Caliban and the Witch (2004) ties this analysis to the original violence of primitive accumulation. The witch-trials of early-modern Europe, Federici argues, were in large part a disciplinary apparatus that drove women out of the wage-labor economy and into the unpaid reproductive role, dismantled women’s economic independence, and reorganized the household as the unwaged base on which the wage relation could be built. The wage relation as we know it is built on this prior gendered violence; the structure was not natural and the violence was not incidental.

The contemporary register of this analysis is care-work: the named labor of attending to bodies, relationships, and conditions of life under conditions where institutional buffers have thinned and the work is increasingly visible as work — even as it remains, in many configurations, unwaged or underwaged.

¶Autonomist Marxism: Refusal of Work, Immaterial Labor

The Italian autonomist Marxist tradition (Mario Tronti, Antonio Negri, Paolo Virno, Maurizio Lazzarato) reads Marx’s labor analysis through an inverted angle. Rather than treating capital as the active agent and labor as its passive substrate, autonomism positions the working class as the active force that capital must continuously respond to.

Tronti’s Operai e Capitale (1966) inverts the standard Marxist sequence. Capital is structured around containing the threat working-class struggle poses; capital’s reorganizations (Taylorism, automation, post-Fordism, financialization, platform capitalism) are responses to working-class refusal, not autonomous capitalist developments. The refusal of work — the worker’s demand for less work, or for work organized differently, or for the wage-labor relation to end — is the active force; capital reacts.

Negri (Marx Beyond Marx, 1991) extends this through a periodization of the working-class composition. The mass worker is the Fordist factory laborer of the twentieth-century industrial economy. The socialized worker is the laborer whose entire life-activity is folded into productive capital — communicating, learning, attending, performing affectively. As production becomes increasingly immaterial (Lazzarato), classical Marxism’s labor categories require extension: knowledge work, affective work, brand-creation, attention-management all become load-bearing for value production, even though they do not look like factory labor.

This connects to the analysis cybernetic postliberalism develops at the platformal scale. The neurotic platformal intellectual and the savior-slave subject are the contemporary subject-forms in which immaterial labor is performed; californication is the operation by which structural contradictions of late-capitalist labor get routed through subjects’ affective lives as personally manageable conditions.

¶Indigenous Critique: Labor Without Separation

Indigenous perspectives challenge the framing of labor as something separable from the rest of life. In many Indigenous traditions, what Western categories call “labor” — growing food, making tools, building homes, conducting ceremony — is not a distinct activity performed for compensation or even for survival in isolation. It is a relational practice embedded in obligations to land, kin, ancestors, and community.

The separation of labor from these relations — its abstraction into a commodity that can be bought and sold — is itself a product of colonization and enclosure. Glen Coulthard (Red Skin, White Masks, 2014) argues that the dispossession of indigenous peoples from land is not just an economic transaction but a destruction of the relational conditions within which work was integrated into life. The wage relation requires the prior severance.

Leanne Betasamosake Simpson (As We Have Always Done, 2017) develops the alternative under the name grounded normativity: practices of land-based relation that are simultaneously productive, reproductive, and ceremonial. Harvesting, processing, gifting, sharing — these are not “production” in the capitalist sense; they are the substance of the relational life within which any particular activity has meaning. To ask “is this labor?” is already to have separated the activity from its relational ground; the answer to that question presupposes the colonial-economic frame the question was asked from.

The Indigenous critique is not that the Marxist or feminist analyses are wrong about wage-labor; it is that the wage-labor frame itself is the problem to be named, not the universal frame from which other forms appear as residue or pre-modern. The temporal-middle position — inheriting and continuing a tradition without freezing it or abandoning it — is the practical register in which this critique lives.

¶Adjacent Distinctions

Labor is not the same as work, though English uses both. Work often denotes employment as an institution; labor is the underlying activity. The distinction sometimes matters: when work disappears (mass unemployment), labor continues (cooking, caring, organizing); when labor is restructured (the platform economy), work as institutional form changes shape.

Labor is not the same as production. Production typically denotes the bringing-into-existence of a particular product; labor is the human capacity expended in production — and in reproduction, and in care, and in many activities production-discourse does not register at all.

Labor is not the same as the worker. The worker is the person who labors; labor is the activity. Capital purchases labor-power, not the worker. The conflation of person with activity is a category error that obscures the structural relation between them.

Labor is not the same as value-creation in the strict economic sense, though Marxist analysis says they coincide under capitalist conditions. Other regimes (gift economy, ceremonial economy, subsistence) involve labor that does not create value in the political-economic sense at all; the labor is real, the value-form is not the universal frame.

¶Open Questions

What labor exists outside the wage relation, the reproductive-labor analysis, and the indigenous-relational frame? Subsistence labor in the global south, informal economies, gift-economy production in non-monetary networks, the labor of organizing politically — each is partially captured by the existing analyses but not fully.

How does the labor / labor-power distinction operate under platform capitalism, where the worker’s entire affective and attentional life becomes productive substrate? Autonomist Marxism’s account of immaterial labor opens the question; whether the distinction holds in the same form, requires modification, or breaks down is contested.

What practices recover the relational embedding of labor without re-romanticizing pre-capitalist forms? This is the live question Indigenous-resurgence politics works on; the framework here gestures at it without claiming to systematize the answer. The distinction between recovery and romanticism is itself contested.

How does the analysis of unwaged reproductive labor extend to the contemporary platform-mediated condition where the labor of self-presentation, attention-curation, and affective participation becomes productive without registering as work to the worker?
