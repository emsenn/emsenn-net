Race

2026-04-02 A system of classification that assigns human populations to hierarchically ranked categories based on phenotype, ancestry, or origin — not a biological reality but a political technology of domination with material consequences.

Race is a system of classification that assigns human populations to hierarchically ranked categories based on phenotype, ancestry, or geographic origin. The critical insight of modern scholarship is that race is not a biological reality but a political technology — a way of organizing domination that produces the categories it claims to discover.

Karen and Barbara Fields (2012) call this “racecraft”: the practice of treating race as a natural kind, analogous to witchcraft’s treatment of supernatural causation as real. Just as witchcraft creates witches (by accusing people and organizing social life around the accusations), racecraft creates races (by classifying people and organizing social life around the classifications). The races are real in their consequences — they determine who lives where, who works at what, who is killed by police, who inherits wealth — but they are not real in the sense that biological science can detect them. Human genetic variation does not cluster into the categories racial classification imposes.

Michael Omi and Howard Winant (2014) theorize race as “racial formation”: an ongoing, historically situated process in which social, economic, and political forces determine the content and importance of racial categories. Racial categories shift across time and place — the Irish were not “white” in 19th-century America, then became white; Jews were racialized in Europe, then deracialized in some contexts. The categories are political, not natural, and they change when the politics change.

Race intersects with genring in specific ways. The Pahlavi regime’s “Aryanist racism” (Asgharzadeh 2007) deployed racial categories to claim Kurds as fellow Aryans — incorporation through racial classification — while simultaneously suppressing Kurdish culture as inferior. Race provides the classificatory infrastructure; genring provides the narrative content. The racial category says “these people belong to us.” The genre figure says “and this is what they look like.” Together, they produce a population that is claimed and reduced in a single operation.

Race differs from ethnicity in that racial categories are primarily imposed from outside (by the state, by the dominant group) while ethnic categories can be self-adopted. In practice, the boundary between the two is unstable and politically contested.
