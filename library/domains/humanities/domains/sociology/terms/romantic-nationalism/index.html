Romantic Nationalism

2026-04-02 The 18th–19th century movement fusing Romantic aesthetics with national identity — claiming that each people has a unique spirit (Volksgeist) expressed through language, folklore, and custom, and that this spirit must be made visible, preserved, and defended.

Romantic nationalism is the political movement that fuses Romantic aesthetics — the valorization of feeling, tradition, organic community, and the particular over the universal — with national identity. Its founding premise, articulated by Johann Gottfried Herder in the 1780s, is that each people (Volk) possesses a unique spirit (Volksgeist) expressed through its language, songs, myths, and customs. This spirit is what makes a people a people, and it must be made visible, collected, preserved, and defended.

The movement emerged against Enlightenment universalism — the claim that there is one human nature, one reason, one set of rights applicable to all. Romantic nationalists countered that universalism was a mask for French cultural hegemony, and that authentic human life is particular: rooted in a specific language, a specific landscape, a specific tradition. Herder’s intention was pluralist (all peoples are equally dignified in their particularity), but the framework he created was immediately weaponizable. If each people has a spirit, someone must curate it — decide which songs, which tales, which costumes count as authentic. The curation is where genring begins.

The Brothers Grimm, Fichte’s Addresses to the German Nation (1808), the Finnish Kalevala, the Czech National Revival, the Irish Literary Revival, the Pahlavi regime’s deployment of the Shahnameh — all are instances of Romantic nationalism producing the cultural materials that prove the nation exists. Benedict Anderson (1983) called nations “imagined communities”: not fictions, but communities that exist because enough people imagine them the same way. Romantic nationalism is the machinery that produces the shared imagination — the songs, stories, costumes, and festivals that make the nation feel real.

The movement’s legacy is ambivalent. It gave colonized and peripheral peoples a framework for asserting their cultural dignity against imperial homogenization. It also gave dominant nations a framework for genring their own populations and their neighbors into consumable folk types, producing the genre figures that fascism would later recruit as its cast.
