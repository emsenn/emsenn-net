Confederacy

2026-03-31 A union of autonomous groups bound by shared directives but retaining independent governance, where the center coordinates without commanding.

A confederacy is a union of autonomous groups that share a common purpose and bind themselves to shared directives while retaining independent governance. Each member group governs its own internal affairs, sets its own decision-making processes, and can withdraw. The confederacy coordinates across groups but does not command them. Authority rests with the constituent groups; the confederacy holds only what they delegate upward.

The structural distinction from a federation is where final authority sits. In a federation, delegates carry revocable mandates from base assemblies — authority flows upward but the federation itself has no standing to override constituent groups. A confederacy is looser still: the constituent groups are not assemblies of individuals sending delegates but entire self-governing entities that choose to cooperate under shared terms. A federation coordinates people through delegates; a confederacy coordinates groups through directives. The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) confederacy coordinated sovereign nations, each with full internal governance, through the Great Law of Peace — shared principles, not shared administration.

A confederacy differs from a company in that a company has staff (ongoing personnel bearing roles), a governance mechanism that produces decisions attributable to the company as a whole, and institutional standing as an entity. A confederacy has none of these: it has member groups, not staff; coordination, not unified governance; and no single entity-status that makes “the confederacy decided” coherent the way “the company decided” is. The confederacy is the agreement, not an actor.

What a confederacy can do is charter ships — commission specific ventures with specific purposes, crews, and command structures. A confederacy that charters a ship delegates authority to that ship’s captain for the voyage without surrendering the confederacy’s own autonomy. The ship operates hierarchically under its charter; the confederacy that issued the charter does not. This is how autonomous groups produce coordinated action without becoming a hierarchy: not by centralizing, but by commissioning.

¶Chartering capacity

A confederacy can charter multiple ships, for the same or different endeavors. Each ship has its own captain and crew. The confederacy’s directives bind all its ships, but each ship’s charter may add ship-specific rules that do not contradict the confederacy’s directives. The Haudenosaunee confederacy coordinated multiple sovereign nations — Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, and later Tuscarora — each conducting its own affairs, waging its own campaigns, governing its own people. The Great Law of Peace bound them all, but no nation’s internal governance was dictated by the confederacy.

This is how a confederacy produces coordinated action at multiple scales without becoming a hierarchy: each ship it charters has hierarchical command (the captain commands), but the confederacy itself remains a covenant between equals. The ships answer to the confederacy’s directives, not to each other.

¶The flag relation

The confederacy is the flag state for ships it charters. In admiralty, the flag state is the sovereign entity that registers a ship and confers its legal identity. The ship’s identity — its name, its registration, its right to sail — comes from the flag state. A ship without a flag is stateless: it has no legal identity, no port of registry, no authority backing its operations.

When a confederacy charters a ship, it confers identity. The ship sails under the confederacy’s flag. The confederacy’s directives are the highest authority the ship recognizes. The ship’s charter may not contradict the confederacy’s directives — a charter that does is structurally invalid, not merely overridden. This is the same relationship as a bylaw to a constitution: the lower instrument operates within the higher one’s bounds.

The confederacy retains certain powers over every ship it charters: the power to relieve the captain, the power to amend or revoke the charter, and the power to decommission the ship. These powers are not delegated to the ship — they are retained by the commissioning authority. The captain commands the ship; the confederacy commands whether there is a ship.

¶Directive-charter hierarchy

The confederacy’s directives occupy a higher position than any individual ship’s charter. The hierarchy:

  1. Confederacy directives — the shared principles that bind all member groups and all chartered ships. These are the covenant.
  2. Ship charter — the constitutive document for a specific ship, issued under the confederacy’s authority. Must be consistent with confederacy directives.
  3. Captain’s orders — operational decisions within the charter’s bounds.

A conflict between a ship’s charter and the confederacy’s directives is resolved in favor of the directives. A conflict between the captain’s orders and the charter is resolved in favor of the charter. The hierarchy is strict — not advisory, not subject to override by the captain’s judgment. If the captain believes the directives are wrong, the captain can petition the confederacy for amendment. The captain cannot unilaterally override.

The word comes from Latin confoederatio — con (together) + foedus (covenant, treaty). A confederacy is a covenant between equals, not a government over subjects.
