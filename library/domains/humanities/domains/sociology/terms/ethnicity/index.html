Ethnicity

2026-04-02 A social category constituted through shared language, descent, religion, custom, or territory — not a natural kind but a political formation produced through boundary-drawing, state classification, and the ongoing negotiation of who belongs.

Ethnicity is a social category constituted through shared language, descent, religion, custom, or territory. The critical insight of modern scholarship is that ethnic groups are not natural kinds — they are not discovered but produced. Fredrik Barth’s foundational argument (1969) is that ethnicity is constituted not by the cultural content within a group but by the boundaries between groups. What makes an ethnic group is not that its members share all the same practices but that they maintain a distinction between themselves and others. The boundary persists even as the cultural content on either side changes.

This means ethnicity is a political formation, not a biological or cultural essence. States produce ethnic categories through census classification, legal recognition, language policy, and territorial administration. Colonial states drew ethnic boundaries that had no prior existence, then administered populations according to those boundaries until the boundaries became real — people organized around them, fought over them, died for them. Post-colonial states inherited these categories and the conflicts they generated.

Rogers Brubaker (2004) pushes further: we should study ethnicity without assuming the existence of “ethnic groups” as bounded, coherent entities. What exists are ethnic categories (classifications imposed or adopted), ethnic identifications (acts of self- and other-classification), and ethnic organizations (institutions that claim to represent an ethnic constituency). The reification of these into a “group” — a thing with interests, will, and agency — is itself a political act.

Kurdish ethnicity is a paradigmatic case. Denise Natali (2005) demonstrates that Kurdish identity is “not fixed or natural” but shaped by the development of nation-states: Kurds in Turkey, Iran, Iraq, and Syria developed different ethnic self-understandings in response to different state formations. The genring operation — reducing a people to a fixed type — treats ethnicity as essence. The lived reality is that ethnicity is process: boundary-drawing, negotiation, contestation, and the ongoing production of who counts as what.
