Ethnocide

2026-04-02 The deliberate destruction of a people's culture — language, religion, custom, social organization — while leaving the physical population alive. Distinguished from genocide (which targets physical existence) but operating through the same logic of elimination.

Ethnocide is the deliberate destruction of a people’s culture — their language, religion, customs, social organization, and systems of meaning — while leaving the physical population alive. Pierre Clastres (1974) distinguished ethnocide from genocide: genocide kills bodies; ethnocide kills cultures. The boarding school system in the United States and Canada (“Kill the Indian, save the man”) is the paradigmatic case: the physical person survives, but the Lakota, the Mohawk, the Cree, the Navajo are destroyed as cultural and political entities.

The distinction matters analytically but can mislead politically. Patrick Wolfe’s structural analysis of settler colonialism demonstrates that physical and cultural elimination are not separate phenomena but two expressions of a single logic: the elimination of the native. The boarding school, the language ban, the linguicide, the forced renaming, the suppression of ceremony — these are not lesser versions of genocide. They are the same operation at a different scale.

The Kurdish case demonstrates ethnocide across four states simultaneously. Turkey banned the Kurdish language, dress, music, and public identity for decades. Iran classified Kurdish as a “rustic dialect” and suppressed Kurdish education, media, and cultural expression. Syria stripped Kurdish citizens of their nationality. Iraq destroyed Kurdish villages and displaced populations while maintaining Kurds as a demographic category. In each case, the target was not the Kurdish body but Kurdish heteroglossia — the linguistic, religious, political, and cultural diversity that makes Kurdish civilization what it is.

Ethnocide is what genring accomplishes when it succeeds completely. If the genre figure fully replaces the people — if the living culture is reduced to a consumable type and the type is all that remains — the culture has been destroyed while the bodies persist. The people continue to exist biologically. But what made them that people — their particular languages, their internal debates, their intellectual traditions, their ways of organizing life — is gone.
