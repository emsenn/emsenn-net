Tradition

2026-04-02 The transmission of practices, knowledge, and meanings across generations — not a static inheritance but an active process of receiving, interpreting, and transforming what was passed down. The concept genring freezes and Romantic nationalism worships.

Tradition is the transmission of practices, knowledge, and meanings across generations. The word comes from Latin traditio (“handing over”), and the handing over is the key: tradition is not a fixed deposit but an active process. Each generation receives what was passed down, interprets it in light of present circumstances, transforms what needs transforming, and passes the result forward. Tradition is living when this process is ongoing. It is dead when the process has been frozen into repetition.

Hans-Georg Gadamer (1960) argued that all understanding occurs within tradition — that we are always already embedded in inherited frameworks of meaning, and that the act of interpretation is a dialogue between our present horizon and the horizon of what was handed down. Tradition on this account is not the opposite of reason but its condition: we think from tradition, not against it. Edward Shils (1981) extended the analysis sociologically, showing that all societies depend on the transmission of beliefs, practices, and institutions, and that even revolutionary movements transmit their own traditions.

The political problem arises when tradition is opposed to modernity as a binary. Under this binary, “traditional” means static, pre-modern, unchanged — and “modern” means dynamic, progressive, transformed. This binary is the engine of both colonial domination (Indigenous peoples classified as “traditional” and therefore available for displacement or assimilation) and Romantic nationalism (which worships tradition as the authentic life modernity has corrupted).

Both poles falsify what tradition actually is. A living tradition is neither static nor abandoned. It is the temporal middle — the position of inheriting, continuing, and transforming. A Lakota ceremony adapted for contemporary conditions is traditional. Kurdish dengbej performed on satellite television is traditional. What makes them traditional is not that they are unchanged but that they are continuous — that the thread of transmission has not been cut, even as it has been stretched, knotted, and rewoven.

Genring freezes tradition. The invention of tradition manufactures it. Both operations destroy the living process by replacing it with an image of itself.
