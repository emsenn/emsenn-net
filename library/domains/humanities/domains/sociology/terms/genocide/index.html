Genocide

2026-04-02 The deliberate destruction of a people as such — not merely mass killing but the systematic elimination of a group's capacity to exist as a group. Raphael Lemkin's coinage, expanded by subsequent scholarship to include cultural, linguistic, and epistemic dimensions.
Defines genocide

Genocide is the deliberate destruction of a people as such. Raphael Lemkin coined the term in 1944 (from Greek genos, “race/people,” and Latin -cide, “killing”) to name what the existing legal vocabulary could not: the systematic effort to eliminate not individuals but a group’s capacity to exist as a group. The 1948 UN Convention defines genocide as acts committed “with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnical, racial or religious group” — including killing, causing serious bodily or mental harm, inflicting conditions calculated to bring about physical destruction, preventing births, and forcibly transferring children.

The concept’s analytical power lies in what it names beyond mass killing. Genocide is not defined by body count but by intent to destroy the group as a group. The Indian boarding school system (“Kill the Indian, save the man”) is genocidal not because it killed students (though many died) but because it was designed to destroy Lakota, Mohawk, Navajo, and other nations as cultural and political entities. The Anfal campaign against Kurdish peoples (1986–1989) combined mass killing with village destruction, displacement, and cultural erasure — the physical and the cultural operating as a single structure.

Patrick Wolfe (2006) extended the analysis: under settler colonialism, genocide is not an event but a structure. The elimination of Indigenous peoples continues as long as the settler state exists on Indigenous land — through legal erasure (blood quantum), epistemic erasure (suppression of knowledge systems), linguistic erasure (linguicide), and spatial erasure (land seizure). Physical killing is one mechanism among many. The structure is the elimination of the native, and it operates at every scale.

The Yezidi genocide of 2014 — approximately 5,000 killed, 10,800 women and girls enslaved — demonstrates how genring authorizes genocide. The “devil-worshipper” genre figure, circulated for centuries, provided the narrative infrastructure: it told the Islamic State’s fighters who Yezidis were and what could be done to them. The genre preceded the killing by hundreds of years. It was ready when the killing came.
