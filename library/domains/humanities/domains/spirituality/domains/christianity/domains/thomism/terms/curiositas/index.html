Curiositas

2026-04-27 The Thomist vice of disordered cognitive appetite: the desire to know that has been severed from its proper ends and that consumes its subject without orienting them to truth. Curiositas is restless, distracted, and self-perpetuating; it desires the knowing-as-such, regardless of whether the known is worth knowing or fits the knower's vocation. Structurally opposed to studiositas (the virtuous discipline of cognitive appetite).

Curiositas is the Thomist vice of disordered cognitive appetite: the desire to know that has been severed from its proper ends and that consumes its subject without orienting them to truth. Aquinas treats it (II-II, q. 167) as the deformation of an appetite that is good in itself: humans naturally desire to know, the desire is part of their constitution, and the desire becomes vicious when it loses its orientation. The curious subject knows in order to know more; the knowing has become the end. The result is restlessness, scattering, an inability to be present to any one thing because the next thing is always pulling.

¶Etymology and lineage

The Latin curiositas (from cura, care, attention) names a kind of attention that has gone wrong — attention that scatters across objects without resting in any. Augustine’s Confessions (Book X) is the upstream source: Augustine analyzes the concupiscentia oculorum (lust of the eyes), the appetite for spectacle and for new sights that operates under the cover of legitimate inquiry. The Augustinian critique is preserved through the monastic tradition; Bernard of Clairvaux’s On the Steps of Humility and Pride (1124) places curiositas as the first step on the ladder of pride — the soul’s withdrawal from its own end through restless looking-elsewhere.

Aquinas systematizes the patristic-monastic critique within his Aristotelian-virtue framework. He treats curiositas as the vice opposed to studiositas, the virtue that moderates cognitive appetite. Aquinas identifies several species of curiositas (knowledge sought from a defective end, knowledge sought from defective sources, knowledge sought beyond one’s capacity, knowledge that displaces the goods one is obligated to attend to) and several characteristic effects (vagrancy of mind, talkativeness, restlessness, the inability to remain in one place or with one practice).

The concept’s history is decisively shaped by early modernity. Blumenberg (1983) traces the rehabilitation of curiositas in the scientific revolution: Bacon, Galileo, and the broader new-science movement recoded curiositas as curiosity, a virtue rather than a vice, the engine of legitimate inquiry into nature. The Thomist categories survived in religious and pedagogical contexts but lost their normative dominance in the broader intellectual culture. Daston and Park (2007) document the institutional channels (cabinets of wonder, scientific societies, museums) that operationalized the rehabilitated curiosity.

The contemporary recovery (Griffiths 2009 most explicitly) argues that platformal conditions have made the original Thomist diagnostic newly relevant. The contemporary subject is not deprived of information but flooded with it; the appetite that brings them to the platform is curiositas in something close to the strict Thomist sense; the platform is engineered to produce and sustain curiositas as the disposition that maximizes engagement.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a disposition as curiositas in the Thomist sense.

Severance of knowing from its ends. The curious subject pursues knowledge without orientation. The question of what the knowledge is for has dropped out; the activity of knowing has become self-sustaining. This is what distinguishes curiositas from a studious investigation that happens to range widely: the studious investigation is held by an end; the curious activity has no end other than itself.

Restlessness as constitutive. Curiositas is structurally restless. The subject who has just acquired one fact, one update, one piece of news cannot stay with it; the next fact is pulling. This is not a moral weakness in the curious subject but the form of the vice: the appetite, severed from its ends, has nothing to come to rest in. The restlessness is the condition; the constant looking-elsewhere is what curiositas is, not how it manifests.

Vacuity of the curious knowing. The subject who has spent years pursuing curiositas has not become wiser. They have become more saturated, more current, more aware of more things — but the saturation is not knowledge in the strong sense. Knowledge in the strong sense binds, orients, deepens; curiositas does none of these. The curious subject can recite a great deal and use very little of it.

¶Examples

The doomscroller. The subject who refreshes the feed every few minutes, absorbing news, reactions, takes, threads, none of which orient action, all of which produce a feeling of being-informed that displaces the question of what one would do with information if one had it. This is curiositas in its contemporary mass form; the platformal apparatus is engineered to produce and sustain it.

The compulsive Wikipedia browser. The subject who follows links from one article to the next across an evening, accumulating facts with no purpose, never returning to any subject deeply enough to build understanding. The accumulated facts function as quasi-knowledge — the subject can drop them in conversation — but they do not bind into a usable understanding.

The graduate student in unfocused reading. The student who reads three papers from each of fifteen subfields and develops familiarity with all of them but mastery of none. The reading is sincere; the desire to know is real; but the reading is not held by an end. This is curiositas in an academic register; the contemporary academic apparatus often rewards it.

The contrast case: the studious knower. The investigator who returns to the same question for years, reading deeply, allowing the question to develop, accumulating understanding rather than information. The studious knower may know fewer things than the curious one but knows what they know in a different way: held, oriented, available to use.

¶Adjacent concepts

Curiositas is not the same as curiosity in the contemporary positive sense (the disposition to inquire, to ask questions, to be interested in the world). The contemporary positive sense is closer to what Aquinas means by studiositas: the proper love of knowledge. The historical accident is that the Latin term curiositas was rehabilitated as the English word curiosity without preserving the Thomist distinction. This makes the Thomist vocabulary easy to misread: when Aquinas condemns curiositas, he is not condemning what contemporary English calls curiosity.

Curiositas is not the same as distraction in the ordinary sense. Distraction is the involuntary pulling of attention away from one’s task; curiositas is the voluntary structure of the appetite that produces the involuntary pulling. A subject who is distracted but holds curiositas in check is in a different condition from a subject who has handed themselves over to curiositas as a way of life.

Curiositas is not the same as information addiction. Information addiction is a behavioral-clinical category that names a compulsion; curiositas is a moral-philosophical category that names the structure of an appetite. The two can coincide and frequently do, but the conceptual frames are different and they license different practical responses.

¶Open questions

Is curiositas the right name for what platforms cultivate? The Thomist concept presupposes a knowing subject who has appetites; platformal feedback architecture may produce something less than that — a subject whose attention is being processed without their being fully a subject of the engagement. Whether the contemporary condition is curiositas in the strict sense or something for which the Thomist vocabulary needs amendment is a productive question.

How does curiositas relate to acedia? The two vices are structurally adjacent: acedia is the inability to remain with the good one knows one should pursue, and curiositas is the appetite that pulls one away from such remaining. They reinforce each other. The cybernetic-postliberal framework treats the platformal apparatus as continuously generating both, with each making the other harder to address.

What practices counter curiositas under contemporary conditions? The historical practices (slow reading, monastic silence, sustained scholarly tradition, friendship anchored in shared deep work) presupposed institutional infrastructure that has thinned. Whether contemporary subjects can reconstitute the practices, and whether the reconstitution can survive the platformal apparatus’s continuous pull, is the live question.
