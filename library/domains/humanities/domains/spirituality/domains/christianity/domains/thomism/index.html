Thomism

¶Thomas Aquinas

Thomas Aquinas (c. 1225-1274) was a Dominican friar and theologian whose Summa Theologiae (1265-1273) is the canonical synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy with Christian theology. The Summa covers God, creation, the human soul, the virtues and vices, the law, grace, and the sacraments, in a structure that takes up disputed questions, presents objections, resolves them, and answers each objection in turn. The work was unfinished at Aquinas’s death; the part on the sacraments was completed posthumously by his secretary from notes and reportata.

For cybernetic postliberalism, Thomism is consequential as the conceptual apparatus the medieval Christian pastoral institution used to govern souls. The doctrines of the virtues and vices, the structures of confession and absolution, the categories of sin and merit — these are the upstream institutional grammar that the framework treats as the prior form of californication. The seal of confession, the institutional capacity to keep what passed between confessor and penitent inside the church, is the energetic interior the contemporary platformal apparatus has lost.

The framework draws particularly on the Thomist analysis of the affects: acedia (the spiritual sloth that knows what it should do but cannot rouse itself to do it), spes (the theological virtue of hope, structured as expectation of a good not yet possessed), studiositas (the disciplined and proportioned love of knowledge), and curiositas (the disordered desire for knowledge that consumes its subject without ordering them to truth). These are not historical curiosities; they are the calibrations the pastoral apparatus performed, and the contemporary apparatus performs successor versions of them under different institutional conditions.
