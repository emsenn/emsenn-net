Spes

2026-04-27 The Thomist theological virtue of hope: the disposition by which the soul tends toward a future good that is difficult but possible to attain, structured as expectation rather than certainty. Spes is bounded by despair (the conviction that the good cannot be attained) on one side and by presumption (the assumption that the good can be attained without effort or grace) on the other.

Spes is the Thomist theological virtue of hope: the disposition by which the soul tends toward a future good that is difficult but possible to attain, structured as expectation rather than certainty. Where the natural virtues (prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance) order the soul to natural ends within reach of natural reason, the theological virtues (faith, hope, charity) order the soul to a supernatural end beyond natural reach. Spes is the virtue specifically concerned with the temporal structure of the soul’s relation to that end — what it means to expect, to wait, to trust the good is coming without claiming to have it already.

¶Etymology and lineage

The Latin spes covers the same conceptual range as English hope: expectation of a good not yet possessed. Greek elpis is the Septuagint and New Testament term that the Latin tradition rendered as spes. The theological virtue is one of three (faith, hope, charity / fides, spes, caritas) that Paul names in 1 Corinthians 13 and that the patristic and medieval traditions take as the structural backbone of the Christian moral life.

Aquinas’s treatment in the Summa Theologiae (II-II, qq. 17-22) is the canonical synthesis. He distinguishes the passion of hope (a generic affective tendency toward a difficult good, present in animals and in non-virtuous humans) from the virtue of hope (the perfected disposition oriented to the supernatural good of beatitude). The virtue is bounded by two opposed vices: desperatio (despair, the certainty that the good cannot be attained) on the side of deficit, and praesumptio (presumption, the assumption that the good can be attained without grace or without the effort grace requires) on the side of excess. Acedia is associated structurally with despair as one of its daughters; the vice that opposes hope on the side of weariness is the vice that opposes joy in the good as such.

The concept has a long modern reception. Marcel (1962), writing in the existentialist register, develops hope as the structural condition of the wayfaring (viator) subject — the subject who is on the way without yet being at the end. Bloch (1986), writing in a secular-Marxist register, takes hope as utopian-anticipatory consciousness and as a fundamental category of human existence. Moltmann (1967), writing as a Protestant eschatologist, makes hope the orienting category of theology as a whole rather than one virtue among others. Lear (2006), writing as an analytic philosopher engaging with the Crow leader Plenty Coups’s response to cultural devastation, develops “radical hope” as a category for hoping in the face of conditions that have destroyed the framework within which the hoped-for could be specified.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a disposition as spes in the Thomist sense.

Orientation to a good that is difficult but possible. Spes presupposes that the good is real (against despair) and that it has not yet been attained (against the false-positive presumption that one already possesses it). The temporal structure is the structure of the not-yet: the subject who hopes is between empty desire and fulfilled possession, and the virtue is the disciplined inhabiting of the between.

Tension with the present condition. Spes is not optimism. Optimism is a generalized expectation that things will go well; spes is a directed expectation that a specific good will be attained, which presupposes the recognition that present conditions are inadequate to the good. The hopeful subject sees clearly what is wrong with the present; the hope is what makes the seeing endurable.

Difference from certainty. The hopeful subject does not know that the good will be attained. They expect it on the basis of the trust they have placed in something — in Aquinas’s framework, in God’s promise, mediated by faith and charity. The structural feature of hope is that it does not eliminate uncertainty. A subject who knows the good will be attained does not need hope; a subject who knows the good cannot be attained cannot have hope; the subject of hope inhabits the gap.

¶Examples

The wayfarer’s hope. The pilgrim who has set out for a destination they have not yet reached — Marcel’s homo viator. The pilgrim’s hope is not an opinion about probable outcomes; it is the structural condition of the journey. To stop hoping is to stop being on the way, and to stop being on the way is, in the relevant sense, to die.

Plenty Coups’s radical hope. Lear’s reading of the Crow leader’s response to the destruction of the Crow way of life: a hope structured to outlive the framework within which any specific good could be named. The hoped-for is not specifiable in the old terms; what survives is the bare orientation to a future, in trust that the good can be reconstituted in conditions not yet in view.

The political subject after defeat. The organizer whose movement has been crushed, who continues to do the work, who refuses both the despair that says nothing can be done and the presumption that the work itself guarantees the outcome. This is spes in a secular-political register, structurally adjacent to the theological virtue without taking up its theological frame.

The contrast case: the achievement-subject’s optimism. The achievement-subject’s “you can” runs on optimism — the generalized expectation that things will work out if one performs adequately — rather than on spes. The achievement-subject who burns out has not had hope crushed; they had no hope to begin with. The exhaustion they feel is the exhaustion of optimism colliding with conditions.

¶Adjacent concepts

Spes is not the same as optimism in the contemporary psychological sense. Optimism is a stable expectation about probable outcomes, often framed as a personality trait or a cognitive disposition. Spes is a virtue oriented to a specific good and structured by trust rather than estimation. The two can be in tension: an optimistic subject may have no hope (their optimism is a substitute for hope), and a hopeful subject may be unrelenting in their pessimism about probable outcomes.

Spes is not the same as anticipation in Bloch’s strict sense, though the two are adjacent. Anticipation is the cognitive structure by which subjects orient toward a not-yet that draws them; hope is the affective-volitional register in which the orientation becomes a way of life. Bloch’s secular extension of the concept productively crosses the theological category, but the cross-translation is not a synonymy.

Spes is not the same as resilience in the contemporary sense. Resilience names the capacity to recover from disturbance; hope names the orientation to a good that has not yet been attained. A resilient subject who has lost their hope is not the same as a hopeful subject who is in a phase of being unable to recover; the categories address different dimensions of the inner life.

¶Open questions

Is spes available under conditions of operational closure? If late-liberal governance metabolizes every coherent subject-position, the question is whether hope itself becomes a genre the system absorbs (the inspiration economy, the optimism industry, the “hope is a discipline” hashtag). Lear’s radical hope is one philosophical attempt at an answer; the framework of cybernetic postliberalism holds the question open.

How does spes relate to weak messianic power? Both are structures of relation to a not-yet, but their direction differs: weak messianic power is structured by the past’s claim on the present, while spes is structured by the future good’s draw on the present. Whether they are dual aspects of the same structural condition or distinct registers is a productive question.

What conditions sustain spes when the institutional infrastructure that historically carried it has thinned? The Christian pastoral apparatus once held spes within practices, sacraments, and communal life that gave it durable form. The contemporary subject faces hope without that infrastructure. Whether secular practices (movements, traditions, kinship-networks) can carry the work the institution once carried is the live question.
