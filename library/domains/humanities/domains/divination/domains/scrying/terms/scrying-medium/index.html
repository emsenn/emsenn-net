A scrying medium is a physical surface or substance used as a focus for scrying — a divinatory practice in which the practitioner gazes into the medium to receive images, impressions, or insights.

Common scrying media include:

  • Water — still water in a dark bowl is one of the oldest and most widely documented media, used in ancient Egyptian, Greek, and many Indigenous traditions
  • Mirrors — especially dark or obsidian mirrors; the Aztec tezcatl (obsidian mirror) and the Elizabethan “shew-stone” tradition both use reflective darkness
  • Crystal — polished crystal spheres (the classic “crystal ball”) use internal reflections and optical distortions to create visual ambiguity
  • Fire and smoke — fire-gazing (pyromancy) and smoke-reading (capnomancy) use the shifting patterns of flame and smoke as the interpretive substrate
  • Ink or oil on water — drops of dark liquid on a water surface create temporary patterns that are read as signs

What makes a surface suitable for scrying is not any mystical property of the material but its capacity to present the viewer with visual ambiguity — enough light and pattern to engage perception, but not enough structure to resolve into a clear, stable image. The medium sits at the threshold between seeing and imagining, and the practitioner’s interpretive attention does the rest.
