Scansion

2026-04-30
Table of contents
  • The marks
  • Scansion is interpretation
  • Why scansion matters
  • What scansion is not

Scansion is the analytic technique for marking, on the page, which syllables of a line of metrical verse are stressed and which are unstressed, so that the meter the line is keeping — and the places it departs from that meter — can be read off explicitly. The word comes from Latin scandere (“to climb”), via the medieval grammarians’ practice of climbing a line foot by foot to verify its measure.

To scan a line is to make its metrical contract visible. The reader who can scan can say with precision: this is iambic pentameter, with a trochaic substitution in the first foot and a feminine ending after the fifth. The reader who cannot scan can hear that the line is metrical and feel the substitutions, but cannot name what is happening or why a different line at a different point in the poem is doing something subtly different.

¶The marks

The standard scansion marks date to nineteenth-century classical pedagogy and have not changed substantially since.

  • A breve ( ˘ ) over a syllable marks it as unstressed.
  • A macron ( ¯ ), or sometimes an acute accent, marks a syllable as stressed.
  • A vertical bar ( | ) divides one foot from the next.
  • A double bar ( ‖ ) marks a caesura, a deliberate pause inside the line.

A scanned line of iambic pentameter looks roughly like this:

  ˘   ¯  |  ˘   ¯  |  ˘   ¯  |  ˘   ¯  |  ˘    ¯
Shall I | com-PARE | thee TO | a SUM | -mer's DAY

In practice, scholars use whichever marking system is cleanest in their typesetting environment. Capital letters for stressed syllables and lowercase for unstressed (shall I com-PARE thee TO a SUM-mer’s DAY) is common in informal contexts and in introductory texts.

¶Scansion is interpretation

Scansion is not a mechanical transcription of the line. It is an interpretation — a claim about how the line should be heard. Many lines admit more than one defensible scansion, and the disagreement is itself substantive. Is the second foot of “Shall I compare thee to a summer’s day” com-PARE (an iamb) or COM-pare (a trochee)? The first reading keeps the meter; the second creates a substitution that emphasizes the act of comparison. A skilled scanner does not simply count beats but argues for a hearing of the line.

This is why scansion is taught as a skill rather than a procedure. Two readers can scan the same line two different ways, and the difference between their scansions is the difference between their readings of the poem.

¶Why scansion matters

A reader who learns to scan acquires a tool the unaided ear cannot replace. The ear notices that some lines feel different from others, but it does not always know why. Scansion makes the why explicit. A line that feels slow and weighty often turns out, on scansion, to contain a spondee or a cluster of stressed syllables. A line that feels rushed often turns out to contain anapestic substitutions or a series of unstressed syllables in close succession. A line that feels broken often turns out to have a caesura falling at a metrically unexpected point. Scansion turns a vague impression into a specific observation, and a specific observation can be checked, contested, and built on.

The skill rewards practice. A reader who has scanned a hundred lines hears patterns the unscanning reader misses, and reads metrical poetry with proportionally more pleasure.

¶What scansion is not

Scansion is not the same as recitation. A scanned line is not a script for how to read the line aloud. A reader who delivered every stressed syllable as a thumping beat would produce a parody of metrical reading, not a competent one. Spoken delivery softens the metrical contract — it lets the meter run underneath natural speech rather than over it. Scansion exposes the contract; recitation honors it without exposing it. Both are needed, and they are different activities.

Scansion is also not a verdict on a poem’s quality. A perfectly regular line is not better than a line full of substitutions, and a line full of substitutions is not better than a regular one. What scansion reveals is what the line is doing; the question of whether what it is doing serves the poem is a separate, harder question that scansion cannot settle on its own.
