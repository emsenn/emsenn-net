Foot

2026-04-30

A foot is the basic unit of measure in accentual-syllabic verse — a small, fixed grouping of syllables with a prescribed stress pattern. A line of metrical poetry is built by repeating the same foot a fixed number of times, with allowable substitutions. The term is borrowed from classical Greek prosody, where it referred to the up-and-down movement of the foot in keeping time, and English-language scholarship inherited the word along with the analytic technique.

The foot is an analytic abstraction, not a sounded unit. A reader does not hear the foot as a separate beat; the reader hears the line. The foot is a tool for naming the pattern the line is keeping — a way of saying that iambic pentameter is five iambs rather than restating the entire stress sequence each time.

¶The four primary feet

English-language poetry runs almost entirely on four feet.

  • The iamb is one unstressed syllable followed by one stressed syllable: da-DUM. (“To-DAY,” “a-LONE.”)
  • The trochee is one stressed syllable followed by one unstressed syllable: DUM-da. (“GAR-den,” “FALL-ing.”)
  • The anapest is two unstressed syllables followed by one stressed syllable: da-da-DUM. (“in-ter-VENE,” “un-der-STAND.”)
  • The dactyl is one stressed syllable followed by two unstressed syllables: DUM-da-da. (“MER-ri-ly,” “PO-e-try.”)

The iamb and the anapest are rising feet — they end on a stress. The trochee and the dactyl are falling feet — they begin on a stress. A line built from rising feet feels driven forward toward each stressed landing; a line built from falling feet feels insistent or chant-like, beginning with the emphasis already in place.

¶Substitution feet

Two further feet appear almost exclusively as substitutions inside lines built from the four above.

  • The spondee is two stressed syllables: DUM-DUM. (“HEART-BREAK,” “DEAD WEIGHT.”)
  • The pyrrhic is two unstressed syllables: da-da.

A spondee substituted into an iambic line doubles the weight of two consecutive syllables and slows the line audibly. A pyrrhic substituted into an iambic line drops the expected stress and makes the line lighter. Pyrrhics and spondees often appear together — a pyrrhic-plus-spondee pair fills the metrical space of two iambs while shifting the stress placement, and the combined effect is one of the most common variations in English iambic verse.

¶Why this small set covers so much

Other feet are catalogued in older prosody manuals — the amphibrach, the cretic, the bacchius, the molossus, several others — but they rarely organize whole English lines. The reason is that English’s natural rhythm is binary and rising: a typical English phrase moves in alternations of unstressed and stressed syllables, with the stress slightly more salient. The iamb captures that movement directly, and the trochee captures it inverted. Anapests and dactyls extend the same alternation to triple feet. The remaining feet are either too rigid (the spondee, the pyrrhic, sustained over a whole line) or too foreign to English’s natural patterns (the cretic, the molossus) to organize a tradition.

¶The foot is a unit of pattern, not of sense

A common beginner’s mistake in scansion is to confuse foot boundaries with word boundaries or with sense boundaries. They have nothing to do with each other. The line “Shall I com|pare thee | to a sum|mer’s day” has its words and clauses arranged across the iambic feet without regard for where one foot ends and the next begins. This is not a defect; it is the source of much of the line’s expressive force. The foot pattern runs underneath the syntax, and the two interact — sometimes coinciding, sometimes pulling in different directions — to give the line its particular feel.
