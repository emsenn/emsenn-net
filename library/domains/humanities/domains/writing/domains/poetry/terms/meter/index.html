Meter

2026-03-05

Meter is the abstract pattern that a metrical poem keeps a near-bargain with — a fixed template of stressed and unstressed syllables, of syllable counts, or of long and short syllables, against which each spoken line is heard. The word comes from Greek métron (“measure”), and the measuring is the point: a metrical line is one whose ear-shape has been counted in advance.

Meter does not live on the page. It lives in the listener. A line of iambic pentameter does not become metrical because a poet has typed ten syllables; it becomes metrical because a reader who knows the pattern hears each syllable land on or off the beat the pattern predicts. The poem and the reader together perform the meter — the poem proposes it, the reader carries it, and the spoken line either fulfills, delays, or breaks it. This is why meter has expressive force. A line that meets the pattern exactly settles into it; a line that violates the pattern registers as violation only because the pattern is already in the ear.

¶The four metrical systems

Languages count different things, so meter takes different forms.

  • Accentual-syllabic meter, the dominant system in English from Chaucer onward, counts both the number of syllables in a line and the placement of stresses among them. A line of iambic pentameter has ten syllables arranged into five iambs, each iamb an unstressed syllable followed by a stressed one.
  • Accentual meter, the system of Old English alliterative verse, of much nursery rhyme, and of the looser strong-stress poetry of Hopkins and the Anglo-Saxon revival, counts only the stresses; the number of unstressed syllables between them varies freely.
  • Syllabic meter counts only syllables and ignores stress. It is the dominant system of Japanese (the haiku’s 5-7-5), French, and most Romance traditions. English-language syllabic poetry — Marianne Moore, Dylan Thomas’s “Fern Hill” — sounds metrical to the writer but tends to read as cadenced free verse to ears not trained on syllable counts.
  • Quantitative meter, the system of classical Greek and Latin, counts long and short syllables by their phonetic duration rather than their stress. English lacks the durational distinctions that quantitative meter requires, so attempts to import it (Sidney, Campion, Bridges) have produced curiosities rather than a tradition.

English settled on accentual-syllabic verse because English is a stress-timed language whose stress patterns are loud, regular, and audible. The accentual-syllabic system makes the loud thing the countable thing.

¶Feet, lines, and named meters

In accentual-syllabic verse, the basic unit of measure is the foot: a small grouping of syllables with a fixed stress pattern. The four feet that carry almost all English verse are the iamb (unstressed–stressed: da-DUM), the trochee (stressed–unstressed: DUM-da), the anapest (unstressed–unstressed–stressed: da-da-DUM), and the dactyl (stressed–unstressed–unstressed: DUM-da-da). Two further feet — the spondee (stressed–stressed) and the pyrrhic (unstressed–unstressed) — exist mostly as substitutions inside lines built from the others.

A meter is named by combining the foot with the number of feet per line. Iambic pentameter is five iambs. Trochaic tetrameter is four trochees. Anapestic trimeter is three anapests. The English-language meter is iambic pentameter; almost every other named meter sits in relation to it.

Scansion is the analytic technique for marking which syllables in a line are stressed and which are not, so that the line’s meter — and its departures from meter — can be read off.

¶Meter and rhythm

Meter is not rhythm, and confusing the two empties the concept. Rhythm is what the spoken line actually does: its real distribution of emphasis, pause, speed, and pitch. Meter is the template the line is heard against. The relation between them is the engine of metrical poetry. A line that follows its meter exactly tends to feel mechanical; a line that departs from the meter at a chosen syllable can feel urgent, hesitant, broken, or tender, depending on where and how it departs.

The standard metrical departures — a trochaic substitution at the head of an iambic line, a spondee where an iamb was expected, a feminine ending that hangs an extra unstressed syllable past the last stress — are not failures of the meter but uses of it. They register as expressive only because the unbroken pattern is already running underneath.

¶Meter and free verse

Free verse is poetry that has dropped meter as a governing structure. It is not unrhythmic, and it is not unmusical, but it does not keep a bargain with a counted pattern. What free verse loses is the specific tension between expectation and delivery that metrical poetry runs on; what it gains is the freedom to organize each poem around its own emergent shape.

Metrical patterns nevertheless surface inside free-verse poems. A sudden run of iambs in an otherwise irregular poem will read as a moment of formality, of inevitability, of the poem briefly remembering an older inheritance. The metrical effect is local rather than structural, but it is the same effect: the ear recognizes a pattern, and the poem either continues it or lets it fall away.

¶Why meter survives

The twentieth century declared meter obsolete more than once, and metrical poetry continues to be written. The reason is that meter does something free verse cannot: it binds the poem to a fixed expectation in the body of the reader, and then exploits that expectation for meaning. A reader of metrical verse is participating in a contract — I will hear five iambs, and you will mostly give me five iambs — and every place the poem keeps or breaks the contract is a place where meaning happens. That contract is a constraint, and the constraint is the source of the form’s expressive range.
