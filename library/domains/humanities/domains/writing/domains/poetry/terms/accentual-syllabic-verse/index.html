Accentual-Syllabic Verse

2026-04-30
Table of contents

  • Related systems

Accentual-syllabic verse is the metrical system that counts both the number of syllables in a line and the placement of stresses among them. A line is built from a fixed number of feet, each foot a small repeating unit (the iamb, the trochee, the anapest, the dactyl) with a prescribed stress pattern. The line is named by combining the foot with the count: iambic pentameter is five iambs, trochaic tetrameter is four trochees, anapestic trimeter is three anapests.

This is the metrical system of nearly all canonical English-language poetry from Chaucer through the early twentieth century. Shakespeare, Milton, Donne, Pope, Wordsworth, Keats, Tennyson, Browning, Frost, Auden, Wilbur — almost every poet a reader is likely to encounter in an English-language anthology before about 1920 wrote primarily in accentual-syllabic verse. “Meter” without further qualification, in an English-language context, almost always means accentual-syllabic meter.

¶Why English uses this system

English is a stress-timed language: stressed syllables fall at roughly equal time intervals, and unstressed syllables compress or stretch to fit between them. Stress is therefore the loudest and most reliably audible feature of an English line. Accentual-syllabic verse takes this loudest feature and makes it the countable feature.

The system works because it counts what English speakers are already hearing. A reader does not have to be trained to notice stresses in English the way a reader of syllabic verse has to be trained to notice syllable counts in English; the stresses are simply there. Accentual-syllabic meter binds the counted thing to the heard thing, and the contract between meter and reader becomes nearly automatic.

Iambic pentameter became the central English-language meter inside this system because the iamb’s rising rhythm (unstressed–stressed) mirrors the way English phrases naturally end on emphasis, and because ten syllables is roughly the length of a complete English sentence — long enough for a thought, short enough for a single breath.

¶How a line is built

An accentual-syllabic line is specified by two numbers and one pattern:

  • A foot type — iambic, trochaic, anapestic, dactylic — fixes the stress pattern of the smallest repeating unit.
  • A count — dimeter (two), trimeter (three), tetrameter (four), pentameter (five), hexameter (six) — fixes how many feet make up the line.
  • The combination yields the line specification: iambic pentameter, trochaic tetrameter, anapestic trimeter.

The line specification is a target, not a straitjacket. Accentual-syllabic verse permits — and effectively requires — substitutions: a trochaic foot at the head of an iambic line, a spondee where an iamb was expected, a feminine ending that hangs an extra unstressed syllable past the closing stress. Without these substitutions, the verse would be intolerably mechanical. With them, the verse acquires its expressive range: each substitution is heard against the prevailing pattern and registers as a deliberate variation.

¶Substitutions and their limits

A substitution that the system tolerates is one the reader can hear as a substitution — that is, one the underlying pattern is strong enough to recover from. A trochaic substitution at the head of an iambic line is freely available; the iambic pattern reasserts itself in the second foot. A spondaic substitution mid-line slows the line audibly but does not break it. A feminine ending extends the line by one unstressed syllable without disturbing the foot count.

A substitution that the system does not tolerate is one that would replace the underlying pattern with a different one. A line of iambic pentameter that contains four trochaic substitutions has stopped being iambic pentameter; the meter has changed. The discipline of writing accentual-syllabic verse is largely the discipline of judging which substitutions a line can absorb without losing its metrical identity.

¶Related systems

Three other metrical systems organize verse in other languages and other traditions. Each makes a different choice about what to count.

  • Accentual verse counts only stresses and lets the syllable count vary freely. This is the system of Old English alliterative poetry (Beowulf, The Wanderer), of much English nursery rhyme and ballad, and of Hopkins’s “sprung rhythm.” Accentual verse sounds looser and more conversational than accentual-syllabic verse.
  • Syllabic verse counts only syllables and ignores stress. This is the system of French, Japanese (haiku), and most Romance traditions. English-language syllabic poetry exists (Marianne Moore) but rarely sounds metrical to English-trained ears, because the counted thing is below the threshold of audible attention in English.
  • Quantitative verse counts long and short syllables by phonetic duration. This is the system of classical Greek and Latin verse (the dactylic hexameter of Homer and Virgil). English lacks the durational distinctions quantitative verse requires; English experiments in quantitative meter have not produced a tradition.

Accentual-syllabic verse is, in this comparative frame, the system that fits English’s own audible structure most closely. It is not metrically richer than the others — Greek quantitative meter is in some ways richer — but it is the system English most easily sustains, and the system inside which the bulk of English-language metrical poetry has been written.
