What Is Metrical Poetry

2026-04-30 An introduction to metrical poetry as a contract between writer and reader's ear. Distinguishes meter from rhythm, surveys the four metrical systems, and explains why English settled on accentual-syllabic verse and what metrical writing offers that free verse cannot.

¶Assumed audience

  • Reading level: comfortable with prose; willing to read poems aloud and listen to them.
  • Background: has read The Line and the Break or otherwise understands the line as poetry’s basic unit.
  • Goal: hear what makes a poem metrical, recognize the major metrical systems, and understand what metrical poetry does that free verse does not.

¶A poem is metrical when its line keeps a bargain

A metrical poem is one whose lines have been counted in advance. Before the poet begins, a pattern has been chosen — a number of stresses, a number of syllables, a fixed alternation of stressed and unstressed — and each line is written to keep a near-bargain with that pattern. The reader, hearing the first few lines, learns the pattern. After that, every line lands either inside the pattern, just outside it, or against it, and each landing has expressive force.

This is what distinguishes metrical poetry from free verse. Free verse organizes its lines by image, by syntax, by breath, by the shape of the poem on the page — by anything except a fixed metrical contract. Metrical poetry binds itself to the contract first and finds its meaning inside it.

The contract is what the reader’s ear is doing. Meter is not a feature of the poem on the page; it is a pattern that the reader carries forward in the body and tests each new line against. This is why metrical writing rewards reading aloud and rewards rereading: the contract is an expectation, and expectation only sharpens once the pattern is learned.

¶Meter is not rhythm

The most common confusion in talking about metrical poetry is to treat meter and rhythm as the same thing. They are not.

Rhythm is what a spoken line actually does — the real distribution of emphasis, pause, pitch, and speed when a person says it out loud. Every line of every poem, metrical or not, has a rhythm.

Meter is the abstract pattern that a metrical poem has committed to in advance. It is the template the line is heard against.

Most lines of metrical verse do not match the meter exactly. A line of iambic pentameter is supposed to be five iambs (da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM); almost no actual line is that regular, and the lines that are tend to feel mechanical. The art of metrical writing is in the gap between the abstract pattern and the spoken line — in the trochee that begins where an iamb was expected, in the spondee that doubles the weight of a key word, in the extra unstressed syllable that lets a thought trail past the closing stress. These departures register as expressive because the meter is running underneath them. Drop the meter and the departures become noise.

¶The four metrical systems

Languages count different things, so meter takes different forms. Four systems cover almost the entire history of European-language poetry.

Accentual-syllabic verse counts both syllables and stresses. A line is built from feet — small repeating units like the iamb or trochee — and named by combining the foot with the number of feet per line. This is the system of Chaucer, Shakespeare, Milton, Pope, Wordsworth, Frost, and the great majority of English-language metrical poetry. Accentual-syllabic verse is the system most readers mean when they say “meter” without specifying.

Accentual verse counts only stresses. The number of unstressed syllables between them is free. This is the system of Old English alliterative verse — the four-stress line of Beowulf, divided by a medial caesura — and of much English nursery rhyme, ballad, and Gerard Manley Hopkins’s “sprung rhythm.” Accentual verse sounds looser and more conversational than accentual-syllabic verse, because the line can vary its syllable count freely while still keeping its beat.

Syllabic verse counts only syllables. Stress placement is free. This is the system of Japanese — the haiku’s 5-7-5 — and of French and most Romance-language poetry. English-language syllabic poetry exists (Marianne Moore, parts of Dylan Thomas) but rarely sounds metrical to English-trained ears, because English ears are listening for stresses and the syllable count is below the level of audible attention.

Quantitative verse counts long and short syllables by their phonetic duration. This is the system of classical Greek and Latin: a “long” syllable in Homeric or Virgilian verse takes roughly twice the time to pronounce as a “short” one, and lines are built from sequences of long and short. English lacks the strong durational distinctions Greek and Latin have, so attempts to write English quantitative verse (Sidney, Campion, Bridges) have produced experiments rather than a tradition.

¶Why English settled on accentual-syllabic

English is a stress-timed language. In English speech, the stressed syllables fall at roughly equal time intervals, and the unstressed syllables compress or stretch to fit between them. This makes stress the loudest and most reliable feature of the spoken line.

Accentual-syllabic meter takes the loudest feature and makes it the countable feature. It picks a stress pattern (alternating, rising, falling, triple), fixes a length (a number of feet per line), and lets the language’s own audible structure do most of the work. This is why iambic pentameter sounds natural in English: the iambic foot mirrors the rising rhythm of unstressed-then-stressed that English phrases tend toward, and ten syllables is roughly the length of a complete English sentence — long enough for a thought, short enough for a single breath.

This is also why no other meter has displaced iambic pentameter as the central English-language meter, despite repeated attempts. Trochaic meters in English have to fight the language’s natural rising rhythm. Pure quantitative or syllabic systems leave the audible thing — stress — uncounted. Iambic pentameter wins by counting what English speakers are already hearing.

¶How to recognize metrical poetry

There is no single test, but there are reliable signs.

Read it aloud. If a steady beat falls into place after two or three lines and the rest of the poem keeps it, the poem is metrical. If the lines feel rhythmic but no fixed beat emerges, the poem is probably free verse with strong rhythmic interest.

Mark the stresses. Take a few lines and mark which syllables you naturally emphasize when reading. If the stressed syllables fall in a regular pattern — every second syllable, every third syllable, two stresses per line, four stresses per line — the poem is metrical. Scansion is the technique for doing this systematically.

Count the syllables. Count the syllables in several lines. If they are consistent — ten in every line, eight in every line, alternating ten and eight — the poem is at minimum syllabically organized, and is probably accentual-syllabic. If the syllable count varies widely, the poem is either accentual or free.

Identify the form. Some forms are metrical by definition: a sonnet is fourteen lines of iambic pentameter, blank verse is unrhymed iambic pentameter, the villanelle is metered, the heroic couplet is iambic-pentameter pairs. If the poem announces itself as one of these forms, it is metrical.

¶Why metrical writing is still practiced

The twentieth century declared meter obsolete more than once. Free verse became, and remains, the dominant mode of contemporary English-language poetry. And yet metrical writing did not disappear. Frost wrote almost entirely in meter through the lifetime of free verse. Auden, Larkin, Bishop, Wilbur, Brodsky, and many living poets have continued to write in fixed meters. A young reader who looks for metrical poetry today will find it.

The reason metrical writing survives is that it does something free verse cannot. A free-verse poem organizes itself around the local rhythms of its own sentences; the reader takes each line as it comes. A metrical poem binds the reader’s ear to a counted expectation, and then uses every fulfillment, delay, and break of that expectation as expressive material. The constraint is the source of the expressive range. A poet who writes in iambic pentameter has fewer choices in any given line than a poet writing free verse — and far more meaningful choices, because every choice is heard against the pattern.

This is the bargain metrical writing offers, and the bargain is what makes it worth learning to read.

¶Guidance

  • Pick a sonnet (Shakespeare, Donne, Hopkins, Frost, Heaney) and a free-verse poem of similar length (Williams, Levertov, Glück, Hass). Read both aloud. Notice when, in the sonnet, you can predict the next stress. Notice that you cannot do this in the free-verse poem, and that the poem is doing something else with your attention instead.
  • Take any line of metrical verse and mark the stresses. Write out the meter the line is supposed to keep. Find every place where the line departs from the meter. Ask what the departure is doing — emphasizing a word, slowing the line, making it lurch, mimicking the thing the line is describing.
  • Read Sound and Meter for a closer look at the toolkit of feet and substitutions, and Fixed Forms and the Sonnet for how meter combines with rhyme scheme and stanza pattern in fixed forms.
