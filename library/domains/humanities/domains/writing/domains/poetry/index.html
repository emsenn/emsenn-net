Poetry

Poetry is the literary art whose primary unit is the line. Where prose is organized by the sentence and structured by argument or narrative, a poem is organized by the line and structured by the line’s relations to its neighbors — by enjambment and end-stopping, meter and rhythm, rhyme and sound patterning, stanza and form. The material qualities of language — what words sound like, how long a line takes to read, where a break falls, what an image conjures — are compositional elements alongside (or instead of) propositional content.

The library’s poetry coverage runs along three axes. The first is the line: how the poetic line works, what a line break does, the difference between end-stopped and enjambed lines. The second is sound and meter: meter as a contract between writer and reader’s ear, the toolkit of feet and scansion, the four metrical systems and why English uses accentual-syllabic verse, the relation between metrical poetry and free verse, and the sonic devices (rhyme, alliteration, assonance, consonance, caesura) that bind words together below the level of meaning. The third is form: fixed forms (sonnet, villanelle, ghazal, pantoum, haiku), structural devices (volta, refrain, stanza), and the modal categories (lyric, narrative, dramatic).

Teleology: To understand how poems produce meaning through their line-by-line organization — what fixed forms make possible, what metrical contracts enforce, what free verse takes on as compensating responsibility, and how the sonic and visual dimensions of language carry weight that prose mostly ignores.

Method: Close reading combined with formal analysis — scansion, structural identification, attention to historical traditions and the choices each tradition forces — grounded in the practical experience of reading and writing inside formal constraints.
