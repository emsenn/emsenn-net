Euphemism

2026-03-06
Defines Euphemism

A euphemism is a mild or indirect expression substituted for one considered too harsh, blunt, or direct. “Passed away” for “died,” “let go” for “fired,” “collateral damage” for “civilian deaths.” Euphemism softens — which is sometimes kind and sometimes dishonest, depending on what’s being softened and why.

Euphemism serves two functions:

  • Social courtesy. Some euphemisms smooth social interactions. “Restroom” for “toilet,” “passed away” for “died” in condolence — these reflect genuine care for the listener’s comfort. Writers may use them when the blunt term would cause unnecessary pain without adding clarity.
  • Concealment. Other euphemisms hide reality. “Enhanced interrogation” for “torture,” “downsizing” for “mass layoffs,” “neutralize” for “kill.” George Orwell argued that political euphemism is designed to “make lies sound truthful and murder respectable” [@orwell1946]. The euphemism works by replacing a word that triggers the reader’s moral judgment with one that doesn’t.

The writer’s task is to distinguish between these functions. Euphemism that respects the reader’s feelings without obscuring reality is appropriate. Euphemism that obscures reality to prevent the reader from judging it is dishonest. The test: if the reader would respond differently to the direct term than to the euphemism, the euphemism is doing political work, not social work.

The plain language tradition generally opposes euphemism on the grounds that clear communication requires calling things what they are. This is correct as a default — but a default, not an absolute. A technical document about error handling should say “the process failed,” not “the process experienced a suboptimal outcome.” A condolence note may use “passed away” without being dishonest.
