Architecture Decision Records

2026-03-07 Research summary of Architecture Decision Records (ADRs), a lightweight method for documenting architectural decisions in software projects.

Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) are short text documents that capture a single architectural decision, its context, and its consequences. Michael Nygard introduced the format in a November 2011 blog post titled “Documenting Architecture Decisions,” published on the Cognitect blog (Nygard, 2011). The post argued that agile projects need lightweight documentation that stays close to the code, rather than large design documents that fall out of date.

¶Format

Nygard’s original ADR format draws on the structure of Alexandrian patterns. Each record contains five sections:

  • Title: a short noun phrase with a sequential number (e.g., “ADR 1: Use PostgreSQL for persistence”).
  • Status: one of “proposed,” “accepted,” “deprecated,” or “superseded.” A superseded ADR links to its replacement.
  • Context: the forces at play — technical, political, social, or project-specific — that create the need for a decision.
  • Decision: the response to those forces, stated in active voice.
  • Consequences: what follows from the decision, including both benefits and costs.

ADRs are stored as numbered text files in source control, typically in a directory such as doc/adr/ or docs/decisions/. This keeps decision history versioned alongside the code the decisions govern.

¶Core planning mechanism

The ADR’s planning function is retrospective rather than prospective. Where a design document plans future work, an ADR records the rationale behind a choice already made or about to be made. The accumulation of ADRs creates a decision log — a navigable history that answers “why is the system this way?” rather than “what should the system become?”

The mechanism works because decisions are immutable records. When circumstances change, a team writes a new ADR that supersedes the old one rather than editing the original. This preserves the chain of reasoning over time.

¶Adoption

In November 2017, ThoughtWorks placed “Lightweight Architecture Decision Records” in the Adopt ring of its Technology Radar, indicating strong industry recommendation (ThoughtWorks, 2017). Since then, multiple ADR templates have appeared, including the Markdown Any Decision Records (MADR) format and Y-statement templates. The UK Government Digital Service (GDS) adopted ADRs as a standard practice for documenting technical choices across government projects.

Joel Parker Henderson maintains a widely referenced open-source repository of ADR templates and examples on GitHub (joelparkerhenderson/architecture-decision-record). The University of Queensland’s software engineering curriculum includes ADRs as a teaching topic in its CSSE6400 course materials.
