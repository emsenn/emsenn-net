tag wrangling

2026-03-04

Tag wrangling is a curation practice in which volunteer editors organize a folksonomy by designating canonical tags, linking synonyms, and creating grouping relationships — without restricting what users can tag at the point of creation. The practice was developed and named by Archive of Our Own (AO3), a fanfiction archive launched in 2009, and represents one of the most successful real-world syntheses of free tagging and controlled vocabulary.

AO3 uses six distinct tag types (ratings, archive warnings, categories, fandoms, relationships, characters, and freeform/additional tags). Users can enter any freeform text as a tag. Tag wranglers — a distributed team of volunteers — then organize the resulting namespace through three operations. First, they designate canonical tags as the preferred form for each concept: “Sherlock Holmes/John Watson” becomes the canonical form that “Johnlock” and “Holmes/Watson” redirect to. Second, they link synonyms to their canonical form so that searching either term finds content tagged with any variant. Third, they create metatags that group related-but-not-synonymous concepts — a metatag for “Coffee Shop AUs” might gather freeform tags like “barista AU” and “coffeehouse romance” under a common umbrella.

The tag wrangling model succeeds because it preserves the low barrier to entry that makes folksonomies productive (users tag freely without needing to learn a vocabulary) while applying the precision of a controlled vocabulary after the fact. The cost is labor: AO3’s tag wrangling team is one of the largest organized volunteer efforts on the web. The model works best when the volume of tagging is high enough to justify the curation effort and when the community values discoverability across a large, heterogeneous corpus — conditions that also apply to research vaults, academic databases, and any system where content from many contributors needs to be findable through shared terminology.
