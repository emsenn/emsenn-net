Standardization as Counterinsurgency

2026-03-03 The structural dynamic by which standards bodies absorb insurgent infrastructures, translate their aims into institutionally legible forms, and stabilize them in ways that neutralize their disruptive potential.

Standardization as counterinsurgency (SaC) is a framework emsenn develops to describe how standards bodies interact with insurgent infrastructures — technical systems that originate as challenges to the dominant platform order. The framework draws on James C. Scott’s work on legibility and state-building, and on analyses of protocol as a form of soft power.

The central claim: when a standards body engages with an insurgent infrastructure, the institutional logic of standardization — consensus-seeking, scope management, stakeholder alignment — tends to transform the infrastructure in ways that increase its legibility to dominant institutions while eroding its generativity, autonomy support, and movement value. This isn’t necessarily intentional. It’s a structural effect of the incentives and constraints that shape standards processes.

The five-stage pipeline

SaC identifies five stages in how standards bodies process insurgent infrastructures:

  1. Emergence. The infrastructure exists outside the standards body. Communities develop and deploy it in pursuit of their own aims.

  2. Recognition. The standards body acknowledges the infrastructure as relevant — visible in charters, working groups, and deliverable schedules. The infrastructure becomes a “named object” in institutional discourse.

  3. Translation. The political and social aims associated with the infrastructure are translated into “technical” problems. Concerns about governance, power asymmetry, and economic models are bracketed as “out of scope.” What remains are message formats, endpoints, and error semantics.

  4. Stabilization. A formal standard is produced, reflecting the compromises of translation. It must be general enough for multiple use cases, minimal enough for consensus, and vague enough in contentious areas to avoid blocking publication.

  5. Deployment. The standardized version is adopted. Reference implementations anchor interpretation. Corporate actors selectively implement parts that align with their strategies.

At each stage, the infrastructure’s attributes shift: legibility increases, generativity decreases, movement value declines. The infrastructure isn’t outlawed — it’s normalized.

The analogy to counterinsurgency

The term “counterinsurgency” is deliberate. Like state responses to political insurgencies, standardization aims less at frontal suppression than at structured absorption. The goal isn’t to prevent folk from using the infrastructure, but to ensure they do so in ways compatible with existing institutional logics. The insurgent infrastructure isn’t destroyed; it’s domesticated.

This framing doesn’t imply conspiracy. Many participants in standards processes genuinely support openness, interoperability, and user autonomy. SaC describes a structural tendency, not a coordinated plan.

The five-dimensional attribute model

Any infrastructure can be characterized along five dimensions:

Dimension What it measures
Generativity How much room for new practices, applications, and social formations
Legibility How easily understood and governed by dominant institutions
Autonomy support How well communities can operate independently of dominant platforms
Movement value The functional, symbolic, and affective value for the communities that created it
Corporate extractable value How much large firms can derive from integrating it

An insurgent infrastructure scores high on generativity, autonomy support, and movement value, while its legibility and corporate extractable value are low. Standardization shifts these scores: legibility up, generativity and movement value down.
