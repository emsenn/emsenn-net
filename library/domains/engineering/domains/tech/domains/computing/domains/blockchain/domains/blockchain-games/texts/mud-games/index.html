MUD-Based Games: OPCraft, Sky Strife, and the Next Generation

With the release of MUD v1 in late 2022, Lattice began building production games to validate the framework. These games—OPCraft, Sky Strife, and others—serve as both proof-of-concept and laboratories for autonomous world design, demonstrating what MUD enables and what challenges remain.

OPCraft: A Minecraft-Like On-Chain Universe

OPCraft was a voxel-based world deployed on Optimism (an Ethereum Layer 2), built by Lattice in just 1.5 months using MUD v1. The game is essentially Minecraft run on-chain: a 3D world composed of destructible/placeable blocks where players can mine, build, and craft.

The Game

  • World size: A 3D voxel space (manageable but infinite in principle)
  • Mechanics: Mine blocks, craft tools, place structures, explore
  • Ownership: Players own their creations as on-chain data
  • Extensibility: Third-party developers can extend through plugins and custom clients

Technical Achievement

OPCraft validated that MUD could handle real-time 3D gameplay with millions of entities:

  • Millions of blocks (entities) represented in tables
  • Thousands of player interactions per day
  • Complex spatial queries (what blocks are in this region?)
  • Efficient indexing and client synchronization

The fact that Lattice built it in 1.5 months showed that MUD dramatically reduces development friction compared to building a blockchain game from scratch.

Permissionless Governance: The Communist Society

OPCraft demonstrated the power of permissionless composability through an emergent social phenomenon. One player, SupremeLeaderOP, created an on-chain guild with rules encoded in smart contracts. Members could opt in to the guild, and by doing so, agreed to a binding rule: all their mined resources would be pooled and distributed equally.

This was not a social promise or a Discord agreement. The guild rules were enforced by code. Players who opted in literally could not keep their own resources; transactions attempting to withdraw them would revert.

This kind of emergent governance is unique to autonomous worlds:

  • In a traditional game, such a mechanic would be impossible. The developer controls all logic; players cannot unilaterally create new rules.
  • In Dark Forest, players had custom clients but no access to on-chain logic.
  • In OPCraft, anyone can deploy a new smart contract that reads and writes to the world’s tables, creating new economic systems.

The communist society was not planned by Lattice. It emerged from the permissionless architecture. This is the promise of autonomous worlds: systems and behaviors emerge that the original designers never imagined.

Sky Strife: A Real-Time Strategy Game

Sky Strife is an RTS-inspired game developed by Lattice, deployed on testnet and later on Redstone (a specialized rollup optimized for autonomous worlds). Players battle in turn-based, real-time matches to destroy opponents’ settlements while defending their own.

The Game

  • Genre: Turn-based RTS (blends real-time and turn-based mechanics)
  • Scale: Smaller matches (16-32 players typical) in distinct game instances
  • Strategy: Build settlement, produce units, attack and defend, manage resources
  • Composition: Multiple game “seasons” with different rule sets and cosmetics

Technical Innovation

Sky Strife demonstrates key improvements over OPCraft:

  1. Larger player bases — Designed for hundreds to thousands of concurrent players, not just dozens
  2. Higher update frequency — Real-time gameplay with quick state changes
  3. ECS patterns at scale — Hundreds of thousands of entity-component records per match
  4. Client-side prediction — The client predicts actions optimistically, then reconciles with on-chain truth

Sky Strife also showcases account delegation: players can authorize a burner wallet to sign transactions on their behalf, reducing friction for mobile play and managing gas costs.

Redstone: Specialized Infrastructure

Sky Strife runs on Redstone, an EVM-compatible rollup designed specifically for autonomous worlds. Redstone prioritizes:

  • High throughput — Designed for 1000s of TPS, far higher than Ethereum
  • Low latency — Faster blocks to enable real-time interaction
  • Affordable execution — Gas costs are lower for intensive state operations

This shows a key insight: autonomous worlds may not need Ethereum directly. Layer 2 rollups and specialized chains can provide the scalability and cost-efficiency needed for real-time gameplay while inheriting Ethereum’s security model.

Other MUD-Based Games

Beyond OPCraft and Sky Strife, the MUD ecosystem includes:

  • Word3: A word game demonstrating turn-based, asynchronous gameplay
  • Imminent Solace: A battle royale game using ZK fog of war mechanics (similar to Dark Forest)
  • 2048 On-Chain: A fully on-chain clone of the popular puzzle game, built by the community to learn MUD
  • Loot Survivor (Dojo): A Roguelike adventure game on Starknet
  • Biomes: A Minecraft-like sandbox on Redstone

Lessons from Production Games

Scalability Tradeoffs

MUD games reveal a spectrum of scalability choices:

  • Pure Ethereum: Works for turn-based, asynchronous games with modest entity counts (Dark Forest, Word3)
  • Layer 2 (OP, ARB): Supports real-time games with high TPS but latency of a few seconds (early OPCraft)
  • Specialized rollups (Redstone): Designed for autonomous worlds; can handle real-time RTS gameplay

The cost-latency-decentralization tradeoff is inescapable. Choosing Ethereum means higher costs and latency but maximum security and decentralization. Choosing Redstone means faster, cheaper gameplay but reliance on a specialized rollup operator.

Composability in Practice

MUD’s permissionless extension model is powerful but presents challenges:

  • Namespace collisions: If two developers deploy incompatible systems under the same namespace, conflicts arise
  • Upgrade coordination: Changing core systems (e.g., introducing a new currency) requires coordination or hard forking the world
  • Fragmentation: Different versions of a world can exist; players must choose which version to play

Despite these challenges, permissionless extension has proven valuable. OPCraft’s custom clients (2D isometric views, 3D visualizations) were created by the community without any Lattice coordination. This would be impossible in traditional games.

Economics and Incentives

MUD games must solve an incentive problem: why should players play?

  • Rewards — Cosmetics, NFTs, or tokens that have real value
  • Play-to-earn — Players earn tokens by playing, which can be sold on exchanges
  • Community ownership — Players feel invested in a world they co-create

Sky Strife uses cosmetics and seasonal rewards. OPCraft used on-chain asset ownership (players’ creations are permanent NFTs). The model that works best remains unclear, and different games are experimenting.

Client Experience

One challenge MUD games face is that the on-chain source of truth is always slower than a traditional client. Solutions include:

  • Optimistic updates — The client assumes an action will succeed and updates immediately; if it fails, revert
  • Indexers — Off-chain databases that replicate on-chain state with low latency for reads
  • Rollups — Faster block times reduce latency to game-playable levels

None of these eliminate the inherent latency of blockchain; they just make it tolerable.

The Path Forward

MUD games are transitioning from novelty to playable experiences. Sky Strife has attracted thousands of players. OPCraft demonstrated that a full 3D world is feasible. These games show that autonomous worlds are not just theoretical; they are becoming practical.

The next phase will likely involve:

  1. Specialized infrastructure — More chains (like Redstone) optimized for games rather than forcing games onto general-purpose L2s
  2. Cross-game composability — Assets and achievements spanning multiple worlds
  3. AI agents — Bots playing within autonomous worlds, creating new economic dynamics
  4. Creator economies — Built-in support for creator cosmetics, streaming, and sponsorships

The vision is that autonomous worlds become a new category of digital infrastructure: not owned by any company, but shared by a community, with player creativity and commerce flowing naturally across permissionless boundaries.
