Operator

A computing operator is a mathematical operator realised in a computational system: a typed, effectively-computable transformation whose source and target are representable in finite resources, and whose action is given by a procedure rather than by an existence claim. A computing operator is a specialisation of the broader mathematical notion — its sub-support is exactly the cases in which the transformation is effectively executable.

¶What computing adds to the math

Three constraints distinguish a computing operator from an arbitrary mathematical operator.

Effective computability. The operator must be given by a procedure that can be carried out in finite steps without appeal to an oracle, completed search of an infinite space, or other non-constructive resource. Turing (1936) and Church (1936) independently fixed this requirement: a computing operator is a Turing-computable (equivalently, λ\lambda-definable) function on its representable inputs.

Representability. The source and target must admit a finite encoding. An operator on the real numbers, in the math sense, has continuum-sized source and target; the computing operator on the reals is the operator on a finite representation (floating-point numbers, computable reals à la Weihrauch, exact arithmetic over algebraic closures) — the underlying math operator restricted to the representable sub-support.

Typing and operational semantics. A computing operator is typed: its arity is fixed and its inputs are tagged with the types it accepts. Its action is given by inference rules in the style of Plotkin (1981) — small-step or big-step rules that say how the operator reduces on each shape of input — rather than by a defining equation that may have no procedure for evaluation. Pierce (2002) is the standard reference for the typing-and-rules combination as a modern foundation for operators in programming languages.

¶Why this is a specialisation of math operator

The mathematical operator (in any of its three senses — functional-analytic, categorical, algebraic) need not be effectively computable. The Hilbert-space operator that projects onto the closure of the rationals in R\mathbb{R} is a perfectly good linear operator; the corresponding computing operator does not exist, because no finite procedure can decide for an arbitrary real whether it is rational. The computing operator inherits its source/target/structure-preservation from the math operator and adds the three constraints above. The induced restriction map (in the predicate-fiber sense) takes a math operator and yields the computing operator on the sub-support where the constraints are satisfied; the computing operator does not exist on the rest.

¶Composition and the typed term

Computing operators compose along their types: if f:A→Bf: A \to B and g:B→Cg: B \to C are both computing operators, then so is g∘f:A→Cg \circ f: A \to C, and its evaluation procedure is the sequencing of the two procedures. This makes the typed-operator family a category in the categorical sense — and the corresponding category of computing operators is the setting in which a programming language, a proof assistant, or an algebraic specification is studied.
