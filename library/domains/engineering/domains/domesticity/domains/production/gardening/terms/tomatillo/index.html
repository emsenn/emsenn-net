Tomatillo

2026-04-02 Warm-season annual fruiting crop (Physalis philadelphica, 60–75 days to first fruit). Produces small (2–4 cm) berries enclosed in papery calyx husk. Essential characteristic: self-incompatible (requires 2+ plants for cross-pollination). Single isolated plants produce empty husks. Rio Grande Verde (green) and purple cultivars common. Moderate water and fertility. Prolific and disease-resistant relative to tomatoes and peppers. Ideal for salsa verde and fresh salsas.

¶Growing Season and Climate

Tomatillos are warm-season annuals requiring soil temperatures ≥15°C for growth; optimal growth is at 20–28°C. Frost kills the plant. They are somewhat less heat-demanding than peppers but more heat-tolerant than many tomato cultivars.

Start transplants indoors 6–8 weeks before last spring frost. Harden off and transplant to the garden after frost danger passes and soil has warmed. In warm climates (zones 9–11), direct-seed after soil warms.

Days to first fruit: 60–75 days from transplanting. The crop is relatively quick-maturing.

¶The Critical Requirement: Two or More Plants

Tomatillos are self-incompatible—a single plant will not produce fruit. Self-incompatibility operates at the molecular level: even though flowers contain both male (pollen) and female (pistil) organs, pollen from the same plant cannot fertilize that plant’s flowers. Isolated plants produce abundant flowers but only empty husks (no berry inside).

Solution: Plant at least two tomatillo plants (preferably of different cultivars) to ensure cross-pollination. Two plants of the same cultivar can cross-pollinate each other and produce fruit; however, growing two different cultivars increases genetic diversity and ensures more reliable fruit set.

Common pairings: Rio Grande Verde + Purple Tomatillo, or two different green cultivars.

Do not rely on a single plant, even if you expect good insect visitation.

¶Planting and Soil Preparation

Choose a location with 6–8 hours of direct sunlight. Tomatillos tolerate a wider range of soils than tomatoes; well-draining soil amended with 2–3 inches of compost is sufficient.

Space transplants 24–36 inches apart (two plants need 4–6 feet of combined space). Tomatillos are vigorous, bushy plants that spread widely.

Mulch lightly (2 inches) to retain moisture and moderate soil temperature. Unlike tomatoes, tomatillos do not require deep transplant burial.

¶Watering and Fertility

Tomatillos are moderate feeders and require consistent moisture (1–1.5 inches weekly). Uneven watering reduces fruit production and quality.

Apply balanced fertilizer (10–10–10) at transplanting. Once flowering begins, reduce nitrogen (5–10–10 or 6–10–15) to favor fruiting. Side-dress every 4–6 weeks.

Tomatillos are generally more resilient to nutrient fluctuations than tomatoes; over-fertilization favors excessive vegetative growth.

¶Flowering and Pollination

Flowers are small, pale yellow-green with purple veining, produced continuously in leaf axils. Each flower is perfect (contains pistil and anthers) but requires cross-pollination from a genetically distinct plant.

Insect pollination is essential: native bees, bumble bees, and solitary bees are effective. Flowers open in early morning and are receptive for 1–2 hours. Insect activity at this time ensures pollen transfer between plants.

Without adequate insect activity or with only one plant present, flowers drop without setting fruit.

Flowers drop without setting fruit if temperatures exceed 32°C at night; this is normal and temporary.

¶Fruit Development and Ripeness

Once pollinated, fruit develops rapidly: 3–4 weeks from flower to mature size. The distinctive feature is the papery calyx husk (inflated sepals) that encloses and protects the berry. The husk is initially pale green, ripening to tan-brown as the fruit matures.

Harvest when:

  • Husk has fully enlarged and turned pale tan or beige
  • Fruit inside husk is firm (slightly resists gentle pressure)
  • Green fruits are lime-green to yellow-green (depending on cultivar)

Slightly immature fruits (firm but still pale) can be harvested and ripened indoors (3–5 days at room temperature) for better flavor development.

Overripe fruits become yellow and soft; these are still edible but more prone to splitting.

¶Notable Cultivars

Rio Grande Verde (green type, standard): The most common cultivar, producing firm, tart, bright green fruits. Excellent for fresh salsa and cooked salsas. Widely available.

Purple tomatillo: Produces dark purple-black fruits with complex, slightly sweeter flavor than green types. Less commonly available but worth seeking. More variable in self-incompatibility compatibility with green types.

Tomatillo Verde (another green type): Similar to Rio Grande but with slightly different flavor profile. Often grown paired with Rio Grande for diverse flavor.

¶Harvest and Storage

Harvest when the husk is fully enlarged and tan-colored, and the fruit inside is firm. Cut (don’t pull) the fruit, leaving the husk attached (provides some protection).

Tomatillos store longer than tomatoes: 2–3 weeks at room temperature or 3–4 weeks refrigerated in a paper bag. The husk prevents rapid moisture loss.

Immature fruits (harvested at the pale green stage) will ripen at room temperature over 3–7 days.

¶Preparation and Culinary Uses

Salsa verde (green tomatillo sauce): Roast fresh tomatillos with jalapeños, garlic, cilantro, and onion; blend to a chunky consistency. Serve with tortilla chips or use as a sauce for enchiladas.

Fresh salsa: Chop raw ripe tomatillos (green or purple, depending on preference), add cilantro, lime juice, onion, and chile; serve immediately.

Cooked salsas: Simmer tomatillos with spices, vinegar, and other ingredients for a shelf-stable hot sauce.

The firm, tart flavor (citric and malic acids, pH ~3.5–4.0) is distinct from tomatoes. Tomatillos contribute a fresher, herbaceous quality to salsas, ideal for fresh applications.

¶Advantages Over Tomatoes and Peppers

Tomatillos are remarkably disease-resistant compared to tomatoes and peppers. They rarely suffer late blight (Phytophthora), early blight (Alternaria), or the pest pressure that plague tomato/pepper cultivation. This makes them excellent for gardeners in humid or disease-prone regions.

Plants are vigorous and prolific: a single mature plant produces 50+ fruits over the season.

¶Common Problems

  • No fruit production: The most common issue—only one plant grown. Plant a second tomatillo for cross-pollination.
  • Few fruits despite two plants: May indicate poor insect activity. Hand-pollinate by brushing pollen between flowers with a small brush, or provide bumble bee hives.
  • Poor flavor: Harvest at full maturity (tan husk, firm fruit). Immature fruits are bland. Over-watering reduces flavor concentration.
  • Cracked fruits: Excessive watering, especially after dry periods. Maintain even moisture.
  • Pests: Generally fewer than tomatoes/peppers. Spider mites and whiteflies can occur; increase humidity or use insecticidal soap if needed.
