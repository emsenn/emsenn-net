Cold Frame

2026-04-02 A bottomless box with a transparent lid placed over garden soil to create a microclimate warmer than ambient conditions through solar gain and thermal mass

A cold frame is a bottomless, box-like structure with a transparent cover (typically glass or polycarbonate) placed directly over garden soil to warm the soil and air beneath through solar radiation and thermal mass. It extends the growing season by creating a localized microclimate several degrees warmer than ambient conditions.

¶Thermodynamics

Cold frames operate on simple solar principles. Sunlight passes through the transparent lid, striking the soil below. The soil absorbs this solar radiation and converts it to heat. Unlike open air, the transparent cover traps radiated heat from the soil, reducing wind chill and preventing warm air from escaping. This creates a temperature differential of 5–10°F above ambient conditions. The soil itself acts as thermal mass, absorbing heat during the day and releasing it slowly at night, buffering against temperature swings.

The dominant heat source is direct solar radiation transmitted through the glazing. Heat gain from convective currents in the air inside the frame contributes only 10–20% of total warming; radiant heat from the glass itself adds little compared to soil absorption.

¶Construction and Use

A cold frame requires only four walls (typically built from wood, concrete blocks, or metal), a transparent lid, and good contact between the walls and soil to minimize heat loss. The lid must tilt southward (in the Northern Hemisphere) to maximize solar capture and allow water runoff.

Cold frames excel at hardening off transplants — gradually acclimating seedlings started indoors to outdoor conditions before final planting. They also extend the harvest of cool-season greens (mâche, kale, spinach) into autumn and winter, and can protect cold-hardy crops from the worst frosts of spring and fall.

¶Distinction from Hot Beds

A hot bed adds an internal heat source — typically a layer of fresh, decomposing manure beneath the soil — to maintain temperatures above what solar gain alone provides. Hot beds can generate sustained warmth through microbial decomposition of organic matter, making them useful for early spring propagation or winter forcing. Cold frames rely entirely on solar gain and soil thermal mass, making them effective but seasonal tools.

¶Historical Context

Cold frames were central to French intensive gardening traditions, where they were called châssis. Market gardeners near Paris in the 18th and 19th centuries used banks of cold frames heated by manure to produce out-of-season vegetables for city markets, maximizing yield from limited land.
