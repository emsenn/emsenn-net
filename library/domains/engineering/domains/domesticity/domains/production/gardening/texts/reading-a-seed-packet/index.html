Reading a Seed Packet

The Seed Packet as Information Artifact

A seed packet is not a casual wrapper. It is a compressed growing guide, every specification reflecting actual plant biology. Reading it correctly transforms guesswork into precision.

The packet must communicate across climates, across skill levels, and across decades — a seed lot from 2019 must still be usable in 2026. The information is dense because it must be. It is also cryptic to many gardeners because the terminology reflects scientific plant biology, not casual gardening parlance. This gap is the focus here.

Days to Maturity: Timing Your Plantings

The most commonly misunderstood specification is “days to maturity.” The number does not mean “how long from now until harvest.” It is a calculation with a start point that differs by planting method.

For transplants: “Days to maturity” counts from transplanting the seedling into the garden, not from sowing the seed indoors. A tomato packet listing “70 days to maturity” means 70 days from setting transplants outdoors to harvesting the first ripe fruit. It does not account for the 6–8 weeks required to grow the transplant indoors. A gardener sowing seeds indoors in March for April planting faces 70 days from transplanting (April) until harvest (mid-June) — but 14 total weeks from sowing seed.

For direct sowing: “Days to maturity” counts from sowing the seed directly in the ground. A lettuce packet listing “45 days to maturity” means 45 days from sowing to harvest-ready stage.

This distinction confuses beginners. A gardener unfamiliar with it might sow transplant-type seeds directly in the ground and expect harvest in the listed timeframe, only to find the plant is still a seedling when the calendar says it should be mature. The packet specifies the intended planting method implicitly.

Germination Temperature: The Optimal Window

Every seed packet lists a germination temperature or temperature range. This is the range in which the seed will germinate most reliably.

Tomato seeds germinate fastest at 70–80°F (21–27°C). Bean seeds need >60°F (>16°C) to germinate at all; below this, the seed rots. Lettuce germinates at 60–70°F (15–21°C), but if soil exceeds 75°F, germination rate plummets and seedlings are weak. Cilantro requires cool soil (<65°F) to germinate reliably; heat triggers dormancy.

This is plant biology, not opinion. Each plant evolved in a climate with characteristic soil temperatures during its germination window. The seed’s trigger physiology reflects this ancestry. Sowing cilantro in July when soil is 85°F means waiting for fall’s cooler temperatures to germinate, or accepting poor germination rates. Sowing beans in April when soil is 50°F means waiting weeks for germination and risking rot if rain follows.

The packet sometimes lists “days to germination” — an estimate of how long the seedling will take to emerge, assuming optimal temperature. This is useful for knowing when to watch the soil for signs of life, but the germination temperature range is the more practical specification: it tells you when to sow.

Spacing and Depth: Why Precision Matters

Spacing — the distance between plants — is not aesthetic. It reflects competition and disease dynamics.

Close spacing means roots compete for water and nutrients. Wider spacing means each plant gets more resources but you produce fewer plants per area. Closer spacing also creates a denser canopy, which shades weeds and reduces soil evaporation. But denser canopy also reduces air circulation, increasing humidity in the foliage and encouraging fungal diseases like powdery mildew and leaf spots.

The packet’s spacing recommendation balances yield against disease risk for that cultivar. Interpreting it requires understanding the plant’s vulnerability. Lettuce planted 6 inches apart produces smaller heads but less powdery mildew in humid climates. Planted 12 inches apart, it produces larger heads but more disease. Some gardeners knowingly choose tighter spacing to accept some loss to disease as a trade for higher total yield.

Planting depth follows a principle: large seeds need depth to ensure soil contact for water absorption; tiny seeds need shallow planting because they lack the energy reserves to push through deep soil. Very small seeds — like carrots or petunias — are often surface-sown or barely covered. Medium seeds — beans, peas — are planted 1–2 inches. Large seeds — squash, beans — are planted 1–3 inches.

Light-dependent germination muddies this. Some seeds need light exposure to germinate and must be surface-sown or very shallowly covered. This is an evolutionary adaptation: small seeds in the soil seed bank often will not germinate unless surface soil is disturbed (exposing them to light), signaling an opportunity to grow.

Sun Requirements: Measuring Photosynthesis

Full sun, partial shade, full shade — these terms appear on every packet. They translate to specific daily light hours required for that plant’s photosynthetic rate.

Full sun: 6–8 hours or more of direct sunlight daily. This is the light requirement for most vegetables and fruiting plants (tomatoes, peppers, squash, beans). These plants evolved in open habitats or canopy gaps where light was abundant. They allocate resources to fruit and seed, not stem elongation. In partial shade, they grow tall and spindly, producing fewer flowers and fruit.

Partial shade: 3–6 hours of direct sunlight, or dappled light all day. Leafy greens (lettuce, spinach, kale) often tolerate and prefer partial shade because they evolved as forest-floor understory plants. Shade reduces heat stress in summer and extends the harvest window before bolting occurs. Partial shade is survival strategy, not compromise.

Full shade: Less than 3 hours of direct sun. Ferns, hostas, sedges, and shade-loving herbs thrive. Most vegetables will not fruit in full shade; photosynthetic rate is too low to support the energy cost of reproduction.

Hardiness Zone: Temperature Geography

Hardiness zones map average annual minimum temperatures across regions. A plant listed as “hardy to zone 5” can survive typical winter low temperatures in USDA hardiness zone 5 (–20°F / –29°C). It may survive zone 4 winters (–30°F / –34°C) or may die. It will almost certainly survive zone 6 winters (–10°F / –23°C).

This is critical for perennials (plants that persist multiple years) but irrelevant for annuals that complete their lifecycle in a single season. A tomato is an annual; hardiness zones do not restrict where it can be grown. A blueberry is a perennial; hardiness zone determines whether it overwinters in your climate.

The packet’s hardiness zone specifies the minimum temperature the plant’s tissues can withstand dormant. It does not account for soil moisture, humidity, or winter precipitation patterns that might affect survival in marginal zones.

Determinate vs. Indeterminate: Architecture and Productivity

Tomato packets distinguish determinate and indeterminate varieties. This is plant architecture encoded in genes.

Determinate (or “bush”) tomatoes grow to a fixed height (typically 3–4 feet), stop elongating, and concentrate fruit production into a brief window — most fruit ripens over 2–3 weeks. This makes them ideal for canning and commercial harvest. Growth is genetically programmed to stop.

Indeterminate (or “vining”) tomatoes grow continuously, producing flowers and fruit throughout the season until frost kills the plant. They reach 6+ feet, require trellising or staking, and produce fruit continuously through summer and into fall. Home gardeners prefer indeterminate for extended harvest.

This difference is not a matter of care or fertilizer. It is hardwired. No amount of pruning makes a determinate tomato indeterminate. The genetic architecture is fixed.

Hybrid vs. Open-Pollinated: Genetics and Seed-Saving

Seed packets distinguish hybrid (F₁) and open-pollinated varieties. This distinction determines whether a gardener can save seeds for next season.

Hybrid (F₁) seeds result from crossing two distinct parent lines. The hybrid offspring are vigorous (hybrid vigor) and true-to-type — all seeds produce identical plants. But if the gardener saves seeds from the hybrid plant, the offspring segregate: they produce plants resembling one parent, the other parent, or recombinant forms. The hybrid advantage is lost. Hybrids are engineered for commercial uniformity and cannot be reliably propagated from seed. They must be repurchased each year.

Open-pollinated varieties (sometimes called heirloom plants) are true-breeding. Seeds from an open-pollinated plant produce genetically similar offspring. A gardener can save seeds year to year and maintain the variety. The trade-off: open-pollinated varieties are often less vigorous and less uniform than hybrids. They are more variable, more suited to local selection and adaptation.

This is a choice between agricultural efficiency (hybrids) and agricultural autonomy (open-pollinated). Commercial agriculture chose hybrids. Home gardeners often choose open-pollinated.

Disease Resistance Codes

Packets sometimes list resistance codes: VFN, TSWV, or similar. These abbreviations indicate resistance to specific diseases.

V = Verticillium wilt, F = Fusarium wilt, N = nematodes. A tomato listing “VFN” resists all three. TSWV = tomato spotted wilt virus; a pepper listing TSWV resists that virus. These resistances are genetically selected traits, not inherent to all varieties. A gardener planting in an area with known disease pressure (a vineyard neighbor might mean powdery mildew risk; wet years mean fungal disease risk) should select resistant cultivars.

The Compressed Knowledge

Every specification on the seed packet is a coordinate in a multidimensional space. Days to maturity specifies timing. Germination temperature specifies the window when sowing will succeed. Spacing reflects biological constraints. Sun requirements quantify photosynthesis dependence. Hardiness zone maps survival. Determinate vs. indeterminate encodes architecture. Hybrid vs. open-pollinated specifies genetics. Disease resistance codes signal selection priority.

Read together, these specifications are a growing guide. They tell you when to sow, how deep, how far apart, where to plant, how long until harvest, and whether seeds can be saved. The packet is not a decorative label. It is compressed plant biology.
