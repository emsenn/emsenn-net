Common Bean Cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing techniques for bush and pole bean varieties, including succession planting, cultivar selection, and harvest timing

Common beans (Phaseolus vulgaris) are warm-season crops requiring soil temperatures above 15°C for germination, with optimal growth between 20–30°C. Plant after the last frost date when soil has warmed, typically 1–2 weeks after transplants could grow.

¶Cultivar Selection

Bush types (determinate, 45–60 cm) produce concentrated yields suitable for processing and successive harvests:

  • Blue Lake 274: 50–58 days, mosaic-resistant, green pods, reliable producer
  • Cherokee Wax: Yellow pods, 1948 All-America Selection, excellent flavor
  • Red Swan: 55 days, dual-purpose (snap and dry), ornamental purple flowers
  • Dragon Tongue: Dutch heirloom, dual-purpose, tiger-striped pods, 60 days
  • Vermont Cranberry: Short-season adapted (50–60 days), dried beans, New England reliability

Pole types (indeterminate, 1.5–2.5 m) provide extended harvest windows and space efficiency:

  • Purple Podded: 65–75 days, intensely purple pods, exceptional flavor, heirloom
  • Succotash: 70 days, Narragansett Indian heritage, cream and purple seeds, heritage value
  • 1500 Year Old Cave Beans: Ancestral Puebloan cultivar (germinated after ~1500 years sealed in New Mexico cave), 65–70 days, cultural significance, dual-purpose

¶Planting and Spacing

Direct seed after danger of frost passes. Space bush beans 10–15 cm apart in rows 45–60 cm apart; pole beans 15–20 cm apart around sturdy 2 m supports (trellises, teepees, strings). Plant pole beans slightly deeper (3–4 cm) than bush beans (2–3 cm). Beans fix their own nitrogen and require no supplemental fertilization in reasonable soil.

¶Succession Planting

For continuous harvest, sow new plantings every 2–3 weeks through mid-summer. Last plantings should mature before first frost (typically 60–75 days before expected frost date). In warm climates with monsoon moisture, beans may produce spring and summer crops.

¶Water and Fertility

Water deeply and consistently once established, maintaining 2–3 cm per week. Avoid overhead irrigation, which promotes pod diseases. Beans tolerate modest soil fertility; excess nitrogen stimulates foliage at the expense of pod production. Potassium and phosphorus support flowering and pod fill.

¶Pest and Disease Management

Bean leaf beetles and spider mites represent primary insect pests; hand-removal of beetles or neem oil controls infestations. Fungal diseases (bean rust, anthracnose) develop in humid conditions; improve air circulation, water at soil level, and remove infected foliage. Mosaic-resistant cultivars like Blue Lake 274 prevent viral disease losses. Row covers protect seedlings from early beetle pressure.

¶Harvest Timing

Snap/green beans: Harvest when pods snap cleanly, before seeds bulge visibly (6–8 days after flowering). Daily or every-other-day picking prolongs production. Dry beans: Allow pods to mature completely on plant, browning and drying. Harvest when pods rattle with dry seeds, typically 15–20 days after final pod set. Dry beans store 1–2 years in cool, dry conditions.

¶Seed Saving

Beans cross-pollinate poorly (primarily self-fertile), permitting close cultivar spacing. Allow 2–3 plants to mature for seed (100–150 seeds per plant). Cure in paper bags for 4 weeks, removing immature or moldy seeds. Store at 10–15°C with silica desiccant.
