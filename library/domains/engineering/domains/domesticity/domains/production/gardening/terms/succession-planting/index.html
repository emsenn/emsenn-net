Succession Planting

2026-04-02 The practice of sowing the same crop at regular intervals to maintain continuous harvest rather than a single concentrated glut.

Succession planting is the deliberate practice of sowing the same crop variety at staggered intervals—rather than all at once—to spread harvest across the growing season. Instead of planting thirty lettuce seeds once and harvesting thirty heads simultaneously, a gardener plants ten seeds every two weeks to have harvestable lettuce weekly or bi-weekly.

The interval between plantings varies by crop and season. Cool-season crops mature fastest in spring and fall, forcing a tighter sowing interval: cress every 7–10 days (matures in 3 weeks), lettuce every 2–3 weeks (60-day full head, but leaf harvests begin at 30–35 days), radish every 2 weeks (25-day maturity). Beans mature in 45–60 days and should be sown 3–4 weeks apart to stagger summer harvests. As temperatures warm, crops mature faster, so the interval between successive plantings must compress. Early spring lettuce might be sown every 3 weeks, but by June the same crop is sown weekly to maintain a steady harvest.

Succession planting accomplishes several goals. It distributes harvest labor and processing load (canning, freezing, cooking) across months rather than concentrating it in a few weeks. It maintains living plant material on the bed, preserving soil coverage and reducing pest pressure on isolated plants. It hedges agricultural risk: if one planting fails to germinate or is damaged by insects, others remain.

The practice assumes crops will be harvested at defined readiness, not continuously grazed. It works best with fast-maturing cool-season crops and moderately-paced warm-season crops. Root crops with long maturity (carrots, beets at 70–80 days) are sometimes succession-planted, but the practice is less critical because the window of peak harvest quality is wider.

A related concept is relay planting, where the second crop is sown into the same bed as the first approaches maturity. Beans planted into a lettuce bed as lettuce is harvested, or arugula planted where spring spinach is bolting, reduce fallow time and maximize bed productivity.

Practical limitations: Succession planting requires a clear view of when crops will mature and be harvested—knowing you have a window to reseed when the current planting is removed. It works best for gardeners with small plantings (a 100-foot row of lettuce) and access to seed. It is labor-intensive compared to single large plantings (timing, germination, transplanting multiple times per season). The practice assumes consistent weather; an unseasonable heat wave or drought can accelerate maturity across all successive plantings, collapsing the staggered harvest into a glut.

Succession planting is distinct from multicropping (growing multiple crops simultaneously in the same space, vertically or in guild systems) and from continuous harvest systems (perennial production like asparagus or artichoke). It is the temporal complement to crop rotation, which manages the same space across years; succession planting manages it within a single season or growing window.
