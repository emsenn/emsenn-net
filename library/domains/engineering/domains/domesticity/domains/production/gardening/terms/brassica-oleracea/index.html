Brassica oleracea cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing protocol for cole crops (cabbage, broccoli, kale, Brussels sprouts), adapted from single species through cultivar selection. Cool-season crops, frost-enhanced flavor. Sow 6–8 weeks before transplant timing; transplant at true-leaf stage. 60–120 days to maturity depending on group.

All Brassica oleracea cultivar groups share fundamental growing requirements: cool soil and air temperatures (15–20°C optimal), consistent moisture, rich soil, and fall timing for superior flavor.

¶Sowing and Seedling Stage

Start seeds indoors 6–8 weeks before transplant date. Use sterile seed-starting mix; sow 2–3 seeds per cell. Germination occurs at 15–20°C in 5–10 days. Maintain seedlings at 15–18°C with consistent moisture to avoid leggy growth. True leaves emerge by day 14; thin to one seedling per cell. Transplants are ready at 4–6 true leaves (typically 4–6 weeks after sowing).

¶Transplanting

Harden off seedlings by gradually exposing to outdoor conditions over 7–10 days. Transplant when soil temperature is 10–15°C (spring) or 15–20°C (fall) and nighttime temperatures do not exceed 25°C. Space according to mature size:

  • Cabbage: 18–24 inches apart, single-stem crops; expect 3–5 kg heads from larger cultivars.
  • Broccoli: 18–24 inches apart; harvest central head at 4–6 inches diameter, then leave for side-shoot development (smaller, 2–4 inch heads) over 3–4 weeks.
  • Kale: 18–24 inches apart for whole-plant harvest; 12 inches for continuous leaf-pick harvesting.
  • Brussels sprouts: 24–30 inches apart; lower leaves are smaller and less useful; remove bottom leaves as sprouts mature to redirect energy upward.

¶Growing Season Management

Apply balanced fertilizer (5-10-10 or similar) at transplant and again 4–6 weeks later. Maintain consistent soil moisture (1–1.5 inches per week); inconsistent watering triggers splitting in cabbage and off-flavor in all groups. Mulch with 2–3 inches of straw or compost to moderate soil temperature and conserve moisture.

Monitor for cabbage worms (Plutella larvae), flea beetles, and aphids. Use row covers until flowering is not a concern (for leaf crops), or hand-pick larger pests.

¶Specific Harvesting and Timing

  • Cabbage (var. capitata): Harvest when head is firm, typically 70–100 days from transplant. Squeeze to test firmness; the head should resist finger pressure. Kalibos (pointed red cabbage) matures in 60–80 days. Cut at soil level to allow small secondary heads to form on the stem.

  • Broccoli (var. italica): Waltham and similar cultivars mature 55–70 days from transplant. Harvest the central head at 4–6 inches diameter, while buds are tightly packed and before florets separate. Cut 5–6 inches below the head; side shoots will develop if left on the plant.

  • Brussels sprouts (var. gemmifera): 80–120 days from transplant; timing varies by cultivar. Redarling (130–140 days reported) and Churchill (90–100 days) represent the range. Harvest sprouts when 1–2 inches diameter; start at the bottom and move upward as the plant grows. Remove yellowed foliage as the season progresses.

  • Kale (var. sabellica): Begin harvesting outer leaves when plants are 12–18 inches tall; continue continuous picking through the season. Hard freeze (5–10°F or lower) triggers conversion of starch to sugar; harvest after a frost for sweetest flavor. Scarlet and other purple cultivars develop deeper color after exposure to frost.

¶Fall Timing and Frost Enhancement

For maximum flavor, time transplanting so that maturity coincides with first hard freeze (late September through November in temperate zones). Cool temperatures and frost exposure trigger enzymatic conversion of starch to glucose and fructose, dramatically increasing sweetness and reducing bitterness from glucosinolates.

Spring crops (transplanted March–April) mature in warmer temperatures and are less sweet than fall crops; tolerate this trade-off or target fast-maturing spring varieties if season is short.

¶Storage and Curing

  • Cabbage: After harvest, trim outer leaves and store in high-humidity conditions at 35–45°F; properly stored heads keep 2–4 months.
  • Broccoli: Use fresh within 5–7 days of harvest; freeze excess within 2 days for extended storage.
  • Kale and Brussels sprouts: More cold-hardy; frost-exposed plants keep well for weeks in the garden. For storage, freeze or process for preservation.

¶Cultivar Recommendations by Climate

  • Cold climates (zone 3–4): Churchill Brussels sprouts (90–100 days, earlier maturity), Kalibos cabbage, Scarlet kale, Waltham broccoli. All tolerate hard freeze and produce in compressed seasons.
  • Moderate climates (zone 5–6): Full range of cultivars available; fall planting preferred.
  • Warm climates (zone 7+): Shorter-season cultivars only; fall and winter planting essential; avoid long-season Brussels sprouts.
