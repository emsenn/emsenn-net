Seed Starting

2026-04-02 The practice of germinating seeds indoors under controlled conditions before transplanting seedlings outdoors, to extend the effective growing season for slow-maturing crops.

Seed starting is the intentional germination of seeds indoors in a controlled environment—under grow lights, on heat mats, in a greenhouse or on a sunny windowsill—followed by transplanting the resulting seedlings into the garden. The practice extends the effective growing season for crops that would not reach maturity in a region’s growing season if started from direct seed alone.

The decision to start seeds indoors is driven by crop biology and regional temperature. Peppers and eggplant require 10–12 weeks of warm (70°F+) temperatures from seed to transplant-ready seedling, then another 60–90 days to first fruiting. In regions with frost before late August, seeds started outdoors in May will not flower until autumn. Starting seeds indoors in February allows transplants to be established before the last frost, capturing April and May warmth for vegetative growth.

Tomatoes need 6–8 weeks from seed to transplant size. Starting seeds in March allows transplanting in May after the last frost, with flowering by July and harvest beginning in August. Without the head start, direct-sown tomatoes would not flower until September or later, losing most of the growing season.

Cool-season crops (carrots, beets, lettuce, beans, peas) resent root disturbance and are almost always direct-sown. Carrots develop a taproot that does not tolerate transplanting. Beans germinate quickly (4–5 days) and mature in 50–60 days, so direct sowing in May yields harvest by July without indoor setup.

Indoor conditions require three elements: bottom heat (60–75°F soil temperature via heat mats accelerates germination), consistent moisture (soil kept moist but not waterlogged), and light once seedlings emerge (14–16 hours per day from grow lights). Windowsill light is usually insufficient and produces leggy, weak seedlings.

Hardening off is the critical transition phase. Seedlings grown indoors in warm, still, humid air with moderate light will be damaged or killed by direct outdoor conditions—UV intensity, wind, temperature swings, and low humidity cause cellular water loss and photobleaching if the cuticle and epicuticular wax are insufficiently developed. Over 7–10 days, seedlings are gradually exposed to outdoor conditions: start with a few hours in shade, increase sun exposure daily, and extend time outside until transplants are left out overnight before field planting. This process thickens the cuticular wax layer and develops stress tolerance. Skipping hardening off (moving seedlings directly from indoors to the garden) causes transplant shock—leaves yellow or blacken, growth stalls for weeks, and establishment is delayed.

Transplanting timing depends on crop type and local frost dates. Count backward from your region’s last frost date: peppers transplant at 10–12 weeks before frost, tomatoes at 6–8 weeks before. Soil temperature at transplanting should be 60°F minimum (ideally 65°F+) and night temperatures should stay above 50°F. Transplanting cold-shocked plants into cold soil results in poor root establishment and slow growth.

Damping off, a fungal disease of seedling stems caused by Pythium or Rhizoctonia, is the most common indoor failure. Prevention: use sterile seed-starting mix (not garden soil), provide good air circulation, avoid overwatering, and keep seedlings in bright light.

Infrastructure requirements: Seed starting requires reliable lighting, heat, and space. Fluorescent or LED grow lights running 14–16 hours per day cost money and electricity but are far superior to windowsill light in quality and consistency. Heat mats maintaining 65–75°F soil temperature accelerate germination and reduce time under lights. A shelving unit with lights and mats can start 200+ seedlings in a 4x8 space, justifying the setup for intensive gardeners but not for households planting a dozen tomatoes annually.

Cost and timing trade-offs: Seed starting is labor-intensive—checking soil moisture, potting on as seedlings grow, hardening off, transplanting. The return on time investment is highest for expensive plant varieties, slow-maturing crops that need the head start, or regions where growing season is severely limited. Home gardeners often underestimate the skill required—damping off, leggy growth, cold shock, and hardening off failures are common for first-time indoor gardeners.

Seed starting is optional for: beans, peas, cucumbers, melons, squash (direct seeding is faster and simpler).

Seed starting is essential for: peppers, eggplant, long-season tomatoes in short-season regions.
