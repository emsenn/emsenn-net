Shallot cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing protocol for shallot cultivar group (Allium cepa var. aggregatum), which produces clusters of smaller bulbs from single planted bulblet with milder flavor than common onions.

Shallots (Allium cepa var. aggregatum) produce clusters of 4-10 smaller bulbs from each planted bulblet, offering more delicate flavor and faster multiplication than common bulbing onions. Unlike bulb onions grown from seed, shallots propagate vegetatively from stored bulblets, similar to garlic clove propagation.

Planting and Spacing

Plant individual bulblets 6-8 weeks before last spring frost, or in autumn (4-6 weeks before hard freeze) in mild climates. Set bulblets 4-6 inches apart, 1 inch deep, in well-draining soil amended with compost. Space rows 12-18 inches apart. Each bulblet will multiply into a cluster of 4-10 bulbs during the growing season. Shallots require moderate chilling for bulbing but tolerate warmer conditions than hardneck garlic; zones 4-10 work well with either spring or autumn planting.

Growing Season and Harvest

Shallots mature in 90-120 days from planting. As daylength lengthens in late spring (similar to bulbing onions), the underground cluster of bulblets expands. Foliage yellows and falls over 2-3 weeks before maturity. Harvest when 50 percent of tops have browned and dried; pull clusters from soil and brush off excess soil without washing.

Curing and Storage

Cure in warm (70-75°F), airy location for 2-3 weeks until outer tunics are papery and necks completely dry. Once cured, separate individual bulbs from the cluster carefully, trimming roots and excess stem material. Shallots store 4-6 months at cool room temperature (55-65°F) in darkness with good air circulation.

Selecting and Saving Bulblets for Next Season

Reserve 10-20 percent of the largest bulbs from each cluster for replanting the following year; store these seed bulblets separately in cool (45-50°F), dry conditions. Shallots replicate reliably over many seasons and improve locally adapted vigor with each generation of replanting.

Flavor and Culinary Use

Shallots develop delicate, slightly sweet onion flavor with subtle garlic undertones, making them prized for vinaigrettes, classic French sauces, and refined cooking. Unlike bulbing onions’ more assertive pungency, shallot flavor remains gentle even when raw.

Common Cultivars

French Shallots (true gray shallots) are smallest but most flavorful. Dutch Yellow, Ambition, and Aschalot offer larger bulbs and reliable production. Red Burgundy and Échalion types add color variation.
