Carrot

2026-04-02 Cool-season root crop requiring direct sowing, loose rock-free soil to 10–12 inches, and thinning at 2–3 leaf stage for quality roots.

Carrots are a cool-season root crop grown as an annual, developing thick, sweet, orange (or purple, white, yellow) taproots in the first season. They are one of the few vegetables that must be direct-sown (never transplanted) and demand loose, well-prepared soil free of rocks and hard clods. Proper soil preparation and thinning are the two most critical success factors; neglecting either results in stunted, forked, or poorly colored roots.

¶Soil Preparation and Site Selection

Carrots require loose, friable soil to a depth of 10–12 inches. In clay or compacted soils, roots become misshapen, stunted, and fork into multiple branches as they encounter resistance. Amend beds heavily with compost (2–3 inches), aged sawdust, or sand, worked in to 12 inches depth. Remove all rocks and hard-compacted clods by hand or roto-tilling. Raised beds (8–12 inches high) filled with purchased potting soil or home-made compost-and-sand mix are an excellent solution for poor native soils.

Carrots tolerate a wide pH range (5.5–7.5) and actually perform better in slightly acidic to neutral soil than in alkaline conditions. Avoid fresh manure, which promotes forking; aged compost is preferable.

¶Direct Sowing and Germination

Sow carrot seed directly outdoors 2–3 weeks before the last spring frost, or in late July for a fall crop. Seed is small and must be sown shallowly—1/4 inch deep—in moist soil. Mix seed with dry sand or fine compost to aid in even spacing and visibility. Carrot seed germinates slowly (14–21 days) and weakly; keep soil consistently moist during this period. Germination is nearly impossible in dry soil.

Seed can be sown in single rows or broadcast in wide beds. In beds, space seeds 1/2 inch apart in all directions. Thin dramatically (see below) to achieve final spacing.

Never transplant carrots. Even young seedlings with intact root systems suffer severe transplant shock; the taproot is damaged, resulting in stunted or branched roots.

¶Thinning and Plant Spacing

Thinning is non-negotiable for carrot quality. At the 2–3 leaf stage (when seedlings are 1–2 inches tall), thin ruthlessly to final spacing: 2–3 inches apart for slender cultivars, 3–4 inches for larger, storage-type roots. This single operation separates adequate crops from miserable ones. Underthinnined carrots will remain pencil-thin and bitter.

Thin in two passes: the first at 2–3 leaf stage to 1 inch spacing, the second at 6 leaves to final spacing. Dispose of thinned plants or compost them; pulling them out disturbs adjacent roots.

¶Watering and Cultivation

Water consistently to keep soil moist but not waterlogged (1–2 inches per week). Irregular watering causes root cracking and bitterness. Mulch lightly (1 inch) with compost or grass clippings to conserve moisture, suppress weeds, and protect roots from sunscald (green shoulders from light exposure, which develop alkaloid bitterness).

Carrot roots will push up and out of soil as they enlarge; pull soil gently over them or increase mulch to keep them covered.

¶Cultivar Selection and Maturity

Korral (75 days) is a Nantes type (medium, cylindrical, 6–7 inches long) with excellent flavor, tender core, and good storage.

Rainbow Mix (70–75 days) is a blend of orange, yellow, white, and purple cultivars, visually striking in the garden and on the plate. Varieties within the mix mature at slightly different rates, extending harvest window.

Purple Haze (70–75 days) is an F1 hybrid with striking purple skin and orange interior. The purple color derives from anthocyanin pigments and fades during cooking, so enjoy it fresh for visual effect. The carrot is sweet and tender.

All are suitable for fresh market and storage; choose based on intended use and color preference.

¶Harvest and Storage

Harvest when roots reach the desired size, typically 4–6 weeks after thinning. Snap or dig roots (gently, to avoid breakage), removing soil. Do not leave roots in the ground too long; they become woody and develop strong flavors after full maturity.

For long-term storage, remove the green tops (which leach moisture) and place roots in a cool cellar or refrigerator in damp sand or peat. Properly stored carrots keep 3–6 months. For extended storage (winter), dig roots after first frost when root sugars are highest, trim tops 1/4 inch above the root, and layer in moist sand in a cool location (32–40°F / 0–4°C).

¶Succession Planting and Fall Crop

For continuous supply, sow carrots every 3 weeks from early spring through July. A midsummer sowing (late July) matures in early fall and tolerates light frosts, providing quality roots through late autumn. Fall-grown carrots develop sweeter flavor due to cool-season carbohydrate accumulation.

¶Pest and Disease Management

Carrot rust flies (Psila rosae) lay eggs in soil around the base of young plants; the larvae tunnel into roots. Exclude flies with row covers (lightweight fabric) during the April–July flight period. Carrot weevils bore into roots; remove infested roots and dispose of them. Parsnip webworms (actually moths) spin silken tents on foliage; pick off affected leaves.

Root diseases (cavity spot, damping-off) are fungal and typically affect seedlings in cool, wet conditions. Ensure good soil drainage and air circulation.
