Watermelon

2026-04-02 A heat-loving African melon maturing in 70-100 days (dwarf varieties faster), requiring consistent warmth, full sun, and sandy soil; excellent heat and drought tolerance once established

Watermelon is Citrullus lanatus, an African melon cultivated for large, crisp, sweet fruits ready in 70-120 days depending on cultivar. The species thrives in hot, arid climates and is marginal in cool temperate regions. Modern cultivars mature faster (70-100 days) than heirlooms, with small “icebox” types (3-4 lbs) and standard types (8-15 lbs). Watermelon is heat-loving and requires sustained warmth; even brief cool spells delay maturity and reduce sugar content.

Site Preparation and Soil

Watermelon demands full sun (8+ hours minimum), loose, well-draining sandy loam, and warm soil (70°F+). Poor soil is tolerable if drainage is excellent; the species thrives in sandy, marginally fertile ground where other crops struggle. Work 2-3 inches of compost into the top 6-8 inches; avoid excess nitrogen (promotes vine growth at the expense of fruit).

Ideal soil pH is 6.0-7.0. In clay soils, create mounded hills 6-8 inches high, spaced 4-5 feet apart (allow 10-15 square feet per plant for vining types). Black plastic mulch warms the soil and accelerates early growth in cool regions.

Planting and Temperature Considerations

Direct-seed 2-3 weeks after the last frost into warm soil (70°F+ is ideal). In short-season regions, start seeds indoors 4-6 weeks before transplant date or use black plastic mulch to warm soil pre-planting. Seeds germinate reliably only at 65°F+; below this temperature they may rot in the soil.

Space seeds 6 inches apart in hills, thinning to 2-3 vigorous seedlings once they produce true leaves. Vining cultivars sprawl 8-15 feet; dwarf types remain more compact (4-6 feet). Provide strong trellis support if space is limited; cradle fruits with cloth slings to prevent breaking.

Water Management and Critical Timing

Watermelon requires 1-1.5 inches of water per week, but the timing and distribution are critical. Consistent moisture throughout fruit development (first 4-6 weeks) is essential; uneven watering causes hollow hearts (internal cavities and white “rivers”) and reduces sweetness.

Once fruits are softball-sized (about 4-6 weeks post-flowering), reduce watering slightly; dry conditions concentrate sugars. Cut watering to 0.5-0.75 inches per week in the final 2-4 weeks before harvest. Excessive irrigation in the final weeks produces watery, less-sweet fruit. Drip irrigation at the soil line prevents wet foliage (which invites fungal disease).

Mulch with black plastic (laid pre-planting) or 2-3 inches of straw post-planting to retain soil moisture and regulate temperature. In arid regions, black plastic is essential for early season warmth; in humid regions, reduce it after fruit sets to prevent disease.

Pest and Disease Management

Cucumber beetles transmit bacterial wilt; use floating row covers until flowering (then remove for pollination). Hand-pick beetles in early morning; insecticidal soap or spinosad suppresses populations. Squash bugs are less problematic on watermelon than on squash but may appear; same control as for cucumber beetles.

Powdery mildew appears late in the season on older foliage; it rarely affects fruit by harvest. Fusarium wilt and phytophthora are serious in humid regions; choose resistant varieties and avoid overhead watering. Alternaria leaf spot appears as brown spots on leaves; improve air circulation.

Ripeness Assessment and Harvest

Harvest at peak ripeness—this cannot be “learned” from appearance alone and requires practice. Reliable indicators: (1) The tendril nearest the fruit stem has dried and curled, (2) Ground spot (side in contact with soil) has turned creamy yellow or orange, (3) The “hollow” sound when thumped indicates a ripe melon (experienced gardeners acquire an “ear” for this), (4) A slightly sweet smell emanates from the opposite end (blossom end).

Unripe melons do not ripen post-harvest; harvest only when fully mature. Stem leaves yellowing before fruits ripen indicates heat stress or water issues (inadequate irrigation late season). Cut fruits with 1-2 inches of stem attached; handle carefully to avoid bruising.

Early dwarf varieties mature 60-70 days post-planting; standard varieties 80-100 days. Harvest all fruit before the first expected frost; exposure to frost damages the fruit.

Storage and Quality

Watermelon keeps 1-2 weeks at room temperature (65-70°F) or 2-3 weeks at 50-55°F (refrigerator). Chilling below 50°F can cause flavor loss and textural damage (mealy flesh). Cut melons keep 3-5 days refrigerated if wrapped tightly.

Cultivar Recommendations

Icebox types (small, dwarf): ‘Yellow Petite’ (65-70 days, yellow flesh, 4-5 lbs, excellent for small households), ‘Sugar Baby’ (75-80 days, classic dark green, 8 lbs), ‘Blacktail Mountain’ (70-80 days, reliable, 10 lbs). Standard types (large, traditional): ‘Charleston Gray’ (80-90 days, gray-green striped, 20-25 lbs, excellent shipper), ‘Crimson Sweet’ (80-90 days, red flesh, disease-resistant), ‘Jubilee’ (80-90 days, large, reliable).
