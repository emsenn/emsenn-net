Potato

2026-04-02 Cool-season perennial (cultivated as annual, 70–120 days to harvest). Produces underground stem tubers (swollen stolons) via vegetative propagation from seed-tubers. Purple and blue-fleshed cultivars (Purple Majesty, Purple Viking) accumulate high anthocyanins. Prefers cool soils (15–20°C); poor performance in heat. Direct-plant seed-tubers in spring; hill soil around emerging plants to prevent sunlight-exposed tubers. Moderate water and fertility needs. Cool-season crop distinct from warm-season Solanaceae.

¶Growing Season and Climate

Potatoes are a cool-season crop, very different from warm-season tomatoes and peppers. They thrive at soil temperatures 15–20°C and poor production results from sustained heat (>25°C). In cool climates, plant in spring for early-summer harvest; in mild climates, plant in fall for winter/spring harvest.

Days to maturity: 70–90 days for early cultivars; 110–120 days for late maincrop types. Choose timing and cultivar to fit your local growing season.

Frost kills the leafy shoots but does not kill the underground tubers. In cool climates, you can plant immediately after last spring frost; in warm climates, time plantings to avoid peak summer heat.

¶Planting: Seed Potatoes and Spacing

Potatoes are propagated vegetatively from seed potatoes (small whole tubers or pieces with “eyes”—apical buds). Do not use grocery-store potatoes; use certified seed potatoes from nurseries, which are pathogen-tested and disease-free.

Pieces should be cut 1–2 days before planting and allowed to dry slightly; this reduces rot. Each piece should have 2–3 eyes.

Prepare soil with 2–3 inches of compost or aged manure. Potatoes tolerate a wider range of soil pH (5.0–8.0) than most vegetables but prefer slightly acidic soil (6.0–6.8).

Plant seed pieces 4 inches deep and 12 inches apart in rows 24–30 inches apart. Plant in early spring (4–6 weeks before last frost) or in fall in warm climates (8 weeks before first frost).

¶Hilling and Light Exclusion

As potato shoots emerge (2–3 weeks after planting), exposed tubers will develop green solanine (a bitter alkaloid) and turn inedible. To prevent this:

Hill soil: When shoots are 6–8 inches tall, pull soil up around the stems to create a mound 4–6 inches high. Repeat hilling every 3–4 weeks as the plant grows, until the mound is 8–12 inches high.

Hilling accomplishes two goals:

  1. Prevents light exposure of developing tubers
  2. Provides loose soil for tuber expansion

Heavy mulching (straw or compost) instead of hilling also works: apply 8–12 inches of loose mulch to exclude light and create loose growing medium.

¶Fertilization and Water

Potatoes are moderate feeders. Apply balanced fertilizer (10–10–10) at planting or incorporate compost thoroughly. Too much nitrogen favors leaf growth and reduces tuber yields; avoid high-nitrogen fertilizers during tuber development.

Water consistently: 1–1.5 inches weekly once shoots are actively growing. Potatoes require steady moisture for good tuber development. Uneven watering causes misshapen tubers and cracking.

Avoid waterlogging; potatoes prefer moist but well-draining soil. Poor drainage increases late blight (Phytophthora infestans) risk.

¶Purple and Blue-Fleshed Cultivars

Purple Majesty (patented, Colorado State University): All-purple flesh, high anthocyanin content (dark purple throughout). Excellent nutritional profile. Maturity: 100–110 days. Flavor is slightly earthy with good texture.

Purple Viking (North Dakota State University): Purple skin, white/purple-tinged flesh. Bred for northern climates; cold-hardy. Maturity: 80–90 days. Good for early harvest.

Kennebec (traditional yellow): White flesh, low anthocyanin. Good multipurpose potato; excellent for baking and frying.

Other heirloom varieties include Blue Hubbard (blue skin), Adirondack Blue (blue flesh), and Red Norland (red skin).

Anthocyanin content is influenced by growing conditions: cooler temperatures and longer daylight favor higher anthocyanin accumulation.

¶Disease Management

Potatoes face significant pest and disease pressure. Major issues include:

Late blight (Phytophthora infestans): The pathogen that caused the Irish Famine. Spreads rapidly in wet conditions. Symptoms: dark water-soaked spots on leaves, white mold on undersides; tubers develop brown rot.

Prevention: Ensure good air circulation; avoid overhead watering; grow disease-resistant cultivars (many modern cultivars have resistance); remove lower leaves as plant grows.

Treatment: Fungicide sprays (sulfur, copper, chlorothalonil) every 7–10 days in wet weather.

Early blight (Alternaria solani): Concentric ring leaf spots; worse in warm, wet weather.

Prevention: Remove lower leaves; ensure air circulation; avoid overhead watering.

Potato beetles (Leptinotarsa decemlineata): Yellow-and-black striped beetles that defoliate rapidly. Larvae are red and bump-shaped.

Management: Hand-pick beetles and egg clusters; grow under row covers in early season; use Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) or spinosad insecticide if infestation is severe.

¶Harvest and Storage

Early potatoes (70–90 days): Harvest when plants flower or when tubers reach desired size. Dig carefully to avoid bruising. Early potatoes are small and have thin skin; use immediately (do not store long).

Maincrop potatoes (110–120 days): Allow plants to mature fully; foliage will yellow and die back. Harvest when plants are completely dead. Cure tubers (store at 45–55°F, 85–90% humidity for 2 weeks) to harden skin for long storage.

Storage: Store cured potatoes in cool (7–10°C), dark conditions in paper bags or burlap sacks (not plastic, which traps moisture). Potatoes stored properly (cool, dark, dry) keep 4–6 months. Exposure to light causes greening (solanine); discard green tubers or green portions.

¶Planting for Continuous Harvest

In cool climates with long growing seasons, stagger plantings 2–3 weeks apart (every 3 weeks) to achieve continuous harvest over many weeks. In short-season areas, plant all at once.

¶Common Problems

  • Slow growth or few tubers: Usually from warm soil (>25°C). Potatoes are cool-season crops; avoid planting too late or too early.
  • Green tubers: From light exposure. Hill properly or mulch heavily.
  • Cracked or misshapen tubers: From uneven watering. Maintain steady moisture.
  • Potato beetles: Major pest. Hand-pick or use Bt/spinosad.
  • Late blight: Fungal disease in wet conditions. Ensure air circulation and use fungicide if needed.
