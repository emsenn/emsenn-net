Morning Glory

2026-04-02 Annual vine cultivar of Ipomoea purpurea, 60-75 days to first flower. Vigorous twining vine 2-3.5 m. Blue, purple, pink, or bicolored ephemeral flowers open at dawn, close by mid-morning. Full sun, moderate soil. Poor soil yields more flowers (nitrogen limitation). Prolific seeder; allow plants to complete seed maturation for self-sowing next season or deadhead to extend flowering.

Morning glory cultivation grows Ipomoea purpurea as a vigorous annual vine with prolific blue, purple, pink, or bicolored flowers that open each morning and close by noon. The ephemeral daily flowering pattern is distinctive; sustained bloom from mid-summer through first frost makes morning glories reliable cover for trellises, fences, and pergolas.

¶Site Selection and Soil Preparation

Morning glories thrive in full sun (minimum 6-8 hours) and adapt to a wide range of soil types. The critical principle: poor soil yields more flowers. Rich, heavily manured soils produce lush foliage with sparse flowering. If planting in fertile garden beds, amend with sand or perlite to reduce fertility, or simply select a less-fertile bed. Soil pH 6.0-7.5 is ideal; drainage is important but not critical (morning glories tolerate moderate moisture).

¶Spacing and Planting

Direct-seed or start indoors 4-6 weeks before frost-free date. Seeds have hard, impermeable coats and germinate slowly (7-10 days); accelerate germination by scarifying seed coat with sandpaper or soaking in warm water overnight. Space seeds 6-12 inches apart directly where they will grow, or transplant seedlings 12-18 inches apart. Provide sturdy trellis, fence, or string support; the twining vines climb vigorously once established.

Succession-sow every 2 weeks for extended flowering, or rely on self-sown seed from previous year (plants volunteer readily from dropped seed). In frost-free zones, morning glories can self-perpetuate indefinitely.

¶Water and Fertility

Water minimally—0.5-1 inch per week once established. Drought promotes flowering; excess water and fertilizer produce rank foliage. Do not fertilize. If plants show obvious nutrient deficiency (pale leaves, stunted growth), apply a dilute balanced fertilizer once at mid-season; excess nitrogen invariably reduces flowering.

¶Support and Training

Morning glories twine counterclockwise (when viewed from below) and will naturally climb any vertical support. For maximum coverage, provide string, wire, or netting for young twining stems; the plant will self-support once established. No pruning is necessary unless you wish to control spread; simply remove stems that have ventured into unwanted areas.

¶Pest and Disease Management

Morning glories are remarkably pest-resistant. Japanese beetles occasionally feed on foliage but rarely cause significant damage. Powdery mildew may develop late in the season in humid climates; spray with water or fungicidal soap if severe. Spider mites occasionally appear on drought-stressed plants; spray with water.

The primary management concern is self-seeding: morning glories are prolific seeders and volunteer seedlings appear throughout the garden in following years. Deadhead spent flowers if you wish to prevent volunteering, or accept self-sown seedlings and thin as needed.

¶Bloom Extension and Seasonal Timing

Flowering peak occurs mid-summer through early fall. In cool zones, early June sowings do not flower until late July, leaving only 6-8 weeks of bloom before frost. Earlier indoor starts (10 weeks before frost-free date) advance flowering by 3-4 weeks. Conversely, later sowings (4-6 weeks before first frost) often fail to flower before cold kills the plant.

¶Cultivar Recommendations

  • Classic blue (60-75 days): ‘Heavenly Blue’ (bright sky-blue, vigorous, highly reliable, most common cultivar); ‘Blue Morning Glories’ (generic blue selection, similar to Heavenly Blue).
  • Purple and burgundy (60-75 days): ‘Scarlett O’Hara’ (deep red-burgundy, vigorous); ‘Crimson Rambler’ (burgundy-red).
  • Bicolored and picotee (65-75 days): ‘Flying Saucer’ (white striped with purple-blue, more common in I. nil); ‘Pearly Gates’ (white, occasionally flushed with pale pink).
  • Pink forms (60-75 days): various pink cultivars exist but are less common in I. purpurea than in I. nil.
