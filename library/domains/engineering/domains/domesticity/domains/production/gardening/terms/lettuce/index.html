Lettuce cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing protocol for *Lactuca sativa*, optimized for cool-season production and succession sowing. Seed germinates at 10–20°C but exhibits thermoinhibition above 25°C. Succession-sow every 2–3 weeks for continuous harvest; cut-and-come-again harvesting extends production. 25–55 days to maturity depending on type and temperature.

Lactuca sativa is primarily a cool-season crop. Optimal growth occurs at 15–20°C; temperatures above 22°C trigger bolting and stress. Success depends on timing plantings to avoid heat.

¶Sowing and Germination

Sow directly in garden beds or in containers. Sow seeds 0.25 inch deep, 1 inch apart; barely cover with soil. Germination occurs at 10–20°C in 7–14 days. Thermoinhibition: Seeds will not germinate above 25°C even with ample moisture. If sowing in warm seasons, start seeds indoors and refrigerate trays at 40°F for 7–10 days before sowing to break dormancy; transplant to cool garden areas.

Thin seedlings to 6–12 inches apart depending on cultivar size and harvest method.

¶Succession Sowing and Timing

Sow every 2–3 weeks to maintain continuous supply. Stagger plantings to account for maturity timing:

  • Spring: Begin sowing 4–6 weeks before last frost; soil temperature should be 45–55°F. Continue every 2 weeks until day temperatures consistently exceed 75°F.
  • Fall: Sow 12–14 weeks before first hard freeze for mature head harvest, or 6–8 weeks before first frost for looseleaf types. Fall plantings are more reliable than spring; plants grow steadily into cold weather.

¶Growing Season Management

Water consistently (1–1.5 inches per week); irregular watering causes tip burn (calcium deficiency) and bitter flavor. Fertilize once at 3–4 weeks if soil is lean; avoid excess nitrogen, which delays heading in butterheads.

Looseleaf types tolerate partial shade (50% incident light), extending productive season in warm climates.

¶Harvesting Methods

Whole-plant harvest (25–55 days depending on type): Crispheads and butterheads typically require 40–55 days; looseleafs mature in 25–40 days. Cut at soil level; plants will not regrow from the crown.

Cut-and-come-again: For looseleaf (Merlot, oak-leaf types) and some butterheads, harvest outer leaves when 4–6 inches long; central rosette continues producing. This extends harvest window to 4–8 weeks per plant, making looseleafs extremely efficient for home gardens.

Baby leaf harvest (14–21 days): Thin seedlings early and harvest thinnings fresh; use continuously as plants grow, harvesting leaves when 4–6 inches.

¶Cultivar-Specific Notes

  • Merlot (looseleaf, burgundy): 25–55 days, highly variable depending on light and temperature; cooler conditions extend harvest window. Cut-and-come-again harvesting recommended.
  • Speckled (butterhead, Dutch): 45–55 days to firm heading; less prone to bolting than many butterheads. Intermediate heading tendency allows longer harvest window.
  • Crisphead (Iceberg) types: 50–70 days, require consistent cool conditions and steady growth; sensitive to temperature fluctuations.

¶Bolting Management

Bolting is irreversible once initiated. To delay bolting:

  1. Choose cool-tolerant, slow-bolting cultivars (Speckled, some romaines, specific looseleafs).
  2. Plant in shade or under tall crops (tomatoes, beans) during late spring.
  3. Mulch to moderate soil temperature.
  4. Maintain consistent moisture.

Once bolting begins, harvest immediately; plants become bitter and bitter compounds increase daily.

¶Storage and Handling

Harvest in cool morning hours. Looseleaf and butterhead types store 5–10 days in high-humidity refrigerator storage (90+ humidity, 35–40°F). Crispheads store 2–3 weeks. Do not store with ethylene-producing fruits (apples, tomatoes).

¶Regional Timing Examples

  • Zone 3–4: Spring plantings late April–May; fall plantings July–August. Winter production not feasible unless in cold frames.
  • Zone 5–6: Spring plantings March–May; fall plantings June–September. Cool spring and fall allow 2–3 full cycles.
  • Zone 7–9: Winter and early spring production preferred; fall plantings very productive. Summer production minimal or requires high tunnel/shade.
