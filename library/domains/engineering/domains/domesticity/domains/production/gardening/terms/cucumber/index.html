Cucumber

2026-04-02 A rapid-maturing warm-season vine producing cylindrical fruits in 50-65 days, demanding consistent moisture and heat; varieties segregate into slicing (long, smooth) and pickling (short, bumpy) types

Cucumber is Cucumis sativus cultivated for immature fruit harvested 50-65 days after sowing. Two primary market types exist: slicing varieties (North American fresh market, 20-30 cm, smooth-skinned, for salads) and pickling varieties (5-15 cm, warty, suited to fermentation and brining). Consistent water and warm temperatures are non-negotiable; drought stress triggers bitterness and abnormal fruit shape.

¶Site Preparation and Soil

Cucumber demands full sun (8+ hours), rich, well-draining loam, and warm soil (65°F minimum, 70°F+ optimal). Work 3-4 inches of well-rotted compost or aged manure into the top 8-10 inches. Ideal soil pH is 6.0-7.0. Cucumber is nutrient-intensive; ensure baseline nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium are moderate to high. Consider a raised bed to improve drainage and warm soil faster in spring.

¶Planting and Support

Direct-seed 1-2 weeks after last frost into warm soil. Plant seeds 1 inch deep, spaced 6-12 inches apart. Thin seedlings to 12 inches (slicing types) or 8 inches (pickling types) once they produce true leaves. Trellising is strongly recommended: it saves space, improves air circulation (reducing disease), and produces straighter, cleaner fruit.

Use sturdy stakes (5-6 feet), twine, or a wire trellis. Tie main vines loosely every 12 inches as they grow. Alternatively, allow vines to sprawl and train side branches upward onto the trellis. Vertical growing also facilitates earlier harvest and reduces pest pressure.

¶Watering and Irrigation

Water consistency is critical: provide 1.5-2 inches per week via drip irrigation or soaker hoses. Avoid overhead watering (promotes powdery mildew). Mulch with 2-3 inches of straw to retain soil moisture and regulate temperature. Uneven watering (dry spells followed by heavy rain or irrigation) causes misshapen, bitter fruit and increases susceptibility to powdery mildew.

Drought stress during fruit development dramatically reduces yield and quality; fruit becomes short, bumpy, bitter. In hot, dry climates, irrigate every 2-3 days if rain does not occur.

¶Nutrient Management

Side-dress with balanced fertilizer (5-10-10 or 10-10-10) at flowering and every 3-4 weeks thereafter. Container-grown cucumbers (5-gallon pots or larger) require weekly liquid feeding. Excess nitrogen produces rank foliage and delays flowering; balance nitrogen with potassium and phosphorus for consistent, quality fruiting.

¶Pest Management

Cucumber beetles (spotted and striped) transmit bacterial wilt (Erwinia species) and mosaic viruses; control is essential. Floating row covers protect young plants until flowering (then remove to allow pollination). Once beetles appear, use insecticidal soap, neem oil, or spinosad. Hand-pick beetles in early morning when they are sluggish.

Squash bugs and spider mites are less problematic on cucumber than on squash but may appear in stressed plants (drought, heat stress). Powdery mildew appears as white coating on leaves; improve air circulation and avoid overhead watering. Sulfur dust suppresses mildew; apply preventively in humid regions.

¶Harvest Schedule

Harvest slicing types at 7-8 inches (20-25 cm); pickling types at 4-6 inches (10-15 cm). Frequent harvest (every 2-3 days) promotes continuous production by preventing fruit maturation signals. Use pruning shears to avoid plant damage. Allowing fruits to mature on the vine signals the plant to reduce flowering; harvest all marketable fruit promptly.

A single productive vine yields 10-15 lbs slicing fruit or 20-30 lbs pickling fruit over a 4-6 week peak season.

¶Storage and Use

Refrigerate at 45-50°F; slicing types keep 5-7 days, pickling types 7-10 days. Avoid storing below 45°F (chilling injury causes soggy texture and flavor loss). For pickling, use fruit within 24 hours of harvest for best results.

¶Cultivar Recommendations

Slicing: ‘Straight Eight’ (60 days, reliable), ‘Suyo Long’ (65 days, Asian type, very long), ‘Lemon Cucumber’ (65-70 days, round, yellow, mild). Pickling: ‘Boston Pickling’ (50-60 days, bumpy, reliable), ‘National Pickling’ (60 days), ‘Kirby’ (55 days, smaller fruits).
