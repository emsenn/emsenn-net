Pumpkin

2026-04-02 A large-fruited cultivar of Cucurbita pepo requiring 85-120 days, grown for ornament or culinary use, with cultivars ranging from sugar pumpkins (good for cooking) to giant jack-o'-lantern types

Pumpkin is a cultivar group of Cucurbita pepo selected for large fruit size, distinctive ribbed or flattened orange rind, and long storage life. Unlike zucchini or summer squash, pumpkins are harvested at full maturity (85-120 days seed-to-harvest), cured, and stored for autumn harvest and decoration. Culinary pumpkins (sugar pumpkins, pie pumpkins) have sweeter, denser flesh than ornamental types.

¶Site Requirements and Soil

Pumpkins require full sun (8+ hours), well-draining loam, and moderate fertility (over-fertilizing produces all vine and few fruits). Ideal soil pH is 6.0-7.0. Work 2-3 inches of finished compost into planting area; pumpkins are less nitrogen-hungry than zucchini. In clay soils, create mounded hills 6-8 inches high, spaced 4-5 feet apart (vining cultivars) or 2-3 feet (dwarf/bush types).

¶Planting and Spacing

Direct-seed 2 weeks after last frost into warm soil (65°F minimum, 70°F optimal). Space seeds 4-6 inches apart in hills, thinning to 2-3 vigorous seedlings per hill once true leaves appear. Vining cultivars sprawl 6-10 feet in each direction; bush types remain more compact (3-4 feet diameter). Provide sturdy trellis or fence support if space is limited; cradle developing fruits in slings made from old t-shirt strips to prevent breaking.

¶Water and Nutrition

Provide consistent moisture (1-1.5 inches per week) but avoid waterlogging. Reduce watering once vines are mature (4-6 weeks post-emergence) and fruits begin sizing; this concentrates sugars and toughens the rind for storage. Drip irrigation is preferable to overhead watering (reduces fungal disease risk).

Fertilize at planting with balanced 5-10-10 formulation; side-dress once flowers appear. Do not over-fertilize; excess nitrogen promotes vine growth at the expense of fruit.

¶Pest Management

Squash bugs, cucumber beetles, and vine borers are primary pests. Squash bugs: scout for orange egg clusters on leaf undersides and crush them. Floating row covers protect young plants; remove at flowering. Vine borers: prevent by wrapping the base of main runners with aluminum foil or burying damaged vines under soil to re-root. Serious infestations require insecticide intervention (bifenthrin or spinosad applied in early morning).

Powdery mildew is common in humid climates; improve air circulation by pruning lower leaves once vines are established. Sulfur dust or sulfur-based fungicides suppress mildew without harming beneficial insects.

¶Fruit Development and Thinning

Each healthy vine produces 2-5 mature pumpkins depending on cultivar and growing conditions. Pinch off excess fruits once they reach walnut size, keeping only 1-2 per vine if maximum size is the goal. Remove all flowers and small fruits 8 weeks before the first expected frost; this redirects energy into final sizing of remaining fruits.

¶Harvest and Curing

Harvest when rind is fully orange and hard enough to resist fingernail pressure, 2-3 weeks before the first frost. Cut stems 3-4 inches long; handle carefully to avoid bruising. Do not wash immediately after harvest.

Cure pumpkins at 80-85°F and 80-85% relative humidity for 7-14 days to harden the rind and seal any wounds. A sunny, breezy location (garage corner, shed, or outdoor rack) works well. After curing, store at 50-55°F (unheated basement, cool garage) in dry conditions; properly cured pumpkins keep 3-6 months.

¶Culinary vs. Ornamental Types

Sugar pumpkins (1-2 lbs): ‘Sugar Pie’, ‘Sweetie Pie’, ‘Lil Ironsides’—fine-grained, sweet flesh, ideal for roasting and puree. Pie pumpkins (3-6 lbs): ‘Dickinson’, ‘Gladiator’, ‘Gladys’—classic texture, good flavor. Jack-o’-lantern types (10-20 lbs): ‘Howden’, ‘Connecticut Field’, ‘Gladiator’—bright orange, hollow cavity, mediocre flavor but excellent for carving.
