Shiso cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing protocol for Perilla frutescens (murasakino, purple shiso), frost-sensitive annual with purple or green leaf morphologies, self-seeding readily, used for pickles, garnish, and traditional Japanese preservation.

Shiso cultivation targets Perilla frutescens, particularly the purple-leafed form (murasakino), an annual herb killed by freezing that self-seeds readily in warm regions. The plant is frost-sensitive, thriving in USDA zones 8-11 outdoors as a perennial or warm-season annual; in cooler zones, it is grown as a warm-season annual, planted after all frost danger has passed.

¶Starting from Seed

Shiso seeds are very small (approximately 1 mm diameter) and require light for germination. Surface-sow seeds on moist soil, pressing gently without covering. Seeds germinate in 7-14 days at warm temperatures (70-80°F / 21-27°C). Start seeds indoors 6-8 weeks before transplanting, or direct-sow after last spring frost and soil is warm (May onwards in temperate regions).

Seeds remain viable 3-4 years when stored cool and dry. Shiso self-seeds aggressively; volunteers often appear throughout the garden the following season if plants are allowed to flower and set seed.

¶Planting and Spacing

Transplant seedlings at the 4-6 true leaf stage, spacing 18-24 inches apart. Shiso prefers warmth and full sun but tolerates part shade (4-6 hours daily). Soil should be fertile and moist; the plant grows vigorously in rich soils and is not particular about drainage, though it tolerates some wetness better than most herbs.

¶Foliage Harvest and Growth

Begin harvesting leaves once plants reach 12-18 inches tall (typically 60-90 days from sowing). Pinch or cut leaf stems to encourage branching and bushiness. Young, tender leaves (first 4-6 leaves from the apex) are preferred for fresh use and pickling; they are softer and more flavorful than older, tougher lower leaves.

Shiso grows vigorously in warm weather, producing copious foliage from June through October in most temperate regions. The plant reaches 2-4 feet tall and equally wide if left unpruned.

¶Uses and Timing

Fresh Leaf Harvest: Young, tender leaves are harvested as needed and used immediately as a garnish, palate cleanser with sashimi and sushi, or in salads and grilled dishes. Fresh shiso imparts a distinctive, complex flavor with peppery and minty undertones.

Pickling (Beni Shoga and Shibazuke): Leaves are harvested at the 6-8 leaf stage (largest leaves that are still tender) and immediately fermented with salt and red plums (ume) for beni shoga (red ginger replacement) or salt and cucumber for shibazuke pickles. Traditional pickling begins in midsummer with the mature foliage of early plantings.

Seed Harvest: If preservation of plants for next season is desired, allow some plants to flower and mature seeds in late summer. Seeds ripen 6-8 weeks after flowering and shatter (disperse) readily when ripe, self-sowing into the soil.

¶Seasonal Considerations

Shiso begins to flower as day length shortens in late summer (late August-September in temperate climates). Flowering triggers a shift from vegetative leaf production to reproductive growth. Pinching flower buds extends leaf production but reduces vigor. In zones where the plant overwinters (zones 8-11), mature plants may persist into winter and resume growth the following spring.

¶Pests and Diseases

Shiso is relatively pest-free. Spider mites may infest plants in hot, dry conditions; increase humidity or provide overhead watering. Few diseases affect shiso in well-drained soil with good air circulation.

¶Season-End and Self-Seeding

Before first frost, harvest all remaining tender leaves for fresh use or pickling. If self-seeding is desired, allow at least one plant to flower and mature seeds fully; seeds will shatter into soil and germinate the following spring. In colder zones, self-sown shiso does not overwinter; seeds germinate the following spring when soil warms.

¶Cultivar Notes

Purple forms (murasakino, akajiso) are most common in Japanese cultivation and are preferred for pickles and traditional uses. Green forms (aojiso) are used in some applications but are less common. Purple cultivars accumulate more anthocyanins (contributing color to pickles) and are slightly more vigorous than green forms.
