Sweet Grass

2026-04-02 Perennial grass cultivar of Hierochloe odorata, hardy zones 3-8. Rhizomatous, spreading mat-forming habit. Produces sweet vanilla fragrance (coumarin). Thrives in consistently moist, mineral-rich soil. Propagate by rhizome division (spring or fall). Indigenous sacred medicine of Anishinaabe peoples (Ojibwe, Odawa, Potawatomi); one of Four Sacred Medicines. Dried bundles used for smudging. Three-strand braids represent mind, body, spirit.

Sweet grass cultivation grows Hierochloe odorata for its sweet vanilla fragrance, its role as one of the Four Sacred Medicines in Anishinaabe tradition, and its use in smudging ceremonies and spiritual practice. The plant is called wiingashk in Ojibwe—the sacred hair of O’gushnan (Mother Earth).

Site Selection and Soil Preparation

Sweet grass requires consistently moist to wet soil with pH 6.5-8.0 (mineral-rich, circumneutral to slightly alkaline). Unlike most grasses, it disprefers acidic soils. Amend clay or sand with 4-6 inches of peat moss or aged compost and work in agricultural lime (2 lbs per 100 sq ft for sandy soils) to raise pH toward neutral. The plant is intolerant of permanently waterlogged, anaerobic conditions; ensure soil has adequate drainage (never standing water) while maintaining high moisture.

Full sun (6+ hours) is ideal for robust growth; part shade (4-6 hours sun) is acceptable. Avoid deep shade, which suppresses growth.

Propagation and Planting

Sweet grass is propagated by rhizome division rather than seed (seed is difficult to start and division is faster). Harvest rhizomes from established plants in spring (as new growth emerges) or fall (after growth slows). Divide rhizomes into 2-4 inch segments, each with at least one node (bud) visible.

Plant segments 1-2 inches deep in prepared, moist soil. Space plants 12-18 inches apart (rhizomes spread rapidly and will fill gaps within 2-3 seasons). Press soil firmly around segments and water thoroughly. In containers (12+ inches diameter, drainage holes required), fill with moist potting mix and embed rhizome segments at the prescribed depth.

Divisions establish quickly if kept consistently moist for the first 4-6 weeks; after establishment, the plant thrives in seasonal wet-dry cycles typical of wetlands.

Water and Fertility

Maintain consistently moist soil (never dry, but well-drained). Supplemental water is essential during dry spells; apply 1-1.5 inches per week if rainfall is inadequate. Do not fertilize; the plant requires no supplemental nutrients and thrives in naturally fertile, mineral-rich soils.

Pest and Disease Management

Sweet grass is remarkably pest-free. Occasionally, rust fungi may develop in humid climates; improve air circulation and avoid overhead watering if rust appears. No chemical intervention is necessary or recommended—simply allow the infection to run its course (foliage dies back to ground in fall anyway).

Habitat Development and Long-Term Care

Sweet grass forms dense, spreading mats within 3-5 years from initial planting; established colonies are self-perpetuating. The plant naturally spreads via rhizome proliferation, which can be encouraged by allowing rhizomes to fragment and colonize adjacent soil. In wetland gardens or rain gardens, sweet grass functions as a native grass component that stabilizes soil, improves water infiltration, and provides wildlife habitat.

The plant is deciduous; foliage dies back in late fall and regrows in spring. Cut back dead foliage in early spring or late fall.

Harvest for Fragrance and Smudging

Harvest timing: Peak fragrance occurs in mid-summer (July-August). Harvest stems by cutting them near the base with a sharp knife or shears. Gather stems into bundles of 15-20 stems (roughly fist-sized diameter).

Drying: Hang bundles upside-down in a warm, airy location (garage, shed, attic) away from direct sun. Drying typically takes 2-3 weeks; stems should be completely dry and brittle. Dried stems retain fragrance for months to years in sealed containers.

Braiding: The traditional three-strand braid represents mind, body, and spirit. Take three strands of dried grass and braid tightly from base to tip; tie the end with cotton thread or thin twine. Braids are worn or carried as spiritual protection and connection.

Smudging: Bundle dried stems (15-20 stalks, 4-6 inches long) and wrap tightly with natural cotton thread. Light the bundle and allow it to smolder; the smoke is used to bless, purify, and protect spaces and people. This practice is sacred to Anishinaabe and other Indigenous peoples; it should be performed with respect and cultural understanding.

Sustainable Practices and Cultural Responsibility

Sweet grass cultivation by non-Indigenous gardeners should be undertaken with respect for its sacred significance and with awareness that wild-harvested wiingashk is culturally important to Anishinaabe and other Indigenous peoples. Growing it in gardens:

  1. Reduces pressure on wild populations, supporting conservation.
  2. Allows respectful, abundant personal and community access for ceremonial use.
  3. Should be accompanied by learning about Anishinaabe plant knowledge and teachings.

Cultivar Recommendations

Wild-type Hierochloe odorata (no named cultivars exist; all garden populations are the species proper) is available from specialty nurseries and seed companies emphasizing native plants.
