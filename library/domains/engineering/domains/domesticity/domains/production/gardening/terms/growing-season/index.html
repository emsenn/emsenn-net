Growing Season

2026-04-02 The period between the last spring frost and the first fall frost during which frost-tender plants can thrive outdoors.

A growing season is the window of time in a calendar year when temperatures remain warm enough for frost-tender plants to grow outdoors without risk of freeze damage. It is defined by two critical frost dates: the last spring frost, when temperatures drop to 32°F (0°C) for the final time that year, and the first fall frost, when that threshold is reached again in autumn.

The growing season is not determined by calendar dates but by historical temperature data specific to geographic location. The USDA compiles regional frost date records and produces hardiness zone maps that help gardeners and farmers identify when their local frost window opens and closes. A region at 40°N latitude might have a 120-day growing season, while the same latitude in the Gulf Coast region experiences 270+ days. High-altitude mountain zones have much shorter seasons—Ironwood, Michigan in the Upper Peninsula has only 110–130 frost-free days, making it suitable primarily for cold-season and quick-maturing crops.

The relationship between frost dates and viable growing season is not linear. Soil temperature matters as much as air temperature: seeds will not germinate and seedlings will not grow in cold soil, even if daytime air temperatures are above freezing. Early and late season growth is thus limited by soil temperature lag behind air temperature, usually by 2–4 weeks.

Growing season length determines which crops are viable without season extension. Heat-loving crops that require 10–12 weeks of warm temperatures (peppers, melons) demand an indoor seed-starting head start in short-season regions. Cool-season crops (lettuce, kale, spinach) will mature from direct seed in regions with long springs and falls. The mismatch between a crop’s thermal requirements and your region’s growing season is why seed catalogs and extension services publish crop timing tables keyed to your frost dates.

Effective growing season extends in both directions through season-extension techniques—row covers and cold frames can lengthen spring by 2–4 weeks, while shade cloth and strategic watering can extend fall harvests by several weeks.

Growing degree days is a related metric that accounts for temperature, not just frost dates. A day where the mean temperature is 65°F counts as one growing degree day (base temperature 50°F). Cumulative GDD over a season reflects warmth accumulation and better predicts crop maturity than calendar days. Pepper maturity requires 2000–2500 GDD; a cold spring with the same calendar days as a warm year results in delayed flowering and shorter harvest.

Microclimates within a region extend or shorten growing season substantially. South-facing slopes warm earlier and stay warm later. Proximity to large bodies of water moderates temperature. Urban heat islands extend growing season by 2–4 weeks. These local variations make frost date maps approximate—gardens in the same USDA zone can differ by 10–14 frost-free days based on microclimate.

Understanding growing season is the foundation of regional crop selection. It defines the boundary between what is cultivable, what requires intervention (row covers, heat), and what is impossible.
