Korean Natural Farming

2026-04-02 A soil ecology approach that reconstructs local microbial communities to enhance plant growth without synthetic inputs

Korean Natural Farming (KNF) is not merely an organic technique but a systematic approach to soil ecology grounded in the hypothesis that plants thrive when cultivated within their native microbial context. Developed by Cho Han Kyu beginning in the 1960s and systematized by the 1980s, KNF treats soil not as inert substrate but as a living community whose reconstruction—not replacement—generates fertility and resilience.

¶The Core Principle: Matching Microbial Context to Place

Conventional agriculture disrupts soil structure through synthetic fungicides, fumigants, and intensive tillage. These disrupt the microbial populations that have co-evolved with local plant species and soil chemistry. Healthy soil contains billions of organisms per gram: bacteria, fungi, protozoa, and nematodes whose metabolic activities make nutrients bioavailable to plants.

KNF’s central insight is that restoring fertility requires reintroducing the specific microorganisms native to a location. This is distinct from applying generic compost or microbial inoculants. The method recovers place-specific microbial assemblies that have adapted to local soil pH, mineral profile, climate, and existing flora. This specificity matters: microorganisms are not interchangeable.

¶Indigenous Microorganisms (IMO): Collection and Logic

IMO collection operationalizes this principle. The process is deceptively simple and ecologically sound:

  1. Collection site selection: Locate an old-growth forest or undisturbed woodland where the soil has never been tilled or chemically treated. The leaf litter (duff) should be deep, cool, and damp. This ecosystem represents a stable microbial community in equilibrium.

  2. Inoculation substrate: Prepare cooked white or brown rice—a simple carbohydrate source that attracts local fungi and bacteria. Place this rice in a wooden box, partially bury it in the leaf litter, and cover it with more leaves to maintain humidity and darkness.

  3. Colonization period: Within five to seven days, the rice becomes covered in white mycelium—visible evidence of fungal colonization. The smell shifts to clean forest floor, indicating aerobic decomposition activity. This white mold represents the fast-growing fungi native to that specific forest.

  4. Preservation and multiplication: Harvest the colonized rice and mix it 1:1 with brown sugar (or molasses). This mixture ferments in sealed clay crocks for at least one month. The sugar preserves the microbes while supporting their continued growth, creating a stable, concentrated inoculant that can be stored and diluted for field application.

What occurs at the microbial level during fermentation is symbiosis in miniature: bacteria break down the sugar, producing organic acids that lower pH and preserve the culture. Fungi consume both sugar and bacterial metabolites. Protozoa graze on bacteria, controlling population density and promoting nutrient cycling. The resulting IMO is not a single species but a functioning microbiome, a miniature version of the forest soil that generated it.

¶Fermented Plant Juice (FPJ): Capturing Phytohormonal Signals

KNF’s second core technology captures another ecological relationship: the chemical signaling between plants and their soil microbiota. Fermented Plant Juice (FPJ) is made from fast-growing plant tissue (grasses, young shoots, legume tips) mixed with brown sugar and fermented in sealed containers for 7-10 days until the mixture produces pungent, yeasty aromatics.

Plants synthesize auxins, gibberellins, and cytokinins during rapid growth. When this tissue ferments, these hormones are preserved and concentrated. Applied as a root drench or foliar spray, FPJ delivers plant growth regulators in their bioavailable forms. The mechanism parallels soil chemistry: plants produce exudates through their roots that recruit and prime specific microbial populations; FPJ provides a complementary signal—externally supplied plant hormones that stimulate root development and nutrient uptake.

The fermentation itself is not incidental. Lactobacillus and other bacteria convert plant tissue into organic acids, amino acids, and secondary metabolites that enhance both hormone stability and microbial activity in the rhizosphere (the zone immediately around roots where root secretions and microbial activity intersect).

¶Alignment with Contemporary Soil Science

Recent mycorrhizal research validates KNF’s ecological logic. Approximately 80% of terrestrial plants form symbioses with arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF). These fungi produce both intracellular arbuscules (nutrient exchange organs inside root cells) and extensive networks of hyphae through the soil that increase the effective nutrient-absorbing surface by orders of magnitude.

Critically, different AMF species colonizing the same plant recruit distinct microbial populations. This means that the specific fungi in a location determine not only nutrient delivery but the entire microbial assembly that supports plant health. Common mycorrhizal networks—hyphal connections linking roots of different plants—facilitate nutrient transport and even inter-plant signaling, including defense priming: infected plants transfer signals (jasmonic acid) through the network, which recruits specific bacteria that suppress the pathogen in connected healthy plants.

KNF’s logic of place-specific microbial recruitment directly parallels this research. By sourcing IMO from old-growth forest soil within the region where one farms, KNF ensures that the microbes applied have already co-evolved with local plant species, local mineral profiles, and local mycorrhizal assemblies. The method accelerates what would otherwise be a slow process of microbial colonization and adaptation.

¶Implications: Ecology as Agricultural Method

KNF reveals that agriculture need not operate against ecology. Instead, it can operate within ecological constraints by understanding and working with microbial community assembly. The method requires:

  • Local knowledge: Different regions, soil types, and plant communities require different microbial inputs. No single inoculant works everywhere.
  • Observation and timing: Recognizing when IMO is ready (white mycelium present), when FPJ has fermented properly (aroma and visual changes), when soil microbial activity has recovered.
  • Integration with existing flora: The microbes applied must integrate with whatever wild microbes colonize the field. Success depends on ecological compatibility, not sterility.
  • Long-term thinking: Rebuilding soil microbiome fertility is slower than applying synthetic nitrogen fertilizer. The payoff is resilience: diverse microbial communities suppress disease, buffer against climate stress, and improve nutrient efficiency over time.

KNF thus represents a bridge between indigenous agricultural knowledge—centuries of Asian composting and soil management practices—and modern understanding of soil as a living, structured, information-rich system. It demonstrates that high-input agriculture is not inevitable; ecology itself can become the method.
