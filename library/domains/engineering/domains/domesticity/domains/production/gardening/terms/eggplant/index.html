Eggplant

2026-04-02 Warm-season annual fruiting crop (Solanum melongena, 70–90 days to first fruit). Large-fruited Occidental types produce purple, white, or striped berries (200–500 g). Heat-demanding; slow to establish from seed (requires 12–14 week indoor growing period). Prefers warm soil (25–30°C optimal). Self-compatible but benefits from insect pollination. Requires consistent moisture and fertile soil. White Garden variety (white-fruited, 3-inch heirloom) demonstrates diversity.

¶Climate and Growing Season

Eggplant is one of the most heat-demanding vegetables. It thrives at 25–30°C and ceases productive growth below 15°C. Growth is extremely slow in temperatures below 20°C. In temperate climates, the season is late spring through early fall only.

Start plants indoors 12–14 weeks before the last spring frost. Do not transplant to the garden until daytime temperatures consistently exceed 20°C and nighttime temperatures exceed 15°C. Early transplanting into cool soil results in severe stunting and delayed flowering.

Days to first fruit: 70–90 days from transplanting, depending on temperature and variety. In cool summer climates, you may not harvest mature fruit before first frost; choose early-maturing cultivars (70 days) for success.

¶Starting from Seed

Eggplant seed is slow to germinate (7–14 days) and requires warm soil (70–80°F / 21–27°C). Use a heat mat for reliable germination. Maintain high humidity with a dome or plastic cover for the first 2 weeks.

Once seedlings emerge, grow under bright lights (14–16 hours daily) and maintain soil moisture. Fertilize weakly every 2–3 weeks. Seedlings are slower-growing than tomato or pepper; plan for 12–14 weeks of indoor growing.

Harden off gradually over 10–14 days before transplanting. Do not rush to the garden; cold soil will cause setback.

¶Planting and Soil Preparation

Choose a location with 6–8 hours of direct sunlight (more is better—eggplant is very sun-demanding). Prepare soil with 3–4 inches of well-composted organic matter. Eggplant is a heavy feeder and prefers rich soil.

Target soil pH 6.0–7.0. Space transplants 24–36 inches apart (closer for smaller Asian cultivars, wider for large Occidental types). Do not crowd; good air circulation reduces disease.

Mulch heavily (3–4 inches) to retain soil heat and moisture. Warm soil is critical for eggplant success; dark mulch (not light straw) helps warm the soil.

¶Fertilization and Nutrient Management

Eggplant is a hungry feeder. Apply balanced fertilizer (10–10–10) at transplanting. Once flowering begins, switch to slightly lower-nitrogen (5–10–10 or 6–10–15) to favor fruiting.

Side-dress with compost or balanced fertilizer every 4–6 weeks. Avoid excessive nitrogen, which favors leaves at the expense of fruit.

Apply adequate potassium and phosphorus to support flowering and fruit development. Magnesium deficiency (yellowing of older leaves) occasionally occurs; foliar spray with dilute Epsom salt (1 tablespoon per gallon, 2–3 times during season) addresses this.

¶Watering

Eggplant requires consistent moisture: 1–1.5 inches weekly, more in hot weather. Water deeply and steadily; uneven watering causes fruit malformation and blossom-end rot.

Drip irrigation or soaker hoses are ideal. Avoid wetting foliage, which promotes fungal diseases. Water in early morning.

Mulching heavily helps regulate soil moisture and temperature.

¶Flowering and Pollination

Flowers are solitary or clustered, pale purple to lavender, with yellow stamens and typically a longer pistil. The plant is self-compatible; flowers can self-set fruit. However, insect pollination increases fruit set and seed production.

In home gardens, bees and other insects usually provide adequate pollination. In greenhouses or low-pollinator areas, hand-vibrate flowers by tapping stems with your fingers or a vibrator tool.

Flowers drop without setting fruit if temperatures exceed 30°C at night. This is normal; new flowers will develop once temperatures moderate.

¶Fruit Development and Ripeness

Fruit develops rapidly once set—typically 3–4 weeks from flower to harvest-ready size. The fruit changes color from glossy green to full color (purple, white, or striped) as it matures.

Harvest eggplant when:

  • Skin is glossy and taut
  • Color is fully developed
  • Fruit is still slightly firm to moderately firm (not spongy or dull-looking)

Harvest slightly immature if you prefer tenderer, less seedy fruit. Very large, dull-skinned fruit is likely overripe and will be tough and bitter.

Cut (don’t pull) the fruit from the plant, leaving 1–2 inches of stem attached. Eggplants have prickly calyxes (sepals); wear gloves or use pruners to avoid cuts.

¶Notable Cultivars

Black Beauty (classic purple, 70–80 days): Large glossy purple fruits, reliable, widely available. Standard Occidental cultivar.

Ichiban (Japanese, 65–75 days): Long, slender, thin-walled fruits; tender, early-maturing. Oriental type.

Ping Tung Long (Taiwanese, 60–70 days): Small, slender, pale purple; early maturity.

White Garden (heirloom, 70–80 days): Small white 3-inch fruits; African heritage variety. Prolific, decorative, tender, early-maturing.

Striata di Genova (Italian, 75–85 days): Purple with white striping; meaty texture, excellent flavor.

Graffiti (heirloom, 75–85 days): Purple exterior with white striping; small to medium fruits, excellent flavor.

Thai Green (green-skinned Oriental type): Small, tender, harvested while green.

¶Harvest, Storage, and Preparation

Harvest regularly (every 2–3 days during peak season) to encourage continuous production. Regular harvesting prevents the plant from exhausting itself on a single large fruit.

Fresh eggplant keeps 4–7 days at room temperature or 1–2 weeks refrigerated in a plastic bag. Do not store below 10°C; cold temperatures damage texture and flavor.

Eggplant is best used fresh but can be grilled, roasted, or fried. Salting eggplant slices 30 minutes before cooking (osmotic dehydration) reduces bitterness and water content, resulting in better texture.

¶Common Problems

  • Slow growth or few flowers: From cool soil or air, or insufficient light. Wait for warmer weather; eggplant cannot be rushed.
  • Blossom-end rot: Calcium deficiency from inconsistent watering. Ensure steady moisture and mulch to regulate soil.
  • Fruit malformation: From inconsistent watering or excessive heat. Maintain even moisture; provide afternoon shade in extreme heat (>35°C).
  • Spider mites: Common in hot, dry conditions. Increase humidity by misting; use insecticidal soap if severe.
  • Verticillium wilt: Fungal soil disease causing wilting and death. Prevent by crop rotation and disease-resistant cultivars.
  • Early blight (Alternaria): Leaf spots; worse with overhead watering. Drip irrigate; remove affected leaves; apply sulfur or copper fungicide if severe.
