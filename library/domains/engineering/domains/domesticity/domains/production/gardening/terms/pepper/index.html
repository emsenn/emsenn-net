Pepper

2026-04-02 Warm-season annual fruiting crop (Capsicum annuum, 60–90 days to first fruit). Includes sweet bell peppers and chili peppers (wide Scoville range 0–200,000+ SHU). Requires warm soil and air (germination >70°F optimal). Slow to establish; start indoors 8–10 weeks before last frost. Heat-demanding; production slows below 20°C. Self-compatible but benefits from insect pollination. Moderate water and fertilizer needs.

¶Starting from Seed and Transplants

Pepper seed is slow to germinate (10–21 days) and requires warm soil (70–80°F / 21–27°C). Start indoors 8–10 weeks before your last spring frost. Use a heat mat if ambient indoor temperatures are below 70°F. Provide bottom heat and high humidity (dome or plastic cover) for the first 2 weeks.

Once seedlings emerge, grow under bright lights (14–16 hours daily) and maintain soil moisture (not waterlogged). Fertilize weakly every 2 weeks with dilute balanced fertilizer. Harden off gradually over 7–10 days before transplanting outdoors.

Transplant to the garden only after nighttime temperatures consistently exceed 15°C and soil has warmed to 18°C or higher. Early transplanting into cold soil causes stunting and delayed flowering.

¶Soil, Spacing, and Location

Choose a location with 6–8 hours of direct sunlight. Peppers tolerate a wider range of soils than tomatoes but prefer well-draining, fertile soil amended with 2–3 inches of compost. Target soil pH 6.0–7.0.

Space plants 18–24 inches apart (closer for compact ornamental peppers, wider for large bell types). Peppers develop slowly and do not fill space quickly; generous spacing ensures air circulation and allows room for mature plant canopy.

Mulch heavily (2–3 inches) to retain soil moisture and regulate temperature. Peppers are thermophilic—warm soil and air favor flowering and capsaicinoid accumulation.

¶Flowering and Pollination

Peppers produce perfect flowers (containing both male and female organs) in small clusters. Self-pollination is possible but inefficient: natural pollen transfer is limited because stamens are shorter than the stigma. Insect pollination (bumblebees, solitary bees, flies) increases fruit set 20–40% above self-pollination.

In home gardens, insects typically provide adequate pollination. In greenhouses or low-insect-activity areas, hand-vibrate flowering clusters daily or maintain bumble bee hives.

Flowers drop without setting fruit if stressed (too cold, very hot nights, inconsistent water). This is normal; new flowers will develop once conditions improve.

¶Watering and Fertilization

Peppers require consistent moisture: 1–1.5 inches weekly. Uneven watering causes spotty ripening and fruit deformity (thick-walled fruits are particularly sensitive). Drip irrigation or soaker hoses provide steady moisture without wetting foliage.

Peppers are moderate feeders. Apply balanced fertilizer (10–10–10) at transplanting, then reduce nitrogen once flowering begins. Too much nitrogen favors leaf growth and reduces fruiting. Side-dress with compost every 4–6 weeks or apply dilute balanced fertilizer every 2 weeks.

Phosphorus and potassium support flowering and fruit development; consider a slightly higher-K fertilizer (e.g., 5–10–15) once flowering is underway.

¶Heat and Pungency

Capsaicinoid accumulation (the “heat” or pungency) increases under warm conditions (25–30°C) and full sunlight. Cool summers favor green, less pungent fruit. Cultivars vary widely in maximum heat production: bell peppers produce essentially zero capsaicinoid (homozygous recessive at the P locus); jalapeños produce 2,500–8,000 SHU (Scoville Heat Units); habaneros and superhots produce 100,000–2,000,000 SHU.

Ripening color (green → red/yellow/orange) develops independently of pungency. Fully ripe peppers are sweeter (increased sugar) and hotter (higher capsaicinoid) than immature green fruit.

¶Notable Cultivars

Capsicum annuum (most common peppers):

Jimmy Nardello (sweet Italian heirloom, 1887): Thin-walled, elongated, sweet flavor. Ripens from green to red. Excellent fresh or dried for paprika. Indeterminate, productive.

Korean Hong-Gochu (centuries of farmer selection): Medium pungency, thin-walled, bright red when ripe. Traditional Korean chili, fermented into gochugaru (Korean red chili powder). Essential for kimchi and Korean cooking.

¶Related Species and Cultivars

Capsicum chinense (habanero, Biquinho, datil): Hotter and more aromatic than C. annuum; requires longer season and warmer climate (zones 9–11 for reliable fruiting).

Capsicum frutescens (piri piri, Tabasco): Perennial shrub; small upright fruit; intense heat. More ornamental; grown decoratively in cool climates, commercially in tropical regions.

¶Harvest and Storage

Harvest peppers at any stage—green (immature), ripening (partially colored), or fully ripe (full color development). Green peppers are slightly more astringent; ripe peppers are sweeter and have higher vitamin C content.

Cut peppers from the plant (don’t pull) to avoid damaging branches. Peppers keep 1–2 weeks at room temperature or 2–3 weeks refrigerated in a plastic bag.

Dry fresh peppers by threading them on string and hanging in a warm, dry location (70–80°F), or use a food dehydrator. Dried peppers store for several months in airtight containers.

¶Common Problems

  • Blossom-end rot: Calcium deficiency from inconsistent watering. Keep soil evenly moist.
  • Sunscald: Overly exposed fruit bleaches white. Provide afternoon shade in very hot climates or ensure adequate foliage.
  • Slow growth: Usually from cold soil or air. Wait for warm weather; transplant earlier indoors.
  • Pests: Spider mites, whiteflies, thrips, hornworms. Increase humidity (reduces spider mites); use insecticidal soap or neem oil if needed.
