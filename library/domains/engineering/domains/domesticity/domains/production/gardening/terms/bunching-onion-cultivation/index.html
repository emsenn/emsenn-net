Bunching onion cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing protocol for Japanese bunching onions (Allium fistulosum), day-neutral varieties enabling year-round production with 60-90 day maturation, sequential plantings, and Tokyo Long White heirloom emphasis.

Bunching onions are day-length neutral—unlike bulbing onions that require photoperiod matching to latitude, bunching onions grow identically under any daylength regime. This photoperiodic indifference, combined with rapid maturation (60-90 days), enables 2-3 successive plantings per season in temperate climates, or year-round production in appropriate regions.

¶Variety Selection and Tokyo Long White

Tokyo Long White (also Senju, Naga Negi, Shiro Negi) is the most widely adapted heirloom cultivar, selected in Japan for extended white shanks (6-12 inches) and mild, sweet flavor. The stems reach 18-24 inches tall with crisp, tender white portions and robust green tops. This variety succeeds across all US growing zones (hardier in cool regions, spring-planted in warm).

Other Japanese heirlooms offer shorter stems (15-18 inches) for compact spaces, winter hardiness, or specific flavor profiles. Experimental cultivation with region-specific Japanese heirlooms is rewarding for adventurous gardeners.

¶Planting and Spacing

Direct-sow seed 1/8 inch deep in beds after last frost danger (spring soil temp 50°F+), or start indoors 8 weeks before desired transplant date and transplant 4-6 weeks after germination. Space seedlings or direct-seeded plantings 2-3 inches apart in rows 12-18 inches apart for standard Tokyo Long White; closer spacing (1.5-2 inches) yields thinner, earlier-maturing stems suitable for scallion harvests.

Soil should be well-draining, moderately fertile (balanced 10-10-10 fertilizer at planting), and consistently moist; bunching onions do not tolerate drought stress as well as bulbing types.

¶Sequential Planting for Continuous Harvest

In temperate zones (USDA 5-7), plant every 2-3 weeks from April through August for continuous summer harvests. Spring plantings mature June-July; mid-summer plantings mature August-September; late-summer plantings mature September-October. In zones 8-10, autumn planting (September) provides winter and spring harvests, while spring planting (March-April) produces summer crops with some bolt risk in intense heat.

¶Harvest and Timing

Bunching onions are ready to harvest at 60-90 days from direct seeding or 4-6 weeks from transplanting. Harvest when white shanks are 4-6 inches long and green tops are robust (12-18 inches). They can be harvested slightly earlier (thinner) for scallion use or allowed to mature fully for larger, more flavorful stems.

Harvest by pulling entire plant and trimming roots and excess green leaf material, or cut above ground leaving roots to regenerate 1-2 new stems per plant over 2-3 weeks (though yields decline with each regrowth cycle; single harvest is more efficient).

¶Growing Season Extension and Winter Production

In zones 8-10, autumn-planted Tokyo Long White survives mild winters and produces harvestable stems through December-February. Mulch lightly (2-3 inches straw) after ground freezes to moderate temperature fluctuation. In zones 5-7, autumn plantings (August-September) produce generous harvests through October-November before hard freezes kill foliage.

¶Storage and Preservation

Fresh bunching onions store 1-2 weeks in plastic bags in refrigerator. For longer storage, blanch briefly (2-3 minutes), cool, and freeze in freezer bags for up to 6 months; frozen bunching onions work well in soups, stir-fries, and cooked dishes, though texture is soft.

¶Flavor and Culinary Notes

Tokyo Long White is prized in Japanese cuisine for mild, slightly sweet flavor and crisp texture. The extensive white shank is ideal for grilling, braising, or using raw in salads. Flavor is milder than bulbing onion and more delicate than garlic chives.
