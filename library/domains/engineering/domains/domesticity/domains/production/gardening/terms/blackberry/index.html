Blackberry

2026-04-02 Thorny or thornless perennial shrub bearing aggregate berries on two-year-old canes, hardy to zone 5 with winter protection, productive 20+ years.

Cultivated blackberries are woody perennial shrubs producing biennial canes—stems that grow vegetatively in year one and flower and fruit in year two, then die back. The plant persists 20–40+ years from a permanent rootstock, making it a valuable long-term landscape and production element. Thornless cultivars such as Arapaho have transformed blackberry cultivation from a brutal thorn-picking enterprise into manageable horticulture.

¶Site Selection and Establishment

Blackberries succeed in full sun (6+ hours daily) with well-draining soil, pH 5.5–7.0. Unlike raspberries, they tolerate drier soils once established. Plant dormant canes in spring as soil becomes workable. Space floricane cultivars 2–3 feet apart in rows 6–8 feet wide to permit tractor cultivation and air circulation. Train and tie canes to a T-trellis or wire system to keep them off the ground, reducing disease pressure and facilitating harvest and pruning.

Blackberries are hardy to zone 5b (with winter protection) and zone 4 with heavy insulation. In borderline zones (5a–5b), mulch 4–6 inches of straw or wood chips after first frost, and consider laying canes down horizontally and covering them with soil or boards during the most severe cold spells. This “laying down” practice in deep winter effectively buries the canes beneath the snowpack, providing insulation. Straighten them in spring as they push new growth.

¶Pruning and Cane Management

Remove all first-year primocane growth that does not produce flowers (the stem that grew this year and won’t flower until next year). In floricane-fruiting Arapaho, this means cutting out all non-flowering wood immediately after the previous summer’s growth. Pinch back long canes to 4–5 feet in height during early summer to encourage lateral branch development and sturdier fruiting wood. After harvest, completely remove the two-year-old fruiting canes at their base—they are exhausted and harbor disease and pests.

Thorny cultivars such as Kiowa and Navaho produce canes armed with sharp prickles; thornless types (Arapaho, Chester) lack these but otherwise require identical management. The lack of thorns significantly reduces hand-harvest injuries and enables more intensive hand-thinning and picking.

¶Pollination and Fruiting

Blackberries are self-pollinating and do not require a second cultivar for fruit set, though cross-pollination by native bees may increase yield. Each flower produces dozens of small drupelets clustered around a central receptacle, forming the glossy black aggregate fruit. At full ripeness, the fruit should come cleanly away from the receptacle with a gentle pull. Underripe berries stubbornly cling and may be damaged by forceful picking; patience ensures maximum yield and quality.

¶Water Management and Nutrition

Water deeply and regularly during dry spells, especially during fruiting, aiming for 1–2 inches per week by rainfall or irrigation. Drip irrigation is ideal for avoiding fungal leaf diseases. Mulch with 2–3 inches of wood chips or straw to conserve moisture and suppress weeds. Blackberries are moderate feeders; apply a balanced fertilizer (10–10–10) in early spring before growth resumption, or compost annually.

¶Cultivar Selection for Zone 5

Arapaho (zone 5b–9) is a thornless floricane blackberry developed at the University of Arkansas. It is early-season, self-pollinating, and produces medium-large berries with good flavor. It is moderately hardy and requires winter protection in zone 5b.

Chester (zone 5–9) is another thornless floricane, mid-to-late season, with excellent flavor and size. It is hardy and productive.

Kiowa (zone 6–9) is a thorny floricane with large, firm berries and excellent flavor but is less cold-hardy.

For zone 4 and extreme cold climates, primocane-fruiting raspberries (not blackberries) may be more reliable, as they fruit on current-season growth that is entirely aboveground and not vulnerable to winter dieback.

¶Pest and Disease Management

Cane blight, orange rust, septoria leaf spot, and anthracnose are common fungal diseases. Prevent through:

  • Spacing plants for air circulation
  • Avoiding overhead watering
  • Removing infected canes immediately
  • Cleaning pruning tools between cuts (dip in 10% bleach solution)

Japanese beetles and spider mites can damage foliage; handpicking beetles into soapy water is effective for small plantings. Spider mites thrive in heat and drought; ensure adequate moisture.

Red-tailed bumblebees and native solitary bees are the primary pollinators; avoid insecticides during bloom.

¶Harvest and Storage

Harvest ripe berries (fully black, slightly soft) in early morning when cool. Refrigerate immediately; they keep 5–7 days. For extended storage, freeze whole berries or prepare preserves (jam, syrup, wine).
