Beet

2026-04-02 Cool-season root crop with edible thick taproots and leafy greens, requiring thinning of monogerm seed and tolerance of slight frost; Chioggia varietal offers striking candy-stripe pattern.

Beets are a versatile cool-season crop grown for both the thick, fleshy taproot and the tender leafy greens (beet tops). The plant is biennial but is harvested in the first year before flowering. Beets tolerate slight frost (to -5°C / 23°F) and thrive in cool seasons, making them excellent for spring and fall cultivation in most climates. The roots are nutrient-dense, rich in betalain pigments (antioxidants), and store for months under proper conditions.

¶Soil Preparation and Site Selection

Beets succeed in well-draining soil, pH 6.0–7.5, amended with 1–2 inches of compost worked in to 8 inches depth. They are moderate feeders and tolerate slightly alkaline soil better than acidic conditions. Avoid fresh manure and excessive nitrogen, which promote excessive top growth at the expense of root development and cause oversized, woody roots.

The site should receive full sun (6+ hours daily) and have consistently moist soil; beets in dry conditions develop woody texture and unpleasant flavor.

¶Sowing and Seed Structure

Beet “seed” is actually a seed ball comprising 2–5 fused fruits, from which multiple seedlings emerge. Older seed is predominantly multigerm; modern monogerm cultivars (geneticized to produce single-seeded fruits) greatly reduce thinning labor.

Sow seed 1/2 inch deep, 1–2 inches apart, in rows 12 inches apart. Thin seedlings at the 2–3 leaf stage to 3–4 inches apart. If using monogerm seed, thinner initial spacing and minimal thinning are required; multigerm seed necessitates ruthless thinning for quality roots. Underthinnined beets remain small, misshapen, and fibrous.

¶Watering and Cultivation

Water consistently to maintain soil moisture (1–2 inches per week). Irregular watering causes cracking and bitterness; sustained drought induces bolting (premature flowering, rendering the root woody and inedible). Mulch lightly with compost or grass clippings to suppress weeds and conserve moisture.

Beets are slow-growing; harvest is typically 50–65 days after sowing for small (2–3 inch diameter) tender roots, or 70–80 days for larger, fully mature roots.

¶Cultivar Selection and Flavor

Chioggia (60–70 days) is an Italian heirloom with striking concentric rings of deep red and white tissue throughout the root (visible when sliced in cross-section). The rings create a candy-stripe pattern that is visually stunning—fresh, raw, or in salads. However, the red betalain pigment bleeds during cooking, causing the rings to fade to a uniform dark color. For maximum visual effect, enjoy Chioggia raw or barely cooked.

Detroit Dark Red (55–70 days) is a classic cultivar with uniform deep red roots, excellent flavor, and superior storage. It is reliable, productive, and suitable for fresh market and preservation.

Golden (50–70 days) offers a gentler, slightly sweeter flavor than red types, with yellow-gold flesh. It does not stain hands or cutting boards and is visually striking in salads.

¶Harvest and Storage

Harvest beets when they reach the desired size (2–4 inches for tender roots, 3–4 inches for mature storage roots). Gently dig or pull roots, removing soil. Do not wash before storage; store with skins intact to prevent loss of color pigments.

For long-term storage, twist off the green tops (leaving 1/2 inch of stem to prevent drying and bleeding), brush off soil, and place roots in a cool location (32–50°F / 0–10°C) in damp sand or sawdust. Properly stored beets keep 3–6 months. In an unheated cellar, garage, or cold frame, beets often persist through winter.

Do not discard the greens. Young beet tops are tender, sweet, and highly nutritious; use them fresh in salads or cooked like spinach. Mature greens are tougher but still palatable when blanched.

¶Succession Planting

Sow beets every 3 weeks from early spring through July for continuous harvest. A late-July sowing matures in fall and tolerates light frost, providing quality roots through October in most climates. Fall-grown beets develop enhanced sweetness as cool temperatures promote sugar accumulation.

¶Pest and Disease Management

Flea beetles chew small holes in seedling foliage but rarely cause serious damage. Row covers (lightweight fabric) exclude them during early season. Beet webworm (a moth larva) spins tents and skeletonizes leaves; pick off affected leaves or apply Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) if damage is severe.

Fungal leaf diseases (cercospora leaf spot, powdery mildew) occur in humid, cool conditions. Ensure adequate air circulation and avoid overhead watering.

¶Bolting and Prevention

Beets bolt (flower prematurely) if exposed to sustained cold (below 10°C / 50°F) in the first 3–4 weeks after germination. To prevent bolting, avoid early spring sowing in very cold soil; wait until soil reaches 50°F. A late-spring sowing (April–May in temperate zones) avoids vernalization entirely. Similarly, a fall sowing (July–August) escapes spring-sown exposure to cold and bolting.

¶Betalain Chemistry and Nutrition

The red and purple colors in beets are betalains (betacyanins and betaxanthins), antioxidant compounds unique to Caryophyllales. Betalains are water-soluble and leach readily into cooking water; to preserve color, boil beets whole and unpeeled, or roast them. Beets also contain folate, manganese, and fiber—making them a nutrient-dense vegetable crop.
