Chamomile cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing protocol for Matricaria chamomilla, annual herb with self-seeding tendency, extremely easy cultivation, flower harvesting at bloom peak for essential oil content, distinguished from Roman chamomile (Chamaemelum nobile, perennial).

Chamomile cultivation targets Matricaria chamomilla (German chamomile), an extremely easy-to-grow annual herb distinguished from Roman chamomile (Chamaemelum nobile, a perennial) by its annual life cycle, smaller stature, and suitability for larger-scale tea and essential oil production. German chamomile self-seeds readily, often establishing volunteer populations year after year once planted.

Starting from Seed

Chamomile seeds are extremely fine and should be surface-sown on moist soil without covering; light is required for germination. Sow directly into the garden in autumn (September-November in northern temperate regions) or early spring (March-April) for best results. Autumn sowing produces earlier, larger plants than spring sowing.

Alternatively, start seeds indoors 4-6 weeks before transplanting, maintaining soil temperature at 60-70°F (15-21°C). Germination occurs in 7-14 days. Seeds remain viable 4-5 years when stored cool and dry.

Planting and Spacing

Thin seedlings to 12-18 inches apart once plants have 4 true leaves. Chamomile tolerates crowding more than many herbs and will produce flowers at closer spacing (6-12 inches), though plants are less vigorous and flowers are smaller.

Chamomile prefers full sun (6+ hours daily) and well-drained soil. The plant is extremely drought-tolerant and does not require fertilization; in fact, overly fertile soil produces excessive vegetative growth at the expense of flowers. Poor, well-drained soils produce the most flowers and most concentrated essential oils.

Flowering and Bloom Timing

Chamomile flowers appear 45-60 days after transplanting, with flowers blooming sequentially from late May through July (northern hemisphere). Peak flowering occurs in mid-June to early July. Each plant produces 50-200+ flowers over the season, depending on cultivar and growing conditions.

Flowers develop from a small knot of buds at the apex of the stem. The white ray petals unfurl first, followed by the yellow central disc, which becomes slightly domed as it matures. At peak maturity, the ray petals spread horizontally and the disc is fully yellow and domed.

Flower Harvesting

Harvest flowers at peak bloom (when ray petals are fully extended and just beginning to reflex backward, usually mid-morning once dew has dried). This is when essential oil content is highest. Harvest by pinching or cutting flowers off just below the flower head, leaving the stem and remaining buds on the plant.

Daily harvesting from peak bloom plants yields 1-2 pounds of fresh flowers per plant over a season, or 0.2-0.3 pounds dried flowers (approximately 80 percent water loss during drying).

Drying

Dry flowers immediately after harvest to preserve essential oils and color. Spread flowers in a single layer on screens or in paper bags with holes for ventilation. Dry in a warm, airy location (70-85°F / 21-29°C) with low humidity. Flowers dry completely in 2-7 days depending on humidity. Dried flowers should be brittle and crackling when touched. Store dried flowers in airtight containers in a cool, dark location; they retain potency for 1 year.

Season-End and Self-Seeding

Allow some plants to flower fully and mature seeds late in the season (July-August). Seeds will shatter into the soil when fully mature and ripe. These self-sown seeds germinate the following spring, establishing volunteer chamomile throughout the garden. Self-seeded plants often appear in pathways, beds, and disturbed areas year after year with minimal intervention.

Roman Chamomile vs. German Chamomile

Roman chamomile (Chamaemelum nobile) is a low-growing perennial (12-18 inches tall) hardy in USDA zones 4-9, with double-flowered ornamental forms available. Its flowers are smaller and it produces fewer flowers per plant. German chamomile is taller (20-60 inches), more floriferous, and preferred for commercial tea and essential oil production due to higher essential oil yield and lower production costs.

Pest and Disease

Chamomile is nearly pest-free. Occasional spider mite infestations may occur in hot, dry conditions; overhead watering deters mites. Powdery mildew may occur in humid, poorly ventilated locations; ensure good air circulation.

Common Uses and Tea Preparation

Dried chamomile flowers are brewed as a gentle tea with mild flavor and calming properties. A standard serving is 1-2 teaspoons of dried flowers in 8 oz of hot (not boiling) water, steeped 5-10 minutes. The essential oil is used in cosmetics, pharmaceuticals, and tinctures.
