Miner's lettuce cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing protocol for *Claytonia perfoliata*, native to western North America. Cool-season, shade-tolerant annual suited to spring and fall production in temperate climates. Prefers moist, peaty soil and partial shade; self-seeds readily. 37–40 days to maturity; minimal pest pressure.

Claytonia perfoliata is a cool-season annual ideally suited to partial-shade cultivation in spring and fall; the species self-seeds reliably, allowing continuous production from volunteer seedlings in subsequent seasons.

¶Sowing and Seedling Stage

Sow directly in beds or containers in cool weather (spring after last frost danger or fall 8–12 weeks before first freeze). Sow seeds 0.5 inch deep, 1 inch apart; keep moist but not waterlogged. Germination occurs at 10–18°C in 7–14 days. Thin seedlings to 6 inches apart.

Alternatively, start seeds indoors in cool conditions (15–18°C) and transplant at 3–4 true leaves. Handle seedlings gently; they are delicate.

¶Growing Conditions and Site Selection

Shade: Miner’s lettuce thrives in partial shade (50% incident light); full sun is tolerable but plants bolt earlier and may stress in warm weather. Afternoon shade is particularly valuable in spring plantings.

Soil: Prefers moist (not waterlogged), humus-rich, slightly acidic soil. Amend with compost or aged peat moss; the succulent leaves perform best in consistently moist media. Mulch with 1–2 inches of compost to maintain moisture.

Water: Keep soil consistently moist (1–2 inches per week); irregular watering stresses plants. Container cultivation allows precise moisture control.

¶Growing Season Management

No fertilization required if soil is rich in organic matter. Minimal pest pressure; occasional slugs and snails may affect seedlings. Handpick or use beer traps if damage is significant.

¶Harvesting

Whole-plant harvest (37–40 days): Cut at soil level when rosette is 4–6 inches diameter. Tender and suitable for fresh use or light cooking.

Continuous harvest: For extended production, pinch outer leaves and allow the center to continue growing, extending harvest to 6–8 weeks per plant.

Leaves are mild and slightly nutty; the succulent texture is delicate and suitable for salad mixes or gentle wilting in warm dishes.

¶Self-Seeding and Succession

Allow a few plants to bolt and set seed; self-sown seedlings will emerge in subsequent cool seasons (fall and spring) without replanting. This allows continuous production from volunteer seedlings in perennial beds or containers. Seeds are viable for 2–3 years.

¶Seasonal Timing

  • Spring planting: Sow after last frost in April; harvest through May–June as temperatures warm.
  • Fall planting: Sow in August–September; harvest through fall and into early winter in mild climates.
  • Winter in zones 9+: Winter-sown plants may produce through mild winters without dieback.
  • Summer: Not recommended; heat stress and rapid bolting eliminate productive window.

¶Storage and Use

Harvest in cool morning hours. Use immediately or store in high-humidity refrigerator (35–40°F) for 5–7 days. The succulent leaves are best used fresh or lightly cooked (steamed, wilted into warm dishes).

¶Companion Planting

Miner’s lettuce grows well under taller shade-providing crops (tomatoes, beans) in spring and fall. The shade moderates soil temperature and the groundcover effect suppresses weeds.

¶Regional Suitability

Ideal in temperate zones (4–8) with reliable spring and fall cool seasons. In zones 9+, winter production is possible. In zones 1–3, season may be too brief; extend season with cold frames or high tunnels.
