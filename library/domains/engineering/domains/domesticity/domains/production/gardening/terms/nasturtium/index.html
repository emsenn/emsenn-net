Nasturtium

2026-04-02 Annual cultivar of Tropaeolum majus, 55-70 days to first flower. Trailing vines 1.5-3 m or compact bush types 0.3-0.5 m. Entirely edible—peppery flowers, leaves, unripe seed pods ("poor man's capers"). Premier aphid trap crop; plant 4-5 feet from vulnerable crops (tomato, squash, beans). Full sun, moderate soil, consistent moisture. Cold-sensitive annual.

Nasturtium cultivation grows Tropaeolum majus for edible flowers, leaves, and seed pods as well as its unparalleled function as an aphid trap crop. The plant is highly palatable to black bean aphid (Aphis fabae), cotton aphid (Aphis gossypii), and green peach aphid (Myzus persicae), concentrating pest populations and diverting pressure from tomato, squash, beans, and other vulnerable crops.

Site Selection and Soil Preparation

Nasturtium thrives in full sun to part shade (minimum 4-6 hours sun) and prefers moderate, well-drained soil with pH 6.0-7.0. Rich, overly fertile soil produces excessive foliage at the expense of flowers; amend poor soil modestly with 2-3 inches of compost. The species is sensitive to waterlogging; clay soils should be amended with compost or perlite to improve drainage. Avoid feeding with high-nitrogen fertilizer.

Spacing and Planting

Direct-seed or transplant seedlings after all frost danger passes and soil has warmed to 60°F. Nasturtium seeds have a hard seed coat; soak overnight in water before planting to accelerate germination (7-10 days at 18-22°C). Spacing depends on cultivar form:

  • Trailing/vining cultivars (2-3 m): place 24-36 inches apart; provide trellis, fence, or sprawling ground-cover space.
  • Compact bush cultivars (0.3-0.5 m): place 12-18 inches apart; support not required.

For maximum trap-crop effect, interplant nasturtium at the perimeter of vegetable beds, positioning vines 4-5 feet from rows of tomato, pepper, squash, cucumber, or beans. The volatile compounds and high aphid populations on nasturtium attract and concentrate pest pressure, significantly reducing infestation on economically valuable adjacent crops (up to 60-80% reduction in trials).

Water and Fertility

Water moderately—1-1.5 inches per week combined rain and irrigation. The plant is drought-tolerant once established but flowers more prolifically with consistent moisture. Excess water invites powdery mildew; avoid overhead watering and ensure adequate air circulation. Do not fertilize; excess nitrogen produces lank foliage and few flowers, reducing both ornamental value and trap-crop effectiveness.

Pest and Disease Management

Nasturtium is largely pest-resistant due to glucosinolate-derived defenses. Powdery mildew may develop in humid summers; improve air circulation by pruning lower foliage and spacing plants adequately. Cucumber mosaic virus occasionally infects nasturtium; remove infected plants to prevent spread. The plant is unpalatable to most mammalian herbivores.

Harvest and Use

Harvest flowers continuously once they open; both ornament and flavor peak when petals fully unfurl. Flowers last 3-5 days after cutting and can be used fresh in salads, as garnish, or in edible flower arrangements.

Harvest leaves when young and tender (first 4-6 true leaves per stem); older leaves become fibrous and bitter. Leaves are excellent raw in salads (peppery flavor) or wilted quickly as a green vegetable.

For “poor man’s capers,” collect immature seed pods 3-4 weeks post-anthesis when they are still soft and green. Pickle immediately in vinegar (white or apple cider vinegar, heated to 180°F with pickling spices—mustard seed, dill, coriander seed). Store in sterilized jars; flavor develops after 2-3 weeks. They keep refrigerated for 3-6 months.

Cultivar Recommendations

  • Trailing/vining (60-75 days): ‘Jewel Mix’ (mixed colors: scarlet, orange, yellow); ‘Moonlight’ (pale yellow, vigorous); ‘Peach Melba’ (peach with deep burgundy throat); ideal for vertical space and maximum trap-crop effect.
  • Compact bush (55-70 days): ‘Whirlybird’ (compact, 0.5 m, mixed colors, double-flowered forms available); ‘Tip Top’ (dwarf, uniform colors); excellent for containers and small spaces.
  • Variegated foliage (60-70 days): ‘Alaska Mix’ (white-splashed leaves, assorted flower colors); ornamental but identical in flavor and trap-crop function.
