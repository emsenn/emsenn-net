Tomato

2026-04-02 Annual warm-season fruiting crop (Solanum lycopersicum, 60–90 days to first fruit). Indeterminate varieties produce continuously; determinate varieties produce in concentrated burst. Requires staking (indeterminate) or support. Deep transplant burial for adventitious root development. Buzz-pollinated by bumblebees (greenhouse production needs mechanical vibration). Wide cultivar diversity including cherry, paste, beefsteak, and heirloom types.

¶Growing Season and Climate

Tomatoes are warm-season annuals requiring soil temperatures ≥15°C for growth, but optimal growth occurs at 20–28°C. Frost kills the plant. In temperate zones, start transplants indoors 6–8 weeks before last spring frost, then harden off and transplant to ground after frost danger passes. In warm climates (USDA zones 9+), direct seed outdoors 2–3 weeks before warm weather stabilizes.

Days to first fruit range 60–75 days (cherry types, early cultivars like Spoon) to 90+ days (large beefsteak types, late cultivars). Extend the season in cool climates by starting early and choosing short-season cultivars.

¶Planting and Soil Preparation

Choose a location with 6–8 hours of direct sunlight daily; more sun favors fruit production and flavor concentration. Tomatoes are hungry feeders: amend soil with 2–3 inches of well-composted organic matter before planting. Target soil pH 6.0–7.0.

Transplant burial: Unlike most crops, bury tomato transplants deep—up to the first true leaves. Buried stem sections will develop adventitious roots along the buried portion, creating a stronger root system. This technique improves drought resilience and anchoring for indeterminate varieties.

Space transplants 24–36 inches apart (closer for determinate, wider for indeterminate). Provide consistent moisture (1–2 inches weekly) to prevent cracking and blossom-end rot (calcium deficiency exacerbated by inconsistent watering).

¶Determinacy and Support Requirements

Indeterminate varieties (Spoon, Paul Robeson, Green Zebra, Cherokee Green, Indigo Blue Chocolate, Moonglow, and most heirlooms) produce continuously until frost. They require staking or caging for structural support and air circulation. Use 6–8 foot tall stakes or sturdy cages; tie stems loosely with cloth strips as they grow.

Determinate varieties (most commercial paste tomatoes, bush types) stop growing at a fixed height and produce most fruit in a concentrated 4–6 week window. Suitable for container growing and smaller cages.

Semideterminate varieties offer a middle ground: extended production without the unlimited height of indeterminate types.

¶Flowering and Pollination

Tomato flowers contain both male (pollen) and female (pistil) organs. In outdoor gardens, wind, insects, and vibrations from rain/bees usually provide adequate pollination. In greenhouses or during calm periods, hand-vibrate flowering clusters by tapping stems with your fingers or a vibrator tool. Bumblebees are highly effective; many greenhouse growers maintain bumblebee hives for reliable fruit set.

Flowers drop without setting fruit if temperatures exceed 32°C at night or during the day; this is normal and not a disease.

¶Fertility and Feeding

Apply a balanced fertilizer (10–10–10) at transplanting and then switch to lower-nitrogen (5–10–10) once flowering begins. High nitrogen favors leaf growth at the expense of fruit. Side-dress with compost or balanced fertilizer every 3–4 weeks.

Calcium deficiency (showing as dark, sunken blossom-end rot) is common if watering is inconsistent. Mulch heavily to regulate soil moisture and temperature.

¶Notable Cultivars

Spoon (cherry type, 60–75 days): World’s smallest tomato fruit; prolific on small indeterminate plants. Excellent for containers.

Paul Robeson (indeterminate, heirloom, ~80 days): Named for the singer and activist; dark purple-brown deeply flavored fruits, complex acidity. Requires long season.

Green Zebra (indeterminate, 65–75 days): Striped green and yellow fruit when ripe; complex fruity-herbal flavor; excellent for slicing fresh.

Cherokee Green (indeterminate, early, ~60 days): Rare heirloom; green-fleshed fruit with complex flavor.

Indigo Blue Chocolate (indeterminate, 75–85 days): Deep purple-black skin (anthocyanin-rich) with complex berry flavors.

White Cherry (cherry type): Pale fruit with delicate flavor; indeterminate, productive.

Black Strawberry (indeterminate, 75–80 days): Dark purple-red marbled fruits; strawberry-sized, rich flavor.

Moonglow (indeterminate, 75–85 days): Pale yellow fruits, mild sweet flavor, meaty texture.

¶Pruning (Optional)

Remove lower leaves as the plant grows to improve air circulation and reduce disease pressure. For indeterminate varieties, optionally remove “suckers” (shoots growing between the main stem and branches) to focus energy on main branches and fruiting, though this is not required.

¶Harvest and Storage

Harvest when fully colored but still slightly firm (vine-ripened tomatoes have superior flavor to store-bought ripened fruit). Tomatoes continue to ripen after picking, so you can harvest at the “breaker stage” (when color first develops) and allow ripening indoors at 68–72°F.

Store at room temperature away from direct sun; refrigeration below 13°C damages flavor and texture. Vine-ripened tomatoes keep 3–5 days at room temperature before softening.

¶Common Problems

  • Blossom-end rot (dark sunken spots): Calcium deficiency from inconsistent watering. Ensure steady moisture.
  • Cracking/splitting: From heavy rain after dry periods. Consistent watering prevents this.
  • Leaf spots and blights: Fungal diseases (early blight Alternaria, late blight Phytophthora). Remove infected leaves, improve air circulation, apply sulfur or copper fungicide if severe.
  • Hornworms: Large green caterpillars that defoliate rapidly. Hand-pick or treat with Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt).
