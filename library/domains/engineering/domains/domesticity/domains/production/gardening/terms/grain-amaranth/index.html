Grain Amaranth

2026-04-02 Annual cultivar of Amaranthus cruentus, 75-150 days to grain maturity depending on cultivar. Pendant or erect tassels of dense flowers. Gluten-free pseudocereal grain with 16-17% protein and superior amino acid balance (high lysine). Also produces edible leaves. Deep-red flower bracts yield natural dye. Golden Giant (most productive, 90-110 days); Aurelia's Verde (rapid, 75-90 days); traditional Hopi Red (120-150 days, deep dye color).

Grain amaranth cultivation grows Amaranthus cruentus as a gluten-free pseudocereal with exceptional nutritional profile, ornamental pendant tassels, and secondary uses for natural dye and edible leaves. The crop is exceptionally productive in marginal soils and drought conditions.

¶Site Selection and Soil Preparation

Grain amaranth tolerates poor to moderately fertile soil with pH 6.0-7.5. It is exceptionally drought-tolerant once established; minimal soil preparation is needed. Work 1-2 inches of compost into poor soils; no fertilizer is strictly necessary. The species thrives in hot conditions and marginal soils where other crops fail, making it ideal for water-limited regions and degraded soils. Full sun (6+ hours) is essential for grain development.

¶Spacing and Planting

Direct-seed or transplant seedlings after soil has warmed to 60°F and all frost danger passes. Grain cultivars are typically spaced 12-18 inches apart (closer spacing than ornamental types produces smaller, more numerous plants with slightly lower individual yield but higher field density). Succession-sow every 2-3 weeks for extended harvest and seed staggering. Seeds are dust-fine and should be surface-sown or barely covered; press firmly into moist soil.

Germination occurs in 5-10 days at 70°F+. Seedlings emerge slowly; weed control is essential for the first 4-6 weeks. Once plants reach 6-8 inches, their competitive vigor suppresses most weeds.

¶Water and Fertility

Water minimally: 0.5-1 inch per week once established (after 4-6 weeks). The crop is highly drought-tolerant; excessive water reduces grain quality and flavor. Do not fertilize unless soil is very poor; excess nitrogen delays grain maturation and produces excessive leafy growth. If soil is deficient, apply a light balanced fertilizer (5-10-10) at mid-season.

¶Pest and Disease Management

Grain amaranth is largely pest-resistant. Aphids occasionally colonize young plants; spray with water or insecticidal soap if populations are heavy. Leaf spots and fungal diseases are uncommon and rarely warrant intervention. The main management challenge is weed suppression in the first 4-6 weeks; mulch with straw or use frequent shallow cultivation.

¶Harvest and Processing

For grain harvest: Allow the plant to mature fully in the field; seed develops inside the florets and must be fully mature before harvest. When bracts dry and lose color (typically 100-150 days, depending on cultivar), cut the entire inflorescence and hang upside-down in a warm, airy space for 2-4 weeks. Rub mature dried tassels between gloved hands to release seeds; winnow away chaff and debris using a fan or light breeze. Yield: 0.5-1.5 kg dry seed per plant.

For edible leaves: Harvest young leaves (first 4-6 true leaves per stem) throughout the growing season; leaves are tender and nutritious, excellent raw or wilted. Once the plant begins flowering, leaf quality declines.

For dye: Harvest flower bracts when fully colored (deep red/burgundy), at peak pigment development (early to mid-bloom). Dry bracts in a warm location for 2-3 weeks, then store in airtight containers away from light. Extract dye by steeping dried bracts in hot water (160-180°F) for 30-45 minutes; resulting liquid yields deep pink to red dye stable across wide pH range.

¶Cultivar Recommendations

  • Highest grain yield (90-110 days): ‘Golden Giant’ (compact 1.2-1.5 m, yellow-gold inflorescence, 1+ kg seed/plant, excellent for short seasons and marginal soils).
  • Rapid maturation (75-90 days): ‘Aurelia’s Verde’ (compact, green inflorescence, Mayan origin, excellent for cool short-season zones).
  • Traditional Southwestern (120-150 days, highest dye potential): ‘Hopi Red’ (tall 1.8-2 m, deep burgundy pendant tassel, rich red dye, moderate grain yield, culturally significant).
  • Dual-purpose grain and dye (100-130 days): ‘Dreadlock’ (extremely compact 0.8 m, deeply-lobed pendant tassel, burgundy-red, moderate grain production, outstanding ornamental value).

¶Storage

Store dry grain in cool (50-60°F), dry conditions (below 15% moisture) in airtight containers. Grain remains viable for 5+ years under ideal storage. Popped amaranth can be stored in sealed containers for several months.

¶Culinary Uses

Whole grains cook as porridge (1 part grain to 2.5 parts water, boil 15-20 minutes). Pop uncooked grain in a hot dry pan (similar to popcorn, though some grains remain unpopped). Milled flour is dense and slightly bitter; blend with wheat flour (20-25% amaranth) or use in dense baked goods. The grain is gluten-free, making it valuable for celiac diets.
