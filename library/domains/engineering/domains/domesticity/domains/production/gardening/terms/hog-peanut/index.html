Hog Peanut Cultivation

Hog peanut (Amphicarpaea bracteata) is a native North American legume producing both aerial pods (minor production) and edible subterranean seeds in underground cleistogamous flowers. The species’ underground seed production distinguishes it from all temperate legumes, with a single plant yielding 10–30 small, pear-shaped seeds annually. Historically harvested by Indigenous peoples from meadow vole caches through reciprocal exchange, hog peanut remains underutilized in contemporary food production despite exceptional cultural and nutritional significance.

Growing Conditions and Light Tolerance

Hog peanut exhibits remarkable shade tolerance, thriving from full sun to deep forest shade—a flexibility absent in most food legumes. This permits integration into forest gardens, under-planting in orchards, or cultivation in spaces unsuitable for light-demanding crops. Partially shaded sites (afternoon shade in hot climates) optimize growth while maintaining seed production. The species suits edible forest gardens, perennial polycultures, and native woodland restoration.

Soil and Spacing

Prefer well-draining soils with adequate organic matter and moderate moisture. Direct seed in spring after soil temperatures exceed 12°C, spacing 30 cm apart in rows 60 cm apart or scattered throughout garden beds. Tender shoots appear within 10–14 days. Mulch lightly to suppress weeds without smothering seedlings.

Water and Nitrogen

Water consistently during establishment and through flowering (May–July). Once established, plants tolerate moderate drought. Like other legumes, hog peanuts require no supplemental nitrogen; Rhizobium symbiosis supplies all nitrogen needs. No fertilizer addition typically necessary; excessive nitrogen stimulates foliage at expense of seed production.

Growth Habit and Support

Plants climb via tendrils to 60–120 cm, creating open vines suitable for understory integration. Self-supporting in mixed plantings where adjacent vegetation provides support. Can be encouraged to climb low trellises or left sprawling through herb beds. The vining habit permits three-dimensional garden space use.

Aerial Flowering and Pollination

Small, open purple or lavender flowers appear June–August on aerial flower clusters. These flowers primarily self-pollinate but permit cross-pollination by native bees. Aerial pod production remains modest (few pods, few seeds), and these pods serve primarily genetic function rather than food value. Aerial flowers indicate healthy underground reproductive development.

Underground Seed Production

The critical feature: cleistogamous flowers on underground runners produce single-seeded pods maturing entirely in soil, typically 10–15 cm below soil surface. These pear-shaped seeds (8–12 mm) mature September–October, remaining in soil through winter. Seeds are hardy and persist if left undisturbed.

Harvesting Underground Seeds

Harvest after first frost signals seed maturation (late September–October). Gently excavate around plant base, carefully removing underground runners with attached immature and mature pods. A productive plant yields 10–30 mature seeds per season. Remove dried pod covers; seeds emerge clean. Immature seeds remain soft; allow 4–6 weeks post-harvest drying before storage or cooking.

Traditional reciprocal harvesting: Indigenous peoples harvested from meadow vole caches—voles collect and cache hog peanut seeds naturally—by leaving corn, dried berries, or nuts in exchange. This practice acknowledges ecological relationships and permits sustainable harvesting without depleting wild populations.

Cooking and Nutritional Value

Underground seeds require 45–60 minutes boiling to tenderness, with nutty, bean-like flavor and creamy texture superior to aerial-pod beans. Protein content approximates that of common beans (18–22% dry weight). Dried seeds store indefinitely in cool, dry conditions.

Regeneration and Perennial Use

Plants persist as short-lived perennials in some locations, regrowing from roots for 2–3 seasons. In annual cultivation, self-seeded seedlings emerge from underground seeds left to overwinter, creating volunteer crops. This creates minimal management burden—scatter seeds, harvest progeny seeds, allow regeneration.

Ecological Function and Heritage

Hog peanut represents living connection to eastern woodland Indigenous food traditions. Cultivation provides educational value regarding native legume ecology, alternative food sources, and reciprocal relationships with other species. The species supports nitrogen cycling and contributes to soil development in forest gardens.
