Mustard greens cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing protocol for Tendergreen and related cultivars of *Brassica juncea*, optimized for spring and fall sowing. Extremely rapid bolting in heat; unsuitable for summer cultivation in warm climates. Sow direct; mature in 35–45 days under cool conditions.

Brassica juncea (mustard greens) thrives in cool conditions (10–20°C); warm weather triggers rapid bolting, making it unsuitable for mid-season cultivation. Focus on spring and fall plantings for reliable harvest.

¶Sowing and Direct Seeding

Sow directly in garden beds 4–6 weeks before anticipated temperatures exceed 20°C. Space seeds 1 inch apart, cover with 0.5 inch soil, water gently. Germination occurs at 10–20°C in 5–10 days. Thin seedlings to 6–8 inches apart as true leaves emerge. Succession sow every 2 weeks for continuous supply.

¶Growing Season

Maintain consistent moisture (1–1.5 inches per week). Fertilize once at mid-season with balanced fertilizer if soil is poor. No significant pest pressure in cool weather; occasional flea beetle damage on seedlings is tolerable.

¶Harvesting

Baby greens (14–21 days): Thin seedlings and use thinned plants fresh; tiny leaves are tender and only mildly peppery.

Mature harvest (35–45 days): Once plants are 8–12 inches tall, harvest entire plants by cutting at soil level, or pick individual outer leaves and allow center to continue growing. Tendergreen produces 3–4 harvests per plant.

Harvest before bolting begins; once flowering initiates, leaves coarsen rapidly and bitterness intensifies from elevated glucosinolate levels.

¶Timing and Season Management

  • Spring planting: Sow 4–6 weeks before last frost; mustard greens tolerate light frost and benefit from cool nighttime temperatures. Harvest as weather warms, then transition to other greens.
  • Fall planting: Sow 8–10 weeks before first hard freeze; plants grow through cool fall, producing consistently into early winter.
  • Summer: Avoid sowing; heat-induced bolting produces no harvestable window.
  • Winter in mild climates: Winter-sown mustard may overwinter and produce in spring (acts as winter annual).

¶Cultivar Notes

Tendergreen is standard American selection, producing smooth, tender leaves suited to both raw and cooked use. Red Giant (larger, more ornamental) takes longer but is more heat-tolerant than Tendergreen. Southern Giant Curled produces deeply wrinkled, more visually dramatic leaves. All cultivars bolt quickly in heat; cultivar choice affects leaf texture and size rather than cold tolerance.
