Fernleaf Yarrow

2026-04-02 Perennial cultivar of Achillea filipendulina, hardy zones 3-9. Finely feathery foliage, golden-yellow flat-topped flower clusters (0.8-1.5 m tall). Cut flowers last 2-3 weeks fresh; dry beautifully for winter arrangements. Dye plant: quercetin produces bright yellow-gold. Attracts parasitoid wasps and tachinid flies—beneficial insect reservoir. Drought-tolerant, long-lived, low-maintenance. 'Gold Plate' (largest flowers, 1.2-1.5 m); 'Cloth of Gold' (reliable golden-yellow, 1 m).

Fernleaf yarrow cultivation grows Achillea filipendulina as a long-lived perennial with elegant feathery foliage and prolific golden-yellow flat-topped flower clusters that function as both ornament and beneficial insect attractant. The flowers excel in fresh and dried arrangements.

Site Selection and Soil Preparation

Fernleaf yarrow thrives in full sun (minimum 6-8 hours) and well-drained soil with pH 6.0-8.0. The plant is exceptionally drought-tolerant and prefers lean to moderately fertile soils; avoid rich, heavily manured beds, which promote lush foliage and weak stems prone to flopping. Soil preparation is minimal: amend clay with compost and perlite for improved drainage; sandy soils need no amendment. Once established, the plant requires no supplemental water.

Spacing and Planting

Plant divisions or transplants in spring or fall, spacing them 18-24 inches apart. The plant slowly expands to fill its space; do not overcrowd. Container cultivation (8-10 inch minimum pot) is successful if drainage is excellent and the pot is watered during establishment.

Seed propagation is possible but slow; seedlings flower in year two. Direct-sow seeds in fall or early spring onto prepared, moist soil; germination is slow (14-21 days at 60-70°F). For faster establishment, start transplants indoors 6-8 weeks before planting.

Water and Fertility

Once established (4-6 weeks after planting), fernleaf yarrow requires virtually no water supplementation; the plant is among the most drought-tolerant perennials. In very hot climates, occasional deep watering (1 inch per month) prevents stress, but the plant survives extended dry periods.

Do not fertilize. Excess nitrogen produces weak, floppy stems and diverts resources to foliage at the expense of flowers. The plant thrives in lean soils and actually performs better in poor-to-moderate fertility.

Support and Maintenance

Fernleaf yarrow typically does not require staking in well-drained soil with adequate sunlight. In partially shaded sites or in very rich soil, plants may flop; provide discreet support with stakes and twine if needed.

Deadheading (removing spent flower clusters) extends bloom by redirecting resources from seed production to new flower production. Alternatively, allow flowers to mature fully for drying or seed self-sowing. The plant is deciduous; cut back dead foliage to ground level in fall or early spring.

Pest and Disease Management

Fernleaf yarrow is essentially pest-free. Powdery mildew may develop in very humid climates; improve air circulation and avoid overhead watering if it appears. No chemical intervention is necessary.

The flat-topped flower clusters are attractive to numerous beneficial insects (parasitoid wasps, tachinid flies, native bees), which visit for nectar and pollen; the plant naturally develops a population of beneficial insects that spill over onto adjacent crops.

Flowering and Bloom Extension

Flowers open in early to mid-summer and continue through late summer if deadheaded. Flower color is consistently golden-yellow and does not fade significantly during bloom. Peak flowering occurs June-August in most temperate zones.

Harvest and Use

Cut flowers: Harvest when flower clusters are fully opened but before petals begin to fade (peak color and vase life). Cut stems at a sharp angle; flowers last 2-3 weeks in fresh water with no special conditioning required. They are long-lasting, making them excellent for mixed arrangements.

Drying for winter arrangements: Cut flowering stems as flowers fully open, hang upside-down in a warm, airy location away from direct sun. Flowers dry completely in 1-2 weeks and retain color beautifully (golden-yellow persists) for many months. Dried arrangements last indefinitely.

Dye: Harvest fresh foliage at any time; peak pigment concentration is June-August. Steep fresh or dried foliage in hot water (160-180°F) for 30-60 minutes; the resulting dye liquor is bright yellow. Mordanting with alum produces bright yellow on wool or silk; iron mordant shifts the color toward olive-gold or bronze.

Cultivar Recommendations

  • ‘Cloth of Gold’ (60-75 cm): Bright golden-yellow, reliable, excellent cut flower, moderate spread, widely available, highly recommended.
  • ‘Gold Plate’ (100-150 cm, large flowers): Very tall, largest flower heads (15+ cm diameter), dramatic golden-yellow; excellent architectural plant but may require staking in partial shade or rich soils.
  • ‘Coronation Gold’ (75-100 cm, hybrid): Compact habit, flat-topped golden-yellow flowers, excellent for borders and smaller spaces, less sprawling than pure species.

Wild-type A. filipendulina (generic golden yarrow seed) is available; plants vary in height and flower intensity.

Ecological Benefits

The flat-topped flower clusters attract parasitoid wasps and tachinid flies—natural enemies of aphids, caterpillars, and other garden pests. Integrating fernleaf yarrow throughout gardens creates a reservoir of beneficial insects that provide biological pest control without chemical intervention.
