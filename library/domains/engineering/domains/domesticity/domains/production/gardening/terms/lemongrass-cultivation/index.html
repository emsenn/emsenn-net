Lemongrass cultivation

2026-04-02 Growing protocol for Cymbopogon citratus, tropical perennial treated as warm-season annual or container plant in cold climates, requires warmth above 15°C, container overwintering typical in zones 3-8, vegetative propagation via clump division.

Lemongrass cultivation targets Cymbopogon citratus, a tropical perennial grass requiring consistent warmth above 15°C (59°F) for growth. In cool and temperate climates (USDA zones 3-8), lemongrass is grown as a warm-season annual or container plant, overwintered indoors, and moved outdoors after frost danger passes. In zones 9-11, lemongrass can be grown as a year-round outdoor perennial in frost-free locations. Propagation is exclusively vegetative via clump division; the plant does not produce viable seed in most cultivated regions.

¶Container Culture (Most Practical in Cold Climates)

Container cultivation is the most practical approach in zones 3-8. Use a pot 12-18 inches diameter with excellent drainage (terracotta, fabric pots, or containers with multiple drainage holes). Fill with soil-based potting mix amended for drainage (no more than 25 percent peat moss, the remainder compost, bark, and perlite for aeration).

Place containers outdoors in full sun (6+ hours daily) after all frost danger has passed (typically May-June in northern temperate regions). Water regularly; lemongrass is more moisture-demanding than Mediterranean herbs and should not dry out completely. However, ensure excellent drainage to prevent root rot.

Begin harvesting outer leaf blades once plants are established (typically mid-summer). Only the tender, lower 15 cm of the pseudostem and inner leaf layers are suitable for culinary use; outer, fibrous portions should be discarded.

¶Overwintering Indoors (Cold Climates)

Before first frost (typically September-October), move containers indoors to a warm, bright location. Ideal overwintering conditions are 15-20°C (59-68°F) with 6+ hours of bright light daily. A warm, sunny south-facing window, bright greenhouse, or warm sunroom is ideal. Reduce watering slightly; the plant should be kept moderately moist but not waterlogged.

Overwintering in cooler conditions (10-15°C) is possible but growth nearly ceases; keep moderately dry to prevent root rot in dormancy. Move plants back outdoors after all frost danger has passed in spring.

¶Outdoor Cultivation (Zones 9-11)

In zones 9-11, lemongrass can be planted directly in garden soil in a warm, sunny location with excellent drainage. Space plants 12-18 inches apart. The plant forms expanding clumps 18-24 inches in diameter annually. Mulch around plants to retain moisture and moderate soil temperature.

Water regularly during growing season (spring-summer); reduce watering in autumn and winter as growth slows. In zones 9-10 with occasional freezes, apply heavy mulch (4-6 inches) after ground freezes to insulate the crown and rhizomes.

¶Propagation via Division

Lemongrass is propagated exclusively by dividing established clumps; no seed is produced in most cultivated regions. In spring, carefully dig up an established plant and use a sharp spade to divide the clump into 2-4 sections, ensuring each section has several growing points and some roots attached. Replant divisions immediately in soil-based mix or garden soil; water thoroughly.

Alternatively, pot divisions in individual containers and allow them to establish (2-4 weeks of active growth before moving to larger pots or garden).

¶Harvesting and Culinary Preparation

Lemongrass produces harvestable leaf blades continuously throughout the growing season once plants are established (typically mid-summer onward). Harvest by cutting outer leaf blades at the base of the pseudostem. The entire outer leaf blade can be used for infusions or removed to expose the tender white pseudostem.

For culinary use, only the tender lower 15 cm of the pseudostem is used; the fibrous, tough upper portions are discarded. The pseudostem can be split, thinly sliced, or coarsely chopped depending on the application.

Fresh Use: Use fresh lemongrass immediately in soups (tom yum, tom kha gai), curries, stir-fries, and Southeast Asian dishes. Fresh lemongrass provides the brightest, most vibrant flavor.

Dried Use: Lemongrass can be dried by hanging leaf bundles in a warm, airy location. Dried lemongrass retains potency for 1-2 years when stored in airtight containers. Dried material is suitable for infusions and tea.

Frozen Use: Fresh lemongrass can be chopped and frozen in ice cube trays with water or oil for later use in cooking.

¶Seasonal Considerations and Growth Pattern

Lemongrass grows most vigorously in warm conditions (20-28°C). Growth slows dramatically below 15°C and ceases below 10°C. In warm climates, the plant grows throughout the year; in cool-season climates, growth is concentrated in summer (June-September in northern hemisphere).

Lemongrass does not develop an obvious dormancy but grows slower in cool weather. Overwintered plants that were kept moderately dry may show signs of wilting upon moving outdoors in spring; water thoroughly and wait for new growth to resume as conditions warm.

¶Pests and Disease

Lemongrass is virtually pest-free outdoors. In indoor overwintering conditions, spider mites may infest the plant, particularly in warm, dry conditions. Increase humidity by misting or ensure adequate air circulation with a fan. No serious diseases affect lemongrass.

¶Companion Planting

In outdoor gardens (zones 9-11), lemongrass works well in tropical-themed gardens with other warm-season plants. Its vertical growth habit and long leaf blades provide textural contrast with broader-leaved herbs and ornamentals.
