HEPA Classification

2026-04-04 Standards for high-efficiency particulate air (HEPA) filter performance categorized by particle removal efficiency at the Most Penetrating Particle Size.

HEPA filters are classified into performance tiers (H10–H14 in Europe; True HEPA in the US) based on their ability to remove particles at the most challenging size to filter. The classification depends on which standard is applied.

¶EN 1822 / ISO 29463 Standard

The European standard EN 1822 (now superseded by ISO 29463) establishes HEPA classification by measuring filtration efficiency at the Most Penetrating Particle Size (MPPS), which is approximately 0.21 micrometers. This size represents the diameter at which the filter is least efficient because both diffusion and interception mechanisms are comparatively ineffective.

EN 1822 defines three filter groups across the H10–H14 spectrum:

  • H10: ≥85% overall efficiency and ≥70% local efficiency at MPPS (lowest HEPA tier)
  • H11: ≥95% overall efficiency and ≥85% local efficiency at MPPS
  • H12: ≥99.5% overall efficiency
  • H13: ≥99.95% overall efficiency; individual filters receive serial numbers and test reports
  • H14: ≥99.995% overall efficiency (highest HEPA tier)

Individual filters at H13 and above are tested to produce a specific efficiency value and accompanying test certificate.

¶US DOE Standard

The U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) standard specifies HEPA filters must remove at least 99.97% of particles 0.3 micrometers in diameter. This specification is based on the 0.3 μm particle size, which differs from the European MPPS approach but achieves comparable performance in practice.

The term “True HEPA” (no official legal definition in the US) commonly refers to filters meeting the DOE 99.97% standard, which is functionally equivalent to EN 1822 H13 performance.

¶Why MPPS Matters

MPPS is the weakest point in filter performance. Smaller particles are removed by diffusion (random Brownian motion), and larger particles are removed by interception or impaction. At 0.21 μm, neither mechanism is efficient. By specifying efficiency at MPPS, the standard ensures the filter performs well across all particle sizes, not just at convenient test points.

¶Practical Distinction: H13 vs H11

An H13 filter captures 99.95% of particles at MPPS, while an H11 captures 95%. For residential portable air purifiers, H13 is the standard for “True HEPA” performance. H11 filters are sometimes marketed as “HEPA-type” or used in less demanding applications.

¶Relationship to ISO 29463

ISO 29463 harmonizes global HEPA classification by adopting EN 1822 as its international equivalent. Filters rated under either EN 1822 or ISO 29463 using the same class designation (e.g., H13) are functionally equivalent.
