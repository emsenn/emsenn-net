ASHRAE Standards for Indoor Air Quality

2026-04-04 Ventilation and indoor air quality recommendations from the American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers, including standards for residential and commercial spaces.

ASHRAE (American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers) publishes consensus standards for indoor air quality and ventilation. Multiple standards address residential air quality with different priorities and timelines.

¶ASHRAE 62.2-2016: Residential Ventilation Standard

ASHRAE 62.2 (“Ventilation and Acceptable Indoor Air Quality in Residential Buildings”) is the primary standard for residential IAQ and is referenced in building codes.

Minimum requirement: Homes must receive at least 0.35 air changes per hour (ACH) from outdoor air, with a minimum of 15 cubic feet per minute (CFM) per person. This baseline is intended to dilute odors and general indoor pollutants.

0.35 ACH is a modest requirement. A 1,500 square foot home (approximately 11,250 cubic feet at 8-foot ceilings) at 0.35 ACH requires outdoor ventilation of about 66 CFM continuously. This is easily achieved through HVAC systems but provides minimal protection against airborne pathogens or rapid contaminant removal.

¶ASHRAE 241: Infectious Aerosol Control Standard

ASHRAE 241 (2023) emerged from pandemic-era research on airborne pathogen transmission. It recommends:

  • 30–50 CFM per person for residential spaces
  • Greater emphasis on filtration quality and recirculation in addition to outdoor ventilation
  • Combined outdoor and recirculated air strategies

ASHRAE 241 is newer and more stringent than 62.2, reflecting evolving understanding of indoor pathogens. It is increasingly referenced by public health agencies but has not yet become standard in building codes.

For a 2-person household, ASHRAE 241 guidance translates to 60–100 CFM total ventilation, or approximately 5+ air changes per hour in typical residential volumes.

¶Post-Pandemic CDC/EPA Recommendations

In response to COVID-19, the CDC and EPA now recommend achieving at least 5 total ACH (from combined outdoor ventilation and mechanical filtration) in occupied spaces. This is more aggressive than ASHRAE 62.2 but aligns with ASHRAE 241.

5 ACH is achievable through:

  • Increased outdoor ventilation (HVAC upgrade)
  • Portable air purifiers with high CADR
  • Combination of both

¶Application to Portable Air Purifiers

Portable air purifiers contribute only recirculation ACH, not outdoor ventilation ACH. Their contribution depends on CADR and room volume. For example:

  • 300 CFM smoke CADR in a 500 square foot room (approximately 4,000 cubic feet at 8-foot ceiling)
  • ACH contribution: (300 × 60) ÷ 4,000 = 4.5 ACH

If the home has no outdoor ventilation (0.35 ACH minimum), the portable purifier raises total ACH to approximately 4.85 ACH—close to the CDC/EPA 5 ACH target but still relying on filtering the same air repeatedly.

Combined strategy (0.35 outdoor + 4.5 recirculation ≈ 4.85 total) approaches modern recommendations; 0.35 outdoor alone (ASHRAE 62.2 baseline) does not.

¶Standards Over Time

The evolution of ASHRAE standards reflects changing understanding:

  • Pre-pandemic: 0.35 ACH adequate for general IAQ and odor control
  • Pandemic era: Recognition that higher ACH needed for pathogen control
  • Post-pandemic: ASHRAE 241 and EPA/CDC guidance elevate targets to 5 ACH

Homes built to older codes or not retrofitted with ventilation upgrades typically fall short of current guidance. Portable air purifiers are one remediation path.
