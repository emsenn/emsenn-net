CARB Certification (Air Cleaners)

2026-04-04 California Air Resources Board certification that an air cleaning device meets strict ozone emission limits and electrical safety standards.

CARB certification is a regulatory requirement for air cleaners sold in, shipped to, or used in California. It certifies that the device does not produce harmful ozone and meets electrical safety standards.

Regulatory Basis

California Assembly Bill 2276 (AB 2276, 2006, Pavley) mandated that CARB establish and enforce limits on ozone emissions from indoor air cleaning devices. The regulation went into effect in 2008 to protect public health from ozone pollution generated by air cleaning technologies.

Since 2010, all portable indoor air cleaning devices sold or shipped to people or businesses in California must be CARB-certified before sale.

Ozone Emission Limit

The key standard is an ozone concentration limit of 0.050 parts per million (50 ppb). This is the maximum allowable ozone emission that any air cleaner can produce while operating normally and continuously. To contextualize: 1 ppm is roughly one drop in 15 gallons of water; 50 ppb is 50 parts in one billion.

This applies to all air cleaners, whether mechanical (HEPA-based) or electronic (ionizers, electrostatic precipitators, UV, ozone generators, hydroxyl generators).

Device Categories Under CARB

Mechanical Air Cleaners

Devices that use only physical filtration (pleated filters, HEPA-style filters, mesh screens) without active ion generation, electric field, or radiation. These devices inherently produce no ozone and pass certification easily. The label states: “Meets California ozone emissions limit: CARB certified” (mechanical devices typically state zero ozone risk).

Electronic Air Cleaners

Devices that may generate ozone as a byproduct of operation:

  • Ionizers
  • Electrostatic precipitators (ESP)
  • Photocatalytic oxidation (PCO)
  • Hydroxyl radical generators
  • Devices with UV-C light
  • Ozone-generating air cleaners

Electronic cleaners must be tested and certified to produce no more than 50 ppb ozone. If testing shows the device exceeds this limit, it cannot be sold in California.

Testing and Certification Process

CARB-accredited laboratories test air cleaners for:

  1. Ozone emissions: Measured under standardized operating conditions
  2. Electrical safety: Compliance with relevant safety standards to prevent fire or shock hazards

The certification label must state in bold type with uppercase letters at least 3 mm high: “Meets California ozone emissions limit: CARB certified.”

Impact on Market

Because California represents a large consumer market, most air purifier manufacturers obtain CARB certification for nationwide sales rather than maintaining separate product lines for California and other states. This has effectively made the 50 ppb ozone limit a de facto national standard.

What CARB Does NOT Certify

  • CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate)
  • Filter lifespan or replacement cost
  • Noise level
  • Energy efficiency
  • Effectiveness against specific pollutants

CARB certification ensures the device does not create new air quality problems (ozone) but does not measure how well it cleans air.

Compliance for Non-California Sale

Devices sold outside California may not be CARB-certified. However, most manufacturers pursue certification to simplify supply chains. Uncertified devices sold in other states may emit higher ozone levels.

Relationship to AHAM Certification

  • CARB: Verifies safety and ozone emissions (regulatory requirement in California)
  • AHAM Verifide: Verifies performance (CADR) through independent testing (voluntary)

These certifications address different aspects. A device may be AHAM-certified but not CARB-certified, or vice versa, though most premium air purifiers have both.
