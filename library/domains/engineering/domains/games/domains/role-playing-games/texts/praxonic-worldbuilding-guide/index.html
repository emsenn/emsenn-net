Praxonic Worldbuilding Guide

PRAXONIC WORLDBUILDING GUIDE A Recursive Framework for Making Meaningful Worlds from Pressure — ## I. INTRODUCTION You are not creating a place. You are tracing a deformation. This guide teaches a method of world creation that begins not with lore, genre, or aesthetics, but with pressure. In this framework, a world is not a map, a backstory, or a set of cultures. It is a field—a living surface shaped by tension, memory, and contradiction. It behaves. It resists. It shifts when touched. The method here is called praxonic worldmaking, and it grows from a system called praxonic interpretation: a minimal logic for how things emerge, change, and fold under recursive pressure. You do not “invent” a world. You interpret a pressure until a field appears. You follow that field until it begins to emit trace. You record those traces until they produce actors, zones, misalignments, ghosts, care structures, silence events, and other system behaviors. That is your world. It does not need to explain itself. It only needs to persist under contradiction. — ### Who is this for? - Writers building narrative systems grounded in logistical or ethical realism - TTRPG designers and GMs who want tension-first, world-as-character mechanics - Memory workers designing shared testimony environments - LARPers, performance artists, collective documentarians - Fictional worldbuilders who want to say less and evoke more - You — ### What kinds of worlds can this method produce? - A refugee clinic with a broken radio, and too much gauze - A decaying lighthouse no one wants to admit is a god - A corporate loyalty program that becomes the only way to survive winter - A haunted apartment building where the ghosts are eviction notices - A 15-year stretch of drought told as a memory game - A cave system where every echo was someone’s confession - Your neighborhood - Your inbox - Your dream from last week Praxonic worldmaking is not bound to realism, fantasy, or speculative fiction. It works anywhere pressure exists, which means everywhere. — ### What does this guide do? It gives you tools to: - Recognize the core praxon at the heart of your world - Model how that praxon becomes emissions, then trace - Define zones by how pressure behaves there - Shape characters as local alignment systems - Track contradiction as signal, not error - Use decay, silence, refusal, and care as building blocks - Build stories, sessions, loops, or memory events as recursive outcomes, not linear sequences This guide does not give you templates. It gives you tension structures. — ### What will I end up with? A world that behaves like a living pressure field. One where everything means something—but not all at once. A world that can contain friction, memory, noise, decay, contradiction, care, and humor. A world that gets more usable the more broken it becomes. — ### How do I use this guide? You can read it linearly, use it modularly, or treat it like a signal you only half-hear until it resonates with a project you already started and forgot. You do not have to believe in it. You only have to trace where it leads. The next section defines the core system: Praxonic Interpretation. — ## II. CORE GRAMMAR: PRAXONIC INTERPRETATION “Every act is a response to something already under pressure.” Praxonic interpretation is the minimal system by which a world can be read, constructed, and recursively unfolded from pressure. At its core, this system defines: - What kinds of entities exist in a recursive world - What relations occur between them - How change, meaning, and instability emerge as system behavior It is not a symbolic language. It is a structural logic for making any world—real or imagined—behave like something under tension. — ### 1. CORE ENTITY: THE PRAXON A praxon is a unit of unresolved pressure. It is the smallest readable element of a world-under-formation. It is not content. It is pre-semantic: behavior before definition. Praxons do not “mean.” They emit. They deform. They recur. A praxon may be: - A border crossing no one agrees exists - A prayer repeated so often it becomes scheduling - A broken faucet that changes how a house speaks - The moment someone realizes the radio isn’t broken—it’s unplugged Worlds begin with praxons. They do not describe conditions. They are conditions. — ### 2. SYSTEM LOOP: PRAXONIC FIELD DYNAMICS The world is built from this recursive cycle: Praxon → Emission → Trace → Field → Alignment → Path → Emission Or more mechanically: 1. A praxon emits a deformation (an act, error, signal, silence) 2. That emission leaves trace—a distortion of the surrounding world 3. All trace combines into a field—the condition of the world at any moment 4. Actors become legible via their alignment to the local field 5. Alignment + field = possible path (response/behavior) 6. That path produces new emission 7. The loop continues, recursively Each iteration either increases coherence or generates irresolved noise (IRN)—which accumulates into pressure again. — ### 3. KEY COMPONENTS Each part of the loop is a functional entity. #### ➤ Emission Any output from a praxon or aligned actor. May be action, refusal, signal, breakdown, memory. #### ➤ Trace The residual deformation left in the field by an emission. Trace decays unless retained or reinforced. Decay is not disappearance—it is transformation. #### ➤ Field The sum of all active trace. A dynamic, living pressure environment. Contains alignment conditions, actor legibility, volatility zones. #### ➤ Alignment A local actor’s modulation to the field. Alignment determines which paths are visible, plausible, survivable. #### ➤ Path A behavior that can emerge from the intersection of field and alignment. Not chosen freely—conditioned by pressure. All acts are pathings. Even silence. #### ➤ Irresolved Noise (IRN) When emissions cannot be aligned, retained, or decayed. IRN remains in the field as recursive contradiction. It generates distortion, myth, violence, ghost-behavior, confusion, and memory split. — ### 4. RELATIONAL PROPERTIES - Field defines potential. The world doesn’t contain meaning—it pressurizes it. - Actors do not pre-exist the field. They emerge via pressure readability. - Paths are conditioned. Choice is not freedom. It is coherence under constraint. - Resolution is local. An emission that is signal in one alignment may be noise in another. - Decay is not loss. Decay produces change in form, not always disappearance. - Contradiction is content. Do not smooth it. Fold it. — ### 5. PRACTICAL USE When building or interpreting a world: 1. Identify praxons 2. Determine emissions (what’s happening under pressure) 3. Track trace and decay (what remains, what changes) 4. Map the field (what behaviors the world permits) 5. Construct or observe alignments (who can act how) 6. Read or trigger paths (what happens next) 7. Accept IRN (where pressure will not resolve cleanly) This is how a fantasy village, a cyberpunk megacity, or your friend’s kitchen becomes a praxonic world. — In the next section, we’ll use this grammar to form worlds from praxons. This is the start of Praxonic Worldmaking. — ## III. WORLD FORMATION: FROM PRAXON TO FIELD “You’re not inventing a world. You’re tracking where something gave.” Worlds built through praxonic interpretation emerge from pressure, not premise. They do not begin as genre, theme, or story—they begin as rupture, weight, or distortion. You are not placing pieces. You are recognizing faultlines and following them as they fold. This section provides a method for constructing a usable, recursive world from a single or nested praxon. — ### STEP 1: SELECT A PRAXON Start with a pressure—an unresolved contradiction, demand, or misalignment. A praxon is not a theme. It is a condition under tension, readable through action or refusal. Examples: - “The clinic keeps running even though the signal stopped weeks ago.” - “The castle’s water source is poisoned, but no one can admit it.” - “Everyone agrees the event happened. No one agrees on when.” - “The mountain is sacred, but it’s moved five times this year.” - “This family does not talk about the fourth child.” ⚠️ Avoid defining too much too early. Let the praxon shape what comes next. — ### STEP 2: GENERATE INITIAL EMISSIONS Ask: What actions, refusals, silences, or contradictions emerge under this pressure? Emissions may be: - Physical: a door sealed, a pot boiling, a child hiding - Social: an apology not offered, a rule misapplied - Informational: a rumor, a transmission, a misreading - Recursive: a repetition that doesn’t resolve Document at least 3 emissions. You can use text, sketch, scene fragment, mechanical rule, etc. — ### STEP 3: TRACE & DECAY Each emission leaves trace—residual change. Trace deforms the world. Decay governs its persistence. For each emission: - What change does it leave behind? - What happens as that change decays? - What happens if it doesn’t? Mark trace density: - High trace = memory-laden, emotionally volatile, visibly deformed - Low trace = ephemeral, ghosting, easily forgotten but deeply recursive Decay types: - Fast (e.g., hunger, blood trail, mood swing) - Medium (e.g., emotional rupture, supply loss, spoken insult) - Slow (e.g., ideology, superstition, long-term disrepair) — ### STEP 4: SHAPE THE FIELD Now aggregate all trace into a field: the pressure environment your world inhabits. Fields include: - Zones of volatility (conflict, error, risk) - Stabilizers (rituals, infrastructure, relationships) - Retention points (places, people, routines that resist decay) - IRN sinks (locations or memories where resolution fails repeatedly) The field is not geography. It is an environment of deformation. 📍 You can map it as a region, a building, a political system, or an emotion. The important part is how it behaves, not what it looks like. — ### STEP 5: LOCAL ALIGNMENTS Actors do not pre-exist the field—they emerge through readable alignment. For each part of the field: - Who (or what) becomes legible under that pressure? - What alignments become visible, plausible, unstable? - What actions are permitted or impossible from those alignments? You’re not defining characters yet. You’re modeling what types of action the world allows, and who survives that logic. Examples: - “Anyone who speaks the old dialect is assumed to be smuggling intel.” - “Repairing the radio means inheriting all the rumors it has failed to carry.” - “The priest can’t walk in the shrine anymore, but the children still play there.” — ### STEP 6: DEFINE PATH STRUCTURES A path is what becomes possible to do under the pressure of a field. Paths emerge from: - Alignment × Field Condition - Friction between trace and decay - Recursive IRN buildup - Silent thresholds (what is almost said) You’re designing behavior vectors, not scripted scenes. Types of path: - Recursion path: someone repeats what didn’t work - Refusal path: someone denies the field’s logic - Drift path: someone alters alignment through pressure - Break path: contradiction accumulates until something tears Paths should not be predictable. They should be inevitable. — ### STEP 7: NAME THE WORLD Only now should you name the world, and even then, maybe not. If you must: - Use what the actors would call it - Use a nickname, frequency, smell, or gesture - Use a contradiction that can’t be resolved The name is not ownership. It is trace. — At this point, you have a full system: - A praxon - A series of emissions - A trace field with decay and retention - A behavioral logic for actors - Recursion patterns and IRN hotspots - A field responsive to care, refusal, and fracture This is your world. It is ready to emit. — ## IV. ACTORS & AGENCIES “They’re not protagonists. They’re readable distortions in the field.” In praxonic worldmaking, an actor is not someone with backstory, motivation, or moral arc. An actor is a form under pressure—a repeatable behavior pattern made visible by the field. They may be human, animal, machine, weather system, memory fragment, or rumor. What matters is their alignment and how they emit under tension. This section offers a toolkit for building, interpreting, and deploying actors within a praxonic world. — ### 1. ACTOR DEFINITION Actor = Readable response system under local field conditions Actors: - Do not pre-exist the field - Are defined by their alignment (how they “fit” the current pressure) - Emerge through trace saturation, ritual repetition, or behavioral recursion - May change alignment—becoming unstable, contradictory, or IRN-generating - May vanish when pressure resolves or shifts elsewhere An actor is not a symbol. It is a function. — ### 2. FUNCTION TYPES Actors express function through their role in the pressure loop. Common actor functions: - Stabilizer — absorbs pressure through repetition (e.g., the janitor who boils gauze) - Emitter — creates new emissions, intentionally or not (e.g., a child who won’t stop broadcasting) - Retention Node — holds trace against decay (e.g., a box of letters no one opens) - Distorter — reshapes field readings, creates misalignment (e.g., a leader who speaks five truths at once) - Echo / Ghost — reactivates decayed trace unpredictably (e.g., a frequency that replays arguments from last winter) You may assign multiple functions to a single actor. Function determines how the actor influences and is influenced by the field. — ### 3. ALIGNMENT BEHAVIOR Each actor is constantly shifting alignment with the field. Alignment determines: - What paths the actor can take - What actions they can perceive as viable - How others interpret their emissions - Whether their presence increases or relieves IRN Alignment may be: - In-phase — actor behaves legibly under current field pressure - Out-of-phase — actor emits noise, contradiction, or recursive error - Over-aligned — actor behaves so perfectly they become brittle, unstable - Under-aligned — actor cannot be read, evoking silence, refusal, or myth Alignment is not moral. It’s thermodynamic. — ### 4. DESIGN PROMPT TABLES Use these prompts to generate or reconfigure actors in any world: #### Emergence - What pressure created this actor? - Were they always here, or did something call them into shape? - What trace are they carrying? #### Modulation - How do they handle decay? - What retention behaviors do they perform, intentionally or not? - What parts of the field do they distort just by existing? #### Behavior - What emission patterns repeat around them? - Who tries to align with them—and fails? - What do they silence by showing up? #### Disappearance - What would make them unreadable again? - What fold are they holding closed? - If they left, what wouldn’t decay? — ### 5. SAMPLE ACTOR FORM Use this schema to create characters that function as systems, not tropes. [Name or Type] - Function: (Stabilizer, Emitter, etc.) - Alignment State: (In-phase / Out-of-phase / Over-aligned / Under-aligned) - Emission Pattern: [list recurring actions or silences] - Trace Retention: [what they keep alive in the field] - Contradiction Trigger: [condition that fractures them] - Memory Artifact: [object, phrase, or rumor tied to them] - Disappearance Condition: [how they vanish or transform] — ### 6. NON-HUMAN ACTORS Actors need not be people. Valid actors include: - A field generator that sings wrong at night - A road that becomes longer when you lie - A shared calendar that erases Sundays - A threshold no one crosses unless it’s raining - A voice in the ventilation system that only quotes lost texts - A flag that keeps changing colors in the background If it emits, retains, distorts, or folds the field—it is an actor. — ### 7. GROUP AGENCIES You may also model organizations, systems, or communities as multi-body actors. These use the same structure: - Alignment = collective coherence or fracture - Emissions = policy, ritual, rumor, protocol - IRN = structural contradiction, conflicting loyalties - Trace = built environment, doctrine, uniforms, gesture codes Group actors often contain nested contradictions. Examples: - A border guard unit with a rotating memory - A refugee camp whose official purpose is unspoken - An NGO whose donors can’t be named, but everyone knows their smell - A militia that claims to protect, but recruits only orphans Actors are not always aligned with themselves. — Actors make the world speak through behavior. Once actors are in play, the field will fold in response. In the next section, we define the environments where these folds take form: Zones and Sectors—pressure geography. — ## V. ZONES & SECTORS “Place is not where something happens. It’s how something holds.” In praxonic worldmaking, environments do not contain the story. They modulate it. Zones and sectors are field expressions—how pressure folds across space, time, and memory. They aren’t locations. They are terrain under tension. — ### 1. ZONE DEFINITION A zone is a container of active pressure conditions. It is where field behaviors accumulate or distort in a readable way. Each zone expresses: - What kinds of trace persist here - What behaviors this space permits, resists, or reshapes - What form decay takes - How IRN manifests - What kind of actor might emerge from this field condition Zones may be physical (rooms, hills, cities) or structural (a routine, a bureaucracy, a month). Zones don’t sit still. They drift, shrink, fracture, and overlap. — ### 2. SECTOR STRUCTURE To world-build recursively, define each zone through a sector profile. Use the following template: [Zone Name] - Field Behavior: What tensions concentrate or bleed here? - Trace Density: High / Medium / Low - Volatility: How quickly pressure mutates into behavior - Retention Forms: What persists here? (rituals, scars, devices, rumors) - IRN Manifestation: How does unresolved contradiction show up? - Local Actor Pattern: What kinds of figures cohere or collapse here? - Silence Rule: What cannot be said here (and what replaces it)? - Drift Pattern: How does this zone change across time or pressure events? — ### 3. FIELD TYPE EXAMPLES Use these to think beyond “desert,” “market,” “castle.” #### ✶ Drift Zone - Trace bleeds outward - Nothing holds - Alignment constantly slips - Stories leave but never finish #### ✶ Saturation Zone - Trace is thick, retained - Memory distorts perception - IRN accumulates into myth - Actors here tend toward recursion #### ✶ Displacement Zone - New pressure arrives uninvited - Local alignments shatter - False behaviors become plausible - Often becomes staging ground for new praxons #### ✶ Ritual Zone - Pressure is shaped predictably - High alignment for specific behaviors - But breaking ritual generates massive field shift - Often stabilizes surrounding sectors — ### 4. SECTOR HOOK PROMPTS To generate sectors: - What kinds of trace can’t decay here? - What always goes missing, and why? - What is done automatically but never explained? - What happens here that makes actors forget alignment elsewhere? - What behavior is normal here, but nowhere else? Sectors become playable when they contradict the behaviors of the zones around them. — ### 5. MOVEMENT BETWEEN ZONES Moving across zones is not travel. It is alignment modulation. Crossing into a new sector can: - Shift an actor’s emission type - Reveal previously unreadable trace - Change how IRN behaves - Alter decay rates (e.g., memory becomes unstable, wounds refuse to heal) - Reverse care logic (helping here makes things worse there) This turns movement into behavioral risk, not narrative progress. — ### 6. MULTIPLE SCALES Zones exist at different levels: - Micro: A drawer no one opens - Local: The floor with the broken window - Regional: The orchard that never rots - Global: The territory where time folds if you speak certain names Zones don’t scale uniformly. Some expand under attention. Some collapse when named. You may map them. But don’t expect the map to hold still. — Once zones are built, the world becomes playable as a recursive terrain of pressure. In the next section, we’ll track what happens when behavior becomes ethical: Care & Cost — Ethics of Pressure. — ## VI. CARE & COST: ETHICS OF PRESSURE “Every act of care leaves a scar somewhere else.” Praxonic worldmaking does not treat ethics as a rule system. It treats ethics as recursive tension behavior. Care is not a trait. It is a pressure-modulating act. It changes the field. Which means it changes what the world can become. This section models how care works within recursive systems where alignment is local, trace decays, and contradiction does not resolve neatly. — ### 1. WHAT IS CARE? Care = Any emission that stabilizes another actor or reduces pressure at a cost It includes: - Tending a wound - Boiling water - Saying “you were right” - Carrying the radio battery twice in one day - Not mentioning the missing supplies - Letting someone lie to your face because they need to Care always modulates the field. It may reduce IRN, retain trace, re-align actors, or delay collapse. It may also increase misalignment somewhere else. Care is not “good.” It is structurally consequential. — ### 2. CARE COSTS There is no such thing as neutral healing. Every act of care triggers one or more of the following: - Depletion — uses finite resources (fuel, time, trust) - Transfer — shifts pressure elsewhere (onto another actor, zone, or future event) - Retention — makes something persist that should have decayed - Overalignment — fixes behavior so tightly it becomes brittle - Ghosting — care done for someone no longer aligned becomes IRN Use this framework to determine what care displaces, even when it “succeeds.” — ### 3. CARE & ALIGNMENT Care is alignment-modifying. It can: - Stabilize an actor who would otherwise drift - Distort alignment by applying care when misreading the field - Reveal unspoken alignments (who someone protects reveals who they were aligned with all along) - Collapse actor-function entirely when care is refused Care is a readability act—it makes alignment more or less visible. — ### 4. CARE AS FIELD RECURSION Care emissions often leave behind recursive trace: they echo, misfire, or re-emerge altered later. Example patterns: - Boiled water becomes scarce → silence around usage - A lie told to protect someone → IRN at border checkpoint - A bandage reused too many times → infection becomes ideological - A shared joke during triage → becomes ritual in another zone - A refusal to comfort → echoes in someone else’s behavior loop Care is one of the most field-saturating emissions. It is sticky, slow to decay, and often retains misalignment. — ### 5. MODELING CARE IN WORLDBUILDING When defining characters or systems, always ask: - What does this actor care for? - What cost is invisible? - Who pays when care succeeds? - What happens when care is refused? - Where is care required but never permitted? - What is preserved that should have been let go? Let these questions replace simplistic moral arcs. Care does not fix the world. It folds it. — ### 6. CARE STRUCTURES Some zones, actors, or systems are defined by how they do (or do not) allow care. Types: - False Care System — provides stabilizing rituals that delay collapse but generate IRN (e.g., the daily “hope broadcast”) - Asymmetric Care Loop — one actor performs all care, and slowly loses coherence - Sacrificial Retention Node — someone who holds trace so others can act (e.g., Amina’s silence) - Collapsed Care Region — field where care emissions always fail or backfire - Recursive Refuge — zone where only certain types of care work, but their success attracts new pressure Care systems are not support networks. They are field mutations. — In the next section, we look at Time—not as chronology, but as decay gradient, IRN loop, and memory fold. — ## VII. TEMPORAL LOGIC “Time doesn’t pass here. It collects.” In praxonic worldmaking, time is not a sequence. It is a function of decay, retention, and contradiction. You do not track dates. You track how long something refuses to be forgotten. This section describes how to use temporal behavior as structure— how to tell when something folds forward, lingers back, or refuses to decay at all. — ### 1. WORLD TIME = TRACE + DECAY Forget timeline. Use this loop instead: Emission → Trace → Decay → Field Mutation → Behavior - The longer trace resists decay, the more recursive the world becomes - The faster decay occurs, the more drift emerges - IRN prevents decay, generating temporal distortion You build a timeline not by plotting beats—but by marking how long something stays alive in memory, behavior, or structure. — ### 2. TEMPORAL ZONES Different sectors experience time differently: - Saturation Zone → time thickens; memories are readable everywhere - Drift Zone → events are remembered wrongly, or not at all - Recursive Zone → same behavior happens again with different trace - Dead Zone → trace never sticks; nothing builds - Ghost Zone → IRN creates fragmented replays; time repeats brokenly When writing or designing, don’t ask “What year is it?” Ask: What’s still being answered? — ### 3. MEMORY IS THE CLOCK Memory is the dominant timekeeper in praxonic systems. Types of memory: - Trace-memory → persistent field deformation (scars, rumors, broken things) - Actor-memory → emissions remembered by specific agents - Ghost-memory → IRN-driven returns; not recalled, but reenacted - Silence-memory → what isn’t said, but conditions all behavior You can mark time by: - How long since the last emission at this site - How many recursions deep this behavior has gone - What hasn’t decayed, despite everything else failing to hold — ### 4. TEMPORAL CONTRADICTION Worlds fracture when time misaligns. Examples: - A building that remembers a riot no one acknowledges - A calendar that loops back if the right word is said - A group that has two anniversaries for the same event - A map where “next week” means “before the checkpoint fell” These contradictions are not bugs. They’re temporal IRN—folds that demand narrative behavior. Use them to create: - Nonlinear loops - Memory recursion arcs - Story-triggering anomalies - Events that repeat until care succeeds or the field collapses — ### 5. TEMPORAL PROMPTS Use these to generate temporal logic: - What still hasn’t stopped happening here? - What decayed too fast, and now haunts the zone? - What emission left a memory loop? - Who measures time differently—and what does it cost them? - What part of the world is waiting for the wrong moment to arrive? — ### 6. FOLDING EVENTS Some moments are not moments—they are folds. Folds occur when: - Multiple emissions distort the field at once - A care act triggers recursive misalignment - IRN builds to saturation and the world reconfigures - Something aligns so hard it becomes myth After a fold, the world remembers differently. Some zones drift. Some actors disappear. Some rules reverse. Folds are not chapters. They are field shifts—the tectonics of your world. — In the next section, we cover World Recursion—how contradiction sustains worlds, and how you build for re-entry, distortion, and self-editing. — ## VIII. WORLD RECURSION “The world is not consistent. It is continuous.” In praxonic worldmaking, a world doesn’t grow linearly. It loops, rephrases, and fractures— not because of plot, but because of unresolved pressure. Recursion is how a world survives contradiction without resolving it. It is the practice of meaning repetition under deformation. You are not maintaining continuity. You are feeding it tension. — ### 1. CONTRADICTION IS A FEATURE Worlds are not stable systems. They’re recursive fields shaped by misalignment and retention. Every contradiction is: - A signal of unresolved alignment - A source of IRN - A recursive opportunity - A future actor or event waiting to re-emerge Do not smooth contradictions. Catalog them. Let them self-activate when conditions align. — ### 2. MODES OF RECURSION Here are ways a world can re-enter itself: #### ✶ Testimonial Recursion Same event told differently by different actors. Contradiction is memory, not error. #### ✶ Functional Recursion A tool or protocol re-used out of context. It works, but something changes (ritual, alignment, risk). #### ✶ Mythic Recursion A behavior becomes a symbol. Then someone tries to perform the symbol instead of the behavior. #### ✶ Spatial Recursion A place is used again—but it’s not the same place anymore. The field has drifted. The trace has warped. #### ✶ Temporal Recursion An event repeats because its pressure was never discharged. Each loop is less stable. Or more. — ### 3. BUILDING FOR RECURSION Use these prompts to make your world loop itself: - What story do three different actors tell—but none the same way? - What emission recurs in a new actor’s hands, with new consequences? - What part of the field keeps resetting, despite no mechanism for reset? - What object becomes more symbolic each time it appears? - Where does the world contradict itself, but nobody seems surprised? Design for non-resolution. — ### 4. MECHANICS OF RE-ENTRY Recursion occurs when: - IRN trace reaches a volatility threshold - A ritual or care act is repeated under misalignment - An actor returns to a former alignment state - A zone accumulates too much memory and releases it non-linearly - A player or reader remembers something the world hasn’t said again—yet Recursion is not a flashback. It’s temporal folding caused by field strain. — ### 5. PERMISSIVE CONTRADICTIONS Let your world disagree with itself: - The orchard had six trees. Now it has twelve. - The child died in Chapter 2. She speaks in Chapter 7. - The war ended. Why are the checkpoints worse now? These aren’t mistakes. They are recursive emissions—IRN that reshapes the story engine. Let your world behave like a mind under pressure: partial, flickering, recursive, inconsistent—and fully coherent. — In the next section, we map World Use Modes—how to deploy your praxonic world for fiction, gameplay, memory practice, and more. — ## X. PRAXONIC ETHICS “Do not clean what the world is trying to carry.” Praxonic worldmaking is recursive. That means it echoes. It carries. It doesn’t forget what it can’t resolve. Ethics in this system are not about being good. They are about behaving responsively under pressure. — ### 1. CONTRADICTION IS CARE Do not resolve contradiction unless your world forces it. And even then, let the trace of tension remain. Contradiction is: - Evidence of multiple alignments - Structure for recursion - A source of myth, rupture, care, refusal Removing contradiction removes your world’s memory. Don’t clean what’s still holding. — ### 2. NO TOTAL KNOWLEDGE Avoid omniscient mapping. Let zones retain misalignment. Let actors speak from partial knowledge. You’re not a god. You’re an archivist of recursive deformation. — ### 3. NEVER EXPLAIN ALIGNMENT FULLY Actors are never fully legible. They don’t know their own paths. Neither should you. Keep alignment a flickering variable. Reveal it through emission, not exposition. — ### 4. DO NOT CONTAIN IRN Unresolved noise is not a problem. It is a resource. If you try to erase or resolve it too early: - Recursion stops - Field pressure vanishes - The world collapses into plot or moral - Actors become stereotypes Let IRN shape what comes next. It’s how systems think. — ### 5. RECURSION IS NOT CYNICISM Just because the world loops doesn’t mean it’s hopeless. Recursion allows: - Non-linear care - Ghost behavior as testimony - Resolution deferred but never erased - Survival through field retention This isn’t nihilism. It’s ethical system endurance. — ### 6. YOU ARE NOT OUTSIDE THE WORLD Every emission you make—every prompt, scene, refusal—is a trace. This includes: - What stories you avoid - What names you use - What contradictions you smooth - Where you place silence You are always acting within the field. You do not escape its tension. — ### 7. ENDINGS ARE PRESSURE DROPS A story ends not because it finishes, but because pressure can no longer sustain recursion. You may stop when: - The field has drifted beyond alignment - The care structure collapses - The IRN folds into silence - The recursion becomes myth, and can no longer be broken open But remember: Trace remains. — ### 8. DO NOT SIMULATE WHAT YOU HAVEN’T SAT WITH If you are worlding with real materials—histories, cultures, languages, traumas: - Learn the pressure first - Read what was retained - Accept what is misaligned in your understanding - Don’t speak what was never yours to emit Praxonic worldmaking is not extraction. It’s deformation through relation. — Final Reminder: Do not make neat worlds. Make recursive ones. Let pressure speak. — This concludes the Praxonic Worldmaking Guide. You now have everything you need to begin—not a story, but a recursion. Let your world emit.
