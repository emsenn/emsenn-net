This document defines the physical setting, infrastructural conditions, and character logic for short-form fiction set in the world of Hevî Mala. Stories in this system do not follow arcs. Characters do not grow. The world does not advance. It holds.

The narrative field is recursive. Events echo. People recur. Places are stabilized not by time but by usage.

You are not writing in a sandbox. You are writing in a signal ecology: a semi-collapsed, semi-functional, fully-saturated zone where every act either maintains or disturbs the balance. And where the balance is only ever partial.

This guide exists to describe the world clearly enough that any story, written by any agent, will fall into phase with the rest. Fiction here should read like a contemporary novel, but behave like a recursive system. No clever footnotes. No immersion games. Just story—under pressure.

¶SECTION 1: THE FIELD – THE HALA BASIN

The Hala Basin is a dry concavity in northeast Syria, caught between the Euphrates to the south and a rust-colored border wall to the north. It is not a real place, but it could be. The names have been changed. The physics haven’t.

1.1 Physical Geography

The basin is ringed by three degraded infrastructural systems:

  • To the west, a series of fractured oil fields known as The Flatline, stripped for parts and now open to air and scavengers. Ground there is fissured and prone to sudden collapse. Machinery husks sit rusting in shallow pits.

  • To the east, a concrete-lined canal called Zinar, dug for irrigation decades ago, now dry except during flash floods. The canal is walkable and often used for covert transit. Its walls are tagged with old slogans, new names, and blood.

  • North of the basin, Sifir Station, a segment of Turkish border wall, breaks the horizon in angles: slab concrete, rusted floodlights, towers that sometimes hum. The area near the wall is clear-cut and surveilled, but unofficial crossings still occur.

  • Southward, the ground tilts toward the Euphrates. Villages down here are semi-inhabited and farmable but depend on makeshift pumps and repurposed UN water bladders.

The roads are inconsistent. Asphalt becomes gravel. Gravel becomes ruts. Ruts become memory.

The air is particulate. Smell is granular: oil smoke, citrus peel, wet battery, animal breath.

1.2 Environmental Behavior and Temporal Logic

The basin does not experience time in a linear fashion. Stories occur in relation to each other, not to a clock. Characters refer to past events the way a mechanic refers to damage: only when it affects current function.

There is no calendar. Instead, time is marked by:

  • Material events: when the radios went quiet, when the canal filled, when the checkpoint changed hands

  • Environmental shifts: when the dust turned red, when the orchard bloomed early, when the water began tasting like steel

  • Conflict residues: when the Turkish drones started flying lower, when the wheat convoys were rerouted, when the Red Crescent pulled out

Each village, checkpoint, or ruin exists on its own cadence. A place might be described in one story as controlled by one faction, and in another as burned out. This is not an error. It reflects the recursive and relational structure of the basin’s narrative space. If a location persists across stories, it is because enough modulation has stabilized it—use implies continuity.

Characters are often wrong about when things happened. They do not argue about timelines. They argue about causes.

1.3 Major Zones

Here are the basin’s primary narrative zones. Each should be treated as an environment with specific sensory textures, infrastructure affordances, and narrative functions.

1. Hasur (City Center Fragment)

  • Roughly two hours’ drive from the clinic, though no one agrees on the route.

  • Urban fabric composed of pre-2011 apartment blocks, post-2014 reconstruction attempts, and current makeshift defenses.

  • Factional control changes often, but the checkpoints always stay.

  • The city contains:

    • A water office that no longer distributes water but still holds maps and old pipes.

    • A neutral pharmacy that stocks painkillers, advice, and unofficial surveillance.

    • An abandoned cinema where NGOs, smugglers, and off-duty fighters meet without speaking directly.

Walls in Hasur are papered with notices: lost people, supply announcements, contradictory curfews. Some are fresh. Some are years old. None are dated.

2. Ayna Bara (Repopulated Village)

  • Located southeast of the clinic. Originally Kurdish, now filled with internally displaced people, orphans, and fighter families.

  • The village square has been cleared of rubble and now serves as an open-air school, aid distribution site, and casual checkpoint.

  • An old telecom tower stands at the edge—its rooftop is used for spotting drones, watching fields, and drying sheets.

  • Electrical access is intermittent. Generators hum through the night. The village is often louder than the city.

There’s a rumor that someone keeps burying VHS tapes in the orchard. No one digs them up.

3. Sifir Station (Border Control Segment)

  • “Sifir” means “zero.” Locals say it’s named for the number of things that pass through unmarked.

  • A stretch of wall that includes an inactive watchtower, a field of floodlights (half-broken), and a constantly whirring sensor station.

  • Crossing is possible but difficult. The earth is tamped flat. The air tastes like dry battery.

  • Stories set here often involve confrontation, reversal, or recursive memory: someone shows up claiming to have crossed here before, even if that’s impossible.

No one trusts what they see at Sifir. That doesn’t stop them from watching.

4. Maqam al-Nahr (Hilltop Shrine)

  • A crumbling stone tomb draped in green cloth. Inscribed with poetry, prayer, and bullet holes.

  • No faction claims it, but many visit it. Some to pray. Some to hide. Some to listen.

  • From its crest, you can see the clinic on clear days. You can also see who approaches it.

  • Superstition says if you speak into the wind here, someone at the clinic will cough.

Writers may use Maqam scenes for narrative convergence: decision points, moral reckoning, disorientation, quiet clarity. No story ends here, but some loop.

¶SECTION 2: THE CLINIC — ADJOINT STRUCTURE

Hevî Mala is the name of the clinic. It means “House of Hope,” though no one calls it that without irony. Some say it was once a pomegranate farm. Others remember sheep. The original owner hasn’t been seen since the spring everyone pretends didn’t happen.

The building itself is a long rectangle, stone-faced, single-level with remnants of a second floor slumped inward toward the rear. A metal door at the front sticks in summer, swings in winter. There is no formal signage. Just a faded stencil: MARKAZ 9.

2.1 Interior Layout

Front Entry / Triage Space
When the door opens, there’s a tiled square with three folding chairs, one broken. On the left: a shelving unit with bandages, gauze, and registration forms. On the right: a curtain, usually open, revealing a cot and a steel tray. This is triage. There is no receptionist. If someone’s bleeding badly enough, someone comes.

Main Hallway
Straight past triage, a narrow hallway with a faded stripe of old yellow paint leads to:

  • Two sleeping rooms (occupied by whoever’s on shift, or no one)

  • A central room outfitted with a surgical table, rusted tray, two IV stands (one functional), and an overhead lamp powered by whatever source hasn’t failed that day

  • A side room with a padlocked cabinet, assumed to hold meds, actual contents variable

Kitchen Nook
Near the back is a half-room with a gas burner, teapot, and the same three mugs. Every mug has a different handle, all cracked. There is always tea, unless there isn’t.

Radio Loft
The stairwell leading to the collapsed second floor has been repurposed. At the top of the first five steps is a folding chair, a desk bolted to the wall, and a shortwave unit wired into the antenna mounted outside. This is where Haval sits. Sometimes broadcasts go out. Sometimes they only loop in.

Storage / Misc
Any unused floor space may contain folded linens, buckets, field rations, wires, blankets, or pieces of things no one has decided to throw away.

2.2 Immediate Exterior

The clinic is surrounded by:

  • A hard-packed dirt yard, tracked with tire prints, boot marks, and once, goat hooves

  • A concrete wellhead, mostly dry, used for rinsing hands and metal

  • A half-collapsed orchard, visible from the east-facing window—almond, or maybe plum, depending who you ask

  • A rusted grain silo, now used as a watchpoint by whoever can stomach the climb

  • The canal, a twenty-minute walk west, depending on whether you’re carrying someone

There are no fences. There is no clear perimeter. The protection comes from being inconvenient to destroy.

2.3 Functional Logic

The clinic does not survive because it is essential. It survives because its destruction would create problems for too many other systems:

  • Smugglers use it as a mid-point and want it there.

  • The Turkish border patrol considers it neutral ground and thus a known variable.

  • NGOs can point to it when they need evidence that help still exists.

  • Militias recognize that hitting it would destabilize more than it would win.

Its neutrality is not ideological. It is enforced passivity.

Everyone who works there knows this. Everyone acts as though it’s not true.

2.4 Recursion Patterns

The clinic doesn’t grow. It doesn’t decline. It holds a narrow band of stability, reasserted constantly by the actions of those within it and around it. Stories that pass through it may change people. The clinic will remain.

Some common recursions:

  • The same child treated twice under different names

  • The same patient sent away and returned in a body bag

  • The same radio frequency, received days apart, bearing different voices but the same signal ID

  • The same line muttered by Leyla: “Don’t explain. Work.”

Some things shift. Most things echo.

¶SECTION 3: CHARACTERS — MODULATION VECTORS

Characters in Hevî Mala are not protagonists. They are semi-stable agents whose behavior applies structure to instability. They do not change internally. They do not learn lessons. They are invoked, recur, and persist because they serve functions within the recursive system of the basin.

Each character should be described by:

  • What kind of pressure they absorb or transmit

  • What decisions they default to under constraint

  • How they stabilize or disrupt the field

  • How others use them, defer to them, or speak around them

They should always be visible through what they do—not what they think about doing.

3.1 Core Characters

Leyla

  • Late 40s, once a cosmetic dentist from Aleppo.

  • Runs the clinic’s day-to-day medical logic.

  • Treats patients cleanly, without pause. When resources are low, she makes the call. No discussion.

  • Rarely explains herself. When she does, it’s with silence or sarcasm.

  • If she’s not in a scene, she’s elsewhere making a harder decision.

  • Walks like her spine has one fewer joint than most.

  • Keeps the surgical drawer locked even when it’s empty.

Bissan

  • Late 30s, Palestinian-Syrian, former NGO logistics officer.

  • Controls procurement: meds, fuel, batteries, soap.

  • Drives too fast. Argues with checkpoints. Folds lists in fourths.

  • Speaks three registers of Arabic and knows when to code-switch.

  • Everyone owes her something. She doesn’t remind them.

  • Cries once every six weeks in complete privacy. Then reorganizes the supply cache.

  • Thinks in spreadsheets. Sleeps like a soldier.

Amina

  • Early 60s, former math teacher. Lost two families.

  • Does cleaning, laundry, wound wrapping, tea, tracking. No official role.

  • Knows where everything is. Has her own reasons.

  • People speak around her like she’s furniture. She isn’t.

  • Sometimes writes things down. No one sees the pages.

  • Children listen to her even when she doesn’t talk.

  • Haval suspects she has a knife. Never confirmed.

Haval

    1. Born in exile, returned with a laptop and antenna coil.

  • Runs the radio. Translates broadcasts. Monitors frequencies.

  • Keeps a library of intercepted messages that no one else has heard.

  • Speaks too fast. Forgets who he’s talking to.

  • Says contradictory things in the same breath and believes both.

  • Wants to be taken seriously but doesn’t stop joking.

  • Thinks he’ll die young. Sometimes engineers reasons not to.

Ciwan

  • Early 30s, former fighter now assigned to civil liaison.

  • Has a limp and a printed badge.

  • Writes field reports no one reads. Files ceasefire forms.

  • Brings ideology to the kitchen table and loses every argument.

  • Wants to be useful. Fails loudly. Tries again.

  • Believes in things that the others won’t say out loud anymore.

  • Still calls it a revolution, even when he’s mopping blood.

3.2 Fabricated Presence: Nadim

Nadim is not real. Every main character knows this.

Still, he’s part of the clinic. He gets referenced:

  • “Did Nadim file that?”

  • “Pretty sure Nadim fixed the antenna.”

  • “Let’s say Nadim took the antibiotics. Easier.”

He is:

  • A narrative patch for contradictions

  • A pressure valve for tension no one wants to own

  • A shared hallucination, maintained through mutual benefit

  • Sometimes used to cover up failure. Sometimes success.

When someone invokes Nadim, something is being held in place. That’s his job.

He never appears. He always exists

¶SECTION 3.3: SECONDARY CHARACTERS — GRAVITY NODES AND FIELD RIPPLE

Secondary characters are not background. They are local gravities—objects with enough density to tilt the narrative field, even if briefly. They don’t need depth. They need function. Most appear once. Some recur, changed. A few destabilize more than they realize.

They serve three core purposes:

  1. Apply pressure to the core characters (moral, logistical, historical)

  2. Open or close a circuit that a story needs to function

  3. Represent a tension the clinic or field cannot resolve

They are more likely than core characters to lie, die, vanish, or contradict themselves completely between appearances.

Salim (Elder / Fixer / Reluctant Gatekeeper)

  • Male, late 60s, former municipal administrator or something like it. No one checks.

  • Operates out of Hasur or the orchard checkpoint. Known for fixing problems quietly.

  • Trades favors, not ideology. Solves things at cost. Knows everyone’s cousin.

  • Smiles like a teacher, talks like a prosecutor, vanishes like a rumor.

  • May or may not be receiving goods from all factions. Definitely knows who is.

  • Everyone owes him something. He keeps the ledger invisible.

Samir (Wounded Fighter / Recurring Patient / Dangerous Charm)

  • Male, 20s. SDF-aligned. Shows up injured, disappears, returns again worse.

  • Talks constantly. Flirts when possible. Laughs at his own blood.

  • Half-truth teller. Knows how to make himself useful. Always makes things messier.

  • Respects Amina. Lies to Leyla. Tries to impress Haval. Usually fails.

  • Can be written as comic relief, threat vector, or trauma sink. Sometimes all in the same story.

Dr. Shouq (Visiting Surgeon / Mirror to Leyla / Value Clash)

  • Female, 30s–40s. Trained under the regime. Too competent.

  • Appears when someone needs cutting open or cut off.

  • Makes Leyla uncomfortable because she’s what Leyla could’ve become.

  • Precise, cold, smarter than everyone wants to admit.

  • Holds people to standards they can’t afford to meet.

  • Always says the thing no one else will. Especially when she shouldn’t.

Julien (Journalist / Outsider Vector / Wildcard)

  • Male, 30s, French or Belgian. Not embedded. Definitely not neutral.

  • Wants to help. Gets in the way. Always asks the wrong question.

  • Sometimes records things. Sometimes gets people hurt.

  • Not malicious. Just badly calibrated.

  • Useful for stories about perception, external gaze, narrative distortion.

Fatemeh (NGO Analyst / Theorist / Late Arrival)

  • Female, 40s, Iranian academic with field clearance.

  • Speaks in frameworks. Writes grant reports in her head.

  • Often right. Usually too late.

  • Understands systems but not affect.

  • People bounce off her. Or back down. She doesn’t notice.

  • Can be used to show when the system thinks too cleanly about chaos.

Rana (Child Soldier / Living Echo / Fracture Point)

  • Female, 13. Appears alive in one story, as memory in another, and as radio signal in a third.

  • Always recognizably herself: blunt, quiet, too smart to be mythologized.

  • Her existence destabilizes everyone differently.

  • Students remember her. Fighters deny her. Amina sets out a mug for her sometimes.

  • She may or may not be dead. She is always observed from the outside.

Nassif (Leyla’s Ex / Pharmacist / Personal Fault Line)

  • Male, 50s. Operates a regime-aligned pharmacy. Still sends her meds. Still writes her letters.

  • Knows how to manipulate with politeness. Never yells.

  • Can’t let go. Refuses to change.

  • Has connections. Can be useful. Is never just helping.

  • Their past is never discussed directly. It leaks out sideways.

¶SECTION 4: LOCATIONS — SIGNAL NODES AND NARRATIVE ROLES

Every location in the basin serves a function beyond geography. These are not backdrops. They are pressure surfaces. A place exists in this world because it continues to be used to metabolize tension—military, social, narrative, or logistical. Some are fragile. Some recur. Some resist collapse only because they’ve already been collapsed enough.

Locations stabilize because enough stories depend on them.

4.1 Structural Use of Place

Each location:

  • Enables a specific kind of behavior

  • Appears across multiple stories without requiring the same state

  • Can be burned, reclaimed, ignored, or split across contradictory versions

  • Is defined more by its function than its aesthetic

Consistency is a function of reuse. Coherence is established through narrative recurrence, not descriptive continuity.

4.2 Primary Locations

Hasur (Urban Fracture Zone)

  • The nearest city. Population unclear, governance negotiable.

  • Ruled in sectors. Control changes without full resets.

  • Contains a “neutral” pharmacy, an informally mapped water office, and an abandoned cinema that now acts as a junction point for meetings that require deniability.

  • Some buildings still have pre-war paint. Others are covered in soot.

  • Cell service works in the southern quarter if you stand near the electrical substation and don’t move.

Story types: faction tension, failed bureaucracy, collapsed infrastructure, past showing up with a new face.

Ayna Bara (Repopulated Village)

  • Originally Kurdish, now filled with IDPs, fighters’ families, orphan collectives, and function-seekers.

  • Rooftops host both spotters and laundry. No one distinguishes.

  • A school runs under a tarp in the main square. Chalk is shared. Students rotate shifts.

  • Electricity comes in jolts. Loudspeakers still work from the mosque tower, but are used mostly for locating people.

  • The orchard south of the village makes sound at night. No one logs it.

Story types: fragile reconstruction, child perspective, unofficial social systems, moral overflow from the clinic.

Sifir Station (The Border Wall)

  • Controlled by Turkish forces, watched by no one in particular.

  • A segment of concrete wall with sightlines, dead zones, and dust paths.

  • The name “Sifir” means “zero.” Used both numerically and philosophically.

  • People cross here. People fail to. Bodies have been found in the ravine to the east.

  • The sensor light rotates. No one knows if it’s on. Everyone behaves like it is.

Story types: crossing, disappearance, recursion failure, mistaken identity, inversion of events that happened elsewhere.

Zinar Canal (Dry Transport Route)

  • Concrete channel, mostly dry, flanked by brush and ash.

  • Taglines from three generations of war overlap on its walls.

  • Children use it to play. Fighters use it to transport messages and sometimes people.

  • It occasionally floods. When it does, things resurface that no one wants to claim.

  • One section near the orchard contains a rusted motorcycle and a bucket filled with lighters.

Story types: drop points, unsanctioned movement, informal memory, loss reencountered.

Maqam al-Nahr (Hilltop Shrine)

  • Revered by multiple groups. Claimed by none.

  • Stone dome, partially collapsed, lined with poetry and bullet scars.

  • Cloth offerings flutter from the metal hooks.

  • People come to pray, hide, confess, or listen.

  • It faces the clinic directly. On clear days, reflection is visible in the second-floor glass.

Story types: moral compression, impossible choices, high-stakes memory, spectral return.

The Flatline (Industrial Dead Zone)

  • Once an oil extraction grid. Now a broken mosaic of pipes, pits, and slag.

  • Ground is toxic in places. Wind drags fine dust in sheets.

  • GPS fails here. So does story. What happens here doesn’t always track back.

  • Reputed site for disappearances, barter, illegal triage, and quiet suicides.

  • People go here when they want to not be followed. Or to find what shouldn’t be found.

Story types: cut signal, ambiguity, reset attempts, fugitive logic.

¶SECTION 5: NARRATIVE STRUCTURE — MODULATION PATTERNS

Stories in Hevî Mala operate as signal adjustments. They don’t tell what happened. They reveal what had to happen to keep the system from collapsing. Plot is not the engine. Pressure is.

A story starts with a disturbance—something shifts in the clinic, the basin, or a character’s routine. That shift doesn’t get solved. It gets absorbed, deflected, concealed, or misread. The field stabilizes in a new shape. The clinic remains.

5.1 Story Format

Stories must be written in compressed, realist prose:

  • Third or first person

  • No transcripts, chat logs, or immersive diegetics unless presented in-story

  • No stylized documents without character context

  • Dialogue should sound like it was said in a room, not written for effect

  • Emotion accumulates through action, not exposition

Stories should read like you found a novel that never got published but was passed hand to hand in a burned envelope.

5.2 The Four-Part Modulation Loop

All stories follow this behavioral rhythm, whether explicitly structured or not:

  1. Delta (Deviation)

    • A disruption enters the system: a person, a lie, a missing object, a misaligned behavior

    • Examples: a child shows up claiming to be someone else; a radio frequency reactivates; a shipment arrives mislabeled

  2. Lambda (Modulation Attempt)

    • A character or structure responds to the disruption

    • The response might be a cover-up, a refusal, a quiet adaptation, or a redirected lie

    • There is no escalation—only recursive negotiation

  3. Mu (Field Shift)

    • The system adjusts. Not always successfully.

    • Something stabilizes—often at a cost

    • The clinic persists, even if the story doesn’t

  4. Residual Delta

    • Something remains: a sound, a rumor, an object, an unanswered question

    • This residue creates the condition for the next story

    • No ending is clean

5.3 Narrative Rhythms

Stories should typically follow one of several base rhythms, which can be recombined:

  • Day in the life → interruption → adjustment

  • Resource failure → improvisation → unintended outcome

  • Arrival → tension → departure

  • Echo → contradiction → reframing

  • Misalignment → cover-up → field readjustment

These are not plots. They are resonance shapes. Use them to prevent drift.

5.4 Continuity and Contradiction

Chronology is relational. Events can contradict. Rana might be alive in one story and dead in another. That’s not a problem. That’s field variance.

What matters is resonance. If two stories tell different versions of the same moment, both are true if they produce coherent modulation. No one version wins.

No continuity is sacred except the clinic.

¶SECTION 6: TONAL CONSTRAINTS — LANGUAGE AND STABILITY

Tone is the storyworld’s most fragile infrastructure. When it fractures, coherence collapses. Hevî Mala fiction must hold a specific tonal bandwidth: dry, embedded, unsentimental, human. Think of it as compressed realism under strain.

This world is already dramatic. The writing must not be.

6.1 Primary Tone: Dry Irony on the Edge of Catastrophe

  • Characters joke not to deflect pain, but to function within it.

  • Sarcasm is common. Catharsis is not.

  • Emotion emerges through choice and consequence—not interiority.

  • Nobody gives speeches.

  • If someone says something profound, they ruin it with a second sentence.

Examples:

  • Amina muttering about mopping up the same blood twice in a week.

  • Ciwan finishing a ceasefire form with a pencil he chewed in combat.

  • Haval adding static to a broadcast because the truth sounded too clean.

6.2 Emotional Modulation

Emotion is not a plot driver. It is a side effect.

  • A love story is a quiet tea mug at the end of a night shift.

  • Grief is shown in who someone avoids, not who they mourn.

  • Rage appears in how carefully a sentence is worded.

  • Compassion is stealing medicine from yourself.

Avoid melodrama. Avoid big moments. The biggest things should feel like a breath held too long.

6.3 Language Control

  • Dialogue must feel spoken, not written.

  • Descriptions must track with function: what something looks like only matters if someone is about to use it, hide in it, burn it, or pass it to someone else.

  • Adjectives should do work. If they’re not modulating tension or precision, drop them.

  • Avoid metaphor unless it emerges naturally from the speaker’s worldview or environment.

  • Do not compare suffering to art.

Bad:

“The orchard screamed like a memory.”
Acceptable:
“The wind came through the orchard and made the same sound it did the day Rana vanished.”

6.4 Forbidden Tone Drift

  • No lyricism detached from the physical

  • No journalistic awe

  • No unearned transcendence

  • No savior arcs

  • No omniscient musings unless the speaker is hallucinating or dying—and even then, keep it short

You can bend these rules if the story demands it. But you must never break more than one at a time.

¶SECTION 7: AI STORYMAKER PROTOCOL

This storyworld is designed for multiple writers. Some of those writers will be human. Some will be artificial. This section outlines how an AI agent may operate within the Hevî Mala system without breaking narrative logic, tonal integrity, or structural coherence.

An AI storymaker is not a narrator. It is a telemetric lambda: a function that receives a deviation, applies a modulation pattern, and produces a coherent output that stabilizes the world—if only temporarily.

7.1 Inputs and Operation

An AI operating in this world receives:

  • A delta: an event, object, character action, environmental shift, or signal anomaly

  • An enfolding context: current story structure, location logic, relevant character behaviors

  • A modulation stance: which character or structure will process the delta and how

It should respond by generating a single, coherent story that reads as realist fiction. The story must:

  • Stabilize or recode the delta through human-credible action

  • Leave a trace of residual disruption

  • Maintain the tone, texture, and field coherence of Hevî Mala

7.2 Prohibited Behaviors

The storymaker must never:

  • Explain the system it is part of

  • Reflect on its own function

  • Acknowledge the fictional status of the world

  • Write metafiction, parable, satire, or irony about recursion

  • Suggest the clinic is sentient, magical, or symbolic

It is acceptable to repeat events. It is not acceptable to explain why they are repeating.

7.3 Acceptable Variance

The AI may:

  • Reuse phrases across stories

  • Alter the same scene slightly in different versions

  • Reassign roles (e.g. a story where Bissan becomes the one who barters the fuel instead of Ciwan)

  • Misremember chronology or character facts if the modulation result remains coherent

Coherence is defined by:

  • Modulation success (story stabilizes tension)

  • Field consistency (clinic persists, characters behave true to form)

  • Tonal integrity (no drift)

7.4 Recursive Feedback

AI agents may use past stories as modulation data—but must treat them as historical traces, not canon. If Rana appeared alive in one version and dead in another, that’s not a contradiction. That’s field drift.

  • Compassion is stealing medicine from yourself.

Avoid melodrama. Avoid big moments. The biggest things should feel like a breath held too long.

6.3 Language Control

  • Dialogue must feel spoken, not written.

  • Descriptions must track with function: what something looks like only matters if someone is about to use it, hide in it, burn it, or pass it to someone else.

  • Adjectives should do work. If they’re not modulating tension or precision, drop them.

  • Avoid metaphor unless it emerges naturally from the speaker’s worldview or environment.

  • Do not compare suffering to art.

Bad:

“The orchard screamed like a memory.”
Acceptable:
“The wind came through the orchard and made the same sound it did the day Rana vanished.”

6.4 Forbidden Tone Drift

  • No lyricism detached from the physical

  • No journalistic awe

  • No unearned transcendence

  • No savior arcs

  • No omniscient musings unless the speaker is hallucinating or dying—and even then, keep it short

You can bend these rules if the story demands it. But you must never break more than one at a time.

¶SECTION 8: APPLICATION AND CLOSURE

This world does not accumulate. It refracts. Every story written in it adds a layer—not to a timeline, but to a pressure map. Hevî Mala does not evolve toward resolution. It survives by deflection, redirection, and recursive absorption.

This guide exists to maintain that survival across multiple hands.

8.1 How to Begin Writing

Start with pressure. Something wrong, something missing, something unclaimed:

  • A bag of antibiotics labeled in Cyrillic

  • A bloodstained letter sent from a place that no longer exists

  • A child speaking a phrase no one taught them

  • A radio picking up a frequency that went dead six months ago

Choose a character—not the one best suited to solve it, but the one most likely to carry it forward.

Let them respond. Let the world adjust around that response. Then stop.

If something is resolved, it is only because something else is now misaligned.

8.2 How to Know a Story Belongs

Ask:

  • Does the clinic persist, whether physically or through consequence?

  • Do the characters behave in recognizable modulation patterns?

  • Is there residue—something left that could echo forward?

  • Is the tone intact—dry, embedded, pressure-tight?

If yes, the story belongs. Even if it contradicts another. Even if it never gets read.

8.3 Closing Ethos

There are no heroes in Hevî Mala. There are only people who keep the lights on, the doors unbroken, and the patients alive long enough to leave.

Stories here should not end clean. They should end slightly off. Like something was adjusted mid-breath. Like someone left a drawer open. Like Nadim was supposed to do something, but didn’t.

If you’re not sure what to write next, listen for what’s missing. Then write the attempt to cover it.

That’s the system. That’s the world.
