¶INTRODUCTION

Hevî Mala is a living archive, a fictionalized literary project grounded in a Northeast Syria analog during the post-2011 conflict collapse. It centers on a borderland outpost—part clinic, part radio relay, part shelter—and the people who keep it alive through war, sarcasm, and labor.

This handbook establishes the core principles, constraints, characters, settings, ethics, and tonal rules of the shared world, so that multiple writers can contribute narratives that are distinct in voice but coherent in structure. This isn’t a sandbox. It’s a living system. It has edges, hierarchies, and ghosts.

¶SECTION I: WORLD OVERVIEW

¶The Setting

The storyworld is set in a fictionalized border region of Northeast Syria called the Hala Basin, between the Euphrates and the Turkish wall. It is not a real place. But it is entirely plausible.

The basin is composed of:

  • A war-damaged urban center (Hasur)

  • Abandoned and repopulated villages

  • Smuggling corridors, dry riverbeds, and border surveillance zones

  • Oil field ruins and lost Sufi shrines

  • One outpost that shouldn’t still be standing: Hevî Mala

¶The Outpost

The clinic is called many things:

  • Hevî Mala (“Hope House”)

  • Markaz 9

  • The Post

  • Radio Kobane (though it’s not in Kobane)

It is:

  • A converted farmhouse with surgical capacity

  • A shortwave relay node for independent media

  • A waypoint for the displaced, the hunted, and the broken

  • A political pressure point wearing the mask of neutrality

Its survival depends on pretending not to matter—while mattering to everyone.

¶The Timeframe

Ambiguous, but grounded between 2011–2016.
This is a parallel conflict timeline. Major global events are felt but not named. Writers should think in emotional chronology, not calendar time.

Example:

  • “The time after the Turkish wall went up.”

  • “Before the wheat convoys burned.”

  • “After the drones, before the cameras.”

¶SECTION II: NARRATIVE STRUCTURE

¶Format + Form

Hevî Mala is not a single narrative voice—it is a story mesh. Writers may submit stories in any of the following modes:

  • Standard narrative fiction (third- or first-person)

  • Epistolary fragments (letters, emails, intercepted texts)

  • Radio transcripts / audio logs

  • Incident reports, dossiers, or procedural breakdowns

  • Oral histories or testimony

  • Field diaries or memory reconstructions

¶Core Structural Constraints

  1. All stories must involve or intersect with the clinic.
    Whether it’s a passing visit, an attack, a broadcast, or a rumor—it is the magnetic center.

  2. Every story should carry tension between care and consequence.
    Nothing is apolitical. No act of healing is free of cost.

  3. Characters may recur, be mentioned, or reappear altered.
    Continuity is relational, not cinematic. Think echoes, not cameos.

  4. Truth is local.
    Multiple versions of the same event may appear. Contradiction is not error—it’s evidence.

¶Acceptable Narrative Rhythms

  • Day-in-the-life with political undertow

  • Patient story arcs (arrival → treatment → departure/aftermath)

  • Resource failure → improvisation → unintended outcome

  • New arrival changes internal power dynamics

  • Ghost of past regime/faction returns through a body, object, or voice

  • Transmission intercept changes a decision

  • A lie gets told and nobody corrects it

  • Someone leaves, but not by choice

¶SECTION III: TONE CHARTER

The rhythm of the world. What this story feels like when you sit inside it.

¶1. Primary Tone: Dry Irony at the Edge of Catastrophe

  • Humor emerges from routine, contradiction, and human friction—not from punchlines.

  • The world isn’t funny. People are. They have to be.

  • Characters use wit like gauze: not to stop the bleeding, just to hold the pieces together.

Think:

  • Amina muttering under her breath while boiling gauze for the fifth time today

  • Haval making a meme out of a field report

  • Bissan negotiating a ceasefire with Excel formulas

¶2. Emotional Modulation: Whiplash, Not Melodrama

  • Scene tone can turn on a single word, breath, or wound—but not theatrically.

  • Deaths don’t get elegies. They get reallocation of supplies.

  • Love doesn’t declare itself. It shows up with tea during the night shift.

  • Tragedy doesn’t arrive with music. It arrives with a wet bag of bloodied clothing.

Technique Note:
Let emotion accumulate under action—don’t lead with it. The war’s already dramatic. The writing shouldn’t be.

¶3. Pacing and Compression

  • Language should be compressed, observational, and specific.

  • Avoid over-description; focus on function, texture, and tension.

  • Dialogue is sharp but lived-in. People talk like they have other things to do.

Forbidden Tone Drift:

  • Lyrical reverie unanchored from the physical

  • Moralizing exposition

  • Western journalistic awe at local resilience (ban it)

¶4. Formal Inconsistency Is Allowed. Tonal Incoherence Is Not.

  • Stories can be told via transcript, flashback, monologue, field report—but they must all feel like they happened in the same world.

  • Every piece of text should have some abrasion: an interruption, a contradiction, a missing piece.

  • Memory is glitchy. Transmission is noisy. That’s expected.

Anchor Tip:
If the story feels too clean, introduce distortion—a rumor, a miscommunication, a jammed signal.

¶SECTION IV: ETHICAL FRAMEWORK

This is a war story. It does not perform virtue. It enacts consequence.

¶1. No Act Is Neutral

  • Even boiling water might involve a bribe for fuel.

  • Treating a patient might mark the clinic for attack.

  • Translating a video might amplify propaganda.

  • Ignoring a radio call might save a life.

Writer’s Principle:
Every helpful act has a potential cost. Every omission has a rationale.

¶2. Morality Is Situational, But Not Relative

  • Characters have ethical boundaries. They just don’t agree on where they are.

  • We are not writing “good people in bad situations.” We’re writing compromised people maintaining function under pressure.

  • Characters should make decisions that feel logically inevitable, even if they’re ethically uncomfortable.

Examples:

  • Leyla turns away a regime soldier, knowing he’ll die—but so will the child next door if the clinic is raided

  • Amina lets someone steal antibiotics. Then closes the door.

¶3. No Torture Porn. No Hero Shots.

  • Violence is never aestheticized.

  • Pain is never righteous.

  • Victory is always partial, and always costs someone else.

¶4. Intimacy Exists. Romance Does Not Center.

  • Characters may love, lose, care deeply—but this is not a romance engine.

  • Sex may occur. Longing may emerge. But it must feel situated, not thematic.

  • Nobody is anyone’s reward. No story resolves in a kiss.

¶5. Testimony Is Not Truth.

  • Stories may contradict each other.

  • Witnesses may lie or forget.

  • Memory may serve ideology, guilt, grief, or survival.

Writer Technique:
Use contradiction not as “plot twist” but as moral geography.
Let two characters tell the same story differently—and let both feel right.

¶SECTION V: CORE CHARACTERS

These are the ones who keep the outpost breathing—even when it would be easier not to.

¶Leyla

Role: Clinic’s de facto lead
Age: Late 40s
Background: Former cosmetic dentist from Aleppo. Exiled after quietly treating protestors during the early uprising. She didn’t sign up for war medicine, but she became better at it than anyone.
Function: Moral anchor, not moral compass. Often makes impossible decisions and refuses to justify them.
Narrative Use:

  • Generates high-stakes conflict when logistics and ethics collide

  • Anchor character in stories involving patients, medical decisions, power shifts

  • Past life is full of unopened doors. Writers can glimpse, never explain
    Voice: Dry. Clipped. Wounded but unfazed. She makes jokes after the blood has stopped

¶Ciwan

Role: PYD liaison, political attaché
Age: 30s
Background: Former YPG fighter, now limping through “civil society work.” Wants to be a peacemaker but keeps acting like a militant.
Function: Bridge—and wedge—between the clinic and local governance. Brings ideology to the dinner table.
Narrative Use:

  • Political pressure valve in faction-heavy stories

  • Comic relief when ideology fails real-world logic

  • Tragic undertow when loyalty overtakes judgment
    Voice: Speaks like he’s writing a revolutionary pamphlet, but mumbles when he means it

¶Bissan

Role: Logistics / procurement / supply whisperer
Age: Late 30s
Background: Palestinian-Syrian. Former NGO logistics officer. Understands that every aid delivery is a negotiation with three devils.
Function: Interface between the clinic and the external world of borders, checkpoints, and donors
Narrative Use:

  • Supply chain-focused plots

  • Characters owe her everything and resent her for it

  • Occasionally breaks emotionally, but only when alone
    Voice: Terse, sharp, switches registers depending on who she’s speaking to. Excel sheet in human form

¶Haval

Role: Translator / media archivist / adolescent ghost
Age: 17
Background: Born in exile, returned with a laptop and revolutionary fire.
Function: Radio operator, OSINT scavenger, generational gap insert
Narrative Use:

  • Great POV for stories about propaganda, media manipulation, or coming-of-age in collapse

  • May contradict himself mid-narration—his unreliability is part of his honesty
    Voice: Hyperverbal, ironic, poetic when he forgets to perform

¶Amina

Role: Janitor / medic assistant / social oracle
Age: Late 50s
Background: Former math teacher from Manbij. Lost family, home, and purpose—so she made a new one at the clinic.
Function: Emotional memory of the space. Knows who came through, who left, who lied
Narrative Use:

  • Can hold a scene in silence

  • Appears in nearly every story; sometimes centrally, sometimes as atmosphere

  • When she speaks, it changes direction
    Voice: Sparse. Measured. Knows more than she lets on—and that’s saying very little

¶SECTION VI: RECURRING CHARACTERS

Their presence shifts the gravity in the room. Their absence is never total.

¶Uncle Salim

Role: Local civil authority / unofficial elder of Hasur
Age: 60s
Function: Stabilizing figure who trades favors instead of ideology
Narrative Use:

  • He’s a gatekeeper—of roads, rumors, and reputations

  • Appears when decisions ripple beyond the clinic

  • Represents pragmatic power: doesn’t care what you believe, only what you owe
    Voice: Polite, warm, terrifyingly indifferent to ideology

¶Samir

Role: SDF-affiliated fighter / semi-regular patient
Age: 20s
Function: A reminder that the clinic treats people who hurt others
Narrative Use:

  • Bridges front-line and backroom

  • Often injured, always talking, surprisingly observant

  • May say something profound, then undermine it with a joke about blood
    Voice: Casual, cocky, deeply afraid of silence

¶Dr. Shouq

Role: Visiting trauma surgeon / former regime doctor
Age: Late 30s
Function: Antagonist not through violence, but values
Narrative Use:

  • Appears in high-stakes medical episodes

  • Her past is a mirror to Leyla’s—but cracked

  • Makes readers uncomfortable because she might be right
    Voice: Icy, intellectual, cuts through hypocrisy like a scalpel

¶Julien

Role: French freelance journalist
Age: 30s
Function: Outsider POV and occasional wildcard
Narrative Use:

  • Provides contrast to local logic

  • Unwittingly causes problems by trying to “help”

  • Useful for stories about documentation, media ethics, or international gaze
    Voice: Earnest, oblivious, deeply well-meaning and extremely dangerous

¶Fatemeh

Role: Iranian NGO attaché / academic with opinions
Age: 40s
Function: Systems thinker. Sees the whole war as an equation in collapse
Narrative Use:

  • Challenges core characters with theory that actually makes sense

  • Often underestimates emotional cost of her insights
    Voice: Precise, devastating, sometimes too late to help

¶Rana

Role: Child soldier / Amina’s former student
Age: 13
Function: Haunting. Symbol of cycles. Never just a victim
Narrative Use:

  • Appears briefly, then reappears through radio chatter, memories, or objects

  • Every character who sees her sees something different
    Voice: Blunt. Smart. Unwilling to be pitied.

¶Nassif

Role: Leyla’s ex-husband / regime pharmacist
Age: 50s
Function: Supply node with emotional shrapnel
Narrative Use:

  • Creates tension in stories involving medicine, betrayal, or buried connections

  • Still loves Leyla, still opposes everything she does
    Voice: Polished, passive-aggressive, emotionally weaponized

These characters don’t all appear together—but each can anchor a story or create friction in one. Think of them as drifting satellites, pulling at different characters in different ways.

¶SECTION VII: FUNCTIONAL ROLES & SHIFT DYNAMICS

How the clinic runs—barely.

There is no official hierarchy. But there is a pecking order:

  • Medical leadership: Leyla (reluctant), with rotating support

  • Radio operations: Haval (primary), Rafiq (support)

  • Logistics / supplies: Bissan

  • Sanitation / prep / wound care: Amina

  • Security interface: Ciwan (when present)

Shifts happen based on:

  • Who is awake

  • Who isn’t injured

  • Who isn’t fighting with someone else

Common breakpoints:

  • Night shifts leave the radio unguarded

  • Leyla sometimes refuses to sleep

  • When Bissan leaves for supplies, everyone else panics

  • If Amina disappears for more than an hour, everyone notices—and nobody says anything

SECTION VIII: SETTING SPECIFICS

The Hala Basin: an invented geography with real-world physics, memory, and hazard.

¶1. HEVÎ MALA (THE CLINIC / “MARKAZ 9”)

Function: The narrative’s gravitational center. Surgery ward, radio tower, safehouse, and liability.

Physical Details:

  • Once a farmhouse—collapsed second story, fortified ground level

  • Reinforced with patchwork concrete, sandbags, two solar panels, and a repurposed telecom antenna

  • Rooms are loosely defined: triage space, sleeping corridor, supply storage (locked), stairwell radio loft, tea nook, Amina’s corner

Surrounding Terrain:

  • Nearby: dried canal, half-walled orchard, rusted grain silo used as a lookout

  • Ground: hard-packed dirt, unreliable in rain

  • Distance: 3 hours on foot to Hasur, 15 minutes by car to the border checkpoint if the road is open

Narrative Leverage:

  • Breached but never broken

  • Feels too exposed to last, too embedded to be removed

  • Every story either begins here, ends here, or spirals around its signal

¶2. HASUR (THE CITY)

Hybrid of Hasakah, Raqqa, Qamishli—fictional but rooted.

Function: Nearest major urban zone. Collapsed governance under reconstructed flags.

Key Zones:

  • Checkpoint 27 – Controlled by whoever’s got guns that week

  • Neutral Pharmacy – Allegedly unaffiliated supply point; full of spies

  • Exile Cinema – Once a cultural center, now meeting point, black market, and rumor node

  • The Water Office – bombed-out, but still contains maps, wires, and someone’s cousin’s nephew who knows how to fix valves

Narrative Leverage:

  • The place where characters compromise

  • Where someone’s past shows up with a new face

  • A source of both salvation and surveillance

¶3. ZINAR CANAL (WATERWAY / SMUGGLING ROUTE)

Function: Former irrigation infrastructure, now a covert artery

Details:

  • Concrete-lined canal, mostly dry

  • Covered in slogans, graffiti, bullet scars

  • Intersects Wadi Saraq—a flash-flood riverbed littered with bones and packaging

Uses:

  • USB sticks smuggled in water bottles

  • Patients transported under tarps

  • Reunions that shouldn’t happen

  • Locations for things that must disappear

Narrative Leverage:

  • A place for secrets, drop-offs, or ambushes

  • Metaphor-rich—everything intended to give life now channels death, signal, memory

¶4. AYNA BARA (REPOPULATED VILLAGE)

Function: Former Kurdish village now squatted by a mix of IDPs, fighters’ families, and orphans.

Key Features:

  • Central square repurposed into open-air clinic/school

  • Rooftop of old state radio tower now used for drone surveillance

  • Site of frequent blackouts and ambiguous territorial control

Narrative Leverage:

  • A place of fragile reordering

  • Potential home or battleground

  • Characters may have attachments or obligations here

¶5. SIFIR STATION (BORDER WALL SEGMENT)

Function: Turkish border outpost turned existential horizon

Details:

  • Concrete wall with rusting watchtowers, motion sensors, and floodlights

  • Nicknamed “Sifir” (Zero) because nothing crosses without cost

  • Site of failed crossings, buried hopes, drone trails

Narrative Leverage:

  • The wall isn’t just a boundary—it’s a mirror

  • Characters face decisions here that reset the trajectory of entire arcs

  • Ghosts, smugglers, exiles, and diplomats all leave something behind here

¶6. MAQAM AL-NAHR (SUFI SHRINE / STRATEGIC HILLTOP)

Function: Religious and spiritual node. Claimed by no faction, sacred to many.

Features:

  • Crumbling stone tomb, green flags, walls inscribed with poetry and bullet holes

  • Overlooks the basin, visible from the clinic on clear days

  • Superstition says broadcasting from here “echoes twice”

Narrative Leverage:

  • Emotional convergence point: grief, memory, confrontation

  • Refuge, ritual, negotiation site

  • Characters may come here to decide who they are—and leave changed

¶7. THE FLATLINE (DESERTED INDUSTRIAL ZONE)

Function: South of the basin. Decommissioned oil fields, signal blackout zone.

Terrain:

  • Cracked earth, flame stacks, open pits, broken infrastructure

  • Known for compass confusion, GPS dropout, and rumor-heavy reputations

Narrative Leverage:

  • Site for disappearances, secrets, dead drops

  • No signal reaches here—perfect for characters trying to vanish

  • Sometimes a place for a desperate cure. Sometimes a trap.

¶8. FIELD POINTS (Unnamed Locales)

Use these for episodic specificity:

  • A broken school bus where a child operates a checkpoint

  • A dry orchard full of buried VHS tapes

  • A metal shipping container turned maternity ward

  • A downed Turkish drone repurposed into a charger for the radio

Rule:
The setting is never just background. It’s tactile, tactical, testimonial. It reflects back the choices made inside it.

SECTION IX: CONTINUITY PROTOCOLS

This isn’t canon. It’s chorus.

¶1. Multiplicity Is Canon

Rule:
There is no single authoritative timeline or character arc. Characters may appear differently across stories—because memory, rumor, and perspective distort.

Implementation:

  • Writers are encouraged to contradict details from earlier stories, so long as the emotional truth of the character is honored.

  • For example:

    • One story may claim Rana was killed at a checkpoint.

    • Another may depict her in the present, older, telling stories herself.

    • Both are true within the logic of Hevî Mala.

Why:
Because the war is not a linear narrative. It is a collection of claims, testimonies, rumors, and recordings—some true, some false, all lived.

¶2. Anchor Elements Must Remain Stable

Certain features must remain fixed across all stories for coherence:

¶A. The Clinic

  • Always physically located outside Hasur

  • Always includes: triage room, radio loft, sleeping corridor, tea station

  • Always has a radio signal (even if jammed, corrupted, or ghosted)

  • Solar-powered, often failing

  • Surrounded by: orchard, canal, silo

¶B. The Core Characters

  • Names and baseline roles must remain recognizable

  • Writers may speculate on their pasts or project into alternate presents/futures

  • You may kill a core character in your story—but you must acknowledge that another writer may not

¶C. Locations and Terrain

  • The major locations of the Hala Basin (clinic, Hasur, Zinar Canal, Sifir Station, etc.) are shared, recurring spaces

  • You may add new locations, but they must not contradict the spatial logic (e.g. don’t place the sea 2km from the clinic)

¶3. Timestamping and Time Drift

No fixed calendar exists. Time is relative.

  • Writers may include soft time indicators like “after the last wheat drop” or “before the checkpoint massacre”

  • If multiple stories contradict the order of events, that’s allowed—but characters must feel time’s pressure

Time Loops Allowed:

  • You may re-tell the same incident from different POVs

  • You may echo lines, events, or sounds across stories

  • You may write a story where a character dreams—or hears—another story

¶4. Objects, Fragments, and Echoes

Objects persist.

  • A child’s scarf

  • A voice recording from a dead fighter

  • A broken stethoscope

  • A notebook of field translations

These may reappear across stories, passed between characters or misremembered.

Echo Fragments:

  • Writers are encouraged to “leak” pieces of one story into another

  • A phrase overheard, a name on a casualty list, a frequency mentioned

  • These are the threads that bind the mesh

¶5. Continuity Is Not Consistency

Continuity = internal resonance
Consistency = superficial sameness

We aim for the first. Not the second.

  • It’s okay if the orchard has ten trees in one story, twenty in another.

  • It’s okay if someone thinks they saw Amina die, and then she makes tea two stories later.

  • It’s okay if the same transmission is translated two ways.

This is not a logic error. It’s the world.

¶6. Story Submission Footers (Recommended)

At the end of each written story, writers may optionally submit a short footer note with metadata:

  • Primary Characters: (e.g. Bissan, Rana, Julien)

  • Timeframe Clues: (“After the wall was electrified”)

  • Tone Notes: (“Lyrical, bleak, grounded in memory”)

  • World Impact (if any): (“A new radio frequency is introduced”)

  • Continuity Cross-Reference (optional): (“References the field report from ‘Glass Orchard’”)

This is NOT required for reading—but it helps other writers situate themselves, remix themes, and layer meaning.

SECTION X: STORY ACTIVATION

How to begin. How to stay strange. How to stay true.

¶1. You Are Not Writing Episodes. You Are Tuning Signals.

Each story is not a “chapter” in a linear book. It is:

  • a dispatch

  • a memory

  • a wound report

  • a missed transmission

  • a rumor that became real the moment someone told it twice

Your job is not to “advance the plot.”
Your job is to render the rhythm of continuity under collapse.

¶2. Starting a New Story

¶You may begin with:

  • A location

    • e.g. Checkpoint 27 at dusk

  • A character prompt

    • e.g. Ciwan trying to get a fax machine working again

  • A sensory anchor

    • e.g. The smell of burned grain

  • A lost or found object

    • e.g. A USB full of music from before the war

  • A transmission artifact

    • e.g. A transcript with missing lines

  • A contradiction

    • e.g. Three people swear they saw Amina in three different places at once

¶3. Writing Prompts (Modular, Optional)

These can be used to kick off new entries or for workshops among contributors:

  • “Someone shows up with a body they want treated. They won’t say who it is.”

  • “The clinic receives a broadcast from a frequency they thought was dead.”

  • “Someone leaves without telling anyone. Only a radio signal remains.”

  • “Amina speaks first.”

  • “A fight breaks out over a bag of medicine labeled in Cyrillic.”

  • “Leyla tells the truth, once, but no one hears her.”

  • “A child draws a map. It’s wrong, but something about it is right.”

  • “Fatemeh’s grant report is discovered and does not match reality.”

  • “The water tastes like metal this week.”

  • “Someone finds a bloodstained field manual. It has recipes in the margins.”

¶4. Story Length, Format & Naming

  • Length: Stories may be flash (~500 words), mid (~1.5k–3k), or long (~5k max)

  • Format: Any prose form is valid (see Section II)—letters, transcripts, field diaries, narrative fiction, poetic radio loops

  • Naming Convention:

    • Single-word titles, radio-style tags, or operational nicknames encouraged

    • Examples: Dustline, Maqam Echo, Sifir Protocol, Rinse Cycle, Blue Tape, Auntie’s Hour

¶5. Recurrence and Resonance

Any writer can:

  • Reuse any shared character

  • Reference any shared location

  • Call back to any event—but must be comfortable if other writers contradict or destabilize it

Every story lives in relation to the others, but not in obedience to them.

¶6. Feedback Loops (Optional)

  • Writers are encouraged to leave resonance tags in the margin or comments:

    • Echoes Haval’s rant in “Glass Orchard”

    • Contradicts Rana’s timeline from “Sifir Protocol”

    • Radio frequency [98.3] now repeats this phrase

  • Editors/curators may rotate recurring prompts based on emerging threads:

    • What happened at the Maqam during the drone blackout?

    • Who left the stethoscope in the canal?

    • Why is the orchard louder this month?

¶7. Final Sanity Checks for Submission

Before finalizing a story, consider:

  • Does it violate tone charter? (see Section III)

  • Does it accidentally contradict anchor elements? (see Section IX)

  • Does it create friction, ambiguity, or thematic charge? (Yes? Good.)

  • Would a reader in this world believe it happened? (That’s the bar.)

All information can only be grounded in other information. Nothing here or anywhere should be understood as True, or False.
