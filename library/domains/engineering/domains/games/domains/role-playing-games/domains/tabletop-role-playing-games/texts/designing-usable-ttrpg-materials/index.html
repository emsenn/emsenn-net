Designing Usable TTRPG Materials

Audience: game masters and indie designers who already prepare table materials and want to make those materials clearer, lighter, and more playable.

Learning goal: identify the design principles that make tabletop materials usable in live play, and apply those principles to scenarios, handouts, maps, and reference sheets.

Prerequisites: you should understand Creating Table Materials, because this lesson focuses on improving materials you are already making.

¶Starting from a practical contrast

A GM brings two different clue letters to the table.

The first is a full page of decorative prose. It looks impressive, but the players spend two minutes reading it silently, ask what mattered, and then hand it back.

The second is half a page. It has three named places, one date, one warning, and a seal sketched in the corner. The players circle the date, compare the place names to their map, and immediately change their plan.

Both are handouts. Only one is doing table work.

¶The central rule: every material should do a job

A strong TTRPG material is not merely atmospheric. It performs a specific function during play. Nick Webber’s work on the material life of role-playing artifacts is useful here: objects such as character sheets persist because they hold memory and support action across sessions. The same principle applies to handouts, maps, and reference sheets. If an object does not help the table remember, decide, orient, or resolve, it is probably excess.

Before you make any artifact, ask one question: what decision or memory problem does this solve at the table? If the answer is vague, the material usually needs to be cut or simplified.

¶One artifact, one primary task

Usable materials tend to have a single main job.

A scenario sheet tells the GM what is in motion. A map helps the table reason spatially. A handout preserves fictional information for reuse. A reference sheet accelerates procedure.

Problems start when one page tries to do all four. A crowded page with room descriptions, rumors, NPC motives, boxed text, and combat stats may contain valuable information, but it is hard to use under live pressure. Separating artifacts by task usually produces better play even when the total amount of information stays the same.

¶Design for scanning, not reading

Table materials are usually consulted in bursts of a few seconds. That makes scanability more important than literary smoothness. Professional and indie prep traditions converge on this point. Justin Alexander’s scenario-design writing and Mike Shea’s Lazy GM workflow both rely on short, retrievable notes rather than long prose blocks.

In practice, scanable materials use:

  • short headings
  • lists instead of dense paragraphs
  • bolding or spacing for key items
  • names and clues that stand out visually
  • enough white space that the eye can land quickly

This is not anti-aesthetic. It is a recognition that table documents are tools first.

¶Material should invite annotation

The most useful table materials are not static. Players mark maps. GMs cross out clocks, circle names, and add arrows between factions. Handouts gain meaning when people write on them.

This is one reason rough maps and concise sheets often outperform polished layouts. A material that leaves room for use tends to stay active longer. A page that looks finished in a publishing sense can be awkward in a play sense if no one wants to touch it.

¶Maps should support choices, not just depict places

A map becomes good tabletop material when it helps the group reason about movement, access, danger, and relation. Academic work on role-playing cartography and the long-running practice of indie mapmakers such as Dyson Logos point in the same direction: the map is not just illustration. It is an action surface.

For that reason, useful maps usually foreground traversable structure: entrances, chokepoints, elevations, routes, distances, sightlines, and hidden connections. Decorative texture can help mood, but only after the decision-relevant structure is clear.

¶Handouts should be actionable

A good handout changes what players can do. A rumor sheet creates leads. A letter creates suspicion. A wanted poster introduces a face and a faction. A route note changes travel planning.

This is a stronger standard than “immersive.” Many immersive handouts are inert. If players can appreciate the artifact without needing to act on it, it may be flavor rather than support material. Flavor is fine, but it should not displace the more useful object.

¶Reference sheets should be brutally selective

A reference sheet fails when it becomes a miniature rulebook. Its job is to remove friction from recurring procedures, not to summarize everything the game contains.

The best reference sheets therefore omit aggressively. Keep the table-facing loop: action order, resolution steps, result bands, common costs, status reminders, and anything the group repeatedly forgets. If a rule appears once every three sessions, it usually does not belong on the quick reference.

¶A practical test for material quality

After a session, look at what the table actually used.

  • Which page did people keep pointing at?
  • Which handout was reread?
  • Which note never left your stack?
  • Which rule had to be restated even though you prepared a sheet for it?

That evidence is more valuable than abstract design taste. Good TTRPG materials reveal themselves through reuse.

¶Worked example: upgrading a prep packet

Suppose a GM has prepared these materials for a ruined watchtower session:

  • a two-page setting summary
  • six paragraphs of NPC backstory
  • a fully rendered map without labels
  • a one-page clue letter in dense prose
  • no rules summary

A more usable version would be:

  • a half-page scenario sheet with pressures, locations, and open questions
  • a three-line NPC roster with motives and tells
  • a labeled sketch map with entrances, routes, and hazards
  • a half-page clue handout with names, dates, and one warning players can act on
  • a one-card reference sheet for resolution and consequences

The second packet is less impressive as a reading experience and more effective as play infrastructure.

¶Exercises

  1. Take one handout you have already made and cut it to half its length. What becomes clearer?

  2. Build a one-page reference sheet for Brave Old World that includes only the procedures players actually need during a first session.
