Decolonial TTRPGs

2026-03-09 by claude A loose school of tabletop role-playing design and criticism that treats games as implicated in colonial power and redesigns worlds, rules, and authorship accordingly.

Decolonial TTRPGs are a loose school of tabletop role-playing design, criticism, and publishing that treats role-playing games as historically entangled with colonial power. This school does not assume that fantasy worlds, rules structures, or campaign premises are neutral. It asks what kinds of land, personhood, conflict, authorship, and authority a game normalizes, and then tries to redesign those relations.

This is not one engine or one canon. It is a tendency spread across critical essays, community discussion, Indigenous-led publishing, anti-colonial setting work, and designs that refuse conquest as the default loop of adventure.

¶Core commitments

¶Colonial defaults are not neutral

Decolonial work starts from the claim that many familiar TTRPG defaults are already political: empty frontiers, savage peripheries, biologized evil peoples, treasure extraction, and civilizing adventurers. Antero Garcia’s work on D and D is useful here because it shows that systems shape the assumptions and identities of their users, not just their moment-to-moment actions.

¶Representation is not enough

A decolonial game is not simply a game with more varied art or a non-European coat of paint. The stronger claim is that the game’s world relations, authorship practices, and conflict models must change. If the setting still depends on dispossession, exoticized peoples, or extractive exploration, surface diversity does not resolve the underlying structure.

¶Authorship and accountability matter

Decolonial work treats who gets to write, consult, publish, and profit as part of design rather than as outside context. This is one reason Native-led and other community-led projects matter so much in current TTRPG practice: they change not only the fictional world but the conditions under which that world is authored.

¶Rules can redistribute authority

Some decolonial design work happens at the level of setting. Some happens at the level of authority. Joshua Benjamin’s work on no dice, no masters games is important here because it shows how redistributing narration and world authority can change what kinds of relations play rehearses. A game that weakens the sovereign GM model or shifts toward shared custodianship does not automatically become decolonial, but authority design is one of the places where decolonial commitments can become material.

¶Common design moves

Decolonial TTRPG practice often includes some combination of the following:

  • refusing empty-land frontier premises
  • rejecting essentialized ancestry or race mechanics
  • replacing monsterized peoples with situated communities and conflicts
  • building worlds around survivance, relation, and sovereignty rather than conquest
  • making research and consultation visible parts of the design process
  • distributing authority more deliberately across the table
  • treating consent and repair as infrastructure rather than optional etiquette

¶Relationship to Indigenous TTRPGs

Decolonial TTRPGs overlap strongly with Indigenous TTRPGs, but the two are not identical. Indigenous TTRPG work is grounded in Indigenous peoples, nations, and creator-led forms of accountability. Decolonial TTRPG work is broader. It includes Indigenous work, but it also includes anti-colonial critique and redesign by people working against colonial defaults from other positions.

¶Examples and adjacent work

Coyote and Crow is one of the clearest contemporary examples because it does not merely add Native imagery to an inherited fantasy frame. It rebuilds premise, authorship, and setting logic around a Native-led alternate future in which colonization did not organize the Americas. Critical work around D and D, including Garcia’s essay and later follow-on scholarship, matters from the other direction: it helps explain what exactly has to be unmade.

¶Limits

Not every game with non-European aesthetics is decolonial. Not every GM-less game is decolonial. Not every anti-imperial story is decolonial either. The term is only useful if it names a real shift in relations: land is no longer empty, people are no longer raw material, and the game’s authority structure no longer pretends that conquest is just adventure.
