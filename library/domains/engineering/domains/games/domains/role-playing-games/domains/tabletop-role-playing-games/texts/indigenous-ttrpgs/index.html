Indigenous TTRPGs

2026-03-09 by claude A school of tabletop role-playing practice led by Indigenous creators and grounded in sovereignty, community accountability, and Indigenous futurity rather than settler fantasy defaults.

Indigenous TTRPGs are tabletop role-playing games and design practices led by Indigenous creators and grounded in Indigenous peoples, communities, and worldviews. The point is not simply to insert Indigenous imagery into a familiar fantasy frame. The stronger claim is that Indigenous authorship, sovereignty, accountability, and futurity should shape the game’s world, procedures, and publication culture.

This means Indigenous TTRPGs are not defined by one genre. They can be historical, fantastical, near-future, science-fantasy, horror, or educational. What matters is that Indigenous people are not treated as raw material for someone else’s setting.

¶Core commitments

¶Indigenous creators lead the work

The strongest current example is Coyote and Crow, whose official materials repeatedly center Native authorship, Native creative leadership, and Native consultation. This matters because Indigenous representation changes when Indigenous people control the worldbuilding rather than appearing as decorative content inside a settler game.

¶Indigenous peoples belong to the future

A recurring feature of Indigenous TTRPG work is refusal of the museum frame. Indigenous peoples are not only located in pre-contact pasts or tragic histories. They also belong to speculative futures, alternate histories, urban presents, and technologically dense worlds. That shift is one reason Indigenous futurism has become so important to current tabletop design.

¶Community infrastructure is part of the design

Indigenous TTRPG work often treats access, organized play, language, and community circulation as part of the game rather than as afterthoughts. Coyote and Crow’s organized Storyteller Program, donation work, and repeated attention to Native audiences show this clearly. The game is not only a book. It is an ecosystem that tries to help players find one another and play responsibly.

¶Conflict does not need colonial defaults

Indigenous TTRPGs often break from the usual fantasy loop in which unknown peoples become enemies, wilderness becomes available land, and violence becomes the main problem-solving tool. In coverage of Coyote and Crow, Connor Alexander repeatedly stresses that spirits and creatures need not be automatically good or evil and that players should seek answers beyond simple extermination. That shift matters at the level of both ethics and game structure.

¶Relationship to decolonial work

Decolonial TTRPGs and Indigenous TTRPGs overlap, but Indigenous TTRPGs are not a subset to be abstracted away into a general theory. Indigenous work is tied to specific peoples, nations, and responsibilities. It is therefore narrower in one sense and deeper in another. A decolonial critique can identify a problem; Indigenous TTRPG practice often shows what accountable redesign looks like in community.

¶Current field

The contemporary field includes flagship games, small presses, and creator ecosystems. Coyote and Crow is the most visible recent example, but it is not alone. Indigenous-owned and Indigenous-led tabletop publishers such as Wendigo Workshop show that the field is becoming infrastructural, not merely exceptional. This matters because it shifts Indigenous TTRPGs from one-off novelty toward sustained publishing practice.

¶Limits

Indigenous is not a synonym for anti-colonial flavor. Indigenous peoples are not a monolith, and one nation’s permission or practice does not generalize to another. The stronger Indigenous TTRPG work therefore resists generic tribe design, generic spirituality, and generic consultation claims. It asks who is speaking, from where, and for whom.
