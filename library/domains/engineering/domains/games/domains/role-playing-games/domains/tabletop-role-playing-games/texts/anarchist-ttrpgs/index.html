Anarchist TTRPGs

2026-03-09 by claude A loose school of tabletop role-playing that emphasizes anti-authoritarian authority structures, mutual aid, and prefigurative forms of play.

Anarchist TTRPGs are a loose school of tabletop role-playing design that treats play as a place to rehearse anti-authoritarian social relations. These games often distrust centralized mastery, distribute authority across the table, and center community survival, mutual aid, or shared responsibility over heroic command.

This is not a single lineage. Some anarchist TTRPGs are explicit in name and politics. Others are better described as anti-authoritarian or prefigurative: they do not announce anarchism loudly, but they redesign play around many of the same commitments.

¶Core commitments

¶Authority should be distributed deliberately

One of the clearest anarchist design moves in TTRPGs is refusal of a single sovereign controller. Avery Alder’s Belonging Outside Belonging engine makes this explicit: no dice, no masters, distributed control over setting elements, and collaborative worldbuilding through a community worksheet. Joshua Benjamin’s thesis is useful because it explains how these structures change the rhetoric of play rather than merely adding variety.

¶Community matters more than command

Anarchist TTRPGs often care less about mission execution and more about what it takes to keep a vulnerable community alive. Dream Askew is a strong example. Its question is not how a party clears a dungeon under a GM’s command. Its question is how a queer enclave survives, negotiates scarcity, and builds forms of care amid collapse.

¶Procedure can prefigure politics

These games are often prefigurative. The social relations of play are part of the point. If the game imagines a freer, less coercive world, it often tries to organize the table in a freer, less coercive way too. That does not remove conflict. It changes where conflict lives: in scarcity, desire, competing needs, and community tension rather than in obedience to an external master.

¶Publishing culture matters too

Anarchist tendencies also appear in circulation and licensing practices. Some indie creators pair anti-authoritarian design with open or anti-capitalist sharing norms, community copies, sliding-scale access, or explicit rejection of dominant platform ethics. This publishing layer does not by itself make a game anarchist, but it often travels with anarchist design commitments.

¶Common design patterns

Anarchist TTRPGs often include some combination of the following:

  • shared or rotating scene authority
  • GM-less or GM-light procedures
  • community worksheets or mapmaking as co-governance tools
  • safety and consent procedures built into setup
  • economies of scarcity, care, and obligation rather than loot accumulation
  • settings focused on enclaves, collectives, fugitives, rebels, or precarious communities

¶Relationship to decolonial and Indigenous work

Anarchist TTRPGs overlap with Decolonial TTRPGs and Indigenous TTRPGs where they challenge domination, militarized adventure, and sovereign mastery. But the traditions are not the same. A GM-less game can still ignore colonial power. An anti-authoritarian mechanic does not automatically produce accountable worldbuilding. The strongest anarchist TTRPG practice recognizes that hierarchy is racialized and colonial as well as procedural.

¶Examples

The Belonging Outside Belonging family is the clearest contemporary cluster. Dream Askew centers queer community in apocalypse. Dream Apart centers Jewish communal life under threat. Both distribute authority and focus on belonging, precarity, and self-organization. Zephyr: An Anarchist Game of Fleeting Identities is notable as an explicitly anarchist recent title rather than an adjacent one.

¶Limits

Not every collaborative game is anarchist. Not every game about rebels is anarchist. The term is useful only when a game’s politics and procedures converge: authority is genuinely redistributed, coercion is not naturalized, and collective life becomes more important than mastery.
