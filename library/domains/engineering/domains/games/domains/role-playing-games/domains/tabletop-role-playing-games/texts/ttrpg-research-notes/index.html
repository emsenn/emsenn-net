This text records the source base behind the current tabletop role-playing games module so future revisions can start from a stable corpus instead of repeating web searches. It is a working research map, not a finished essay.

¶Foundational academic sources

These sources are strong starting points for almost any TTRPG page in this module.

  • Gary Alan Fine, Shared Fantasy: Role Playing Games as Social Worlds. Useful for the social-world account of role-playing and for understanding table norms as institutions. https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/S/bo5949823.html
  • Jennifer Grouling Cover, Tabletop Role-Playing Games: Perspectives from Narrative, Game, and Rhetorical Theory. Useful for medium distinction, rhetoric, and the difference between tabletop and software-limited RPGs. https://repository.lib.ncsu.edu/items/32caa2a7-55fa-496f-b9e4-8c8a252f03c2
  • Jose P. Zagal and Sebastian Deterding, eds., The Routledge Handbook of Role-Playing Game Studies. Best broad academic anchor for the field as of 2024. https://www.routledge.com/The-Routledge-Handbook-of-Role-Playing-Game-Studies/Zagal-Deterding/p/book/9781032277783
  • Analog Game Studies, “Analog Game Studies 101.” Strong open bibliography for finding adjacent scholarship fast. https://analoggamestudies.org/about/analog-game-studies-101/
  • Premeet Sidhu, Marcus Carter, and Jose P. Zagal, eds., Fifty Years of Dungeons & Dragons. Useful when the module needs to address D and D critically rather than treating it as the default form. https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262547604/fifty-years-of-dungeons-and-dragons/

¶Materials and artifact design

These sources are the best fit for the module’s current work on scenario, handout, reference sheet, and campaign documents.

  • Nick Webber, “Table Talk: Archives of Role-Playing’s Personal Pasts.” Best source here for why sheets, notes, and other artifacts matter as durable play infrastructure. https://analoggamestudies.org/2019/12/archives-of-role-playings-personal-pasts/
  • Nick Webber, “Player Cartography in Mega-Dungeons.” Useful for maps as play instruments rather than illustrations. https://analoggamestudies.org/2019/04/player-cartography-in-mega-dungeons/
  • Pascal Martinolli, “A Scholarly Character Sheet to Frame Learning Activities and Improve Engagement.” Useful for sheet design, cognitive load, and information framing. https://journals.uu.se/IJRP/article/view/406
  • Justin Alexander, “Don’t Prep Plots.” Core source for situation-first prep. https://thealexandrian.net/wordpress/4147/roleplaying-games/dont-prep-plots
  • Justin Alexander, “Node-Based Scenario Design.” Best fit for scenario packets that need multiple paths without railroading. https://thealexandrian.net/wordpress/799/roleplaying-games/node-based-scenario-design-part-1-the-plotted-approach
  • Michael E. Shea, Return of the Lazy Dungeon Master resources. Strong on short prep packets and usable notes. https://slyflourish.com/returnofthelazydm/index.html
  • Dyson Logos map archive. Useful exemplar for clear, playable maps. https://dysonlogos.blog/maps/
  • Trilemma Adventures blog. Excellent compact scenario and layout exemplars. https://blog.trilemma.com/

¶Campaign and world design

These sources support campaign frame, faction, and the module’s world-design lessons.

  • Michael E. Shea, “Spiral Campaign and World Building.” Strong argument for starting region first. https://slyflourish.com/spiral_campaign_building.html
  • Michael E. Shea, “Lazy World Building.” Useful corrective against encyclopedic worldbuilding. https://slyflourish.com/lazy_world_building.html
  • Michael E. Shea, “Build a Campaign-Unique Faction List.” Direct support for faction sheets and campaign motion. https://slyflourish.com/build_a_faction_list.html
  • Michael E. Shea, “Organizing Your RPG Prep Notes.” Helpful for continuity materials and prep archives. https://slyflourish.com/organizing_notes.html
  • Tim Denee and Gerald Farca, “The Cultural Languages of Role-Playing Games.” Useful for thinking about worldbuilding as a sign system rather than lore dump. https://researchprofiles.herts.ac.uk/en/publications/the-cultural-languages-of-role-playing-games
  • Chris McDowall, Bastionland articles and campaign practice. Strong on sparse but playable worlds. https://www.bastionland.com/
  • Kevin Crawford and Sine Nomine publishing practice. Strong exemplar for faction-forward sandbox materials. https://www.drivethrurpg.com/en/publisher/3482/sine-nomine-publishing

¶Open, FOSS, and open-license ecosystems

These are useful whenever the module needs examples of open publishing, reusable SRDs, or community-built tools.

  • Cairn SRD. Open fantasy rules text with a strong third-party culture. https://cairnrpg.com/first-edition/cairn-srd/
  • Cairn third-party content policy. Useful model for open-compatible ecosystem writing. https://cairnrpg.com/adventures/third-party-content/
  • OpenQuest SRD. Open rules text and explicit licensing. https://openquestrpg.com/srd/
  • OpenQuest licensing page. Useful for source and reuse conditions. https://openquestrpg.com/srd/licensing/
  • Black Flag Reference Document. Major open fantasy reference under newer open-license conditions. https://bfrd.net/
  • Open5e. Useful both as a reference site and as infrastructure example. https://open5e.com/
  • Open5e GitHub repository. Useful when this module discusses openly maintained rules infrastructure. https://github.com/open5e/open5e
  • Foundry World and Campaign Builder. Open campaign-planning tool with direct relevance to prep structure. https://dovrosenberg.github.io/fvtt-campaign-builder/
  • ORC License official page. Useful when discussing post-OGL open publishing ecosystems. https://paizo.com/orclicense
  • ORC Answers and Explanations. Useful for the interpretive layer around ORC. https://azoralaw.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/ORC_AxE_FINAL.pdf

¶Decolonial, Indigenous, and anarchist lines of work

These sources matter because they correct the module’s earlier tendency to treat fantasy worldbuilding, faction design, and authored materials as politically neutral.

¶Decolonial TTRPG sources

  • Antero Garcia, “Privilege, Power, and Dungeons & Dragons: How Systems Shape Racial and Gender Identities in Tabletop Role-Playing Games.” Strong academic source on how systems encode social assumptions. https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10749039.2017.1293691
  • Reagan Yessler and Bethany Craig, “Dungeons and Dragons: Gender, Race, and Power in the Fantasy and Storytelling Space.” Recent follow-on work on how fantasy structure carries social power. https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/2373566X.2024.2352528
  • Joshua Benjamin, Changing the Game: The Rhetorical Approach of No Dice, No Masters Tabletop RPGs. Useful for authority redistribution, marginalized communities, and non-mastered play. https://libres.uncg.edu/ir/uncg/f/Benjamin_uncg_0154N_13801.pdf
  • Andrew M. Hales, Roll for Perception: The Topical Taboos and Critical Prosocial Educational Potential of Tabletop Role-Playing Games. Useful for the recent decolonial and classroom-critical framing of TTRPGs. https://repository.uwtsd.ac.uk/id/eprint/1755/
  • Decolonial TTRPGs. Local synthesis page for this line of work.

¶Indigenous TTRPG sources

  • Coyote and Crow Games, About page. Strong official statement on Native-led authorship and mission. https://coyoteandcrow.net/about/
  • Coyote and Crow Games, “The World Building of Coyote and Crow.” Useful on setting scope, alternate history, and authorship boundaries. https://coyoteandcrow.net/2021/09/08/the-world-building-of-coyote-crow/
  • Coyote and Crow Games, Storyteller Program. Useful on organized play, accessibility, and running the game responsibly. https://coyoteandcrow.net/storyteller-program/
  • Kashton MacLeod, “Coyote and Crow: An Indigenous twist on tabletop role-playing games.” Useful journalistic source on design intent and decolonized setting practice. https://www.aptnnews.ca/national-news/coyote-crow-an-indigenous-twist-on-tabletop-role-playing-games/
  • Chez Oxendine, “Indigenous tabletop roleplaying game breaks $750K in funding on Kickstarter.” Useful on community demand, language, and donation infrastructure. https://tribalbusinessnews.com/sections/entrepreneurism/13440-indigenous-tabletop-roleplaying-game-breaks-750k-in-funding-on-kickstarter
  • Wendigo Workshop itch profile. Useful for tracking an Indigenous-owned contemporary TTRPG publisher. https://wendigoworkshop.itch.io/
  • Indigenous TTRPGs. Local synthesis page for this line of work.

¶Anarchist TTRPG sources

  • Buried Without Ceremony, “Belonging Outside Belonging.” Best official summary of the engine’s no dice, no masters structure. https://buriedwithoutceremony.com/belonging
  • Buried Without Ceremony, “Dream Askew.” Strong official source for queer enclave play, distributed authority, and community survival. https://buriedwithoutceremony.com/dream-askew
  • Buried Without Ceremony, “Dream Apart.” Strong official source for marginalized community play and collaborative governance. https://buriedwithoutceremony.com/dream-apart
  • Buried Without Ceremony, Dream Askew press release. Useful on beginner accessibility and collaboration. https://buriedwithoutceremony.com/dream-askew-press-release
  • Araukana Media, Zephyr: An Anarchist Game of Fleeting Identities. Direct example of an explicitly anarchist TTRPG. https://araukana-media.itch.io/zephyr-roleplaying-game
  • TheGiftOfGabes itch profile. Useful for anti-capitalist licensing and creator-resource ecosystem work. https://thegiftofgabes.itch.io/

¶Module correction notes

These are the main corrections this source base suggests for existing TTRPG pages.

  • Do not treat “frontier,” “wilderness,” or “empty land” as neutral campaign assumptions.
  • Do not treat fantasy peoples as naturally evil, naturally primitive, or naturally available for extraction.
  • Do not frame authored materials as merely technical. Maps, handouts, faction lists, and reference sheets all carry world assumptions.
  • Distinguish GM authority from human adjudication. GM-less and distributed-authority games are not edge cases to ignore.
  • Distinguish decolonial and Indigenous work. They overlap, but they are not interchangeable.
  • Treat organized play, donations, accessibility, and language infrastructure as part of TTRPG worldbuilding ecosystems, not just marketing.
  • Treat open SRDs, open licenses, and community tools as real parts of the contemporary TTRPG field.
