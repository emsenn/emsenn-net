Dice and Resolution

Audience: players and GMs who want to understand how dice mechanics translate fictional uncertainty into concrete outcomes.

Learning goal: explain how different resolution systems work, what modifiers represent, and how the choice of dice mechanic shapes the feel of play.

Prerequisites: you should understand session structure and the scene-choice-consequence loop, because dice resolution is the mechanical layer that connects choices to consequences.

Starting from a concrete example

An adventurer in Brave Old World wants to climb a crumbling wall to reach a window on the second floor. The narrator says this is risky — the wall is unstable, and falling would hurt. The adventurer rolls two six-sided dice and adds the results: 5 + 3 = 8. In Brave Old World, 7–9 means success with a cost. The narrator says: “You reach the window, but a section of wall collapses behind you. You can’t go back the way you came.”

This moment captures the full resolution sequence: a fictional action with uncertain outcome, a mechanical procedure (roll 2d6, check the result band), and a narrated consequence that changes the fiction. The dice did not decide what happened — the narrator did. The dice decided how well it went.

How resolution mechanics work

All TTRPG resolution systems share a common function: they translate a fictional intention with uncertain outcome into one of a few possible results, typically along a spectrum from full success to full failure. The specific implementation varies, but three patterns cover most systems.

Roll against a target number. The player rolls dice and adds a modifier representing their character’s relevant ability. If the total meets or exceeds a target number (set by the GM based on difficulty), the action succeeds. This is the most common pattern — Dungeons & Dragons, Pathfinder, and many other systems use it. The target number encodes the GM’s judgment about how hard the task is; the modifier encodes how capable the character is.

Roll and interpret result bands. The player rolls dice and the total falls into one of several predefined bands, each with a different narrative meaning. Brave Old World uses this: 10+ is clean success, 7–9 is success with a cost, 6 or less is failure with consequences. Powered by the Apocalypse games use the same bands. This pattern puts less emphasis on difficulty calibration and more emphasis on the quality of the outcome — not just pass/fail, but how the fiction changes.

Opposed rolls. Both the acting player and the opposing force (another player, or the GM for a non-player character) roll dice. The higher result wins, sometimes with a margin that determines how decisively. This pattern is common in competitive situations — combat, contests, social conflicts — where both sides have agency.

What modifiers represent

A modifier is a number added to (or subtracted from) a die roll to represent factors that make success more or less likely. In Brave Old World, a character with investigation +1 adds 1 to their roll when investigating — the modifier represents the character’s skill. A character with swimming −1 subtracts 1 when swimming — the modifier represents a weakness.

Modifiers come from a few sources across different systems:

  • Character ability (attributes, skills, training)
  • Situational factors (darkness, time pressure, surprise, advantageous position)
  • Equipment (tools, weapons, magical aids)
  • Narrative circumstances (an NPC who owes the character a favor, a plan set beforehand)

Modifiers are the bridge between the fiction and the mechanics. They translate “my character is good at this” or “the situation is favorable” into a concrete mechanical advantage. When a game master assigns modifiers, they are making a judgment about the fictional circumstances — and that judgment is subject to the group’s social negotiation, especially in games like Brave Old World where skills are set by consensus.

Worked example: tracing a full resolution in Brave Old World

The adventurer Mira (from Character Creation) wants to decipher inscriptions on a ruin wall. She has investigation +1.

  1. Fictional action: “I examine the inscriptions carefully, comparing them to the maps I’ve drawn of similar markings.”
  2. GM judgment: The narrator decides this is risky — the inscriptions might be misleading, and time spent here means the bandits get closer.
  3. Roll: 2d6 + 1 (investigation). Mira’s player rolls a 4 and a 5: total 9 + 1 = 10.
  4. Result band: 10+ is clean success.
  5. Narrated consequence: “The inscriptions are a map. They show a passage through the mountains that matches nothing on your charts. You sketch it quickly — it’s clear enough to follow.”

If Mira had rolled a 7 (total 8 with modifier): success with a cost. Maybe she deciphers the map but the bandits arrive before she finishes, forcing the party to flee with incomplete information. If she had rolled a 3 (total 4 with modifier): failure. Maybe the inscriptions are damaged beyond reading, or she misinterprets them and the party follows a path into danger.

Each result changes the fiction differently, and the narrator’s job is to make each consequence worth playing out — not just “you fail” but “you fail and something happens that creates a new situation.”

The feel of different dice systems

The choice of dice shapes the texture of play in ways that go beyond probability.

Rolling a single d20 (twenty-sided die) produces a flat probability distribution: every number from 1 to 20 is equally likely. This means extreme results happen often — heroes fail easy tasks 5% of the time and succeed at hard ones 5% of the time. The experience is swingy.

Rolling 2d6 (as in Brave Old World) produces a bell curve centered on 7. Results near 7 are common; extremes (2 or 12) are rare. This means skilled characters fail less often, and the “success with a cost” band (7–9) is the most common result. The experience emphasizes complications over clean outcomes.

Rolling dice pools (multiple dice, counting successes) produces results where competence shows as consistency — a character with more dice rolls more reliably, not just higher. The experience emphasizes reliability as a form of skill.

None of these is better. They produce different emotional textures, and choosing a dice mechanic is a design decision about what kind of stories the game wants to tell.

Exercises

  1. An adventurer in Brave Old World has covert +2 and attempts to sneak past sleeping guards. Roll 2d6 (or imagine the result). For each of the three result bands (10+, 7–9, 6−), write one sentence describing what happens. Make each consequence change the fiction in a way that creates a new scene.

  2. Compare a flat d20 roll to a 2d6 bell curve. If a task requires a total of 8 or higher, what is the approximate probability of success on each? What does this difference mean for how reliable skilled characters feel in play?
