Audience: groups preparing to start a new campaign or one-shot.

Learning goal: describe the purpose and content of a session zero, and outline how a GM prepares for the first session of play.

Prerequisites: you should understand dice and resolution mechanics and safety and consent practices, because session zero is where the group sets both up.

Starting from a concrete example

Four people want to play Brave Old World together. None of them has played with this exact group before. Before the first actual session of play, they meet for an hour and a half. The narrator asks:

  • “What kind of stories are we interested in? Exploration? Political intrigue? Survival? Mystery?”
  • “Are there topics anyone wants to keep out of the game entirely?”
  • “Let me explain how Brave Old World works - it is simple, but let us make sure everyone is comfortable with the dice and the consensus rule.”
  • “Let us make characters together. Who is your adventurer, and why are they with the party?”

By the end of this meeting, the group has agreed on a tone (low-fantasy exploration with mystery elements), established lines and veils (no graphic violence against animals; torture is a veil), created four characters who have reasons to travel together, and confirmed logistics (every other Saturday, three hours, at the narrator’s house). No scenes were played. No dice were rolled. But the foundation for good play was laid.

This meeting is a session zero.

What session zero accomplishes

Session zero is a pre-play session dedicated to setting up the social and fictional agreements that underpin a campaign. It serves a few functions that are hard to do during play itself:

Tone and genre alignment. Different players may have very different expectations for the same game. One player imagines gritty survival; another imagines comic adventure. If the group does not surface these expectations before play begins, the group discovers the conflict during play - usually at the worst possible moment. Session zero makes implicit expectations explicit.

Safety agreements. Lines, veils, and the group’s chosen safety tools (X-Card, Open Door, Stars and Wishes) are set up here. This is more effective than introducing safety tools mid-session because it normalizes them as part of the game’s setup rather than as emergency interventions.

Collaborative character creation. When players create characters together rather than in isolation, they can build connections between characters, avoid redundancy, and make sure that everyone’s character fits the agreed tone. The result is a party that has reasons to cooperate and a shared history that can drive early scenes.

Rules orientation. If the group is playing a new system or includes players at different experience levels, session zero is the time to explain core mechanics - not in full, but enough that no one is confused when dice hit the table. In Brave Old World, this means explaining the 2d6 resolution bands and the consensus rule. In more complex systems, it might mean a practice combat or skill check.

Logistics. How often does the group meet? How long are sessions? Where? What happens when someone cannot make it? If the group is remote, what virtual tabletop or video platform will it use? These practical questions are unglamorous but decide whether the campaign survives. Groups that skip this discussion often discover scheduling or tooling incompatibilities three sessions in, after significant creative investment.

Cultural and political alignment. Session zero is also where the group can ask what historical or cultural material the campaign is drawing on, whose stories are being borrowed, and which defaults the table wants to avoid. This matters most when a campaign uses real peoples, Indigenous-coded settings, colonization themes, or fantasy ancestries with obvious racial analogs. These questions are easier to answer before materials are written than after they have already shaped the campaign.

Worked example: a Brave Old World session zero

The narrator has read Brave Old World and decided to run a campaign. Here is a step-by-step session zero:

  1. Introductions and logistics (10 min): confirm scheduling, duration, location, and communication channel for between-session coordination.

  2. Safety setup (15 min): the narrator explains lines and veils and the X-Card. Each player names at least one line or veil. The narrator records them. The narrator states the Open Door policy: anyone can leave any session without explanation.

  3. Tone negotiation (15 min): the narrator proposes a starting premise: “You are travelers in a world where the old civilizations have fallen and the wilderness is reclaiming the roads. There is no grand quest - you are people trying to find something in the ruins.” The group discusses what excites them about this premise and what they would modify. They agree: mystery and discovery are central; combat is possible but not the focus.

  4. Character creation (30 min): each player answers Brave Old World’s two questions. The group negotiates skills by consensus. The narrator checks that the party has reasons to travel together and that character concepts do not create friction the group has not agreed to.

  5. Rules walkthrough (10 min): the narrator explains the 2d6 resolution, the three result bands, and the consensus rule for disputes. One practice roll to make sure everyone understands.

  6. Questions and closing (10 min): anything unresolved? Any concerns? The narrator previews the first session: “Next time, you will start at the edge of the ruins.”

Total: about 90 minutes. No scenes were played. The group is now aligned on tone, safety, characters, and mechanics.

Add a campaign-responsibility check

If the campaign draws on real cultures, colonial histories, or politically loaded fantasy defaults, add five more minutes to session zero and ask:

  • What sources are shaping this campaign’s world?
  • Which real peoples or living traditions are in play, if any?
  • What is off-limits for us to mimic or repurpose?
  • Are we treating any people as naturally evil, primitive, or disposable?
  • If we are using a decolonial or Indigenous game, what responsibilities come with that choice?

These questions do not need academic jargon. They only need enough honesty to stop careless assumptions before they harden into prep.

What the GM prepares beyond session zero

Session zero aligns the group. But the GM also needs to prepare content for the first session of play. In practice, that usually means creating a small packet of table materials rather than writing a plot.

The core artifact is a scenario: a prepared situation with locations, pressures, non-player characters, and clues the players can engage. Around that, the GM often prepares a few support materials:

  • A scenario sheet naming the central problem, the important places, and what is already in motion.
  • A few non-player characters with goals and memorable details.
  • A spatial aid such as a rough map, route sketch, or room layout.
  • Zero to two handouts if the session includes a letter, clue list, map fragment, symbol, or other information players may need to revisit.
  • A rules reminder for procedures likely to recur in play.

For campaigns rather than one-shots, this packet usually grows out of a campaign frame and a small starting region. That broader layer is covered in Campaign and World Design.

In a minimal system like Brave Old World, preparation stays light. The point is to bring playable material to the table, not to pre-solve the session. For a fuller walkthrough, see Creating Table Materials.

Worked example: prep packet for a Brave Old World opener

The narrator wants the party to arrive near a ruined watchtower while two outside forces close in: a merchant caravan looking for a safe pass and a bandit scout trying to secure the same road.

Their prep packet looks like this:

  • Scenario sheet: ruined watchtower, unstable stairs, hidden cellar, merchant urgency, bandit pressure.
  • NPC roster: Lysa the caravan factor, Eren the scout, Old Harn at the river crossing.
  • Map sketch: stream, crossing, tower hill, collapsed road, tree line.
  • Handout: a half-burned route note with two place names and a warning about the mountain pass.
  • Rules card: 10+ clean success, 7-9 success with cost, 6 or less failure with consequence.

Nothing in that packet says how the session must end. It only ensures that when the players choose a direction, the narrator has material ready to support it.

Exercises

  1. Write a session zero agenda for a four-player Brave Old World game. Include at least one tone question, one safety question, and one character-connection question. Estimate the time for each section.

  2. Draft a first-session prep packet for the game you set up at session zero. Name the scenario, three NPCs, one spatial aid, and one handout you would bring to the table.

  3. Add a five-question campaign-responsibility check to your normal session-zero script.
