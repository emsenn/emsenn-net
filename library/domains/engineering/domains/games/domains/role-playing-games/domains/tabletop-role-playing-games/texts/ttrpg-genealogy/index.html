Genealogy of Tabletop Role-Playing Games

2026-01-01

This genealogy treats tabletop role-playing games as a convergence of several lineages rather than as a single invention. The form inherits from war gaming, improvisation, small-press publishing, and face-to-face group ritual all at once. For a sourced synthesis, see Tabletop Role-Playing Games.

The referee lineage. TTRPGs inherit a referee model from tabletop war gaming. One participant mediates hidden information, resolves edge cases, and adapts procedure to unfolding play. In role-playing games this role becomes broader than a war-game umpire: the game master not only arbitrates rules but also frames scenes, portrays non-player characters, and manages pacing.

The conversational-performance lineage. TTRPGs also inherit from improvisation and oral performance. Play happens through talk, turn-taking, listening, and response. The fiction is not displayed all at once. It is performed into existence by the table. This is why the medium can feel close to theater, but it differs from theater because no participant knows the full script and the rules intervene whenever the fiction becomes uncertain.

The rules-as-framework lineage. Tabletop RPGs treat rules as procedures the group interprets rather than as code that executes itself. This lineage runs from flexible war-game rules through early D&D and into later arguments about “rulings not rules.” The result is a culture of house rules, hacks, and local variation that remains stronger in TTRPGs than in most digital game forms.

The material-object lineage. Dice, sheets, maps, tokens, notebooks, and now virtual tabletops form another genealogy. These objects stabilize memory and make shared imagination manageable. A dice mechanic produces public uncertainty. A character sheet stores what is true about a character. A map settles spatial claims. Even when those objects move onto screens, the function remains recognizably tabletop: they support human coordination instead of replacing it.

The small-press lineage. TTRPGs have always depended on low-barrier publication. Early zines, amateur press associations, convention modules, photocopied pamphlets, and blog-post manifestos all fed the hobby. That lineage continues through itch.io, Kickstarter, and print-on-demand. It helps explain why design innovation in TTRPGs often appears first in tiny releases rather than in mainstream product lines.

The governance lineage. Modern tabletop play has also developed explicit practices for consent and repair. Session zero, table culture, and safety tools formalize negotiations that earlier groups often left implicit. This is genealogically important because it shows the table learning to govern itself more explicitly as the hobby widened its themes, audiences, and emotional range.

The documented-play lineage. Finally, contemporary TTRPGs inherit a new branch: actual play. Sessions are now not only played but recorded, streamed, archived, and discussed as media objects. This adds a public layer to a form that was historically private and local.
