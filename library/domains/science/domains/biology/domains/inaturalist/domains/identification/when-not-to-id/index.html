When not to identify

2026-03-04

Withholding an ID can be the most responsible action when evidence is insufficient or when an ID might cause harm.

¶Practice guidance

  • Do not guess species-level IDs for cryptic taxa without diagnostic traits.
  • Avoid high-impact IDs (rare, invasive, or protected species) without strong evidence.
  • Let observations remain at a higher taxon when needed.

¶High-risk identification contexts

  • New or unexpected range extensions without supporting documentation.
  • Single-photo observations lacking diagnostic traits.
  • Observations of legally protected or highly collectible taxa.

¶What goes wrong if you do this poorly

Overconfident IDs can trigger incorrect management responses, including unnecessary eradication or misguided conservation planning.

¶Sources

  • iNaturalist guidance on identification quality: https://www.inaturalist.org/pages/help#identification
  • Chandler et al. (2017) on best practices in citizen science: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biocon.2016.09.017
  • Kremen et al. (2011) on the value and limits of citizen science: https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-ecolsys-102710-145026

¶High-risk ID checklist

  • Is the evidence diagnostic for species-level ID?
  • Would a misidentification trigger management or conservation action?
  • Is the taxon known to require microscopy or genetics?
  • Is this a first record for the region without supporting documentation?

¶Using “No, but” IDs responsibly

Use “No, but” IDs to move a record to a higher, defensible taxon when evidence does not support a specific ID. Explain why in a comment to maintain an audit trail.

¶Reviewer etiquette

  • Be specific about which traits are missing.
  • Avoid dismissive language; uncertainty is normal.
