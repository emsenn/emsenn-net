Evidence: photos, sounds, and metadata

Evidence is the substrate for identification and verification. Provide media and metadata that allow another person to independently assess the observation.

Practice guidance

  • Capture diagnostic features across multiple angles (e.g., leaf arrangement, dorsal vs. ventral views).
  • Include scale cues or habitat context when relevant.
  • Verify date, time, and location accuracy before upload, especially for imported photos with incorrect EXIF data.
  • If the organism is not visible, record indirect evidence (tracks, scat, galls) only when diagnostic features are clear.

Taxon-specific evidence notes

  • Plants: include leaves, stems, flowers, and fruit if available; single floral shots are often insufficient.
  • Fungi: capture substrate and underside features; spore-bearing structures are critical for ID.
  • Insects: include dorsal and lateral views; wing venation or antennae can be diagnostic.
  • Birds: note behavior and vocalizations; attach audio when possible.

Metadata matters

Poor metadata yields unusable records even if the ID is correct. Precision and accuracy are different: precise coordinates that are wrong are worse than imprecise but honest ones. If you are unsure of a location, widen the accuracy radius rather than guessing.

What goes wrong if you do this poorly

Missing or misleading evidence can lock in incorrect IDs and propagate to shared datasets. This is especially harmful for rare or invasive species where false positives carry management costs. In some regions, misidentified invasives can trigger unnecessary responses.

Sources

  • iNaturalist photo tips: https://www.inaturalist.org/pages/help#photos
  • Chapman (2005) on data quality: https://doi.org/10.15468/doc.jr7y-rh90
  • Wiggins et al. (2011) on data validation in citizen science: https://doi.org/10.1007/s11280-011-0140-3

Minimum viable evidence (quick rubric)

  • Plants: clear leaves + reproductive structures if possible.
  • Fungi: cap and underside + substrate.
  • Insects: dorsal + lateral views; include size reference.
  • Birds: diagnostic plumage or audio; note behavior.

Audio documentation guidance

  • Record at least 10–30 seconds of audio with minimal handling noise.
  • Note overlapping species or background noise in comments.
  • Include time of day and weather if vocalization is context-dependent.
