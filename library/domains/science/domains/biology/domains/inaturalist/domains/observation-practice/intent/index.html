Intent: why you are observing matters

Intent shapes what you record, where you go, and how you document. Treat your observation as a data contribution with potential scientific use, not an isolated personal record.

Practice guidance

  • Define a purpose for the observation session (e.g., documenting phenology, verifying range, recording host associations).
  • Consider what evidence will make the observation useful to someone who is not you.
  • Record absences only through structured projects elsewhere; iNaturalist is presence-only.

Example intents and evidence

  • Phenology: prioritize repeated visits and clear life stage notes (flowering, fruiting, larval stage).
  • Range verification: capture diagnostic traits and double-check location accuracy.
  • Host association: document the host organism and interaction context in the same observation notes.

What goes wrong if you do this poorly

Without intent, observations cluster around convenience, inflating biased occurrence maps and under-representing less visible taxa or habitats.

Sources

  • Dickinson et al. (2010) on citizen science as a research tool: https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev.ecolsys.39.110707.173628

Aligning intent with iNaturalist tools

  • Use projects to group observations with shared protocols (e.g., repeated site surveys).
  • Use collection filters (place, taxon, date) to review whether your intent is being met.
  • Add observation fields for structured attributes (e.g., host plant, life stage).
