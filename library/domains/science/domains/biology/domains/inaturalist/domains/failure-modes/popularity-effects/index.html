Failure mode: popularity effects

2026-03-04

Popularity effects arise when certain species or observations receive disproportionate attention, driving more uploads and IDs for those taxa while others lag.

¶Indicators

  • High ID throughput for birds and charismatic mammals.
  • Slow or absent review for cryptic insects, fungi, or plants.
  • Comment and identification backlogs in taxon groups with few experts.
  • Heavy reliance on computer vision suggestions for common taxa.

¶Mitigation practices

  • Prioritize understudied taxa in your own observations.
  • Identify within groups that need more reviewer attention.
  • Provide explanatory comments instead of quick agreement clicks for difficult taxa.

¶What goes wrong if you do this poorly

Popularity-driven data become self-reinforcing: common taxa get better data, while scarce taxa remain poorly documented. This can distort perceived biodiversity patterns and research priorities.

¶Feedback loops and algorithmic visibility

Records that receive faster IDs are surfaced more often, while backlogged taxa become harder to review. This is not a moral failure; it is a workload reality that shifts data quality toward already popular groups. Practitioners can counteract this by intentionally reviewing neglected taxa and leaving evidence-based guidance for future identifiers.

¶Sources

  • Troudet et al. (2017) on taxonomic bias: https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0186592

¶Balancing identification effort

  • Set a weekly quota for under-reviewed taxa.
  • Leave IDs only when evidence supports the taxon; avoid “agree” clicks without review.
