From Seed to Seedling

2026-04-02 Explanation of germination as a developmental transition: seed dormancy as adaptive bet-hedging, dormancy-breaking signals, the germination process from imbibition to seedling establishment, and the energy costs of growth

A seed is a package containing a plant embryo, energy reserves, and a protective coat. It can remain dry and dormant for years, then spring to life in days when conditions favor growth. This transition from inert to active is one of the most dramatic developmental changes in a plant’s life. The same organism that was not perceptibly alive—a dry seed absorbs minimal water, respires slowly, undergoes no growth—suddenly awakens. Roots emerge, shoots elongate, leaves unfold, and within a few weeks the seedling is photosynthetically independent. Understanding this transition reveals that germination is not a simple response to water and warmth but a tightly regulated developmental program controlled by environmental signals and hormonal gradients. The seed does not merely wait for good conditions; it actively assesses the environment and germinates only when the probability of seedling survival is high.

¶The Adaptive Logic of Dormancy

Before understanding germination, consider why seeds are dormant. A seed could, in principle, germinate immediately upon ripening while still on the parent plant. This would ensure rapid colonization of available space. But such plants would suffer catastrophic loss: a late-season warm spell might trigger germination, seedlings would emerge, and then frost would kill them all. Alternatively, rain could trigger germination in unseasonably wet conditions; seedlings would dry out in the following drought. A plant that invests all its seed production in a single temporal window takes enormous risk.

Seed dormancy solves this problem through bet-hedging. Instead of putting all eggs in one temporal basket, a plant distributes germination across time. Some seeds germinate immediately (or after minimal dormancy), but others remain dormant for weeks, months, or even years. This staggering of emergence reduces catastrophic loss: even if one cohort of seedlings fails to a late frost or unexpected drought, other cohorts germinate at different times and have a chance to succeed.

Moreover, seed dormancy keeps seeds in the soil longer. An annual weed’s seeds fall and persist in the soil, forming a seed bank. When a field is plowed, this disturbance stimulates dormancy-breaking signals; masses of seeds germinate, colonizing the disturbed space. This is adaptive for the weed: plowing indicates a gap in vegetation, a window of opportunity. By delaying germination until disturbance occurs, the weed avoids wasting energy on seedlings that would be shaded by existing vegetation.

In harsher climates, dormancy ensures that germination occurs in seasonal windows most favorable for seedling survival. A temperate-zone tree’s seeds must germinate in spring, not autumn. A desert plant’s seeds must germinate only when soil moisture is sufficient to sustain growth to maturity. In each case, dormancy is broken by specific environmental cues that reliably correlate with favorable conditions.

¶Types of Dormancy: The Mechanisms

Plant seeds employ five primary dormancy mechanisms:

Physical dormancy (PY) involves an impermeable seed coat. Many legume seeds are coated with a hard, waxy layer that water cannot penetrate. Without water uptake, the embryo cannot activate its metabolism. This dormancy is broken by scarification: mechanical abrasion (sanding, filing), acid treatment, or heat. In nature, scarification occurs through microbial action, passage through animal digestive systems, or the weathering action of soil particles over time.

Physiological dormancy (PD) is maintained by the hormone abscisic acid (ABA), which is synthesized during seed maturation and accumulates in the embryo and endosperm. ABA suppresses the expression of genes required for radicle elongation and cell expansion, keeping the embryo in a quiescent state. This dormancy is broken by stratification: exposure of imbibed seeds to cold temperatures (0–10°C) for a prolonged period (4–12 weeks, depending on species). Cold treatment triggers enzymatic breakdown of ABA—specifically, hydroxylation to 8’-hydroxy-ABA, an inactive metabolite. As ABA levels drop, gibberellin (GA) accumulates and promotes germination when the seed is subsequently warmed.

Morphological dormancy (MD) is rare and results from incomplete embryo development within the seed. The embryo must grow to maturity before germination is possible. This can extend over an entire growing season.

Morphophysiological dormancy (MPD) combines incomplete embryo development with physiological inhibition, requiring both warm and cold periods in specific sequences to break dormancy fully.

Combinational dormancy combines physical and physiological components; seeds must be scarified to allow water uptake, then stratified to reduce ABA, before germination is possible.

¶Dormancy-Breaking Signals in Nature

Natural ecosystems employ multiple signals to break dormancy:

Cold stratification: Winter cold breaks dormancy in many temperate-zone species. Seeds land in autumn, are buried in soil, and experience winter temperatures. This is particularly important for biennials and winter annuals—plants that germinate in fall or early spring and overwinter before flowering. The cold is not random; it is predictable. A plant that has evolved to germinate after winter knows that spring will follow and that soil moisture will be available for young seedlings.

Scarification and weathering: Dry seeds can remain dormant for years in the soil until some event—fire, plowing, erosion—exposes them or damages their coat. Fire is a particularly important cue: smoke compounds and the heat pulse itself can break dormancy in many fire-adapted species, signaling that a seedbed has been cleared of competitors.

Light: Some seeds require light for germination, detected through phytochrome. These are typically seeds from open habitats—meadows, grasslands—where light indicates that the seed is on the soil surface, not buried under leaf litter. Seeds of shade-adapted species often fail to germinate in light, ensuring they do not establish in unsuitable microhabitats.

Temperature fluctuations: Many seeds require alternating temperatures—warm days and cool nights—to break dormancy. This is adaptive for species in seasonally variable climates, where such fluctuations indicate that spring is arriving and summer is likely to follow.

Nitrate: Seeds of some species are stimulated to germinate by soil nitrate, which indicates that biological activity (decomposition) is occurring and nitrogen is becoming available. This is a sophisticated cue, as it correlates with soil conditions favorable for growth.

Gibberellin: In agricultural practice, seeds that refuse to germinate are sometimes treated with gibberellin or gibberellin-releasing compounds to overcome dormancy artificially, forcing rapid, uniform germination for commercial purposes.

¶The Germination Process: Three Phases

Germination occurs in three physiologically distinct phases:

Phase I: Imbibition. Water is absorbed rapidly, and the dry seed swells to many times its original volume. This imbibition is initially passive, driven by the osmotic potential of the dry seed; even dead seeds will imbibe. But as water penetrates the seed coat, it rehydrates the seed’s cytoplasm, activating enzymes and initiating respiration. A seed imbibed but not yet germinating is in a state of high metabolic activity: enzyme systems are activated, DNA repair mechanisms work to restore genetic integrity, and respiratory rates increase dramatically.

Phase II: Metabolic reactivation and lag. Metabolism accelerates, but visible growth remains minimal. The duration of Phase II varies enormously: some species (lettuce, radish) proceed rapidly to radicle emergence, while others (parsley) remain in Phase II for weeks, accumulating reserves and completing enzymatic preparations. During this phase, germination-promoting hormones (gibberellins) accumulate, and germination-inhibiting hormones (abscisic acid) are degraded. The seed is “deciding” whether germination should proceed, based on environmental cues.

Phase III: Radicle emergence and growth. The primary root, called the radicle, elongates and penetrates the seed coat, emerging as the first visible sign of germination. Cell elongation occurs as cells absorb water and the cell wall loosens under the influence of gibberellins, allowing cells to expand. The root cap, a specialized tissue covering the root apical meristem, is pushed through the seed coat first, protecting the delicate meristem.

Simultaneously, the shoot begins to elongate. In dicots, this occurs through the hypocotyl (the stem-like structure between root and cotyledons); in monocots, through the coleoptile (a protective sheath around the first true leaves). The shoot grows toward the light, aided by gravity-sensing cells that reorient growth toward the vertical. Light drives the seedling’s development toward photosynthetic competence.

¶Energy Mobilization: From Reserves to Photosynthesis

A germinating seed is heterotrophic—it cannot photosynthesizeand must live on stored reserves. These reserves are of two types: endosperm (in monocots and some dicots) and cotyledons (seed leaves in dicots and some monocots).

The endosperm contains starch (in grains like wheat and rice), fats (in castor bean), or proteins (in legumes). Upon germination, the seed mobilizes these reserves through enzymatic breakdown. Gibberellin produced by the embryo signals the aleurone layer (the outermost layer of the endosperm) to synthesize and secrete hydrolytic enzymes: amylase (breaks starch), protease (breaks proteins), and lipase (breaks fats). These enzymes diffuse into the endosperm, breaking down reserves into monosaccharides, amino acids, and fatty acids that the seedling can transport and use for growth.

Cotyledons are nutrient-rich tissues; as the seedling grows, the cotyledons shrivel as their reserves are depleted. In beans and peas, large cotyledons push above ground and photosynthesize for a time, supplementing the seedling’s diet. In other species, cotyledons remain below ground and are entirely absorbed by the growing seedling.

Once the shoot emerges and unfolds true leaves, photosynthesis begins. Light is captured, CO₂ is fixed, and the seedling begins producing its own sugars. It has transitioned from heterotrophy (living on stored reserves) to autotrophy (producing its own food). This transition is crucial; failure at this point—inadequate light, unsuitable temperature, or continued water stress—will kill the seedling.

¶Variation in Germination Speed

Germination speed varies dramatically among species and is often adaptive. Fast germinators include:

  • Garden cress (Lepidium sativum): 2–3 days to radicle emergence
  • Radish (Raphanus sativus): 3–7 days
  • Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum): 5–10 days
  • Lettuce (Lactuca sativa, seeds): 2–7 days

Slow germinators include:

  • Parsley (Petroselinum crispum): 14–30 days
  • Carrot (Daucus carota): 14–21 days
  • Onion (Allium cepa): 7–14 days, but slow to mature
  • Parsnip (Pastinaca sativa): 20–30 days

The ecological logic of slow germination is not always obvious. Parsley has physiological dormancy and requires cold treatment (stratification) or extended moist stratification at cool temperatures to reduce ABA. This means parsley seeds naturally germinate in spring, after winter cold. The slow germination rate may be a historical remnant of its evolutionary history in Mediterranean climates with late-spring rains.

In cultivation, fast-germinating species are often used in teaching and in gardens where rapid results are desired. Slow-germinating species require patience and consistent moisture; they are easily lost to drying, fungal attack, or disturbance.

¶Seed Viability and Vigor

Seed viability is the proportion of seeds capable of germinating under optimal conditions. Viability declines with age, regardless of storage conditions, though it declines much faster in warm, moist conditions than in cool, dry ones. A seed stored cool and dry may retain viability for decades; the same seed stored at room temperature in humid conditions may lose viability within months.

Seed vigor encompasses not only viability but also the speed and uniformity of germination and the ability of seedlings to establish in suboptimal conditions. High-vigor seeds germinate rapidly and synchronously, producing uniform seedlings that establish reliably even in cool or dry field conditions. Low-vigor seeds germinate slowly and asynchronously, producing uneven stands.

Vigor is heritable—some varieties are intrinsically more vigorous than others—but is degraded by age and environmental stress during seed production and storage. Commercial seed is tested for vigor through accelerated aging tests (heating seeds to degrade them and then measuring remaining viability) and cold tests (germinating seeds in cool, wet soil that simulates field conditions).

¶The Seedling’s First Challenges

Once visible growth begins, the seedling faces new challenges. It must push through soil, which can be a mechanical barrier. It must reach light before its reserves are exhausted. It must avoid damping-off disease caused by soil fungi like Pythium and Rhizoctonia. It must establish roots in the soil while shoots reach for light.

Young seedlings are vulnerable to drought, cold, and herbivory. The transition from seed (protected, dormant, self-sufficient) to seedling (exposed, active, dependent on environmental conditions) is one of the most dangerous phases of the plant’s life. A seedling’s mortality rate is often 50% or higher in natural populations. Yet for those that survive to photosynthetic competence, the stage is set for a long life of growth and eventually reproduction.
