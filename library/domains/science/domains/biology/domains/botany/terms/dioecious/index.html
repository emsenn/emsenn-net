Dioecious

2026-04-02 A plant species in which male and female reproductive organs are borne on separate individual plants — each plant is either male or female, never both

A dioecious species is one in which male and female reproductive organs are borne on separate individual plants. Each plant produces either pollen (male function) or ovules (female function), never both. The word derives from Greek di- (two) + oikos (house) — “two houses,” meaning the sexes reside in separate individuals.

Approximately 6% of angiosperm species are dioecious. Common examples include willows (Salix), holly (Ilex), cannabis (Cannabis sativa), kiwifruit (Actinidia deliciosa), and asparagus (Asparagus officinalis).

¶Contrasting sexual systems

System Description Example
Hermaphrodite Each flower has both male and female parts Most flowering plants
Monoecious Separate male and female flowers on the same plant Corn, oaks, birches
Dioecious Male and female flowers on separate plants Willows, holly, kiwi
Polygamous Mix of hermaphrodite and unisexual flowers Some maples

¶Functional dioecy in knotweeds

Japanese knotweed is described as functionally dioecious — individual plants produce either female-only flowers (male-sterile) or bisexual flowers that produce functional pollen. In its invaded range (Britain, North America), virtually all established plants are female clones of a single introduction. They cannot self-fertilize and require pollen from a compatible source — giant knotweed (R. sachalinensis) or the Bohemian hybrid (R. × bohemica) — to produce viable seed. This reproductive limitation has historically restricted knotweed’s spread to vegetative reproduction, but hybridization is producing fertile offspring with greater genetic diversity.
