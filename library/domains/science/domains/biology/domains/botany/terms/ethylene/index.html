Ethylene

2026-04-02
Table of contents

Ethylene: the only gaseous plant hormone (C₂H₄), diffusing freely through air and soil to regulate fruit ripening, senescence, leaf and flower abscission, defense responses, and the “triple response” in dark-grown seedlings. Operating at nanomolar concentrations, ethylene is produced in response to stress, wounding, and fruit maturation, coordinating plant responses across multiple tissues simultaneously.

¶Biosynthesis

Ethylene is synthesized through the Yang cycle: methionine → S-adenosylmethionine (SAM, via SAM synthetase) → 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid (ACC, via ACC synthase / ACS) → ethylene (via ACC oxidase / ACO). ACS is the rate-limiting enzyme; it is encoded by a multigene family and regulated at both transcriptional and post-translational levels. Phosphorylation by MAP kinases (MPK3/MPK6) stabilizes certain ACS isoforms. ACC can also be conjugated to malonyl-ACC or gamma-glutamyl-ACC as a storage form, sequestering the hormone and rendering it inactive until deconjugation is needed.

Ethylene production increases dramatically in ripening fruits (the climacteric phase), in response to wounding, pathogens, and flooding. The autocatalytic nature of fruit ripening — ethylene stimulates more ethylene production — drives coordinated ripening across an entire fruit and explains the adage “one bad apple spoils the bunch”: ethylene from a ripening apple diffuses to neighbors, triggering their ripening.

¶Perception and signaling

Ethylene receptors are unique: they are negative regulators. Five ethylene receptors (ETR1, ETR2, ERS1, ERS2, EIN4) are localized to the ER membrane and require copper cofactor (delivered by the RAN1 copper transporter) for ethylene binding. Without ethylene, these receptors activate CTR1 (CONSTITUTIVE TRIPLE RESPONSE 1), a Raf-like MAP kinase kinase kinase, which phosphorylates and inactivates EIN2 (ETHYLENE INSENSITIVE 2), an ER-membrane protein. When ethylene binds the receptor, receptor activity ceases, CTR1 is inactivated, and EIN2’s C-terminal domain is cleaved and translocated to the nucleus. There, EIN2 stabilizes EIN3/EIL1 transcription factors by suppressing their ubiquitination by the SCF^EBF1/EBF2 complex. EIN3/EIL1 then activate ethylene-responsive genes, including ERF (ETHYLENE RESPONSE FACTOR) transcription factors, which control the full program of ethylene responses.

¶Developmental roles

Climacteric fruit ripening. In climacteric fruits (banana, tomato, apple, avocado), ethylene triggers a cascade: fruit softening (pectinase activation), color development (chlorophyll loss, carotenoid synthesis), sugar accumulation, and volatile organic compound production (aroma). The process is autocatalytic: low ethylene primes the fruit for increased ethylene sensitivity, which triggers higher ethylene production. Pre-climacteric and climacteric stages are distinguished by whether ethylene is being actively synthesized.

Non-climacteric fruits (strawberry, pineapple, citrus) do not undergo this ethylene-driven ripening; their color, sugar, and flavor develop in the orchard before picking.

Abscission. Ethylene promotes the senescence of leaves and flowers, inducing expression of cellulase and pectinase enzymes that weaken the abscission zone — the band of cells at the leaf petiole or flower pedicel. Coordinated ethylene signaling causes synchronized leaf drop in autumn and flower shedding.

Triple response. In dark-grown seedlings, ethylene causes the “triple response”: exaggerated apical hook formation (to push through soil), inhibition of cell elongation in the hypocotyl, and radial swelling. Light inhibits ethylene sensitivity, allowing normal photomorphogenesis.

Defense signaling. Ethylene is synergistic with jasmonate in defending against necrotrophic pathogens and herbivorous insects. Wounded leaves rapidly increase ethylene production, priming the plant’s defenses.

Root development. Ethylene inhibits root elongation and promotes root hair development and lateral root branching. High ethylene in waterlogged soils (produced by anaerobic bacteria) stimulates aerenchyma formation — the development of air spaces in roots that allow survival under hypoxia.

¶Commercial applications

The ripening of bananas, avocados, and other produce is routinely controlled with exogenous ethylene gas, ensuring uniform color and ripeness in markets. Conversely, 1-methylcyclopropene (1-MCP), a cyclopropene that irreversibly blocks ethylene receptors, is used to inhibit ripening and senescence, extending the shelf life of cut flowers and produce.
