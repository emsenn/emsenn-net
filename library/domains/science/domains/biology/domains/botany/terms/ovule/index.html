Ovule

2026-04-02

Ovule: the structure within a flower’s ovary that contains the female gametophyte (embryo sac) and develops into the seed after fertilization. The ovule is the female counterpart to the pollen grain, enclosing the egg cell, supporting tissues, and protective layers. Its development is simultaneous with pollen maturation in the anther, but ovule development is typically much slower and often stops at a specific stage until fertilization triggers resumption.

Structure

A mature ovule consists of several layers and regions:

Integuments. One or two protective layers surrounding the nucellus and embryo sac. In most angiosperms, there are two integuments (bitegmic); in some derived families, there is only one (unitegmic). After fertilization, the integuments develop into the seed coat (testa). The integuments do not completely enclose the ovule — there remains an opening called the micropyle, through which the pollen tube enters to deliver sperm.

Nucellus. A tissue of maternal origin surrounding the embryo sac, providing nutritive support during embryo development in the early stages. In many species, the nucellus degenerates as the endosperm expands after fertilization.

Embryo sac. The female gametophyte, typically consisting of seven cells: three antipodal cells opposite the micropyle (function unknown in most species), two synergid cells flanking the egg cell at the micropyle end (which guide the pollen tube’s entry), one egg cell (the female gamete), and one central cell containing two polar nuclei. This is the mature megagametophyte — the product of three meiotic divisions followed by mitotic divisions of the megaspore.

Chalaza. The basal end of the ovule, opposite the micropyle. This is where vascular tissue enters the nucellus, supplying nutrients.

Funiculus. The stalk attaching the ovule to the placental tissue of the ovary wall. It conducts water and nutrients.

Developmental variations

Ovule structure varies considerably across plant families, reflecting different evolutionary histories:

Placentation. The arrangement of ovules within the ovary is called placentation. Marginal placentation (ovules along the margins of a simple pistil, as in legumes) is thought to be ancestral. Axile placentation (ovules on internal septa, as in tomato and peppers) and basal placentation (ovule or few ovules at the base, as in grasses) are derived conditions.

Number. Ovule number per ovary varies from one (in cherry, peach, many grasses) to thousands (in orchids, some aquatic plants). Plants that invest in few ovules often provision each with more resources, while those producing thousands of ovules are “bet hedging” — the pollen-limited species produce more seeds per flower to compensate for lower seed set.

Type. Ovules are classified by the orientation of the integuments: orthotropous (straight, axis aligned with micropyle at one end), anatropous (inverted 180°, most common in dicots), and campylotropous (curved). These differences affect the position of the seed within the fruit and influence dispersal and germination.

Development and megagametogenesis

Ovule development begins in the ovary’s placental tissue as an integument-less primordia. Integument growth encircles the nucellus, leaving the micropyle. Simultaneously, the megasporocyte (2n) in the nucellus undergoes meiosis, producing a linear tetrad of four haploid megaspores. The functional megaspore (typically the one furthest from the micropyle) undergoes three mitotic divisions without cytokinesis (in the Polygonum type, most common). This produces an 8-nucleate stage: three nuclei migrate to the antipodal end, two to the polar region (forming the central cell when cell walls form), and one becomes the egg cell — the mature embryo sac.

Development arrests at this stage until pollination occurs. Fertilization triggers rapid endosperm and embryo development.

Relationship to double fertilization

The ovule’s embryo sac is the female partner in the angiosperm’s signature reproductive innovation: double fertilization. One sperm fertilizes the egg, initiating embryo development; the other fuses with the central cell to form the primary endosperm nucleus (triploid, 3n). The endosperm develops rapidly, providing the nutritive tissue that nourishes the embryo throughout development and often remaining as a significant component of the mature seed (as in wheat and rice endosperm).

Evolutionary significance

The ovule is an ancient innovation predating flowering plants — gymnosperms (conifers, cycads, gnetophytes) also produce seeds from ovules. However, in angiosperms, the ovule is enclosed within the ovary, which develops into a fruit. This enclosure is significant: it protects developing seeds and allows the plant to invest in fruit structure for dispersal, linking reproduction to ecology in ways unique to flowering plants.
