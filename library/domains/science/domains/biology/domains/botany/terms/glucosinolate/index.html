Glucosinolate

2026-04-02 Sulfur-containing secondary metabolite characteristic of Brassicaceae, hydrolyzed by myrosinase to produce bioactive isothiocyanates upon tissue damage.

Glucosinolates are sulfur-containing secondary metabolites characteristic of the Brassicaceae family (mustards, cabbages, broccoli, kale). The compound class comprises a central carbon atom bonded to a β-thioglucose moiety, an N-linked sulfate (forming a sulfated aldoxime), and a variable side chain derived from aliphatic, aromatic, or indolic amino acids. This structure enables rapid enzymatic conversion to defense metabolites upon herbivory.

Structure and Compartmentalization

The glucosinolate molecule contains three core structural elements: the glucose group (β-D-glucopyranose), the thioglucosidic linkage providing sulfur fixation, and the N-hydroxysulfate oxime conferring reactivity. Brassicaceae plants maintain strict subcellular compartmentalization: glucosinolates are sequestered in vacuoles of specialized “S-cells,” while the enzyme myrosinase is stored separately in adjacent “myrosin cells” within vacuolar myrosin grains or as cytosolic membrane-bound forms.

The Myrosinase Hydrolysis System

Upon herbivore feeding or mechanical damage, cell disruption brings glucosinolates and myrosinase into contact. The enzyme cleaves the β-thioglucosidic linkage, releasing D-glucose and generating an unstable aglycone intermediate. This aglycone undergoes rapid Lossen-type rearrangement, releasing sulfate and forming the bioactive product class: isothiocyanates, thiocyanates, or nitriles depending on microenvironment pH and the presence of epithiospecifier protein (ESP).

At neutral pH, isothiocyanates are the predominant product; acidic conditions and ferrous ions (Fe²⁺) favor nitrile formation. This biochemical flexibility allows plants to generate diverse defensive compounds from a single glucosinolate.

Ecological Function and Specialist Herbivores

Glucosinolate-derived compounds inhibit cytochrome P450 detoxification enzymes in generalist herbivores, conferring broad-spectrum defense. However, specialist insects—particularly the cabbage white butterfly (Pieris spp.)—have evolved cytochrome P450 variants that detoxify isothiocyanates, and some species even sequester glucosinolates in their own tissues as acquired defense.

Examples and Food Chemistry

Sinigrin (black mustard, Brassica nigra) hydrolyzes to allyl isothiocyanate, producing mustard’s pungent character. Glucoraphanin (broccoli, Brassica oleracea) yields sulforaphane upon myrosinase activation—a compound under investigation for anticarcinogenic activity in epidemiological studies. Cooking inactivates myrosinase through heat denaturation, reducing isothiocyanate production in processed brassicas.
