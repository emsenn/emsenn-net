Crocus sativus

2026-04-02 Saffron crocus, a sterile triploid geophyte bearing autumn flowers with three crimson stigmas harvested as the world's most expensive spice.

Crocus sativus L. is a cormous perennial herb in the iris family (Iridaceae), grown exclusively for the three red stigmas borne by its purple flowers. It is a sterile allotriploid (2n = 3x = 24 chromosomes), reproducing only vegetatively through corm division, and has no known wild population—its parents likely derived from Crocus cartwrightianus and Crocus pallasii subsp. pallasii or close relatives, now extinct or geographically isolated from saffron cultivation zones.

¶Morphology and Corm Anatomy

The underground corm is the plant’s primary storage organ—a solid, compact, flattened globe 2–5 cm in diameter with a papery outer tunic (the corm coat), composed of parallel fibrous strands. Above the flattened basal plate, vertical fibres extend upward 3–5 cm, forming a net-like structure (sometimes called “roots” colloquially, though they are merely fibrous tissues lacking root hairs or vascular bundles). True roots develop from the basal plate downward during the growing season.

The aerial portions emerge in autumn (September–November, depending on latitude). Each corm produces 5–10 threadlike leaves (linear, 20–30 cm long, 0.5–1 mm wide) arranged in basal rosettes, and 1–4 flowers on short inflorescences. The flower is hermaphrodite, actinomorphic, with six petaloid segments forming a tube 2–4 cm long and spreading limbs 3–4 cm across; flowers range from purple to violet or, rarely, pale mauve. The flower tube is deeply inserted into the soil, a trait that protects the developing carpel from frost and herbivory.

¶Reproductive Biology and Sterility

The pistil—the female reproductive organ—comprises a three-lobed ovary, style, and stigma. Critically, the stigma divides into three branches (approximately 2.5–3 cm long when fully developed), each elongated and clubbed at the apex. These three stigmas, when dried, yield the entire saffron spice of commerce. The stamens produce non-viable pollen, as does the carpel lack viable ovules. This universal sterility is a consequence of triploidy: the odd number of chromosome sets (three) prevents proper pairing during meiosis, resulting in aneuploid gametes and failed sexual reproduction.

Propagation occurs entirely through manual division of corms during the summer dormant season, a labor-intensive process that has been the sole means of perpetuation for millennia.

¶Cultivation and Seasonal Phenology

Crocus sativus flowers in autumn (typically October in the northern hemisphere), a trait unusual among spring-blooming Crocus species. The flowers are short-lived, opening in early morning and closing by afternoon. Harvest must occur within this brief window—stigmas are carefully excised by hand using forceps or fingernails. Approximately 75–100 flowers yield one gram of dried saffron; the yield is 1 tablespoon (3 grams) per ~225–300 flowers, or ~0.1–0.15% of fresh stigma mass as dried product.

The plant then enters a dormant phase until the following autumn, with leaves emerging but no secondary growth. The corm gradually accumulates starch and biomass. Saffron cultivation is thus a nine-month dormant-plus-one-month flowering cycle, demanding well-drained soil, minimal moisture during dormancy, and protection from pests that might consume the corm.

¶Soil and Ecological Requirements

Saffron is notoriously dependent on exquisitely well-drained soil. Waterlogging or poorly aerated substrates cause corm rot (Fusarium and Botrytis infections). Traditional cultivation regions (Iran, Kashmir, Spain) occur in semi-arid plateaus with calcareous soils. The plant tolerates saline and alkaline conditions better than acidic substrates.

¶Economic and Cultural Significance

Saffron is the world’s most expensive spice by weight (historically 2–5 times the cost of gold), a premium reflecting the labor-intensive harvest and tiny yield. Its scarlet-to-brown dried stigmas contain the natural colorant crocin and the bitter compound picrocrocin, imparting distinctive flavor and hue to cuisines across the Mediterranean, South Asia, and Middle East.
