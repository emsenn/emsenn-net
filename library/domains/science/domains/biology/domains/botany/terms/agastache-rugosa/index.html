Agastache rugosa

2026-04-02 Short-lived perennial herb in the mint family native to East Asia, cultivated as a traditional medicinal plant in Korean and Chinese medicine, with anise-flavored leaves and distinctive terminal purple-violet inflorescences.

Agastache rugosa (Fisch. & C.A. Mey.) Kuntze, commonly called Korean licorice mint or Korean hyssop, is a short-lived perennial herbaceous plant in the family Lamiaceae (mint family), native to temperate East Asia, particularly Korea, China, and Japan. It is distinguished by wrinkled (rugose) opposite leaves, a branching habit reaching 60-100 cm, and terminal spike-like inflorescences bearing bilabiate flowers in whorls, typically purple to violet. Leaves contain volatile oils with prominent anise-like (anethole-containing) notes, and the plant is a cornerstone of East Asian herbal medicine.

¶Taxonomy and Morphology

Agastache rugosa represents one of approximately 12 Agastache species in East Asia, distinguished from North American Agastache species by its preference for cool, moist habitats and its historical integration into East Asian culinary and medicinal traditions. Some botanical treatments consider it a subspecies or variant of Agastache foeniculum (giant hyssop), though current consensus recognizes it as a distinct species.

The plant grows 60-100 cm tall with square stems and opposite, petiolate leaves 5-10 cm long with wrinkled surfaces and serrated margins. Leaf color is typically dark green with a grayish tint. Inflorescence is a dense terminal spike, 8-15 cm long, bearing purple or violet flowers in dense whorls; calyx is five-lobed and campanulate; corolla is two-lipped with a broad lower lip. Flowers bloom sequentially from base to apex over 4-6 weeks. Seeds are small nutlets, enclosed in the persistent calyx.

¶Chemistry

The essential oil comprises 0.5-2.0 percent of fresh leaf weight and is dominated by anethole (40-70 percent), methyl cinnamate, estragole, and limonene. Anethole imparts the characteristic sweet, licorice-like aroma and flavor. Secondary compounds include flavonoids (apigenin, luteolin) and polyphenols with antioxidant properties. The chemical profile varies by geographic provenance and growing conditions.

¶Ecology and Distribution

Agastache rugosa is native to temperate forests and grasslands of Korea, China, Japan, and eastern Russia, where it grows in moist, partially shaded habitats at moderate to high elevations (300-1,500 m). It is a pioneer species in disturbed areas and is shade-tolerant compared to other Agastache species. In temperate climates (USDA zones 4-9), it functions as a short-lived perennial, persisting 3-4 seasons before declining. Self-seeding perpetuates populations if plants are allowed to mature seeds.

¶Traditional and Modern Uses

In Korean and Chinese traditional medicine, Agastache rugosa (Korean: baechohyang) is one of the 50 fundamental herbs in the traditional pharmacopoeia. It is used to treat digestive complaints, colds, and fever, and is a last-minute addition to the soups chueo-tang (loach soup) and maeun-tang (spicy soup), where its aromatic oils are believed to enhance digestion and add complexity to broths. The plant is also used in Japanese and Vietnamese cuisines as an edible garnish and flavoring. Essential oil extraction yields 0.5-1.5 percent oil, used in herbal pharmaceuticals and cosmetics. Annual cultivation in Korea and China exceeds 5,000 hectares, with production concentrated in traditional medicinal herb growing regions.
