Capsaicin

2026-04-02 Alkaloid compound responsible for pungency in Capsicum peppers, activating the TRPV1 pain receptor in mammals through vanilloid pathway interactions.

Capsaicin is an alkaloid alkalamine produced in the placental tissue of Capsicum fruits as a secondary metabolite and ecological defense against herbivores. It functions as the primary pungent principle in chili peppers, measurable in Scoville Heat Units (SHU), ranging from 0 SHU in bell peppers to 3.18 million SHU in Pepper X.

¶Biosynthesis

Capsaicin biosynthesis follows a convergent pathway combining two precursor streams: the phenylpropanoid pathway and fatty acid metabolism. L-phenylalanine serves as the aromatic precursor, while valine or leucine provide aliphatic chain carbon skeletons. The pathway culminates in the vanilloid moiety—the characteristic aromatic ring bearing methoxy and hydroxyl groups—coupled to a long branched aliphatic tail. Capsaicin accumulates in epidermal vesicles of the fruit placenta, reaching maximum concentration 40–50 days post-anthesis.

¶TRPV1 Receptor Mechanism

Mammals perceive capsaicin as “heat” through activation of TRPV1 (transient receptor potential vanilloid 1), a cation channel in nociceptive nerve endings. The compound adopts a “tail-up, head-down” configuration within TRPV1’s vanilloid-binding pocket, formed by transmembrane segments. Binding involves hydrogen bonding and van der Waals interactions, stabilizing the open channel state through mechanical contact with the S4-S5 linker. This activation triggers sodium and calcium influx, generating the burning sensation characteristic of capsaicin consumption.

Birds lack functional TRPV1 receptors for capsaicin; the single amino acid divergence in avian TRPV1 confers insensitivity. This ecological distinction reflects the evolutionary strategy of Capsicum: the compound deters mammalian herbivores while remaining transparent to avian seed dispersers that lack mammalian taste aversion pathways.

¶Scoville Scale and Capsaicinoid Variants

The Scoville scale quantifies pungency as heat units per million dilutions required to eliminate sensation. Pure capsaicin registers 16 million SHU, while common variants include dihydrocapsaicin and nordihydrocapsaicin, which generate similar thermal sensations but differ in molecular structure and bioactivity.

¶Medical and Nutritional Applications

Capsaicin cream produces topical analgesia through transient TRPV1 desensitization in dermal afferents, approved clinically for arthralgia and neuropathic pain relief. Systemic capsaicin consumption increases metabolic rate and thermogenesis, though long-term metabolic effects remain modest. The compound exhibits antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties in in-vitro studies, yet clinical translation remains limited.
