Reynoutria

2026-04-02 A small genus of large rhizomatous perennial herbs in Polygonaceae, containing the Japanese, giant, and Bohemian knotweeds — among the world's most aggressive invasive plants and a source of major pharmaceutical compounds

Reynoutria Houtt. is a small genus of large, rhizomatous herbaceous perennials in the family Polygonaceae, native to East Asia. The genus contains the three species collectively known as the “knotweed complex” — Japanese knotweed (R. japonica), giant knotweed (R. sachalinensis), and their hybrid Bohemian knotweed (R. × bohemica) — which together constitute one of the most damaging groups of invasive plants in the temperate world.

Taxonomic history

The genus name Reynoutria was established by Maarten Houttuyn in 1777 for the species now known as Japanese knotweed. The subsequent taxonomic history is among the most confusing in angiosperm botany, involving at least three genera and dozens of name changes:

1777: Houttuyn describes Reynoutria japonica from Japanese material.

1784: Thunberg independently describes the same species as Polygonum cuspidatum, placing it in the large, broadly defined genus Polygonum.

1856–1857: Meissner subsumes Reynoutria into Polygonum sect. Tiniaria. For over a century, the knotweeds are known as Polygonum species.

1971: Holub resurrects Reynoutria as a distinct genus based on morphological characters (large stature, dioecious flowers, winged fruit).

1988: Ronse Decraene transfers the species to Fallopia (as Fallopia japonica), arguing that Reynoutria is not sufficiently distinct from Fallopia (which includes black bindweed, F. convolvulus). This name becomes widely adopted in ecological literature.

1990s–2010s: Molecular phylogenetic studies consistently show that the knotweeds form a well-supported monophyletic clade distinct from Fallopia sensu stricto. The clade is more closely related to Muehlenbeckia than to Fallopia convolvulus.

2010s–present: Based on molecular evidence, Plants of the World Online (POWO) at Kew and most recent taxonomic treatments restore Reynoutria as the accepted genus. Molecular data place Reynoutria in subtribe Reynoutriinae, closer to Muehlenbeckia than to Fallopia s.s. — confirming that lumping them into Fallopia was incorrect. The principal species in current use are:

Accepted name Common name Principal synonyms
Reynoutria japonica Houtt. Japanese knotweed Fallopia japonica, Polygonum cuspidatum
Reynoutria sachalinensis (F.Schmidt) Nakai Giant knotweed Fallopia sachalinensis, Polygonum sachalinense

R. multiflora (Chinese knotweed, He Shou Wu in TCM) is a significant medicinal plant but is not part of the invasive knotweed complex in Europe and North America. Additional species (R. forbesii and others) exist in the native East Asian range but are rarely encountered outside it.

In practice, all three naming systems remain in active use for the invasive complex. Polygonum cuspidatum dominates pharmaceutical and TCM literature. Fallopia japonica dominates ecological literature from 1990–2015. Reynoutria japonica is the currently accepted name and is increasingly used in new publications.

Morphological characters of the genus

Reynoutria species share the following diagnostic characters that distinguish them from other Polygonaceae:

Habit: Large herbaceous perennials, 1–5 m tall, dying back to the rhizome annually. Stems are hollow between nodes, erect, forming dense stands.

Rhizomes: Extensive, branching, penetrating to 3+ m depth. Dark brown externally, bright orange internally (a diagnostic character). Capable of regenerating from fragments containing a single node.

Leaves: Large (7–40 cm depending on species), alternate, petiolate, with a caducous (quickly falling) ochrea at each node.

Flowers: Small, white or cream, in axillary or terminal panicles. Functionally dioecious (male-sterile female plants and hermaphrodite plants with functional pollen). Tepals 5, the outer 3 winged in fruit.

Fruit: Trigonous (three-angled) achene, 2–4 mm, enclosed by the persistent winged perianth.

The three species: identification key

The three members of the knotweed complex can be distinguished by leaf characters:

Reynoutria japonica Houtt. — Japanese knotweed

  • Leaf size: 7–14 cm long
  • Leaf base: Truncate (straight-cut, squared-off) — the key diagnostic character
  • Leaf underside: Glabrous (hairless); scabrous (bearing minute papillae visible under magnification) along veins
  • Height: To 3 m (occasionally 4 m)
  • Stem diameter: To 2.5 cm
  • Ploidy: Octoploid (2n = 88) — this high ploidy contributes to its vigor

Reynoutria sachalinensis (F.Schmidt) Nakai — Giant knotweed

  • Leaf size: 15–40 cm long — dramatically larger than Japanese knotweed
  • Leaf base: Deeply cordate (heart-shaped) — the key diagnostic character
  • Leaf underside: Pubescent with multicellular trichomes (hairs) along veins — visible to the naked eye, distinctly hairy
  • Height: To 5 m — the tallest member of the complex
  • Stem diameter: To 3 cm
  • Ploidy: Tetraploid (2n = 44) in most populations

Reynoutria × bohemica Chrtek & Chrtková — Bohemian knotweed

  • Leaf size: 10–25 cm — intermediate between parents
  • Leaf base: Variable — ranges from slightly cordate to subcordate to truncate, often on the same plant. This variability is itself diagnostic: if leaves on a single stem show a mix of heart-shaped and flat bases, the plant is likely the hybrid.
  • Leaf underside: Intermediate — scattered short trichomes, less dense than giant knotweed but more than Japanese knotweed. Trichomes may be visible under a hand lens but not always obvious.
  • Height: To 4 m — intermediate
  • Ploidy: Hexaploid (2n = 66) — combining the octoploid Japanese and tetraploid giant genomes

The hybrid was first described by Chrtek and Chrtková in 1983 from material collected in the Czech Republic [chrtek1983]. It is now recognized as widespread wherever both parent species co-occur, and in some regions (parts of Germany, Belgium, and northeastern North America) it is more common than either parent. Unlike the largely clonal R. japonica, the Bohemian knotweed produces viable seed and shows substantial genetic diversity — making it a potentially more adaptable and dangerous invader than either parent [bailey2003].

Reproductive biology

The reproductive biology of the knotweed complex is unusual and has major implications for invasion dynamics:

Japanese knotweed outside its native range is almost entirely represented by a single female clone. In the British Isles, molecular studies have found that virtually all populations are genetically identical — descended from a single introduction, probably Siebold’s original 1850 material. This clone produces functional female flowers but no viable pollen. It cannot self-fertilize and cannot set seed without pollen from a compatible source.

Giant knotweed produces functional pollen. Where it co-occurs with Japanese knotweed, cross-pollination occurs, and the female Japanese knotweed plants produce hybrid seed — Bohemian knotweed.

Bohemian knotweed is fertile in both directions. It produces viable pollen and can be pollinated by either parent species or by other Bohemian individuals. This means Bohemian knotweed can backcross with Japanese knotweed (producing offspring intermediate between the hybrid and Japanese parent) and with giant knotweed, creating a complex hybrid swarm with a continuous range of morphological variation. This gene flow makes the knotweed complex increasingly difficult to classify into discrete species in areas where all three taxa co-occur.
