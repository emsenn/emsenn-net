Tropaeolum majus

Taxonomy and Distribution

Tropaeolum majus L. (Tropaeolaceae) is an annual herb native to the high-altitude cloud forests of the Andes (Peru and Bolivia), domesticated in pre-Columbian times. The genus Tropaeolum comprises ~90 species, most herbs and vines from South America; T. majus is the only species widely cultivated outside its native range. The plant is now grown globally in temperate and subtropical zones, from sea level to 2,500 m.

Morphology and Growth Architecture

Herbaceous annual herb with prostrate to climbing vines 1.5–3 m long (depending on cultivar and support). The stems are succulent, hollow, and glabrous. Leaves are alternate, long-petiolate (petiole 5–15 cm), with a peltate (shield-shaped) blade (5–10 cm diameter) and entire margins; the leaf base connects at the center of the blade, creating a distinctive umbrella-like form. The plant is pale to medium green, often with white variegation in ornamental cultivars (‘Alaska’, ‘Variegatum’).

Flowers are zygomorphic, with five sepals fused into a spur (5–10 mm), five petals (the two dorsal petals upright, three ventral petals smaller and fringed), and eight stamens with short filaments. Flower color ranges from bright yellow to orange-red to burgundy, depending on cultivar. Flowers appear continuously from mid-summer until first frost.

The fruit is a syncarpous schizocarp separating into three one-seeded indehiscent units (mericarps). Immature fruits are soft and unripe; mature fruits develop hard seeds.

Edibility and Flavor Chemistry

The entire plant—leaves, flowers, stems, and immature seeds—is edible with a distinctly peppery, mustard-like flavor derived from glucosinolates (particularly glucotropeolin) and their breakdown products (isothiocyanates). The concentration of glucosinolates is highest in young leaves and flowers, declining as leaves age. The pungency intensifies when leaves are crushed or cooked. Immature green seeds (collected 3–4 weeks post-anthesis) develop a caperlike texture and peppery taste; pickling in vinegar preserves them as “poor man’s capers”—a traditional preparation in Andean cuisine and contemporary farm-to-table cooking.

The flowers are ornamental and edible, used fresh in salads or as garnish. The leaves are tender and slightly succulent, excellent in salads or wilted as a quick-cooking green. The stems are edible when young and tender, with a mild flavor.

Pest Ecology and Aphid Trap Crop Function

Tropaeolum majus is one of the most effective natural trap crops for aphids, particularly black bean aphid (Aphis fabae Fabricius), cotton aphid (Aphis gossypii Mordvilko), and green peach aphid (Myzus persicae Sulzer). The mechanisms are multifold:

  1. Feeding preference: Nasturtium foliage is highly palatable to aphids; populations establish rapidly and reproduce prolifically on nasturtium compared to adjacent crops (tomato, squash, bean).

  2. Volatile attraction: Tropaeolum majus emits volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that attract aphids from distance—likely including aldehydes and terpenes—creating a semiochemical plume that diverts pest pressure from nearby crops.

  3. Population support: The abundant soft foliage supports high aphid densities (100–500 per plant) without significant plant damage or fitness loss. This concentrates pest populations on the trap crop, reducing infestation pressure on economically valuable adjacent plants.

  4. Parasitoid integration: High aphid densities on nasturtium attract parasitoid wasps (Aphidius, Diaeretiella, Lysiphlebus) and predatory insects (ladybeetles, lacewings, hoverflies), creating a biological control reservoir that overflows onto adjacent crops.

Cultivation and Phenology

Tropaeolum majus is a warm-season annual requiring frost-free conditions. Germination is rapid (7–10 days at 18–22°C). The plant is indeterminate in flowering: once established, it produces new flowers continuously until killed by frost. Day length is immaterial to flowering initiation; temperature and plant size are the primary drivers.

Cultivar Diversity

Ornamental breeding has produced numerous cultivars:

  • Trailing/vining (‘Jewel Mix’, ‘Moonlight’, ‘Peach Melba’): vigorous 2–3 m vines, ideal for trellising or ground cover.
  • Compact bush (‘Tip Top’, ‘Alaska’, ‘Whirlybird’): 0.3–0.5 m bushy plants, no support needed, suitable for containers.
  • Variegated foliage (‘Alaska Mix’, ‘Variegatum’): white-splashed leaves, ornamental but otherwise identical in flavor and pest function.
  • Double-flowered ornamentals (‘Hermine Grashoff’): fully double petals, minimal seed production, purely ornamental.

Agronomic cultivars (unnamed) are selected for robust growth, prolific flowering, and dense foliar mass that maximizes aphid population support.
