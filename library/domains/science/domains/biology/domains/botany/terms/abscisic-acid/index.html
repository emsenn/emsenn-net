Abscisic Acid

2026-04-02

Abscisic acid: a C₁₅ terpenoid hormone primarily regulating water-stress responses. Despite its name — historically derived from its role in abscission (leaf/fruit drop) — ABA is not the primary driver of abscission (that role belongs to ethylene). Instead, ABA is the plant’s central drought and stress-response hormone, coordinating stomatal closure, seed dormancy, and tolerance mechanisms across tissues. ABA accumulates rapidly in response to water deficit, activating a cascade that prioritizes survival over growth.

Biosynthesis and catabolism

Abscisic acid is synthesized indirectly from zeaxanthin through the carotenoid cleavage pathway: zeaxanthin → violaxanthin (via zeaxanthin epoxidase, ZEP/ABA1) → neoxanthin → xanthoxin (via 9-cis-epoxycarotenoid dioxygenase, NCED). NCED3 is the rate-limiting enzyme and is upregulated within minutes of water stress, allowing rapid ABA accumulation. The final step — conversion of abscisic aldehyde to ABA — is catalyzed by abscisic aldehyde oxidase (AAO3, which requires molybdenum cofactor from ABA3). Catabolism of ABA is mediated by CYP707A cytochrome P450 enzymes, which catalyze 8’-hydroxylation, eventually inactivating the hormone and allowing ABA levels to drop when stress subsides.

Perception and signaling

Abscisic acid is perceived by the PYR/PYL/RCAR family of receptor proteins (14 members in Arabidopsis), soluble START-domain proteins. ABA binding to PYR/PYL creates a surface that inhibits type 2C protein phosphatases (PP2Cs: ABI1, ABI2, HAB1, PP2CA). In the absence of ABA, these phosphatases dephosphorylate and inactivate SnRK2 kinases (SNF1-RELATED KINASE 2: SnRK2.2, SnRK2.3, SnRK2.6/OST1). When ABA binds PYR/PYL and PP2Cs are inhibited, SnRK2s autophosphorylate and activate downstream targets: AREB/ABF transcription factors (activating ABA-responsive element-containing genes), SLAC1 anion channel (causing stomatal closure), KAT1 potassium channel (inhibited, contributing to guard cell depolarization), and other effectors.

Developmental roles

Stomatal closure. ABA triggers rapid closure of stomata in response to drought, the most rapid adaptive response. ABA produced in roots under water stress is transported to leaves, where it activates guard cells’ S-type anion channels (SLAC1, SLAH3) and outward-rectifying K⁺ channels (GORK). Ions flow out, turgor drops, and the pore closes within minutes. This prevents further water loss while slowing photosynthesis and CO₂ uptake.

Seed dormancy. Abscisic acid maintains seed dormancy, especially in seeds from wet environments. High ABA keeps seeds dormant even if environmental conditions (moisture, temperature, light) are favorable for germination. The ratio of ABA to gibberellin determines whether seeds germinate: high ABA suppresses germination; gibberellin counteracts ABA and promotes germination through the aleurone layer’s synthesis of amylase.

Cold acclimation. Low temperatures increase ABA synthesis in leaves, triggering expression of cold-responsive genes (COR genes), including cryoprotective proteins and osmolytes (proline, betaine) that stabilize proteins and membranes. Sustained exposure to cold activates the ABA pathway for freezing tolerance.

Bud dormancy. ABA accumulates in terminal buds in autumn, preventing growth and promoting dormancy until sufficient chilling has occurred and daylength signals spring. The ABA:GA ratio controls when buds break dormancy in spring.

Growth inhibition under stress. ABA suppresses growth hormone synthesis (gibberellin, auxin) and promotes expression of growth-inhibitory genes, redirecting resources from growth toward stress tolerance. This trade-off — survival over expansion — is fundamental to ABA’s function.

ABA-GA antagonism

Throughout development, ABA and gibberellin act antagonistically. In seed germination, flowering, shoot elongation, and fruit ripening, the balance between these hormones determines the developmental outcome. Stress increases ABA, which suppresses GA signaling; relief from stress decreases ABA, allowing GA-driven growth to resume.

Ecological significance

ABA is the plant’s primary sensor of environmental water status. Climate change projections increasingly focus on whether plants can produce adequate ABA and stomatal responses to prevent catastrophic water loss during unprecedented drought. Tree dieback under extreme drought is partly a failure of ABA signaling to close stomata quickly or completely enough.
