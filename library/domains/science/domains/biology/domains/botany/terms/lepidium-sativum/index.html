Lepidium sativum

2026-04-02 Rapid-cycling annual from Brassicaceae, native to western Mediterranean and North Africa. Extremely fast germination and development (15–20 days); produces peppery, glucosinolate-rich seedlings and young leaves. Bolts quickly but allows many successive plantings in a season.

¶Taxonomy and Origin

Lepidium sativum L. (Brassicaceae) is a small annual herb native to semiarid regions of the Mediterranean basin and extending east into Central Asia and western India. The species has been cultivated since classical antiquity; references appear in Sumerian and Egyptian texts. The genus Lepidium comprises 200+ species, most native to the Americas and Africa, but L. sativum is the primary cultivated species.

¶Morphology

Lepidium sativum is a diminutive herb typically 20–50 cm tall with a single primary stem bearing alternate, pinnately compound or bipinnate leaves. The basal leaves are larger and less divided; upper stem leaves are progressively more finely lobed. The entire plant is glabrous and somewhat succulent.

Flowers are tetramerous, small (2–4 mm), pale pink or white, arranged in dense terminal and axillary racemes. The fruit is a distinctive silicle—a type of fruit characteristic of Brassicaceae—short, broad, oblong, notched at the apex, bearing two seeds per locule (four seeds total per fruit). Silicles are approximately 8–10 mm long, winged along the margins in some cultivars.

¶Rapid Phenological Development

Lepidium sativum has one of the shortest life cycles of any vegetable crop. Seed germinates in 2–4 days at 15–20°C. Seedlings produce true leaves by day 5–7. A harvestable rosette of tender leaves develops by 15–20 days under optimal conditions; full flowering occurs by 25–35 days. This extraordinary speed allows gardeners and farmers to harvest five to eight successive crops per growing season in temperate climates, and year-round in controlled environments.

Bolting is irreversible once initiated; plants transition directly from vegetative to reproductive growth with no intermediate phase. Seed maturity follows bolting by 10–14 days.

¶Biochemistry and Flavor

Like other Brassicaceae, Lepidium sativum accumulates glucosinolates, particularly sinalbin (which hydrolyzes to para-hydroxybenzyl isothiocyanate) and smaller amounts of sinigrin. The volatile isothiocyanates are released when leaf tissues are crushed or chewed, imparting a sharp, peppery, mustard-like burn. The intensity of pungency is greatest in seedlings and young leaves; mature leaves, while still peppery, are less volatile.

The species also produces erucic acid in seed oil, limiting its use as an oil crop compared to other Lepidium species bred for culinary oil production.

¶Ecology and Cultivation Breadth

In its native Mediterranean habitat, Lepidium sativum occupies disturbed, well-drained soils and is tolerant of mild salinity and poor fertility. The species tolerates a broad range of temperatures (germination from 10°C to 25°C) and is not particularly photoperiod-sensitive, making it suitable for succession planting throughout the growing season.

The plant is minimally affected by pests in most climates; cabbage white butterfly may occasionally infest plants destined for seed production. Fungal diseases are rare under normal conditions.

¶Culinary and Horticultural Uses

Lepidium sativum is primarily used as a spicy salad green, microgreen, and garnish. The seedling stage (5–10 days old) produces the most tender tissue and is increasingly popular in microgreens production. Mature plants are too coarse for salad use but are occasionally cooked as a potherb. Seeds are rarely used directly but have been employed historically in herbal medicine (reputed diuretic and antiscorbutic properties).

The species is commonly grown as a simple, fast, and reliable hobby crop for children, requiring only soil, water, and sunlight; seeds germinate reliably and plants develop visibly within days.
