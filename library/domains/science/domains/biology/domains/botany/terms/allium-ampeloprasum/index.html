Allium ampeloprasum

2026-04-02 Bulbous perennial in the amaryllis family, native to Mediterranean and Middle Eastern regions, with multiple cultivar groups including leek, kurrat, and ornamental types.

Allium ampeloprasum L. is a perennial bulbous species in the family Amaryllidaceae, native to the Mediterranean basin, North Africa, and southwestern Asia. It comprises multiple cultivar groups, the most economically important being the Porrum Group (culinary leeks), characterized by thick, elongated white shanks formed by soil blanching of leaf bases. Unlike the true wild leek of eastern North America (Allium tricoccum), A. ampeloprasum has commercial cultivars and a history of deliberate domestication spanning millennia.

¶Taxonomy and Cultivar Groups

The Porrum Group (leeks) consists of cultivars selected for rapid growth, large white shank development, and robust annual or biennial production. The Kurrat Group, popular in Middle Eastern cuisine, produces smaller, faster-maturing plants without pronounced shank development. Ornamental and wild types exist across Mediterranean coastal regions and warm Asia. Some perennial selections form small bulbils at the base and persist as hardy landscape plantings in temperate zones.

¶Morphology and Shank Development

Leeks develop 40-80 cm tall plants with a tubular white shank (the blanched basal portion) extending 10-20 cm, surrounded by broad, flattened, grayish-green leaves. The shank is not a true bulb but rather tightly overlapped leaf bases—the edible portion—extending from the basal plate to where chlorophyll develops at the transition to green foliage. Bulbils are rare in commercial cultivars but common in wild types and older perennial selections.

The inflorescence is a dense, rounded umbel (4-8 cm diameter) of pale purple to pink flowers, produced on a scape extending 60-100 cm in the second year of biennial types or during bolting in perennial selections.

¶Hardiness and Geographic Adaptation

A. ampeloprasum is hardy to USDA zone 5 with winter mulching and to zone 6 without protection. Perennial selections hardy to zone 5-9 can persist and regrow after mild winters, offering multiple harvests over years. This hardiness exceeded only by the hardier Allium tricoccum, making A. ampeloprasum unique among Allium as a cultivated perennial vegetable adapted to temperate climates.

¶Growing Season and Ecology

In temperate regions with cold winters, cultivated leeks overwinter as dormant plants, sprouting in late winter (February-March in northern hemisphere) and producing harvestable shanks by late spring or early summer. In milder zones, autumn planting yields winter and early-spring harvests. The species tolerates moist, organically rich soils and benefits from consistent moisture; it does not adapt to dry conditions as readily as garlic or onion.
