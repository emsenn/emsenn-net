Pollen

2026-04-02

Pollen: the male gametophyte of seed plants, produced within the anthers of the stamen. Each pollen grain is a compact cellular package containing the male gametes (sperm cells) and the tissues necessary to deliver them to the ovule. Pollen is the most abundant organism in the global biosphere by cell count and is fossilized with extraordinary resistance to degradation — pollen grains recovered from sediments millions of years old remain identifiable to species, making palynology (the study of pollen) a cornerstone of paleobotany and paleoclimatology.

¶Structure

A mature pollen grain is typically 20-100 micrometers in diameter. Its three-part structure encodes its life history:

Exine. The outer wall, composed of sporopollenin, a complex heteropolymer of phenylpropanoids and fatty acids. Sporopollenin is one of the most chemically stable biopolymers known, degraded only by concentrated sulfuric acid or prolonged oxidation. This extreme durability is why pollen grains persist in sediments for millions of years. The exine surface is often sculptured — smooth, granular, spiny, or reticulate — and these features are diagnostic for species identification. Anemophilous (wind-pollinated) pollen often has smoother, more hydrodynamic exine; entomophilous (insect-pollinated) pollen is often more sculptured, with stickier surfaces for adhesion.

Intine. The inner wall, composed of pectin and cellulose, more chemically fragile than the exine. The intine anchors the vegetative and generative cells and allows water uptake during pollen germination.

Protoplast. The living contents, consisting of two cells formed by meiosis and mitosis. The vegetative cell is larger and controls pollen tube growth and guidance. The generative cell is smaller and will divide — either before or after pollination, depending on species — to form two sperm cells (male gametes).

¶Gametogenesis and development

Pollen develops through microsporogenesis in the anther’s pollen sacs (microsporangia). Diploid microsporocytes undergo meiosis, producing tetrads of haploid microspores. As microspores develop into pollen grains, the vegetative nucleus undergoes mitosis to form the generative nucleus and vegetative nucleus. This two-celled stage is the mature pollen at shedding in many species; in others (grasses, some dicots), the generative cell divides before shedding, producing three-celled pollen.

¶Dispersal and pollination

Pollen is dispersed by four primary mechanisms:

Wind (anemophily). Dominant in grasses, sedges, conifers, and many trees. Wind-pollinated plants produce enormous pollen quantities — a single ragweed plant produces millions of pollen grains. Wind pollen is small, light, smooth-walled for aerodynamic transport, and lacks viscous coatings. Atmospheric pollen is the primary allergen in hay fever, as the human immune system recognizes pollen proteins as threats.

Insects (entomophily). The dominant pollination mode among flowering plants. Insect-pollinated pollen is larger, sticky, sculptured, and often colored. It is often provisioned with pollenkitt — a lipid-rich coating that increases adhesiveness and provides nutritional rewards. Bees collect pollen as a protein source; butterflies and moths pollinate while feeding on nectar; beetles, flies, and thrips are often secondary pollinators.

Water (hydrophily). Rare in flowering plants, more common in aquatic and semi-aquatic species. Seagrasses and a few aquatic flowering plants release pollen directly into water; their pollen has an elongated filamentous form to increase contact with female structures.

Vertebrates. Hummingbirds, sunbirds, and bats pollinate flowers adapted with high nectar rewards, strong colors (red for birds), or night-blooming with musty scents (for bats).

¶Pollen tube and double fertilization

Upon landing on a receptive stigma, a pollen grain germinates: the vegetative cell extends a pollen tube that grows through the style toward the ovule, guided by chemotactic signals (peptides, calcium gradients, lipids) emanating from the ovule. The pollen tube is a unique cellular extension — a long, thin tube of protoplasm extending from the vegetative cell and enclosed by the intine and a wall of callose. The tube grows by apical expansion and can traverse the entire style (up to several centimeters in some species) within hours.

The generative cell divides during pollen tube growth, forming two sperm cells. When the pollen tube penetrates the ovule, it ruptures within the embryo sac and releases the sperm. One sperm fertilizes the egg cell to form the diploid zygote (→ embryo); the other fuses with the central cell’s two polar nuclei to form the triploid endosperm. This double fertilization is the defining reproductive innovation of angiosperms.

¶Pollen as a research tool

Pollen is remarkably easy to observe under the microscope and has been central to plant evolutionary studies. Pollen morphology (size, wall texture, apertures) is used in taxonomy. Palynology has revolutionized our understanding of past vegetation and climate: pollen cores from lake sediments and ice sheets record shifts in plant communities over thousands of years. The pollen record is particularly sensitive to the transition from ice age to interglacial periods, revealing rapid vegetation shifts.
