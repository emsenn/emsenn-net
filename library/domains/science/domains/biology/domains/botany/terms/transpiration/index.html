Transpiration

2026-04-02

Transpiration: the evaporative loss of water vapor from plant surfaces, primarily through open stomata in leaves and green stems. While small amounts of water escape directly through the cuticle (cuticular transpiration), stomatal transpiration dominates — a single large tree can transpire 200 to 400 liters of water per day, an amount equal to the annual rainfall in some regions. Transpiration is thus a massive water flux through plants, far exceeding the water needed for growth or metabolic reactions.

Transpiration is essential to plant physiology for two reasons. First, it drives the transpiration stream — the continuous upward movement of water and dissolved mineral nutrients from roots to leaves. Water is pulled upward from the soil through the root’s xylem by the negative pressure (tension) generated at leaf surfaces. As water evaporates from leaf cells into the air spaces of the mesophyll (the photosynthetic tissue), the water potential of those cells becomes more negative, creating a water potential gradient that draws water in from the adjacent xylem. This pull propagates down through the xylem column to the roots, where the same water potential gradient draws soil water into the root. This passive, pressure-driven transport is elegant because it requires no metabolic energy in the xylem (the xylem is dead) — the energy is supplied by the sun, which evaporates the water.

The mechanism is often explained by the cohesion-tension theory (Dixon-Joly theory): water molecules are held together by hydrogen bonds (cohesion), forming a continuous column from root to leaf. Evaporation at the leaf surface creates a negative tension in this column, pulling the entire water chain upward. The theory is supported by evidence of tensions (negative pressures) measurable in the xylem and the observation that the xylem can be pulled apart (cavitation) under extreme stress, breaking the water column.

Transpiration is also the primary mechanism by which plants obtain mineral nutrients from the soil — water is the vehicle carrying dissolved minerals (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and trace elements) from the soil solution into the plant. Without transpiration, minerals would accumulate only near the root surface and would not reach developing leaves and growing tissues.

Guard cells in stomata regulate the rate of transpiration by controlling stomatal opening. In response to drought stress, abscisic acid (ABA) causes guard cells to lose turgor and close the stomata, reducing water loss. This creates a fundamental trade-off in plant physiology: open stomata allow photosynthesis (CO₂ uptake) but increase water loss; closed stomata conserve water but prevent carbon fixation. This tension between carbon gain and water conservation shapes plant form, ecology, and evolution, and is especially acute in arid and semi-arid environments.
