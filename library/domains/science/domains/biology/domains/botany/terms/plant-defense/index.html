Plant Defense

2026-04-02

Plant defense: the suite of physical, chemical, and behavioral strategies plants use to resist herbivory, pathogen attack, and environmental stress. Plants cannot flee or fight back directly, so they have evolved an astonishing arsenal: thorns and spines, toxic alkaloids, protease inhibitors, volatile alarm signals, and systemic resistance networks that rival animal immune systems in complexity.

Constitutive defenses

Constitutive (always-present) defenses are maintained regardless of herbivore pressure. These are costly to produce but provide baseline protection:

Physical barriers:

  • Spines and thorns. Sharp, hardened structures that deter large herbivores. Modified leaves (spines in cacti, stems in roses) or stipules (thorns in Acacia) are effective against browsing mammals.
  • Trichomes. Fine hairs on leaves and stems, often with toxic secretions. Glandular trichomes (hairs with secretory cells) actively produce defensive compounds; non-glandular trichomes create physical barriers.
  • Thick cuticle and wax layer. The waxy coating on leaves deters small arthropods and reduces spore adhesion.
  • Silica bodies. Phytoliths of silica, embedded in cell walls, make plants physically harder to chew. Grasses are particularly rich in silica, which wears down herbivore teeth.
  • Lignification and vascular reinforcement. Cell walls fortified with lignin are harder to break down and digest.

Chemical barriers:

  • Alkaloids. Toxic, nitrogen-containing compounds (nicotine, caffeine, cocaine, strychnine) that disrupt neurotransmission, respiration, or other critical processes in herbivores. Alkaloid-rich tissues are typically avoided by non-specialist herbivores.
  • Glucosinolates. Bitter, sulfur-containing compounds found especially in Brassicaceae. Upon tissue damage, glucosinolates are hydrolyzed by myrosinase enzymes, releasing volatile isothiocyanates.
  • Tannins. Phenolic polymers that bind proteins, reducing digestibility. High tannin levels in oak leaves make them poor food; herbivores must overcome this through specialized gut chemistry or high feeding rates.
  • Terpenes. Volatile hydrocarbons that repel insects or act as toxins. Essential oils in mint, sage, and other aromatic plants are terpene-rich.
  • Cardiac glycosides. Steroids (cardenolides) that disrupt heart function. Milkweed and digitalis (Digitalis) contain cardenolides; specialist insects sequester them as personal defenses.
  • Latex and resin. Sticky secretions that gum up insect mouthparts or trap them entirely. Opium poppy latex is famously alkaloid-rich.

Induced defenses

Induced defenses are activated in response to herbivory or pathogen attack, allowing the plant to allocate resources only when needed. Induction is mediated by rapid signaling cascades triggered by wounding:

Jasmonate signaling. When leaves are damaged by chewing herbivores, cell damage causes the release of linolenic acid from chloroplast membranes. This is converted via the octadecanoid pathway to jasmonic acid (JA) and the bioactive jasmonoyl-isoleucine (JA-Ile). JA-Ile is perceived by the COI1 receptor, triggering degradation of JAZ repressor proteins and activation of MYC2 and other transcription factors. This cascade activates the production of protease inhibitors — enzymes that jam up herbivore digestive systems — and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that attract predators and parasitoids of the herbivores.

Protease inhibitors. Within hours of herbivory, induced plants produce serine and cysteine protease inhibitors that bind herbivore digestive enzymes, reducing food conversion efficiency. A herbivore forced to eat high-protease-inhibitor leaves must increase consumption 2-3 fold to gain the same nutrition.

Secondary metabolites. Induced plants increase alkaloid, glucosinolate, and terpene production, rendering previously palatable tissues toxic. Some plants can distinguish between chewing herbivores and mechanical damage (wind-driven) and induce defenses selectively in response to actual herbivory.

Volatile organic compounds (VOCs). Damaged plants release a “chemical cry” — a blend of VOCs including green-leaf volatiles (GLVs), terpenes, and indole. These volatiles signal to predatory insects (ladybugs, parasitoid wasps) and predatory mites that prey is available, recruiting them to the plant. Some species can even distinguish between herbivores — a plant attacked by one herbivore species can emit a VOC blend that specifically attracts the parasitoids of that species.

Systemic acquired resistance

After local infection by a pathogen, plants develop enhanced resistance in distant, uninfected tissues — a phenomenon called systemic acquired resistance (SAR). The signaling hormone is salicylic acid (SA). After pathogen detection, SA is synthesized and transported throughout the plant, priming cells for rapid defense against future pathogen attacks. Primed tissues upregulate pathogenesis-related (PR) genes encoding antimicrobial enzymes and proteins. If a second pathogen arrives, the primed plant responds faster and more strongly than an uninfected plant.

Ecological signaling among plants

Evidence suggests that plants can “hear” their neighbors’ defensive VOCs and upregulate their own defenses preemptively. When one plant in a population detects herbivore damage and releases VOCs, neighboring plants increase their defenses even before being attacked. This kin-selection or group-benefit mechanism — where defending oneself helps relatives resist the same herbivores — has been documented in grasses and in lab systems with Arabidopsis. Controversy persists about whether this is adaptive or an incidental consequence of VOC perception.

Defense against specific herbivores

Over evolutionary time, plants and herbivores engage in an arms race, each driving the evolution of the other:

Specialist herbivores. Some insects specialize on defended plants, evolving the ability to tolerate or even sequester defensive compounds. Monarch butterflies feed on milkweed despite (and because of) its toxic cardenolides, sequestering them as personal defense against bird predators. Pieris butterflies specialize on glucosinolate-rich Brassicaceae, using glucosinolates as host-finding cues.

Plant counter-evolution. In response to specialist herbivores, plants diversify their chemical defenses, producing multiple alkaloids or glucosinolate variants, making it harder for any single specialist to overcome all variants.

Defense trade-offs

Constitutive defenses are metabolically expensive — a plant that invests heavily in thorns and alkaloids has fewer resources for growth and reproduction. Thus, plants allocate defenses strategically:

  • High defense in young leaves. Young leaves are energetically valuable; they receive disproportionate protection.
  • Lower defense in mature leaves. Mature leaves are cheaper to replace; herbivory on them is tolerated.
  • Induced over constitutive. In low-herbivory environments, plants reduce costly constitutive defenses and rely on rapid induction, saving resources.
