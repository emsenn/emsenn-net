Carotenoid

2026-04-02 Lipid-soluble C40 terpenoid pigments producing yellow, orange, and red coloration in plants; include carotenes (β-carotene, lycopene) and xanthophylls (lutein, zeaxanthin).

Carotenoids are a diverse class of over 600 lipid-soluble pigments composed of C40 tetraterpene backbones assembled from eight 5-carbon isoprene units joined in tail-to-head configuration. They produce yellow, orange, and red coloration throughout plant tissues and are the dominant visual signal for fruit ripeness in seed-dispersal ecology. Unlike water-soluble anthocyanins, carotenoids partition into lipid bilayers and plastoglobuli (lipid droplets within chloroplasts).

Chemical Structure and Classification

The carotenoid skeleton comprises 40 carbons arranged in a linear conjugated polyene chain containing typically nine conjugated double bonds. At either end of the polyene lie varied functional groups defining subclasses: carotenes contain only carbon and hydrogen and are either linear or cyclized at one or both terminals; xanthophylls are oxygenated carotenes bearing hydroxyl, keto, or epoxide moieties.

β-carotene exemplifies the linear carotene: two β-ionone rings linked through 11 conjugated double bonds, giving a symmetric C40 structure. Lycopene, acyclic and with 13 conjugated double bonds, is a linear carotene characteristic of ripe tomato fruit. Lutein and zeaxanthin are xanthophylls bearing hydroxyl groups, abundant in leafy greens and functioning as photoprotective blue-light-absorbing pigments in the macula lutea of human retinas.

Biosynthesis and Cyclization

Carotenoid biosynthesis begins with condensation of eight isoprene units (geranylgeranyl pyrophosphate) to form phytoene, the C40 precursor. Successive desaturations introduce conjugated double bonds, producing the yellow-pigmented lycopene intermediate. Lycopene cyclase (LCY) catalyzes cyclization of lycopene’s terminal ends, introducing β-ionone rings to form β-carotene or asymmetrically cyclizing one end to produce α-carotene. This cyclization is reversible: free lycopene accumulates in ripe tomato fruit partly because lycopene cyclase is downregulated during ripening, maximizing the red linear form.

Photosynthetic and Photoprotective Functions

In chloroplasts, carotenoids function as accessory light-harvesting pigments absorbing photons (400–500 nm) and transferring excitation energy to chlorophyll-a. More critically, they quench harmful singlet oxygen (¹O₂) generated during photosynthesis when excess light exceeds electron-transport capacity. This photoprotection prevents photoinhibition and lipid peroxidation—a process explaining why carotenoid-deficient mutants exhibit stunted growth and chlorotic tissues under high light.

Nutritional and Evolutionary Perspectives

β-carotene (and α-carotene) serves as provitamin A: intestinal enzymes cleave the β-ionone ring asymmetrically, yielding retinol (vitamin A) essential for vision and immune function in humans. Humans evolved no carotenoid biosynthetic capacity and depend entirely on dietary absorption; this nutritional reliance drove agricultural selection for high-carotenoid crops.

The orange carrot illustrates human-directed evolution: wild Daucus carota subspecies were white and purple (anthocyanin-rich) with minimal carotenoid content. Dutch breeders in the 17th century selected for orange mutants with elevated β-carotene production, inadvertently creating the iconic crop variety—demonstrating how pigment chemistry can be reshaped through selective breeding.

Ecological and Ripeness Signaling

Carotenoid accumulation during fruit ripening coincides with chlorophyll degradation, allowing the yellow-orange-red palette to emerge. This visible maturation signal evolved to attract seed-dispersing animals; primates and birds with trichromatic vision can discriminate ripe (red) from unripe (green) fruit, maximizing seed viability through selective consumption. The evolutionary arms race has tuned both pigment production and consumer visual systems to the same wavelengths.
