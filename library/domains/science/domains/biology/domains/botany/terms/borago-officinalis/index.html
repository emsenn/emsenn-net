Borago officinalis

2026-04-02 Annual herb in the borage family with coarse, hairy leaves and distinctive star-shaped bright blue flowers, one of the few truly blue edible flowers, with cucumber-flavored leaves and exceptional pollinator value.

Borago officinalis L., commonly called borage, is an annual herbaceous plant in the family Boraginaceae (borage family), native to the Levant and Mediterranean regions. It is distinguished by coarse, hispid (bristly) leaves and distinctive inflorescences bearing star-shaped flowers with five reflexed petals in a deep, bright blue color (occasionally pink or white). The plant develops a long taproot, does not transplant successfully, and leaves contain volatile oils and mucopolysaccharides producing a mild cucumber aroma and flavor.

Morphology and Cultivation Characteristics

Borage grows 60-100 cm tall with a branching, somewhat ungainly habit. Stems and leaves are covered with fine, stinging hairs (trichomes) that release irritating compounds when crushed. Leaves are alternate, simple, coarsely veined, and 4-15 cm long, blue-green in color. The flowers are borne in terminal, glandular-hairy cymes; each flower is 2-3 cm diameter with five petals reflexed and spreading, revealing prominent stamens in the center. The flowers are rarely self-pollinating and depend on insect visitation for fruit set.

The long taproot descends 30-60 cm and does not tolerate transplanting—borage must be direct-seeded into its final location. Germination is rapid (5-10 days) in warm soil (18-25°C). The plant matures in approximately 60-70 days from sowing to first flowering.

Chemistry

Leaves contain 0.01-0.05 percent volatile oil, dominated by volatile compounds producing a mild, fresh, cucumber-like aroma. The plant also accumulates mucopolysaccharides (0.5-2 percent fresh weight), contributing to a slightly viscous texture. Minerals, particularly potassium and calcium, accumulate in leaves. The flowers contain no volatile oil but develop intense blue color from anthocyanin pigmentation, particularly delphinidin glycosides. The blue color is notably stable, remaining vibrant even when dried.

Ecology and Pollinator Value

Borage is native to Mediterranean regions where it grows as a summer annual in disturbed areas and grasslands. It has naturalized throughout temperate regions and is now pantropical in distribution. The flowers are exceptionally attractive to diverse bee species, including honey bees (Apis mellifera), bumblebees (Bombus species), and solitary bees (Andrena, Halictus, Osmia species). Flowers also attract butterflies, hoverflies, and parasitic wasps. The plant is ranked among the most valuable pollinator plants in temperate gardens, with continuous bloom from mid-summer until frost attracting insects throughout the season.

Culinary and Medicinal Roles

Borage leaves have been used since medieval times in cooling beverages and salads, imparting a light, refreshing cucumber flavor. Young leaves are edible when their hairs are softened by cooking or blending. The flowers are among the few truly blue edible flowers—most “blue” flowers are actually purple, violet, or indigo. Borage flowers are valued in contemporary cuisine as a garnish for cocktails, salads, and desserts, and are crystallized for decorative use on cakes and confections.

Traditionally, borage has been used in herbal medicine for respiratory complaints and as a cooling herb. The seeds accumulate gamma-linolenic acid (GLA), a polyunsaturated fatty acid, and borage oil supplements have been studied for anti-inflammatory properties in clinical trials. Global cultivation for culinary flowers is concentrated in temperate regions, with smaller-scale production in Mediterranean and European specialty markets.
