Brassicaceae

2026-04-02 Mustard/crucifer family — ~372 genera, ~4,060 species. Diagnostic 4-petaled cross-shaped (cruciform) flower, tetradynamous stamens (4 long + 2 short), glucosinolate-myrosinase defense system. Includes cole crops (cabbage, broccoli, kale), spices, and important model organism Arabidopsis.

Brassicaceae (the mustard, cabbage, or crucifer family) is a medium-sized angiosperm family comprising approximately 372 genera and 4,060 species distributed worldwide, with highest diversity in temperate and Mediterranean regions. The family includes essential food crops, medicinal plants, ornamentals, and the genetic model organism Arabidopsis thaliana.

¶Diagnostic Morphology

The defining characteristics of Brassicaceae are consistent and recognizable across the family:

Flowers exhibit the characteristic cruciform (cross-shaped) structure: four petals arranged in a cross pattern (thus the former family name Cruciferae, meaning “cross-bearing”). The sepals number four. This tetramerous flower is unique to Brassicaceae and is immediately diagnostic.

Stamens are six in number, arranged in a pattern called tetradynamous: four longer stamens and two shorter stamens. This arrangement is also characteristic of the family and aids in early identification.

Fruit is a specialized pod called a silique (when elongate) or silicle (when roughly as wide as long). The fruit consists of two valves (carpels) separated by a persistent replum (a membranous partition). Seeds are arranged in two rows on the replum and are released when the valves dehisce and fall away at maturity. The silique/silicle morphology is key to genus-level identification within the family.

Leaves are typically alternate, simple to pinnately lobed, and entire or serrate. Basal leaves are often rosette-forming in biennial species.

¶Chemistry: The Glucosinolate-Myrosinase Defense System

Brassicaceae are chemically defined by their production of glucosinolates—a class of nitrogen- and sulfur-containing secondary metabolites unique to the family. Glucosinolates are non-toxic precursors stored in cell vacuoles. The family also produces the enzyme myrosinase, housed in specialized cells separate from glucosinolates.

When plant tissue is damaged (by herbivory, cutting, or chewing), cell compartments rupture, bringing glucosinolates into contact with myrosinase. The enzyme catalyzes hydrolysis of the glucosinolate, releasing volatile, acrid compounds called isothiocyanates (mustard oils), thiocyanates, and nitriles. These breakdown products are pungent, toxic to many organisms, and serve as feeding deterrents and antimicrobial agents.

The specific glucosinolate profile varies by genus and species: Brassica species accumulate glucobrassicin; Raphanus accumulates glucoraphanin (released as sulforaphane); Lepidium and related genera show different profiles. This chemical diversity is thought to reflect evolutionary adaptation to different herbivore and pathogen communities.

Human consumers experience the pungency of isothiocyanates as the sharp, biting taste of mustard, horseradish, radish, and wasabi. Cooking denatures myrosinase, abolishing the “mustard oil bomb” reaction and making Brassica crops milder in flavor. Some studies suggest isothiocyanates have potential anti-cancer properties through induction of detoxification enzymes in human cells, though conclusive epidemiological evidence remains limited.

¶Phylogeny and Diversity

Brassicaceae belongs to order Capparales (or Brassicales, depending on classification system; APG IV places them in Brassicales). The family’s internal phylogeny has been extensively studied, particularly because of the importance of Arabidopsis thaliana as a genetic model. Modern molecular phylogenetics recognizes multiple clades, though formal subfamily taxonomy remains unstable.

The five largest genera are Lepidium (700+ species), Draba (350+ species), Rorippa (150+ species), Capsella (40+ species), and Brassica (35+ species). Notably, Brassica is small in species count but enormous in economic importance and phenotypic diversity—a single species, B. oleracea, has diversified into cabbage, broccoli, kale, Brussels sprouts, and collards through artificial selection.

¶Ecological and Agricultural Importance

In their native habitats, Brassicaceae occupy temperate and Mediterranean zones, thriving in disturbed sites, rocky slopes, and cool-season habitats. Many are weeds in agricultural systems (Sinapis arvensis, charlock; Brassica rapa, wild mustard). Others are pioneer plants colonizing recently cleared or burned land.

Seed dispersal is primarily wind-mediated via the winged siliques or silicles; some species show zoochory (animal dispersal) via adhesive or buoyant fruits.

Food crops: Brassica oleracea (cabbage, broccoli, kale, etc.) is one of the world’s major vegetables. Brassica rapa (turnip, bok choy, mizuna) is important in Asian and European cuisines. Raphanus sativus (radish) is widely cultivated. Lepidium sativum (garden cress) and Barbarea verna (land cress) are salad greens. Sinapis alba (white mustard) produces mustard condiment.

Spices and condiments: Wasabi (from Wasabia japonica, though some commercial wasabi uses Raphanus), horseradish (Armoracia rusticana), and brown and black mustard seeds (Brassica juncea and B. nigra).

Medicinal and phytochemical interest: The potential chemopreventive properties of isothiocyanates have made Brassica crops subjects of extensive nutritional epidemiology research.

Model organism: Arabidopsis thaliana is the most widely used genetic and genomic model organism in plant biology, with a fully sequenced genome and thousands of mutants available.
