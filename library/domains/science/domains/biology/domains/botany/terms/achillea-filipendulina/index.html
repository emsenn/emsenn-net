Achillea filipendulina

2026-04-02 Perennial herb from Asteraceae, native to Central Asia (Caucasus, western Asia). Hardy zones 3-9. Herbaceous perennial 0.8-1.5 m tall with finely pinnately-divided feathery foliage (hence "filipendulina" = threadlike leaves). Produces dense, flat-topped inflorescences (corymbs) of golden-yellow tiny disk florets. Named for Achilles—historically used as wound treatment for millennia (Trojan War mythology). Dye plant: quercetin and luteolin produce yellow-gold color. Flat-topped flower clusters function as landing pads for beneficial insects (parasitoid wasps, tachinid flies). Drought-tolerant, long-lived, cut-flower cultivar.

¶Taxonomy and Distribution

Achillea filipendulina Lam. (Asteraceae, tribe Anthemideae) is a perennial herb native to the Caucasus, western Asia, and central Asian mountains (1,500–3,500 m elevation). The genus Achillea comprises ~140 species distributed across temperate Eurasia and North America; A. filipendulina is one of the most robust and ornamentally significant. The species was introduced to European gardens in the 19th century and is now widely cultivated globally in temperate zones.

¶Morphology and Foliage

Herbaceous perennial 0.8–1.5 m tall with erect, unbranched to sparsely branched stems. Leaves are alternate, finely pinnately-divided (2–3× pinnate), creating feathery, thread-like foliage (hence the species epithet filipendulina, from Latin filum = thread, pendulum = hanging). Individual leaflets are extremely narrow (linear-filiform, <1 mm wide). The leaf surface is typically glabrous or very finely pubescent, often with a slight glaucous (waxy) appearance.

The foliage has a characteristic feathery appearance that distinguishes A. filipendulina from the coarser-leaved common yarrow (A. millefolium). Crushed foliage releases a pungent, aromatic odor—characteristic of Achillea and related Asteraceae—derived from volatile essential oils (primarily 1,8-cineole and camphor).

¶Inflorescence and Flower Structure

The inflorescence is a dense, flat-topped corymb (a cymose cluster with a flat or slightly convex surface) measuring 8–15 cm diameter, composed of hundreds to thousands of tiny disk florets packed closely together. Individual florets are yellow and perfect (hermaphrodite), with five fused petals, five stamens, and a two-lobed stigma. Ray florets are absent. The flowers are borne on achenes (single-seeded fruits) enclosed in chaffy bracts.

The corymb structure is distinctive: the closely-packed, flat-topped cluster of flowers presents a large, unified “landing platform” that attracts numerous small insects, particularly parasitoid wasps, tachinid flies, and small native bees.

¶Dye Chemistry and Historical Use

Achillea filipendulina is a dye plant producing yellow and gold colors. The coloration is derived from flavonoid aglycones, particularly:

  1. Quercetin (3,5,7,3’,4’-pentahydroxyflavone): major flavonoid, producing bright yellow in plant tissue and dye baths.
  2. Luteolin (5,7,3’,4’-tetrahydroxyflavone): secondary flavonoid, contributing to yellow-gold hue.

Extraction: Fresh or dried foliage is heated in water (160–180°F) for 30–60 minutes; the resulting dye liquor is bright yellow. Mordanting (pre-treating fiber with metal salts) shifts the color toward olive, gold, or bronze depending on the mordant (alum produces bright yellow; iron produces olive-bronze).

Yarrow has been used as a dye plant for millennia in European and Asian textile traditions. The Trojan War mythology identifies Achilles as the plant’s namesake: the hero treated wounded soldiers with yarrow poultices, giving the plant profound historical and symbolic significance. Contemporary dyers value A. filipendulina for its consistency and bright, stable yellow dyes.

¶Ecological Role and Beneficial Insect Attraction

The flat-topped flower cluster is morphologically specialized as a landing platform for small insects. Parasitoid wasps (Aphelinidae, Braconidae, Ichneumonidae), tachinid flies (Tachinidae), and minute anthomyiid flies (Anthomyiidae) forage for nectar and pollen at the densely-packed florets. Many of these insects are natural enemies of agricultural and garden pests (aphids, caterpillars, spider mites); the abundance of small flowers on a single inflorescence maximizes foraging efficiency for these tiny parasitoids.

In native Central Asian mountains, A. filipendulina is visited by native bees and various fly taxa; pollination is obligate for seed set.

¶Historical Medicinal Use

Yarrow’s association with Achilles in Greek mythology led to its use in Greco-Roman and medieval European medicine as a wound-healing herb. Topical poultices of yarrow leaves were applied to arrow wounds (hence the application to Achilles’ mythological practice). Contemporary herbalism and historical texts document yarrow as a vulnerary (wound healer), with active compounds including sesquiterpenes and flavonoids that demonstrate mild anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties in vitro. However, clinical evidence for internal use is limited, and the plant is less widely used medicinally today than in historical times.

¶Reproductive Biology

The plant is largely self-compatible but also outcrosses via insect pollination. A single plant produces thousands of tiny seeds; seed set is prolific when pollinator visitation is adequate. Seeds germinate readily in spring or fall with no dormancy requirement.

¶Cultivar Diversity

Named cultivars of A. filipendulina include:

  • ‘Cloth of Gold’: bright golden-yellow flowers, 1 m tall, excellent cut flower, reliable and widely available.
  • ‘Gold Plate’: very large flower heads (15+ cm), bright yellow, tall (1.2–1.5 m), outstanding ornamental.
  • ‘Coronation Gold’ (hybrid with A. clypeolata): flat-topped golden-yellow flowers, 0.8–1 m, compact, excellent for borders.

Wild-type A. filipendulina (unseeded) is available from seed companies; plants vary slightly in height and flower intensity.

¶Related Species

Achillea millefolium (common yarrow) is more coarsely divided and more sprawling; it is equally useful as a dye plant and beneficial insect attractor but lacks the architectural elegance of A. filipendulina. A. ‘Moonshine’ (hybrid) produces pale yellow flowers and very fine foliage.
