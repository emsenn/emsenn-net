Common Bean

2026-04-02 Annual legume native to Mesoamerica, cultivated worldwide for nitrogen-fixing capacity and edible seeds

Common Bean (Phaseolus vulgaris)

Phaseolus vulgaris is an herbaceous annual legume in the subfamily Papilionoideae, native to Mesoamerica and domesticated in multiple independent centers over millennia. The species encompasses both determinate (bush) and indeterminate (climbing) growth habits, with exceptional morphological diversity across thousands of landraces and cultivars.

Taxonomy and Distribution

Phaseolus vulgaris belongs to the genus Phaseolus in the tribe Phaseoleae. Its closest wild relatives occur in Mexico and Central America. The species has become cosmopolitan through cultivation and is the most widely consumed legume globally after the soybean.

Morphology

Plants produce compound trifoliate leaves on climbing or erect stems, typically 30–60 cm tall in bush types and 2–3 m in pole types. Flowers are small, zygomorphic, and occur in white, pink, red, or bi-colored varieties. Pods are typically indehiscent, containing 4–8 seeds per pod. Seed morphology exhibits extraordinary diversity: shape ranges from spherical to reniform to elongate; color varies through white, black, red, purple, speckled, and striped patterns. This morphological plasticity reflects centuries of artificial selection across different agroecological zones.

Rhizobial Symbiosis and Nitrogen Fixation

Phaseolus vulgaris establishes symbiotic associations with rhizobial bacteria, primarily Rhizobium etli in centers of origin, along with R. leguminosarum bv. phaseoli, R. gallicum, and R. giardinii. This symbiosis generates nitrogen-fixing nodules on roots where atmospheric N₂ is reduced to ammonia and made available to the plant in exchange for plant-derived carbohydrates.

The efficiency of nitrogen fixation in common bean is notably lower than in soybeans or alfalfa, reflecting the plant’s relatively shallow nodulation response and dependence on soil nitrogen availability. Despite moderate fixation rates, beans significantly reduce nitrogen mining from soil and increase residual soil nitrogen for subsequent crops.

Seed Chemistry

Bean seeds contain high proportions of protein (19–25% dry weight) and carbohydrates, along with bioactive secondary metabolites including tannins, lectins, and protease inhibitors that require cooking to denature. Lipid content remains low (1–2%), distinguishing beans from oilseeds. Mineral composition is substantial in potassium, phosphorus, magnesium, and iron, though phytate chelation reduces bioavailability without traditional soaking and cooking methods.

Ecological Role

As a legume, Phaseolus vulgaris participates in nitrogen cycling critical to soil fertility maintenance, particularly in polyculture systems. In the traditional Mesoamerican milpa system, beans climb maize stalks while enriching soil for associated crops. Wild populations function as pioneer species in disturbed habitats, though cultivated populations depend entirely on human management.

Cultivation History

Archaeological evidence places bean domestication in Mexico around 5,000–7,000 years ago, with secondary domestication centers in the Andean region producing distinct seed morphologies. European colonization introduced beans to Europe, Africa, and Asia in the 16th century, where they became fundamental dietary staples. Modern breeding has produced cultivars selected for bush growth, disease resistance, pod texture, and synchronous pod maturity for mechanical harvest.
