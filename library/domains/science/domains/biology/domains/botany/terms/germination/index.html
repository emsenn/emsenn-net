germination

2026-04-02 The process by which a seed emerges from dormancy and develops into a seedling through imbibition, radicle emergence, and shoot elongation

Germination is the resumption of growth by a seed following dormancy, culminating in radicle emergence (protrusion of the primary embryonic root) and, subsequently, shoot elongation. It is a tightly regulated developmental process controlled by environmental conditions and endogenous signals.

¶Phases of Germination

Germination occurs in three distinct phases:

Phase I: Imbibition. The dry seed rapidly absorbs water, swelling to many times its original volume. Water penetrates the seed coat and rehydrates tissues, activating metabolic enzyme systems. Imbibition occurs passively regardless of viability; even non-viable seeds will imbibe and swell.

Phase II: Metabolic Reactivation. Enzyme activity increases, respiration accelerates, and DNA repair mechanisms restore genetic integrity in seeds that may have accumulated damage during dormancy. Growth remains minimal during this phase. The duration of Phase II is species-dependent: it can last from hours to weeks.

Phase III: Radicle Emergence and Growth. The radicle (primary root) elongates and penetrates the seed coat, emerging as the first visible sign of germination. The root cap protects the root apical meristem as it grows into the soil environment. Simultaneously, the hypocotyl (in dicots) or coleoptile (in monocots) elongates, pushing the shoot apical meristem and cotyledons toward the soil surface. Seedling development continues until the plant is photosynthetically competent.

¶Environmental Requirements

Four primary conditions determine germination success:

Water. Seeds require water uptake to hydrate tissues and activate metabolism. Water availability must be continuous; seeds subjected to wet-dry cycles may lose viability or reenter dormancy.

Temperature. Germination rate increases with temperature; a 1°C rise in seed temperature increases respiration rate and germination speed by approximately 10%. Each species has an optimal germination temperature—temperate species typically optimize between 15–25°C, while tropical species require 25–35°C. Temperatures above 35°C are inhibitory as heat denatures enzymes. Many species exhibit thermodormancy, a state in which seeds fail to germinate above a threshold temperature; for example, mache and corn salad fail to germinate at temperatures above 68°F (20°C).

Oxygen. Most seeds require adequate aerobic respiration during germination. Anaerobic conditions inhibit radicle emergence in most species, though some can respire anaerobically for limited periods.

Light. Some species require light for germination; others are indifferent; a few are inhibited by light. Light requirement is detected through phytochrome and is adaptive—species of open habitats often require light for germination, ensuring seeds do not germinate beneath leaf litter.

¶Germination Speed Variation

Species differ dramatically in germination rates. Rapid germinators include garden cress (2–3 days) and radish (3–7 days); these are often used in teaching. Slow germinators include parsley (14–30 days), carrots (14–21 days), and parsnip (20–30 days), requiring patience in cultivation.

¶Seed Viability and Vigor

Seed viability is the proportion of seeds capable of germinating under optimal conditions. Viability decreases with age, improper storage, and exposure to environmental stress; seeds stored cool and dry may remain viable for years or decades, while seeds in warm, moist conditions lose viability within months.

Seed vigor encompasses germination speed, uniformity of emergence, and capacity of seedlings to establish in suboptimal field conditions. High-vigor seeds germinate rapidly, produce uniform seedlings, and establish reliably. Vigor is heritable but degraded by age and stress; it is measured through accelerated aging tests and cold tests in commercial seed testing.
