Coevolution

Coevolution: the reciprocal evolutionary change between two or more species, each exerting selective pressure on the other. In plant-animal interactions, coevolution produces some of the most spectacular and precise adaptations in nature — flower shapes fitting pollinator morphologies, seed toxins countered by herbivore detoxification mechanisms, fruit colors matching pollinator vision — all reflecting millions of years of reciprocal change.

Plant-pollinator coevolution

The relationship between flowering plants and their pollinators is the classic example of coevolution. Darwin’s observations of orchid flowers led him to propose that flowers must coevolve with their pollinators, even before the pollinator was known. His prediction — that Angraecum sesquipedale, an orchid with a 11-inch nectary spur, must be pollinated by a moth with a correspondingly long proboscis — seemed absurd to contemporary biologists. Yet in 1903, the hawk-moth Xanthopan morganii was discovered with a 10-inch tongue, confirming Darwin’s evolutionary foresight.

Mechanisms of plant-pollinator coevolution

Flowers evolve traits that attract specific pollinators:

  • Morphology. Flower shape, size, and orientation evolve to fit the pollinator’s body. Hummingbird-pollinated flowers are typically long and tubular, matching the hummingbird’s bill; bee-pollinated flowers have landing platforms; moth-pollinated flowers are often pale and night-blooming, matching nocturnal moth vision.

  • Color. Flower color coevolves with pollinator color vision. Bees see ultraviolet and blue; red flowers are invisible to bees, making them “choice” for hummingbirds, which see red. Red flowers are rare in bee-pollinated communities but common where hummingbirds are abundant.

  • Scent. Pollinator preference for specific volatile compounds selects for changes in the plant’s secondary metabolism. Moth-pollinated flowers often have sweet, heavy fragrances; flies are attracted to putrid smells; some flowers mimic female insect pheromones, tricking males into visiting for copulation.

  • Nectar reward. Nectar volume, sugar concentration, and chemical composition (amino acids, alkaloids) evolve to match pollinator physiology and behavior. High-energy flowers reward large pollinators; low-reward flowers “trick” small pollinators into frequent visits.

Pollinators simultaneously evolve counter-adaptations: longer proboscises (moths), larger bodies to access larger flowers (bumblebees), color vision tuned to the flowers they visit (some bees have spectral sensitivity shifted toward the wavelengths of abundant flowers in their habitat), and behavioral patterns synchronized with flower phenology.

Pairwise vs. diffuse coevolution

Coevolution can be pairwise (one-to-one), where a single plant species is pollinated by a single insect species. This is spectacularly apparent in orchids pollinated by specific male wasps through sexual deception — the flower mimics the female’s pheromone and body shape, achieving pollination through pseudocopulation.

More commonly, coevolution is diffuse (many-to-many): a plant is pollinated by several species, each exerting selective pressure; each pollinator visits several plant species, each affecting the pollinator’s evolution. This creates a coevolutionary network, where the shape of a flower reflects the “average” selectivity of all its pollinators.

Plant-herbivore coevolution: the chemical arms race

Plants and their herbivorous consumers engage in a relentless chemical arms race. Plants evolve defensive compounds (alkaloids, glucosinolates, tannins, terpenes); herbivores evolve detoxification enzymes and sequestration mechanisms to tolerate these toxins.

Glucosinolates and Brassicaceae

The classic example: Brassicaceae (cabbage family) produce glucosinolates — bitter, sulfur-containing compounds. These are hydrolyzed by myrosinase enzymes upon herbivory, releasing isothiocyanates that are toxic to most insects. However, some caterpillars (e.g., Pieris butterflies) have evolved the ability to sequester glucosinolates, avoiding myrosinase activation. These specialist herbivores are actually attracted to glucosinolates — they use them as host-plant cues for finding suitable plants. In response, Brassicaceae has diversified glucosinolate profiles, creating subspecies-level variation that selects for herbivore specialization.

Sequestration and toxin tolerance

Some herbivores sequester plant toxins unchanged (e.g., grasshoppers accumulating cardenolides from milkweed). The insect stores the toxin in specific tissues, preventing damage. This creates an honest signal of the herbivore’s toxin-resistance to predators: monarch butterflies sequester milkweed cardenolides and are protected from bird predation.

Mycorrhizal coevolution

One of the oldest and most profound coevolutionary relationships is between plants and fungi. Mycorrhizal associations are 400+ million years old, pre-dating the evolution of leaves. Glomeromycete fungi form arbuscular mycorrhizae, penetrating plant root cells; ectomycorrhizal fungi form a mantle around roots. In both cases, the fungus provides phosphate and nitrogen in exchange for plant-derived carbohydrates.

This mutualism is so fundamental that many plants cannot grow without their fungal partners. The evolution of plant nutrient uptake mechanisms, root architecture, and nutrient allocation reflect tens of millions of years of selection for effective mycorrhizal function.

Predator-mediated coevolution

When herbivores attack plants, plants evolve defenses. But plants also evolve traits that benefit herbivore predators and parasitoids — a third-order coevolution. When some plants are damaged by herbivores, they release volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that attract predatory insects and parasitoid wasps, which then feed on or parasitize the herbivores. Over evolutionary time, plants that produce particularly attractive VOCs experience reduced herbivory through predator recruitment. Both the plant and the predator coevolve toward more effective signaling and response.

Phylogenetic evidence

Modern phylogenetic analyses reveal coevolutionary patterns by comparing evolutionary trees of plants and their partners:

  • If a plant and herbivore tree are congruent (the same branching pattern), they show tight coevolution and may have speciated in parallel.
  • If the trees are incongruent, the coevolution is more diffuse, with host-shifts or dispersal to new hosts.

The dating of divergence times in plant-pollinator pairs (using fossil records and molecular clocks) reveals when coevolutionary associations became established.

Coevolution and biodiversity

Coevolution is one of the primary drivers of biodiversity. In tropical rainforests, where species diversity peaks, coevolutionary interactions are particularly intense: high pollinator diversity selects for flower diversity, high herbivore diversity drives plant chemical diversity, and high predator diversity creates complex food webs. The interdependence of these relationships means that losing one species can cascade through the community.
