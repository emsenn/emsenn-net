Rhizobium

2026-04-02 A genus of soil bacteria that form nitrogen-fixing symbioses with leguminous plants through root nodule formation and bacteroid differentiation

Rhizobium is a genus of gram-negative soil bacteria that establish symbiotic associations with leguminous plants (members of the family Fabaceae), forming root nodules within which the bacteria fix atmospheric nitrogen in exchange for carbon nutrients from the plant. The symbiosis is among the most economically important biological processes in agriculture, as it reduces dependence on industrial nitrogen fertilizers and maintains soil nitrogen fertility in sustainable farming systems.

¶Infection and Nodulation Process

The infection process begins in the soil rhizosphere, where Rhizobium cells are attracted to root exudates (compounds released by plant roots) and migrate toward the root hair zone. Upon contact with root hair cells, Rhizobium cells secrete nodulation (Nod) factors, small lipopolysaccharide molecules with a specific chemical structure recognized by the host plant. These Nod factors trigger several responses:

  • Root hair curling: The root hair cell wall is disrupted and the hair curls around the bacterial cells, enclosing them.
  • Nodule initiation: The presence of Nod factors induces the plant to reactivate mitotic cell division in the root cortex, dedifferentiating cortical cells that will form the nodule structure.
  • Infection thread formation: The Rhizobium cells divide within the curled root hair and form a tubular invagination called an infection thread—a composite structure derived from both bacterial and plant membranes. This thread grows through the epidermis and into the cortical cells that form the nodule.

Inside cortical cells, Rhizobium cells are released from the infection thread through an endocytosis-like process, becoming enclosed within membrane-bound compartments called symbiosomes (or peribacteroid membrane compartments). This membrane is plant-derived and functions as a semi-permeable barrier regulating metabolite exchange.

¶Bacteroid Differentiation

Within symbiosomes, individual Rhizobium cells undergo dramatic terminal differentiation into bacteroids, specialized cells adapted for nitrogen fixation. Bacteroids exhibit:

  • Genome endoreduplication: Multiple rounds of DNA replication without cell division, resulting in polyploid cells containing 16–32 copies of the chromosome.
  • Cell enlargement: Bacteroids can reach 2–3 times the size of free-living Rhizobium cells.
  • Loss of reproductive capacity: Bacteroid cell walls are modified, and the cells are unable to divide; they are functionally terminal cells.
  • Increased membrane permeability: Allowing rapid nutrient exchange with the plant.

Once differentiated, bacteroids express high levels of the nitrogenase enzyme complex and become the nitrogen-fixing units of the symbiosis.

¶Nitrogen Fixation and Metabolite Exchange

Bacteroids reduce atmospheric N₂ to ammonia (NH₃) using the nitrogenase enzyme, a process requiring substantial ATP and reducing power. The ammonia is immediately exported outside the bacteroid and assimilated by surrounding plant cells in the presence of carbon skeletons (organic acids) derived from photosynthesis.

The plant translocates carbohydrates from photosynthetically active leaves to the root nodules, where they are catabolized to provide energy (ATP) and reducing equivalents (NADPH) required by nitrogenase. The primary compounds exchanged are dicarboxylic acids (succinate and malate) exported by bacteroids in exchange for carbon from plant photosynthates.

Active nitrogen-fixing nodules fix 80–300 kg N per hectare per year, depending on plant species, Rhizobium strain effectiveness, soil conditions, and plant nitrogen demand. This rate is comparable to or exceeds industrial nitrogen fertilizer application rates in many agricultural systems.

¶Host Specificity and Species Interactions

One Rhizobium strain cannot form symbiosis with all legume species, and most legumes are restricted to infection by compatible rhizobia—a phenomenon called host specificity. This specificity operates at multiple levels:

Nod factor recognition: The plant has legume lectin proteins localized at root hair tips that recognize and bind specific oligosaccharide structures on compatible bacterial Nod factors. Different Rhizobium species produce Nod factors with distinct chemical modifications (lipid chain length, substituents on the sugar moiety) that are recognized only by compatible plant genotypes.

Species examples:

  • Rhizobium etli forms nodules with Phaseolus vulgaris (common bean)
  • Rhizobium leguminosarum infects Pisum sativum (pea), Vicia faba (fava bean), and Lens culinaris (lentil)
  • Sinorhizobium meliloti (formerly Rhizobium meliloti) partners with Medicago sativa (alfalfa)

Post-infection specificity: Host specificity is not confined to early recognition. Incompatible host-strain combinations may form nodules initially, but bacteroid differentiation and nitrogen fixation are defective, resulting in the Fix- phenotype (non-fixing nodules).

¶Agricultural Significance

Rhizobium inoculants—cultures of select, effective strains—are applied to legume seeds or soil to ensure rapid nodulation and maximize nitrogen fixation in regions where compatible wild Rhizobium populations are low or absent. Inoculant quality is critical: only strains with demonstrably high nitrogen fixation rates (effectiveness) should be used; ineffective strains form nodules but fix little nitrogen, wasting the plant’s investment in nodule tissue.

In sustainable agriculture, legume-based crop rotation relies entirely on symbiotic nitrogen fixation to restore soil nitrogen, eliminating synthetic fertilizer inputs. Understanding Rhizobium strain performance and host compatibility is essential for managing these systems.

¶Related Genera

Symbiotic nitrogen-fixing relationships with legumes are not limited to Rhizobium; related genera include Mesorhizobium, Sinorhizobium, Bradyrhizobium, and Azorhizobium. These genera occupy distinct ecological and agronomic niches; for example, Bradyrhizobium species preferentially colonize woody legumes and some tropical legumes, while Sinorhizobium predominates in alfalfa and clover.
