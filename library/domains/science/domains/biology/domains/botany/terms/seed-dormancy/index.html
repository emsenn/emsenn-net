Seed Dormancy

Seed dormancy is a state in which a viable, intact seed fails to germinate despite exposure to conditions (water, appropriate temperature, oxygen) that would normally induce germination. Dormancy is an adaptive trait that ensures seeds do not germinate immediately upon ripening; instead, it distributes germination over time and space, reducing catastrophic loss of the entire seedling cohort to a single adverse event (frost, drought, or seedling competition).

Types of Dormancy

Dormancy is classified into five primary categories based on mechanism:

Physical dormancy (PY) results from an impermeable seed coat that resists water imbibition. This is common in legumes (beans, peas), vetches, and some woody plants. The seed coat is constructed so that water cannot penetrate; the embryo remains metabolically inactive because imbibition is blocked. Breaking physical dormancy requires scarification: mechanical abrasion (sanding, filing), chemical treatment (soaking in sulfuric acid), or heat (brief exposure to high temperature or flame). In nature, scarification occurs through microbial action, passage through animal digestive systems (where acid weakens the coat), or weathering.

Physiological dormancy (PD) results from internal chemical inhibitors or developmental immaturity of the embryo, not from physical barriers. Dormancy is maintained by abscisic acid (ABA), a growth inhibitor synthesized during seed maturation and stored in the embryo and endosperm. ABA suppresses expression of genes required for radicle elongation and cell expansion. Breaking physiological dormancy typically requires stratification: exposure of imbibed seeds to cold and moist conditions (1–10°C for 4–12 weeks). Cold treatment gradually reduces ABA levels through catabolism (8’-hydroxylation), allowing the antagonistic hormone gibberellin (GA) to accumulate and promote germination upon warming.

Morphological dormancy (MD) is rare and results from incomplete development of the embryo within the seed. The embryo must complete development before germination is possible; this often requires warm, moist conditions and can extend over a full growing season.

Morphophysiological dormancy (MPD) combines both morphological and physiological components.

Combinational dormancy combines physical and physiological dormancy; such seeds require both scarification (to permit water uptake) and stratification (to reduce ABA) before germination.

Ecological Function

Dormancy distributes germination risk temporally:

  • Seed annuals in seasonally variable environments germinate only when conditions are sufficiently favorable (sufficient moisture and warmth for seedling establishment).
  • Persistent seed banks accumulate in soil, with individual seeds germinating in successive years or decades; a fire, plowing, or gap-opening disturbance will trigger massive emergence of dormant seeds, colonizing disturbed space.
  • Staggered emergence from a single plant’s seed crop reduces competition among sibs and distributes risk.

This strategy is particularly important in harsh or unpredictable climates where germinating at the wrong time carries severe cost.

Thermodormancy

A special case is thermodormancy, in which seeds fail to germinate above a threshold temperature regardless of other conditions. This is common in cool-season crops (mache, corn salad, carrots, lettuce); these seeds require cold or cool conditions to break dormancy and inhibit germination in high summer heat, ensuring seedling establishment during cooler seasons when water stress is lower.

Agricultural Implications

Crop seeds are selected for rapid, synchronous germination; agricultural cultivars typically have low dormancy. However, dormancy can reappear in crops when environmental stress (cold, wet conditions) during seed maturation induces dormancy inadvertently (a problem in winter grains after early-season frost during grain filling).

Conversely, in some crops—rice, wheat—dormancy interferes with commercial germination; seeds are treated with abscisic acid (ABA) antagonists or gibberellins to overcome dormancy and ensure uniform rapid germination.

Breaking Dormancy: Methods

Beyond scarification and stratification (described above):

  • Smoke treatment: Smoke compounds can break physiological dormancy in some species; this is common in fire-adapted plants.
  • Light exposure: Phytochrome-mediated light requirement for germination (in species with photodormancy).
  • Dry storage: Extended dry storage at cool temperatures can break dormancy in some seeds by allowing ABA degradation.
  • Hydration-dehydration cycles: Repeated wetting and drying can facilitate water uptake in partially impermeable seeds.
