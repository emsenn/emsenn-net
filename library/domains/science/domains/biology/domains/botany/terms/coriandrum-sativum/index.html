Coriandrum sativum

2026-04-02 Annual herb in the carrot family with bipinnate foliage in juvenile stage and pinnate foliage in adult stage, producing both fresh cilantro leaves and dry coriander seeds with distinct essential oil compositions.

Coriandrum sativum L., commonly called cilantro (referring to fresh leaves) and coriander (referring to seeds), is an annual herbaceous plant in the family Apiaceae (carrot family), native to the Mediterranean basin and southwestern Asia. It is distinguished by marked heterophylly—juvenile plants produce finely divided, bipinnate “frilly” foliage, while reproductive-stage plants develop more coarsely pinnate leaves. Seeds and fresh leaves contain distinct essential oils, with linalool dominating in seeds and aldehydes dominating in leaves, producing notably different flavor profiles. This single species provides two distinct ingredients in world cuisines.

¶Taxonomy and Morphology

Coriandrum sativum is monotypic within its genus. It grows 40-80 cm tall with an initially erect, unbranched rosette that branches extensively once flowering is initiated. Juvenile leaves are finely divided and feathery, resembling parsley; these are the “cilantro” leaves harvested for culinary use. Mature flowering plants bear coarser, pinnate upper leaves. Inflorescences are compound umbels with 8-15 primary rays; flowers are small, white to pale pink, with unequal petals. The fruit is a cremocarp (split into two single-seeded fruits) 3-4 mm diameter, pale brown, with prominent ribs.

¶Chemistry and Phenology

Fresh cilantro leaves contain 0.005-0.02 percent volatile oil, dominated by aldehydes (hexanal, octanal, trans-2-hexenal, 2-methylbutanal), which produce the characteristic, pungent, soap-like or musty aroma that 10-20 percent of the population perceive as intensely unpleasant due to olfactory receptor polymorphisms. Secondary components include linalool, pinene, and myrcene.

Coriander seeds develop an essential oil comprising 0.5-1.5 percent of dry weight, dominated by linalool (50-80 percent) and α-pinene (5-15 percent), producing a warm, sweet, lemony aroma distinctly different from fresh cilantro. The seed also accumulates fixed oils (15-30 percent), proteins, and carbohydrates.

Heat and drying convert some aldehyde compounds in aging leaves, explaining why some people who dislike raw cilantro can tolerate it cooked.

¶Ecology and Cultivation History

Coriandrum sativum is native to temperate Mediterranean and southwestern Asian regions, particularly the Levant and Anatolia. Wild and partially domesticated forms persist in Mediterranean and Asian centers of diversity. The species has been cultivated for at least 3,000 years, with evidence in Egyptian tomb texts and Indian Sanskrit texts. Domestication involved selection for larger seeds and increased oil yield.

The plant is a cool-season annual, germinating at soil temperatures as low as 10°C and thriving at 15-20°C. Heat triggers rapid bolting and flowering; temperatures above 25°C accelerate transition to reproductive growth, shortening the cilantro leaf-harvest window to 30-45 days. Bolting is photoperiod-responsive in some cultivars, with short-day conditions (spring or autumn) delaying flowering compared to long days (summer). Seeds require 100+ days from sowing to physiological maturity; leaf harvest occurs at 40-50 days.

¶Culinary Roles and Dual Harvests

Cilantro leaf is essential to Mexican, Thai, Vietnamese, Indian, and Middle Eastern cuisines, used fresh as a garnish, in salsas, chutneys, and garnishing curries. Coriander seed is one of the world’s most widely used spices, essential to Indian, Middle Eastern, Mediterranean, and Chinese cuisines, used whole or ground in curries, pickling, baking, and spice blends. The two forms—fresh leaf and dried seed—are sufficiently distinct in flavor that they are rarely interchanged.

Global annual production exceeds 300,000 tonnes of coriander seed, with major cultivation in India, Russia, Guatemala, and Thailand. Cilantro leaf production is concentrated in North America and Europe, with fresh market and processing demand from Hispanic and Asian communities. Essential oil extraction yields 0.3-1.0 percent from seeds, used in liqueurs, perfumery, pharmaceuticals, and food flavoring.
