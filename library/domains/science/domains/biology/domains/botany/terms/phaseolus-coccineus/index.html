Scarlet Runner Bean (Phaseolus coccineus)

Phaseolus coccineus is a vigorous perennial or annual climbing legume native to the mountainous regions of Mexico and Guatemala, where it grows as a true perennial in frost-free climates. Unlike its domesticated relative P. vulgaris, this species is cultivated primarily for ornamental display alongside nutritional use, with flowers that attract hummingbirds and other pollinators.

Taxonomy and Native Range

Phaseolus coccineus occupies a distinct evolutionary lineage within Phaseolus, with closest relatives in Mesoamerica. The species occurs naturally at elevations of 1,500–2,500 meters in mountain valleys where cool, misty conditions prevail. Its perennial growth form and climbing habit represent adaptations to forest understory niches.

Morphology

Runner bean plants produce vigorous vining growth to 3–4 meters or more if supported, bearing compound trifoliate leaves on angular twining stems. The defining feature is the brilliant scarlet (or pink in some cultivars) zygomorphic flowers arranged in racemes, reaching 3–4 cm in length. These flowers are entomophilous, adapted for pollination by hummingbirds in native ranges and by bumblebees in temperate cultivation. Pods elongate to 20–30 cm, containing 6–12 large bicolored seeds with scarlet or black patterning.

Biochemistry and Seed Composition

Unlike P. vulgaris, runner bean seeds exhibit higher lipid content (3–4%), providing greater energetic density. Seeds contain phytohaemagglutinin and other lectins requiring thorough cooking. Immature pods are regularly harvested for consumption in the manner of snap beans, while mature seeds store well and supply protein (18–22% dry weight).

Nitrogen Fixation

Runner beans establish rhizobial symbioses similar to common beans, though the extensive vegetative biomass and prolonged growing season create conditions for substantial cumulative nitrogen accumulation. A single established plant can return 50–100 kg N/ha to soil over a perennial crop cycle.

Cultivation and Perennial Habit

In Central American highlands, runner beans grow as managed perennials in agroforestry systems, often planted against support trees that remain productive for a decade or more. In temperate regions with killing frosts, they are grown as annuals. The three-sisters polyculture system, documented extensively in Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) agricultural practice, pairs runner beans (or common beans) with maize and squash, where bean nitrogen fixation enhances soil fertility while vining beans climb maize stalks.

Ecological Interactions

The intense floral display attracts diverse pollinators and supports hummingbird populations during migration periods. Seed predation by beetles and bruchids represents ongoing selection pressure on seed coat hardness. The species remains less extensively bred than P. vulgaris, retaining greater genetic diversity and pest-resistance alleles.
