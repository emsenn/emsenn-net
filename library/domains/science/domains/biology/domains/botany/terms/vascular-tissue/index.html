Vascular Tissue

2026-04-02 Vascular tissue: the transport system of plants, composed of two complementary tissues — [xylem](./xylem.md) and [phloem](./phloem.md) — that together solve the fundamental problem of moving water and organic compounds across distances in a large organism.

Vascular tissue: the transport system of plants, composed of two complementary tissues — xylem and phloem — that together solve the fundamental problem of moving water and organic compounds across distances in a large organism.

Xylem is the tissue of upward water transport. It is composed of two cell types: vessel elements and tracheids, both of which are dead at maturity. Vessel elements are arranged end-to-end, with perforated end walls creating continuous conduits; tracheids are tapered and overlapping, with pits (thin spots in the secondary wall) allowing water movement between cells. Both cell types have heavily lignified secondary walls, providing rigidity. The xylem moves water and dissolved mineral nutrients from the roots upward through the shoot, driven by the transpiration stream — the evaporative loss of water from leaf surfaces that generates a negative pressure gradient pulling water upward. In some situations (e.g., in spring when leaves are just emerging), root pressure can drive water upward, though transpiration is the dominant driver in most plants.

Phloem is the tissue of organic solute transport. It contains living sieve elements (in angiosperms, sieve tube members) stacked end-to-end, with sieve plates (areas of large pores in the end walls) allowing cell-to-cell movement. Each sieve element is associated with a companion cell, a living parenchyma cell that provides metabolic support and maintains the sieve element through plasmodesmatal connections. Phloem translocates sugars (principally sucrose), amino acids, hormones, and other organic compounds bidirectionally — downward from photosynthetic source tissues to storage and growth sinks, and upward from storage organs during times of demand. The driving mechanism is the Münch pressure-flow hypothesis: active transport of sugars into sieve elements at the source creates a positive turgor pressure that propels solution toward sinks where sugars are unloaded.

Xylem and phloem are organized into vascular bundles — discrete strands running through stems, roots, and leaves. In eudicots, vascular bundles are arranged in a ring within the stem, with xylem on the inner face (toward the center) and phloem on the outer face (toward the periphery); cambium lies between them. In monocots, vascular bundles are scattered throughout the stem. In leaves, vascular bundles form the venation pattern — the veins that visibly traverse the leaf surface.

The presence of vascular tissue enables plants to achieve large size. Without xylem, water could not reach the upper reaches of a tall plant; without phloem, photosynthetic products made in sunlit leaves could not be exported to shaded roots and growing apices. Vascular tissue also provides the scaffold for the cambium and secondary growth, enabling woody plants to accumulate biomass over decades or centuries.
