Gibberellin

2026-04-02

Gibberellin: a class of plant growth-promoting hormones that stimulate stem elongation, break seed dormancy, promote flowering, and regulate fruit development. Over 130 distinct gibberellin structures have been identified (GA₁, GA₃, GA₄ being among the most bioactive), but all share a common four-ring gibban skeleton. The hormone was first discovered through study of the “bakanae” (foolish seedling) disease of rice, caused by the fungus Gibberella fujikuroi, which secretes gibberellins causing extreme stem elongation.

¶Biosynthesis and deactivation

Gibberellins are synthesized from geranylgeranyl diphosphate through the terpenoid pathway: GGDP → ent-copalyl diphosphate (ent-CPS) → ent-kaurene (ent-KS) → ent-kaurenoic acid (CYP701A) → GA₁₂ (CYP88A) → bioactive GAs via GA 20-oxidases and GA 3-oxidases. GA 2-oxidases catalyze deactivation, maintaining hormone homeostasis. The rate-limiting biosynthetic steps are tissue- and developmental-stage specific: young vegetative tissues produce high GA levels; leaves typically accumulate less.

¶Perception and signaling

Gibberellins are perceived by GID1 (GIBBERELLIN INSENSITIVE DWARF 1), a soluble nuclear receptor. GA binding to GID1 promotes interaction with DELLA proteins — growth repressors named for their conserved N-terminal DELLA domain (GAI, RGA, RGL1-3 in Arabidopsis; SLR1 in rice). The GA-GID1-DELLA complex is recognized by the SCF^SLY1/GID2 ubiquitin ligase complex, leading to DELLA ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation. DELLA degradation releases growth — when DELLA proteins are absent or GA is present, cell elongation, flowering, and seed germination proceed.

¶Developmental roles

Seed germination. Gibberellins break seed dormancy by activating the α-amylase gene in the aleurone layer of cereal seeds, mobilizing starch reserves to fuel embryo growth. Abscisic acid maintains seed dormancy; the GA:ABA ratio determines whether seeds germinate.

Stem elongation. GA drives cell elongation in the internodes. Dwarf mutants lacking functional GA biosynthesis or signaling remain short because DELLA proteins are never degraded. The Green Revolution depended on this: semidwarf wheat and rice varieties (Norin 10 background) carry mutations in GA-insensitive DELLA proteins, allowing them to respond to high nitrogen fertilizer without lodging (falling over). These mutant alleles redirect nitrogen toward grain rather than vegetative growth.

Flowering. Gibberellins promote bolting (stem elongation) and flowering in long-day plants under short days, overriding the photoperiod requirement. This is why plants treated with exogenous GA flowering in otherwise inhibitory conditions.

Fruit development. GA promotes parthenocarpic fruit development (fruit without fertilization), allowing seedless grape and apple production. GA is also involved in seed coat hardness and other aspects of fruit maturation distinct from ethylene-mediated ripening.

¶Ecological significance

The dramatic effect of gibberellins on growth makes them targets for agricultural enhancement. However, exogenous application is less common than synthetic auxins or cytokinins because GA effects are development-specific and tissue-dependent.
