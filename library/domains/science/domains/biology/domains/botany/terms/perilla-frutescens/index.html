Perilla frutescens

2026-04-02 Annual herb in the mint family with broad, serrated leaves that develop deep purple pigmentation, cultivated across Asia for leaf, seed, and pickling uses, and for perillaldehyde's antimicrobial properties.

Perilla frutescens (L.) Britton, commonly called shiso in Japanese and perilla in English, is an annual herbaceous plant in the family Lamiaceae (mint family), native to temperate and subtropical East and Southeast Asia. It is distinguished by large, opposite, broadly ovate leaves with acute serrated margins, prominent veining, and variegated or solid purple anthocyanin pigmentation (particularly in var. frutescens murasakino, the purple form). Seeds and leaves are rich in perillaldehyde (30-60 percent of essential oil), a volatile aldehyde with potent antimicrobial properties.

¶Taxonomy and Morphology

Multiple color forms and leaf morphologies exist, including green-leaved (aojiso), purple-leaved (murasakino or akajiso), curly-leaved, and smooth-leaved types. The purple cultivar murasakino contains anthocyanins and has been selectively bred in Japan for deep, uniform purple coloration. A third form, Laotian perilla, develops larger leaves and is cultivated in Southeast Asia.

The plant grows 60-120 cm tall with a branching, bushy habit. Leaves are 5-15 cm long, 4-12 cm wide, with petiolate attachment and palmate venation. The leaf surface is rough or slightly wrinkled in some cultivars. Inflorescence is a terminal, dense raceme bearing bilabiate flowers in whorls; flowers are typically white with purple tinges. Seeds are small, round, 1-2 mm diameter, and disperse explosively at maturity.

¶Chemistry

Perillaldehyde (or perillyl aldehyde) comprises 30-60 percent of the leaf and seed essential oil, depending on cultivar and growing conditions. Secondary components include limonene, myrcene, and methyl cinnamate. Perillaldehyde has documented antimicrobial activity against bacteria and fungi, including Vibrio species, Bacillus species, and Candida species, with minimum inhibitory concentrations (MIC) in the range of 0.025-0.1 percent. The compound also activates sensory neurons and may have analgesic properties. Leaves accumulate anthocyanins (up to 2 percent dry weight in purple forms), polyphenols, and vitamin A precursors.

¶Ecology and Cultivation History

Perilla frutescens is native to temperate and subtropical Asia, with secondary centers of diversity in China, Japan, Vietnam, and Korea. Wild and feral forms persist throughout East Asia. Domestication into distinct color and leaf forms began in China at least 2,000 years ago, with evidence in Chinese herbals and medicinal texts. Purple cultivars became especially important in Japan, where they have been integrated into traditional food preservation and medicine for over 1,000 years.

The plant is a frost-sensitive annual, killed by freezing temperatures. It prefers warm soil and air temperatures (20-28°C) and tolerates partial shade. Self-sowing is vigorous; volunteer plants often appear in gardens the following season.

¶Culinary and Medicinal Roles

Perilla leaves (shiso) are used as a palate cleanser and digestive aid in Japanese sushi and sashimi preparation—the antimicrobial properties of perillaldehyde make it a functional garnish that prevents bacterial growth and food spoilage. Purple shiso is fermented with salt and red plums (ume) to produce beni shoga (red pickled ginger substitute) and umeboshi (pickled plums), traditional Japanese preserved foods. Shibazuke pickles use perilla leaves to impart flavor and color. Young leaves are tempura-battered and fried. In Korean and Vietnamese cuisines, perilla leaves (Korean: sook-kae) are grilled whole with meats. The seeds are used in traditional Chinese medicine and pressed for oil. Global cultivation remains concentrated in East and Southeast Asia; commercial cultivation exceeds 100,000 hectares in China alone.
