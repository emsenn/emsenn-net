Ipomoea purpurea

2026-04-02 Annual vine from Convolvulaceae, native to tropical Mexico. Herbaceous, twining vine producing ephemeral flowers that close by mid-morning. Flowers are funnel-shaped, typically purple-blue (purple-veined white, bicolored, or solid burgundy cultivars exist). Flowers mature acropetally along the inflorescence; each flower persists one morning. Pollinated by bumblebees and morning-flying bees. Seeds are hard-coated, toxin-containing (ergot alkaloid-like compounds), germinate slowly.

¶Taxonomy and Distribution

Ipomoea purpurea (L.) Roth (Convolvulaceae, subfamily Convolvuloideae) is an annual vine native to tropical Mexico, naturalized in warm regions worldwide. The genus Ipomoea comprises ~600 species primarily from the Americas and Old World tropics, ranging from herbaceous annuals to woody perennials. I. purpurea is closely related to the sweet potato (I. batatas) and Japanese morning glory (I. nil). The species is now cultivated globally as an ornamental and has become naturalized in warm temperate and subtropical regions.

¶Morphology and Growth Architecture

Herbaceous annual twining vine 2–3.5 m long (occasionally to 4 m under optimal conditions), with angular, glabrous to finely hirsute stems. Leaves are alternate, long-petiolate (2–8 cm), simple, cordate-ovate with acuminate apex, and 5–12 cm long; margins are entire. The plant climbs by twining counterclockwise (dextral twining), with the apex rotating in a spiral pattern to search for support structures.

The inflorescence is a cymose cluster of 1–3 flowers arising at leaf axils; flowers open sequentially acropetally (basipetal to acropetal along the stem). Each flower opens shortly after dawn and closes by mid-morning, persisting for only 2–6 hours—hence the common name “morning glory.”

¶Flower Morphology and Color Diversity

The flower is a typical Convolvulaceae funnel-corolla, with five fused petals forming a trumpet shape (3–5 cm long, 2–4 cm wide at the mouth). The corolla is typically purple-blue to violet, with darker purple veining on the inner surface. Cultivar diversity has produced:

  • Solid colors: deep purple, pale blue, pink, white, burgundy.
  • Bicolored forms: purple-veined white, white with deep purple throat, white edged with purple.
  • Picotee patterns: white flowers with distinct colored margin.

Five stamens are inserted on the corolla tube, with versatile anthers. The ovary is superior, two-celled, with one ovule per locule. The style is filiform with a capitate, two-lobed stigma. Flowers are protandrous (anthers dehisce before stigma maturation), though self-pollination occurs. Outcrossing is promoted by early-morning bee visitation.

¶Pollination and Phenology

The primary pollinators are native bees (bumblebees Bombus spp., mining bees Andrena) and morning-flying honeybees, which visit flowers at dawn when pollen is fresh and nectar abundant. The flower’s blue coloration and funnel shape are typical pollinator attractants. The flower’s brief anthesis (2–6 hours) appears to be an adaptation promoting visitation by day-active pollinators early in the day before resources are depleted.

Flowering is indeterminate: the plant produces new flower buds continuously throughout the growing season until killed by frost. Day length has minimal effect on flowering initiation; temperature and plant size are the primary drivers.

¶Reproduction and Seed Biology

The ovary develops into a broadly ellipsoid, dry, dehiscent capsule (1–1.5 cm diameter) containing 4–6 (occasionally 1–2) hard, dark brown to black seeds. The seed coat is thick and contains resinous deposits; the testa is hard and impermeable, causing dormancy. Germination is slow (7–10 days at 25°C) and can be accelerated by scarification (file or sandpaper the seed coat) or soaking in warm water overnight.

Seeds contain ergot alkaloid-like compounds and other indole alkaloids (similar to those found in the psychoactive jimsonweed Datura); seeds are toxic in large quantities and have been used (historically, dangerously) as recreational drugs. The toxicity is sufficient to deter most mammalian seed predation.

¶Ecological and Agricultural Roles

In native Mexico and throughout its naturalized range, I. purpurea is pollinated by native bees and serves as a nectar source during mid-summer. The persistent twining vines provide structural complexity in weedy habitats. In agricultural systems, the plant is often considered a weed in tropical and warm-temperate crops, though it is easily removed and poses no serious threat to most crops.

¶Cultivar Diversity

Ornamental breeding has produced dozens of cultivars:

  • Standard trailing (‘Heavenly Blue’, ‘Scarlett O’Hara’, ‘Pearly Gates’): vigorous 2–3.5 m vines, single flowers, suitable for trellising or ground cover.
  • Compact dwarf (rarely available): 0.5–1 m bushy types, few in cultivation compared to I. nil.
  • Double-flowered ornamentals (‘Burpee’s Blue’, ‘Flying Saucer’ and others, more common in I. nil than I. purpurea): petals doubled, prolonging flower persistence slightly.

Notable cultivars include Blue Morning Glories (generic “heavenly blue” type, 60–75 days to first flower), and specific named selections such as ‘Crimson Rambler’ (deep burgundy) and ‘Scarlett O’Hara’ (red-burgundy).
