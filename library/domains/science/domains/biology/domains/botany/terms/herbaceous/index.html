Herbaceous

2026-04-02 A plant or plant part that lacks persistent woody tissue above ground — the stems are green, flexible, and die back annually in temperate herbaceous perennials

Herbaceous describes a plant or plant part that does not develop persistent woody tissue (secondary xylem) above ground. Herbaceous stems are green, photosynthetic, flexible, and typically short-lived — they die back at the end of the growing season in temperate climates, or persist in mild climates without becoming woody.

The botanical sense of “herb” (a herbaceous plant) differs from the culinary and medicinal sense (a plant used for flavoring or medicine). A plant can be herbaceous without being a culinary herb, and many medicinal “herbs” are actually woody shrubs or trees.

Herbaceous plants include annuals (wheat, basil), biennials (carrot, parsley), and herbaceous perennials (grasses, ferns, Japanese knotweed). In herbaceous perennials, the above-ground stems die completely each winter, but the plant survives underground as a rhizome, bulb, corm, or crown. The annual regrowth from underground reserves — sometimes dramatically rapid, as in knotweed’s 8 cm/day spring growth — is a defining feature of herbaceous perennials.

The contrast with woody plants is not absolute. Some plants are semi-woody (subshrubs), with woody bases and herbaceous upper growth. Some herbaceous stems become lignified (stiffened with lignin) late in the season without qualifying as true wood.
