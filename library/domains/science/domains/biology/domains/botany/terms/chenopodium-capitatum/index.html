Chenopodium capitatum

2026-04-02 Hardy annual or short-lived perennial from Amaranthaceae, native to western North America. Produces spinach-like leaves in juvenile phase; bolts to produce dense fruiting spikes bearing small red berries. Dual-harvest crop: early leaves, then post-bolting berries. Self-seeds aggressively; hardy to USDA zone 2.

Taxonomy and Native Range

Chenopodium capitatum Hook. f. (Amaranthaceae, formerly Chenopodiaceae) is a vigorous annual or short-lived perennial native to western North America, from the Pacific Northwest south to the mountain regions of California, and eastward to the Great Basin. The species occurs in disturbed, open habitats and semi-shaded forest edges at elevations from sea level to 8,000 feet. It is the only North American Chenopodium species that produces notable colorful fruit, distinguishing it sharply from closely related goosefoot species.

Morphology

Chenopodium capitatum forms an erect to sprawling herb, typically 30–60 cm tall, with alternate, simple, rhomboid to triangular leaves bearing a prominent petiole. Leaves are thin-textured, without the mealy (farinose) powder typical of many Chenopodium species; the lamina is bright green and slightly succulent. Leaf margins are entire to irregularly dentate.

The inflorescence is a dense, terminal, spike-like panicle. Flowers are minute, greenish, and inconspicuous, arranged in dense clusters. Upon maturation, the inflorescence transforms dramatically: the floral bracts and perianth tissue swell and become deep red, creating the appearance of a strawberry—hence the common name. The actual fruit is a one-seeded utricle enclosed within the swollen, fleshy perianth tissue, creating a pseudocarp (false fruit) resembling a berry.

Dual-Harvest Phenology

Chenopodium capitatum exhibits a distinctive two-phase phenology favorable for home and commercial gardening. In the vegetative phase (20–35 days under optimal conditions), plants produce spinach-like leaves suitable for salad harvest or cooked greens. Leaves are mild-flavored, lacking the astringency of some relatives; oxalic acid content is moderate.

As daylength exceeds 14 hours and temperatures warm (>18°C), plants irreversibly transition to the reproductive phase. The apical meristem terminates in inflorescence, and the central axis develops the characteristic red, berry-like fruiting clusters. These “berries” ripen over 2–3 weeks and are suitable for fresh eating, cooking, or drying. The flavor is mildly sweet, slightly tart (malic acid), with a texture resembling mulberry.

Pseudocarp Development and Flavor

Unlike true berries (developing from ovary tissue), the strawberry spinach “berry” is a pseudocarp: the fleshy, red tissue is derived from the swollen perianth (calyx and bracts) surrounding the true fruit. This developmental origin is shared with strawberry (Fragaria) and mulberry (Morus), despite distinct phylogenetic origins.

The red color is due to high concentrations of betalains (specifically betacyanins), the same pigments that color beets and amaranth. Betalains are water-soluble, antioxidant compounds distinct from anthocyanins (found in reds of other plant families).

Reproduction and Self-Seeding

Chenopodium capitatum produces hundreds to thousands of seeds per plant, dispersed primarily by gravity and wind; the small seeds are readily consumed and dispersed by birds. The species self-seeds aggressively in moist, disturbed soil; volunteer seedlings emerge reliably from year to year if seeds are allowed to mature.

The species is fully self-compatible and self-fertile; flowers are wind-pollinated, with pollen release occurring early in the morning. Cross-pollination is possible but minor in natural populations.

Cold-Hardiness and Perennial Potential

In USDA zones 3 and warmer, Chenopodium capitatum overwintering as a low rosette, resuming growth in spring to produce flowers and fruit (thus functioning as a short-lived perennial). In harsh winters (zone 2), seedlings establish in fall, grow slowly over winter under snow, and flower in spring. The species is exceptionally cold-hardy for a member of Amaranthaceae; seedlings tolerate hard freeze without mortality.

Ecology and Weed Status

In its native range, Chenopodium capitatum is a pioneer colonizer of disturbed mineral soils, rockfall, and areas recovering from disturbance. The aggressive self-seeding and volunteer seedling emergence make it a minor volunteer weed in gardens where populations have established; however, the dual-harvest benefit usually justifies tolerating or managing volunteer plants.

In cooler climates (zones 4 and north), the species does not invasively expand; in warmer zones, volunteer seedlings can proliferate.
