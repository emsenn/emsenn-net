Salvia rosmarinus

2026-04-02 Tender perennial woody shrub in the mint family native to Mediterranean limestone hills, with needle-like leaves rich in carnosol and rosmarinic acid, and blue, purple, pink, or white flowers.

Salvia rosmarinus Spenn. (formerly Rosmarinus officinalis L.), commonly called rosemary, is a tender perennial woody shrub in the family Lamiaceae (mint family), native to rocky, limestone hills and coastal regions of the Mediterranean basin. It is distinguished by persistent, needle-like, opposite leaves with revolute (rolled) margins, stiff, woody stems reaching 1-2 m in cultivation, and terminal or axillary spike-like inflorescences bearing bilabiate flowers in white, blue, purple, or pink. Leaves contain 0.5-2 percent essential oil dominated by camphor, α-pinene, and 1,8-cineole, and polyphenols including rosmarinic acid and carnosol, with documented antioxidant and antimicrobial properties.

¶Taxonomy and Morphology

Multiple cultivars and growth forms have been selected, ranging from upright and bushy to trailing and prostrate. ‘Arp’, selected at the Arp, Texas experimental garden, is the most cold-hardy cultivar, surviving to -10°C (-14°F) with protection. Other notable cultivars include ‘Tuscan Blue’ (upright, strong flavor), ‘Gorizia’ (cold-hardy, from northeastern Italy), and prostrate forms suitable for containers and groundcover.

Leaves are 1-2.5 cm long, typically gray-green (sometimes darker green in some cultivars), linear-lanceolate, with smooth or slightly hairy surfaces and a waxy cuticle. Stems are angular, woody, and persistent; older plants develop gnarled trunks. Flowers are two-lipped; the upper lip is often hooded and entire; the lower lip is three-lobed. Stamens are 2, with long filaments exserted from the corolla. Flowering is long and continuous in warm climates (6-12 months); in temperate climates, flowers appear in spring and early summer (April-June in northern hemisphere).

¶Chemistry

Essential oil composition varies by cultivar and geographic origin, but principal components are α-pinene (15-30 percent), 1,8-cineole (10-25 percent), camphor (5-15 percent), and limonene (3-8 percent). These volatile compounds have antimicrobial properties against Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, and antifungal activity. Polyphenolic compounds, particularly rosmarinic acid (0.5-2 percent dry leaf weight) and carnosol, are strong antioxidants, with scavenging capacity for hydroxyl and peroxyl radicals. Carnosol also has anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective properties in biochemical assays.

¶Ecology and Cultivation History

Salvia rosmarinus is native to rocky, well-drained Mediterranean hillsides and coastal cliffs from Spain to Turkey. The species is drought-tolerant, with deep root systems exploiting soil moisture unavailable to shallow-rooted plants. It is killed by sustained freezing and requires winter protection in temperate zones (USDA zones 8-10 hardiness for most cultivars; cold-hardy cultivars persist to zone 7). Domestication into distinct forms began in Mediterranean antiquity, with evidence in Roman herbals and medieval European texts. The plant has been integrated into Mediterranean, French, and Italian cuisines for over 2,000 years.

¶Culinary and Medicinal Roles

Rosemary is foundational to Mediterranean cuisine, paired particularly with lamb, roasted potatoes, olive oil, and bread. It is used fresh, dried, or as an infused oil. The herb is also central to European herbal medicine traditions, approved by the German Commission E for internal use to support circulation and digestion. Global annual production of dried rosemary exceeds 20,000 tonnes, with major cultivation in Mediterranean countries, China, and India. Essential oil extraction yields 0.5-2.0 percent by weight, used in perfumery, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals.

¶Container Cultivation in Cold Climates

In cold climates, rosemary is grown in containers outdoors during warm months and overwintered indoors in cool, bright conditions (10-15°C) with reduced watering. Cold-hardy cultivars such as ‘Arp’, ‘Gorizia’, and ‘Tuscan Blue’ can be planted directly in protected garden locations in zones 7 and warmer, though winter mulch is advisable in zone 7.
