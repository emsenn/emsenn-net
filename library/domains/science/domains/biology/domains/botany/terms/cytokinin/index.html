Cytokinin

2026-04-02

Cytokinin: a class of plant hormones that promote cell division (cytokinesis) and delay senescence (leaf aging). Synthesized primarily in root tips, transported upward in the xylem, and perceived through a two-component phosphorelay system. The name derives from their role in cytokinesis, though their regulatory functions extend far beyond cell division.

Biosynthesis and activation

The rate-limiting step in cytokinin synthesis is catalyzed by isopentenyltransferase (IPT) enzymes, which transfer an isopentenyl group from dimethylallyl diphosphate (DMAPP) to adenine nucleotides, forming isopentenyl adenine (iP)-type cytokinins. CYP735A cytochrome P450 enzymes then convert these to the trans-zeatin (tZ) types, which are typically more bioactive. Inactive cytokinin nucleotides are converted to bioactive free bases by LONELY GUY (LOG) phosphoribohydrolases. The active forms — isopentenyl adenosine, zeatin, and zeatin riboside — are perceived by the plant’s sensory machinery.

Perception and signaling

Cytokinin signaling uses a two-component phosphorelay, a system originally discovered in bacteria: membrane-localized histidine kinases (AHK2, AHK3, AHK4/CRE1/WOL in Arabidopsis) autophosphorylate when cytokinin binds. These kinases transfer their phosphoryl group to histidine phosphotransfer proteins (AHPs), which shuttle to the nucleus and activate type-B ARR (ARABIDOPSIS RESPONSE REGULATOR) transcription factors. Type-A ARRs act as negative regulators, creating a feedback loop that limits cytokinin signaling. This cascade is rapid and sensitive, allowing the plant to respond to local cytokinin accumulation within minutes.

Antagonism with auxin

The relative concentration of auxin and cytokinin controls a fundamental developmental decision: high auxin:low cytokinin drives root initiation; high cytokinin:low auxin drives shoot formation; intermediate ratios promote callus tissue (undifferentiated growth). This principle, discovered in tissue culture, applies throughout development. High cytokinin in axillary buds (from root-derived cytokinins transported in the xylem) antagonizes auxin’s apical dominance effect, promoting lateral bud growth.

Developmental roles

Cell division. Cytokinins directly promote the cell cycle by activating cyclin-dependent kinases and cyclin genes, especially in actively dividing tissues like meristems, root tips, and developing fruits.

Shoot initiation. Cytokinins promote shoot formation, both in regeneration and in normal development. Callus tissue treated with high cytokinin concentration produces shoots; high auxin produces roots.

Senescence delay. Cytokinins markedly delay leaf senescence — old leaves remain green and photosynthetically active when exposed to cytokinins. Detached leaves that would yellow within days can be kept green for weeks by cytokinin application. This effect is so pronounced that cytokinins were initially called “anti-aging” hormones and subsequently studied for agricultural preservation of cut flowers and vegetables.

Chloroplast development. Cytokinins promote chlorophyll synthesis and chloroplast biogenesis. This is why cytokinin-treated leaves remain greener than untreated controls.

Nutrient mobilization. Cytokinins promote nitrogen uptake in roots and remobilization of nutrients from senescent leaves to growing tissues. This makes cytokinins important regulators of plant nutrient status.

Root-to-shoot communication

The cytokinins produced in root tips are transported upward in the xylem sap, carrying information about root growth status and nutrient availability to the shoot. Low root cytokinins signal water or nutrient stress; high cytokinins signal favorable root conditions. This root-to-shoot signaling complements the auxin signal that travels basipetally from the shoot apex.
