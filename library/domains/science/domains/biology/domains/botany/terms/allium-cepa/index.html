Allium cepa

2026-04-02 Bulbous biennial herb with concentric tunicate bulb structure, highly responsive to photoperiod in controlling bulb initiation and flowering.

Allium cepa L., the common or bulb onion, is a biennial species in the family Amaryllidaceae, native to southwestern Asia. It is distinguished by its concentric, tunicate bulb structure—layers of modified leaf bases that wrap around each other like paper—and by its exceptional sensitivity to photoperiod, which controls both bulb initiation and the timing of bolting to flowering.

Taxonomy and Bulb Morphology

The species contains multiple cultivar groups, the most economically important being cepa var. cepa (common bulb onions) and cepa var. aggregatum (shallot and potato onion groups). The bulb consists of swollen, fleshy leaf bases arranged in concentric rings around a central basal plate, with an external tunic of dried, papery outer scales. Bulb diameter ranges from 4-15 cm depending on variety and growing conditions. Unlike Allium sativum, which segments into discrete cloves, A. cepa forms a single unified tunicate bulb.

The plant is typically grown as an annual, with seed sown in early spring, transplanted seedlings set out after frost danger passes, and mature bulbs harvested in midsummer. The inflorescence is a terminal umbel containing 1,200-2,000 small flowers on a leafless scape that can exceed 1 meter in height.

Photoperiod and Bulbing Physiology

Bulb initiation in A. cepa is controlled by photoperiod: bulbs form when day length exceeds a cultivar-specific critical threshold, typically 12-16 hours depending on variety. Cultivars are classified as short-day (bulb at 12-13 hours, adapted to latitudes 25-35°N), intermediate-day (bulb at 14-15 hours, adapted to 35-40°N), or long-day (bulb at 15-16+ hours, adapted to 40°N and poleward). This photoperiodic control is independent of temperature and represents an adaptation to local growing seasons across a wide geographic range.

Flowering, by contrast, requires both vernalization (prolonged cold exposure) and long-day photoperiods, preventing bolting in young plants and allowing bulb enlargement before reproductive commitment.

Cultivar Diversity

Within cepa var. cepa, diversity encompasses numerous skin colors (yellow, red, white), flavors (sweet to pungent), storage longevity, and bulb shapes (flattened, globe, elongated). The aggregatum group, commonly called shallots, produces clusters of smaller bulbs and offers more delicate flavor profiles than common bulbing onions. Yellow cultivars dominate commercial production and store best; red and white onions are typically used fresh or for processing.

Chemistry and Flavor

Onion flavor derives from volatile sulfur compounds released when cell walls are damaged, including diallyl disulfide and thiosulfates. Pungency correlates with sulfur content, which varies by cultivar and growing conditions. Sweet onion cultivars, often grown in low-sulfur soils, develop higher proportions of sugars relative to sulfur compounds. Quercetin, a flavonoid antioxidant, accumulates in red onion cultivars.
