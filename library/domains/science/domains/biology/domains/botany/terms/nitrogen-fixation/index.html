nitrogen fixation

2026-04-02 The biological conversion of atmospheric N₂ to ammonia (NH₃) via the nitrogenase enzyme, particularly in symbiosis with legumes and through free-living soil bacteria

Nitrogen fixation is the biological process by which atmospheric dinitrogen (N₂) is reduced to ammonia (NH₃), a form that organisms can assimilate. This occurs through the catalytic action of the enzyme nitrogenase, which catalyzes a reaction requiring substantial energy input (ATP) and electron transfer. The nitrogenase complex comprises two proteins: an iron-molybdenum cofactor (FeMoco) that binds and reduces substrate nitrogen, and an electron-transfer component that channels electrons and protons to drive the reaction.

¶Rhizobium-Legume Symbiosis

The most agriculturally significant fixation pathway occurs in root nodules of legumes, where soil bacteria of the genus Rhizobium (and related genera including Mesorhizobium, Sinorhizobium, and Bradyrhizobium) form symbiotic partnerships with host plants. The infection process begins when Rhizobium cells secrete nodulation (Nod) factors—specific lipopolysaccharide molecules recognized by root hair cells. These factors trigger root hair curling and formation of infection threads, tubular structures that grow from the epidermal cell through the cortex into dividing nodule cells.

Inside nodule cells, rhizobia undergo terminal differentiation into bacteroids, cells that are polyploid, enlarged, and specialized for nitrogen fixation. These bacteroid cells are enclosed by a plant-derived membrane within structures called symbiosomes, which function as semi-autonomous organelles. This spatial organization is critical: the plant provides carbohydrates (succinate and malate) translocated from photosynthetically active leaves, while bacteroids export ammonia directly for plant assimilation.

Under active nitrogen fixation, symbiotic nodules fix 80–300 kg N per hectare per year, depending on plant species, soil conditions, and rhizobial strain efficiency. The plant’s red-pigmented protein leghemoglobin, concentrated in nodule cytoplasm, maintains oxygen concentrations at levels suitable for nitrogenase activity (nitrogenase is oxygen-sensitive and rapidly denatured at atmospheric concentrations).

¶Free-Living Nitrogen Fixers

Beyond legume-Rhizobium symbiosis, other organisms fix nitrogen independently. Cyanobacteria (Nostoc, Anabaena) are photosynthetic fixers abundant in soil and water, often forming associations with plant roots. Azotobacter, a free-living heterotrophic bacterium, fixes nitrogen aerobically in soil and decomposing organic matter. These free-living populations are generally less efficient than symbiotic systems but are ecologically important for N recycling and in systems where legume hosts are unavailable.

¶Agricultural and Ecological Significance

Nitrogen fixation underpins sustainable polyculture and crop rotation practices. By intercropping or rotating legumes with cereals, farmers reduce reliance on synthetic nitrogen fertilizers and restore soil nitrogen pools. Organic agriculture depends heavily on this biological process; the Haber-Bosch industrial process (synthetic ammonia production) requires high energy input and accounts for ~2% of global energy use, while biological fixation operates at ambient temperature and pressure, powered by photosynthetically derived carbon.
