Hog Peanut

2026-04-02 Native North American legume producing both aerial and subterranean fruits, historically harvested by Indigenous peoples

Hog Peanut (Amphicarpaea bracteata)

Amphicarpaea bracteata, the American hog peanut or ground bean, is a native North American annual or short-lived perennial legume exhibiting amphicarpy—the production of two distinct types of fruits from morphologically different flowers. This dual reproductive strategy generates both aerial pods and subterranean cleistogamous seeds, an evolutionary adaptation supporting reproductive assurance across variable environmental conditions.

Taxonomy and Distribution

Amphicarpaea bracteata represents the only North American species in its genus, which includes related taxa in East Asia. The species ranges throughout woodlands, thickets, and moist slopes from Nova Scotia west to the Pacific Northwest and south through the Appalachian region. Disjunct populations occur in the Upper Peninsula of Michigan and adjacent regions, where it thrives in disturbed woodland edges and wetland margins. The species constitutes a relict of ancient Tertiary flora patterns connecting North American and East Asian temperate forests.

Distinctive Amphicarpous Morphology

Hog peanut produces vining, tendril-bearing stems reaching 60–120 cm in length, bearing compound trifoliate leaves. The plant generates two types of flowers on separate structures:

Aerial flowers appear in small, open inflorescences producing typical legume flowers with purple or lavender coloration. These chasmogamous (open) flowers produce small, few-seeded pods that mature above ground, primarily functioning in genetic diversity through cross-pollination.

Subterranean flowers develop on below-ground stolons (rhizome-like runners) extending outward from the base of the plant. These cleistogamous (self-pollinating, closed) flowers produce pear-shaped, single-seeded pods 8–12 mm in length that mature entirely underground. Seeds from underground pods are “sweet and delicious,” tasting similar to garden beans when cooked, with a nutty undertone.

Underground Seed Production and Harvesting

The subterranean seed production distinguishes A. bracteata from most temperate legumes. A single plant can produce 10–30 underground seeds per growing season, which mature to hard, dark seeds adapted for overwinter survival in soil. These seeds remain the primary food value. Indigenous peoples including the Iroquois and Cherokee discovered that meadow vole (Microtus spp.) caches of hog peanut seeds could be harvested by replacing the cached seeds with corn or dried fruit, a reciprocal harvesting practice documented in Haudenosaunee ethnobotanical tradition.

Nitrogen Fixation

Like other legumes, Amphicarpaea bracteata establishes rhizobial associations with Rhizobium species, producing nitrogen-fixing nodules on roots. Symbiotic nitrogen fixation supplements plant nutrition in nitrogen-limited soils, contributing to ecosystem nutrient cycling in native woodland communities.

Light and Shade Ecology

Hog peanut exhibits remarkable shade tolerance, thriving from full sun to deep forest shade. This flexibility permits occurrence in diverse microhabitats—open meadows, woodland understories, and transitional zones. The species functions as a minor contributor to nitrogen cycling in temperate deciduous forests where it occurs as scattered individuals.

Indigenous Use and Ethnobotany

Historically, Amphicarpaea bracteata provided supplemental protein sources for Indigenous nations throughout the eastern woodlands. Beyond food use, Amphicarpaea preparations served medicinal purposes including antidiarrheal, gastric tonic, and treatment for gastrointestinal parasites, documented in Cherokee and Haudenosaunee traditional medicine.
