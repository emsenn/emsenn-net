Allium fistulosum

2026-04-02 East Asian native bunching onion with day-neutral photoperiod response, non-bulbing, and rapid maturation, widely cultivated as heirloom cultivar group in Japan and adapted to diverse climates.

Allium fistulosum L., the Japanese or bunching onion, is a non-bulbing species in the family Amaryllidaceae, native to East Asia (China, Japan, Korea) and cultivated across temperate regions as both culinary vegetable and ornamental. It is distinguished from Allium cepa by its strictly non-bulbing habit, day-neutral photoperiod response (bulbing does not occur regardless of daylength), and rapid maturation within 60-90 days of sowing, making it valuable for sequential plantings and year-round production.

Morphology and Cultivar Diversity

A. fistulosum grows as dense clumps of hollow, tubular, green leaves 4-8 mm diameter, reaching 30-60 cm tall depending on cultivar. Unlike A. cepa, which forms single enlarged bulbs, A. fistulosum produces no swollen basal structure—the entire edible portion consists of the white shank (blanched leaf bases), green leaves, and minimal root development. The terminal inflorescence is a loose umbel 3-6 cm diameter of white, purple-tinged, or purplish flowers blooming in the second year or upon bolting in cool conditions.

Cultivar diversity is substantial, particularly within Japanese heirloom selections. The Tokyo Long White group (also called Senju, Naga Negi, Shiro Negi) is the most commercially important, producing stems 18-24 inches tall with extended white shanks and mild flavor. Other types include red-tinged cultivars, shorter spring varieties, and winter-hardy types selected for fall and winter harvest.

Photoperiod and Cultivation Implications

A. fistulosum exhibits complete day-length neutrality: plants grow and mature identically under 12-hour (short-day) and 16-hour (long-day) conditions. This photoperiodic indifference allows cultivation across all latitudes and multiple plantings throughout the year in appropriate climates. Unlike A. cepa, which requires matching cultivar day-length classification to latitude, A. fistulosum cultivars are universally adapted.

Growth Rate and Sequential Planting

Maturation occurs within 60-90 days of sowing, enabling 2-3 successive plantings per growing season in temperate climates, or year-round production in mild regions with autumn-spring primary seasons and summer dormancy. Cool-season growth (spring and fall) produces larger, more tender stems than summer growth in hot climates; summer growth in cool regions (maritime zones) produces excellent yields.

Japanese Heirloom Traditions

Japanese agricultural and horticultural traditions preserve dozens of A. fistulosum cultivars, many dating to the Edo period (1603-1868) and maintained as family or regional selections. These heirlooms reflect optimization for specific regional climates, soil conditions, and culinary preferences. Tokyo Long White and related northeastern cultivars are adapted to cool climates and offer extended white shanks suited to Japanese cuisine; Kansai-region heirlooms are earlier-maturing and better suited to warm climates.
