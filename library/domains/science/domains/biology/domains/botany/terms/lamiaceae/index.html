Lamiaceae

2026-04-02 Mint/labiate family — ~236 genera, ~7,200 species. Diagnostic square stems, opposite leaves, bilabiate (two-lipped) flowers, aromatic essential oils in glandular trichomes. Includes basil, oregano, rosemary, sage, mint, thyme, lavender; major culinary and medicinal importance.

Lamiaceae (the mint or labiate family; formerly Labiatae) is a large angiosperm family comprising approximately 236 genera and 7,200 species distributed worldwide, with highest diversity in Mediterranean, temperate, and tropical regions. The family is characterized by extraordinary aromatic secondary metabolism and is among the most economically important plant families for culinary herbs, medicinal plants, and ornamentals.

Diagnostic Morphology

Lamiaceae display several consistent and immediately recognizable morphological features:

Stems are characteristically square (quadrangular, tetragonal) in cross-section, arising from the opposite, decussate (alternately crossing) arrangement of leaf primordia and the four-sided arrangement of vascular bundles. The square stem is so diagnostic that “if it’s square, it’s mint” is a common botanical adage. (Note: some genera have rounded stems and belong to Lamiaceae by other characters; and some non-Lamiaceae herbs have square stems, so the rule is not absolute but highly useful.)

Leaves are opposite (pairs of leaves at each node, alternating perpendicular to those above and below), typically simple, and often aromatic when crushed. Leaf margins vary from entire to serrate to lobed.

Flowers are characteristically bilabiate (two-lipped, zygomorphic): the corolla is divided into an upper lip and lower lip, which are morphologically and functionally distinct. The upper lip (often called the “hood” or “galea”) typically has two lobes and may be arched or recurved. The lower lip (called the “labellum”) typically has three lobes and often serves as a landing platform for pollinators. This bilabiate structure is unique to Lamiaceae (and Scrophulariaceae, a related family). The calyx is also typically five-lobed and bilabiate.

Stamens are typically four (or two in some genera), attached to the corolla tube. Anther orientation and dehiscence vary. The ovary is superior and four-lobed (resulting from the gynoecium structure), developing into four separate nutlets (one-seeded fruits) at maturity.

Inflorescences are typically verticillasters (whorled cymes appearing as dense rings of flowers around the stem) arranged into spikes or panicles.

Chemistry and Aromatic Oils

Lamiaceae are chemically defined by the production of aromatic essential oils (volatile compounds) synthesized in specialized glandular trichomes (hair-like outgrowths on the leaf surface). These oils are responsible for the distinctive scents of the family and provide most of the culinary and medicinal value.

The essential oils are complex mixtures of volatile terpenoids and phenylpropanoids:

Monoterpenoids (10-carbon compounds): limonene, α-pinene, β-pinene (fresh, piney notes); linalool (floral); menthol and menthone (cooling, minty); carvacrol and thymol (peppery, antimicrobial).

Sesquiterpenoids (15-carbon compounds): caryophyllene (peppery, clove-like).

Phenylpropanoids: eugenol (clove-like), anethole (anise-like), estragole.

These compounds are typically present at highest concentration in leaves and flowers just before and at flowering, and their production is thought to serve as chemical defense against herbivores and pathogenic microorganisms.

Ecological Distribution and Pollination

Lamiaceae occupy diverse habitats: temperate woodlands, Mediterranean scrubland (where many genera originate), tropical forests and grasslands, mountains, and disturbed sites. The family is particularly abundant in Mediterranean zones (genera like Salvia, Origanum, Thymus).

Pollination is primarily by insects: bees (attracted to nectar and pollen), butterflies, moths, and sometimes hummingbirds in tropical taxa. The bilabiate flower structure with a lower lip serving as a landing platform is specialized for bee pollination. Some genera are self-pollinating; others show self-incompatibility.

Seed dispersal is primarily autochorous (explosive dispersal from the fruit) or zoochorous (fruits clinging to fur or clothes; some have hooked appendages).

Phylogeny and Internal Diversity

Lamiaceae belongs to order Lamiales (APG IV). Molecular phylogenetics has resolved the family into several well-supported clades, though formal subfamily classification remains under development. Some major clades include:

Nepetoideae: Includes Mentha (mint), Nepeta (catnip), Agastache (hyssop), Lavandula (lavender), and others.

Scutellarioideae: Includes Scutellaria (skullcap), with distinctive fruit structure (nutlets bearing a peltate shield on the dorsal surface).

Lamioideae: Includes Lamium (deadnettle), Stachys (betony), Phlomis (Jerusalem sage).

Ajugoideae: Includes Ajuga (bugle), Teucrium (germander).

Salviinae: Includes Salvia (sage, the largest genus in the family with ~1,000 species), Rosmarinus (rosemary), and other Mediterranean and tropical taxa.

Economic and Culinary Importance

Lamiaceae is one of the most economically important plant families for human use, with nearly every genus producing edible or medicinal properties:

Culinary herbs (fresh and dried):

  • Ocimum basilicum (basil) — essential to Italian, Thai, and African cuisines
  • Origanum vulgare (oregano), O. majorana (marjoram)
  • Salvia rosmarinus (rosemary)
  • Mentha spp. (mints) — peppermint, spearmint
  • Thymus vulgaris (thyme)
  • Satureja (savory)
  • Lavandula (lavender) — culinary and aromatic
  • Perilla (shiso, perilla leaf) — important in East Asian cuisines

Medicinal herbs (traditional and modern):

  • Mentha piperita (peppermint) — digestive, antimicrobial
  • Scutellaria baicalensis (Chinese skullcap) — important in traditional Chinese medicine
  • Melissa officinalis (lemon balm) — calming, antimicrobial
  • Leonurus sibiricus (motherwort) — traditional women’s medicine
  • Leonotis leonurus (wild dagga) — traditional African medicine
  • Nepeta cataria (catnip) — feline attractant, human herbal remedy

Ornamentals: Lavender, salvias, catmint (Nepeta), and numerous other genera are widely cultivated for flowers and foliage.

Volatile oil production: Mentha piperita, Salvia rosmarinus, Lavandula angustifolia, and others are commercial sources of essential oils for perfumery, aromatherapy, and food flavoring.
