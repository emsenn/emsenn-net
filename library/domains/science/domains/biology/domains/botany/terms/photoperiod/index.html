photoperiod

2026-04-02 The duration of light exposure within a 24-hour cycle as an environmental signal regulating plant development, particularly flowering time and seasonal responses

The photoperiod is the duration of light exposure in a 24-hour cycle, measured as the length of daylight or—more precisely in plant physiology—the duration of continuous darkness. Plants measure photoperiod through photoreceptor proteins and use this signal as a seasonal cue to time critical developmental transitions, most notably the initiation of flowering.

The Phytochrome System

The primary photoreceptor for photoperiodic sensing is phytochrome, a protein with two interconvertible forms. Pr (red-light-absorbing form) is the inactive state; upon exposure to red light (during daytime), Pr converts to Pfr (far-red-light-absorbing form), the physiologically active state. At night, Pfr reverts to Pr through absorption of far-red wavelengths or through gradual chemical reversion called dark reversion. This reversible system, coupled with the plant’s internal circadian clock, creates a light-dependent timer that accumulates Pfr during day-length and dissipates it at night, generating a measurable circadian signal of day length.

Long-Day and Short-Day Plants

Plants are classified by their photoperiodic flowering response:

Long-day plants (LDP) flower when night length falls below a critical threshold—typically when days exceed 12–16 hours. These plants flower in late spring and summer as days lengthen. Examples include wheat, barley, lettuce cultivars, onion, and spinach. The critical photoperiod varies by species and cultivar: long-day onion bulb formation requires 14–16 hours of light daily; Arabidopsis flowers under ~16 hours of light.

Short-day plants (also called long-night plants) flower when night length exceeds a critical threshold. They require continuous, uninterrupted darkness above a certain duration; a brief pulse of light during the night can cancel flowering induction. Examples include chrysanthemum, cannabis, and soybeans. These plants flower as day length shortens in autumn.

Day-neutral plants flower regardless of photoperiod, responding instead to age, temperature, or other cues.

Molecular Control: CONSTANS and Florigen

In model organisms like Arabidopsis, photoperiodic flowering integrates phytochrome detection with circadian rhythm of the CONSTANS (CO) gene. CO protein accumulates and remains stable specifically when light is present during the circadian phase when it is normally expressed in darkness; under short-day conditions, CO degrades before the light period arrives, preventing signal output. Under long-day conditions, when light coincides with CO expression, the protein persists and activates the FLOWERING LOCUS T (FT) gene, which encodes florigen, a mobile flowering hormone that moves through the phloem from leaves to the shoot apical meristem to initiate flower development.

Practical Examples

  • Onion bulbing: Bulb initiation depends strictly on photoperiod; long-day varieties require 14–16 hours of light and fail to bulb under short-day conditions.
  • Spinach bolting: A long-day plant; plants flower when day length exceeds ~13–15 hours, independent of temperature in some cultivars.
  • Lettuce flowering: Bolts under long days (>12 hours) combined with high temperature (>24°C).
  • Chrysanthemum: Short-day plant used commercially; short-day chambers artificially shorten light exposure to force flowering for flower production year-round.
