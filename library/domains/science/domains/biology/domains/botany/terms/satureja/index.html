Satureja

2026-04-02 Genus of aromatic perennial and annual herbs in the mint family with narrow leaves and small pink or purple flowers, two species cultivated as seasoning: summer savory (annual) and winter savory (perennial).

Satureja L., commonly called savory, is a genus of approximately 30 perennial and annual herbaceous or woody plants in the family Lamiaceae (mint family), native to Mediterranean and temperate regions of southern Europe and western Asia. Two species are widely cultivated as culinary herbs: Satureja hortensis L. (summer savory), a frost-sensitive annual, and Satureja montana L. (winter savory), a hardy perennial. Both are distinguished by narrow, linear or lanceolate leaves, small pink or purple bilabiate flowers, and volatile essential oils comprising 0.5-2.5 percent of fresh leaf weight, dominated by carvacrol and thymol.

¶Taxonomy and Morphology

Summer savory (S. hortensis) is an annual, reaching 30-40 cm tall, with a bushy, branching habit. Leaves are 1-2 cm long, linear-lanceolate, soft-textured, and hairless or sparsely pubescent. Flowers are small, pink or pale purple, borne in dense, interrupted spikes. The plant is frost-sensitive and dies after first hard frost.

Winter savory (S. montana) is a woody perennial, reaching 20-40 cm tall, with stiffer, more wiry stems and slightly thicker, dark green leaves 0.5-1.5 cm long. The plant is fully hardy in USDA zones 5-9 (some sources cite zones 4-9 for selected cultivars). Flowers are similar in color and arrangement but appear later in summer.

¶Chemistry

Both species are dominated by carvacrol (30-80 percent of essential oil depending on cultivar and growing conditions) and thymol (10-30 percent), with secondary components including γ-terpinene, p-cymene, and limonene. Carvacrol and thymol are potent antimicrobial compounds, with documented activity against Staphylococcus aureus, Escherichia coli, Salmonella, and Vibrio species. The aroma is spicy and warm, with peppery undertones more assertive than in related herbs.

¶Ecology and Cultivation History

Satureja hortensis is native to Mediterranean regions and has been cultivated since ancient times, with evidence in Roman texts and Greek herbals. S. montana is native to rocky, dry hillsides of southern Europe and has been integrated into Mediterranean herbal medicine and cuisine for at least 2,000 years. Both species prefer well-drained, even lean soils and are drought-tolerant once established.

Summer savory is an annual of Mediterranean origin that thrives in warm-season growing; it has a 90-day maturity and bolts to seed in late summer. Winter savory is a woodier, longer-lived perennial that tolerates neglect and winter cold.

¶Culinary Roles and the “Bean Herb”

Savory has the common name “the bean herb” (German: Bohnenkraut) across multiple languages due to its traditional pairing with legumes—particularly green beans, fava beans, and dried beans. This pairing is both culinary (the herb’s spicy character complements beans’ mild flavor) and potentially functional (the herb is believed to reduce intestinal gas). Summer savory’s more tender, delicate flavor makes it preferred for fresh use and light dishes; winter savory’s woodier texture suits dried forms and longer cooking. Both are used in European bean stews, soups, and traditional sausage seasoning. Global commercial cultivation focuses on Mediterranean countries, with annual dried savory production exceeding 5,000 tonnes.
