Rhizome

2026-04-02 A modified subterranean stem that grows horizontally, producing adventitious roots and aerial shoots from its nodes — the organ responsible for clonal spread in grasses, ferns, gingers, irises, and invasive knotweeds
Defines Rhizome, rhizome, rhizomatous

A rhizome is a modified stem that grows horizontally below the soil surface, producing adventitious roots from its lower surface and aerial shoots from its nodes. Despite growing underground, a rhizome is a stem, not a root — it has nodes, internodes, scale leaves, and axillary buds, features that true roots lack. The distinction matters: rhizomes store starch and nutrients in stem parenchyma tissue, generate new shoots from meristematic buds at each node, and can regenerate a complete plant from a fragment containing as little as a single node.

Anatomy

A rhizome in cross-section reveals the internal anatomy of a stem: an epidermis (often replaced by a periderm in older segments), cortex, vascular bundles, and pith. In monocots (grasses, gingers, irises), vascular bundles are scattered throughout the ground tissue. In dicots and basal angiosperms, they are arranged in a ring or in concentric cylinders. The vascular tissue connects the rhizome to both the adventitious root system below and the aerial shoot system above, forming a continuous transport network between the underground reserve and the aboveground photosynthetic apparatus.

Key anatomical features:

Nodes bear scale leaves (reduced, non-photosynthetic leaves), axillary buds (each capable of producing a new aerial shoot or a lateral rhizome branch), and adventitious root primordia. The node is the regenerative unit — rhizome fragments containing at least one node can produce a complete new plant.

Internodes store carbohydrates (primarily starch) in parenchyma cells. This stored energy funds the rapid shoot emergence characteristic of rhizomatous perennials. In Japanese knotweed, the rhizome’s carbohydrate reserve is so large that the plant can produce 3-meter stems within weeks of spring emergence, and can resprout repeatedly after cutting for years before reserves are exhausted.

Apical meristem at the rhizome tip drives horizontal growth. The meristem is positively geotropic (grows parallel to the soil surface) or negatively geotropic (grows downward), depending on species. The direction and rate of growth determine the spread pattern of the clone.

Rhizome vs. root

The distinction between rhizome and root is not always obvious in the field — both are underground, both can be thick and woody, and both branch. The reliable diagnostic characters are:

Feature Rhizome Root
Nodes and internodes Present Absent
Scale leaves Present at nodes Absent
Buds Present (axillary and terminal) Absent (except adventitious)
Internal anatomy Stem-type (pith, cortical bundles) Root-type (central stele)
Origin From stem tissue From root tissue or pericycle

In practice, the presence of nodes — visible as slight swellings with remnant scale leaves or bud scars — is the quickest field identification.

Rhizome vs. other underground storage organs

Rhizomes are one of several types of modified stems or roots used by plants for underground storage and vegetative reproduction:

Stolons (runners) are horizontal stems that grow at or above the soil surface rather than underground (strawberry runners). Some plants produce both stolons and rhizomes.

Tubers are swollen tips of stolons or rhizomes (potato), serving as discrete storage units rather than continuous branching networks.

Bulbs are shortened stems surrounded by fleshy leaf bases (onion, tulip). The storage tissue is modified leaves, not stem parenchyma.

Corms are short, thick, vertical underground stems (crocus, gladiolus), storing energy in stem tissue but not growing horizontally.

Ecological significance

Rhizomatous growth enables three ecological strategies of major importance:

Clonal spread: A single genetic individual (genet) can occupy a large area through vegetative expansion. The largest known clonal organisms are rhizomatous: a single clone of quaking aspen (Populus tremuloides, “Pando”) covers 43 hectares and weighs an estimated 6,000 tonnes. Rhizomatous grasses dominate prairies, tundra, and wetlands through clonal expansion.

Perennial persistence: The rhizome survives conditions that kill aboveground tissue — winter frost, fire, drought, mowing, grazing. Energy stored in the rhizome funds regrowth when conditions improve. This makes rhizomatous perennials extraordinarily resilient and, in the case of invasive species, extraordinarily difficult to eradicate.

Disturbance colonization: Rhizome fragments can be transported by water, soil movement, or human activity and regenerate in new locations. This is the primary mechanism of spread for invasive rhizomatous species, including Japanese knotweed, where fragments as small as 0.7 grams can produce new plants.

Examples

Rhizomatous growth is found across diverse plant lineages:

  • Ferns: bracken (Pteridium aquilinum) — among the oldest and most widespread rhizomatous plants
  • Grasses: bamboo, quack grass (Elymus repens), reed (Phragmites australis)
  • Monocots: ginger (Zingiber officinale), turmeric (Curcuma longa), iris (Iris spp.), lily of the valley (Convallaria majalis)
  • Dicots: Japanese knotweed (Reynoutria japonica), mint (Mentha spp.), poison ivy (Toxicodendron radicans)
