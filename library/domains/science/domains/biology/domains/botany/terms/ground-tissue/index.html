Ground Tissue

Ground tissue: the tissue system that comprises the bulk of plant organs, filling the space between the dermal tissue and vascular tissue. The ground tissue system is composed of three cell types, each optimized for different functions: parenchyma, collenchyma, and sclerenchyma.

Parenchyma cells are the most abundant cell type in plants. They are thin-walled, isodiametric (roughly equal in all dimensions), living at maturity, and retain the capacity for mitotic division. Parenchyma cells are the principal site of photosynthesis in leaves and green stems, the principal storage cells for starch and other reserves in roots and tubers, and the matrix cells of nearly every plant organ. They are also totipotent — under appropriate conditions, a single parenchyma cell can regenerate an entire plant, a capacity that underlies vegetative propagation and plant tissue culture.

Collenchyma cells have thickened cell walls, particularly at the corners where cell walls meet. This selective reinforcement provides flexible support — collenchyma resists bending and stretching without eliminating growth. Collenchyma is abundant in leaf petioles and the angular edges of stems, where it provides mechanical support while remaining extensible. The visible “strings” in celery are collenchyma strands.

Sclerenchyma cells have thick, heavily lignified secondary cell walls and are typically dead at maturity. Sclerenchyma cells come in two forms: fibers, which are elongate and grouped in strands providing tensile strength; and sclereids, which are shorter and variously shaped, forming hard structures like the pits of fruits (endocarp), the seed coat, and the grit cells in pears. Sclerenchyma provides structural support but, because it is rigid and dead, does not undergo growth.

These three cell types form a spectrum of mechanical function. Parenchyma is soft and expandable, suitable for growth and metabolic activity. Collenchyma is flexible and reinforced, providing support to growing organs. Sclerenchyma is rigid and load-bearing, the structural foundation of woody plants and hard plant structures. Together, they enable the plant body to grow, photosynthesize, store reserves, and maintain shape — the essential functions of a modular, sessile organism.
