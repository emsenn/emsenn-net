Asteraceae

2026-04-02 Daisy/composite/aster family — ~1,911 genera, ~32,000+ species (largest angiosperm family). Diagnostic composite flower head (capitulum) composed of ray and disc florets, inferior ovary, pappus for wind dispersal. Includes sunflower, lettuce, chamomile, dandelion, artichoke.

Asteraceae (the daisy, composite, or aster family; formerly Compositae) is the largest angiosperm family by species count, comprising approximately 1,911 genera and 32,000 or more described species distributed across every continent and nearly every terrestrial ecosystem. The family represents roughly 10% of all flowering plant species and includes fundamental food crops, medicinal plants, ornamentals, and invasive species.

¶Diagnostic Morphology: The Capitulum

The defining characteristic of Asteraceae is the capitulum (plural: capitula or capitulums)—a specialized inflorescence that superficially resembles a single flower but is actually a densely packed cluster of many tiny flowers (called florets) all arranged on a common receptacle (floral disk) and surrounded by a whorl of protective bracts called the involucre.

To a casual observer, a sunflower, daisy, or dandelion appears to be one large flower. In reality, what appears to be the “petals” are specialized florets, and what appears to be the “center” is made up of hundreds of fertile florets.

Ray florets (or ray flowers) are sterile or female florets with a single, strap-shaped corolla lobe extending outward like a petal. They are arranged at the periphery of the capitulum and serve a visual attraction function (mimicking a single large flower to pollinators) while the actual reproductive work is performed by the disc florets. Not all Asteraceae have ray florets; some genera (e.g., Chamomilla) produce capitula of disc florets only.

Disc florets (or disc flowers) are the fertile florets occupying the center of the capitulum. They are typically small, radially symmetric, and often yellowish or reddish. Each disc floret has a small tubular corolla with five lobes and ten stamens (five pairs fused into a tube—anther tube—through which the style passes). The stigma is often forked.

Ovary and fruit: The ovary in Asteraceae is inferior and develops into a one-seeded fruit called a cypsela (often loosely called an “achene”). Cypeselae are typically small and hard; distinctive pappus appendages often crown the fruit.

Pappus: A characteristic structure unique to Asteraceae is the pappus—a modified calyx consisting of bristles, scales, hairs, or a crown surrounding the fruit. The pappus aids in wind dispersal: the feathery, parachute-like pappus of dandelion (Taraxacum) is iconic. In some taxa, the pappus is absent or reduced to a rim. The pappus is a major diagnostic feature used in identifying Asteraceae genera.

Leaves are typically alternate, simple to compound, with variable margins; some genera show opposite leaves.

¶Chemistry and Secondary Metabolism

Asteraceae show great chemical diversity, but several secondary metabolite classes are characteristic:

Latex and resins: Many Asteraceae produce white or colored latex (milky sap) containing complex organic molecules; notable in Taraxacum (dandelion). The latex may contain bitter compounds and resins with antimicrobial and anti-herbivory properties.

Polyacetylenes and terpenes: Volatile and non-volatile compounds contributing to the distinctive scents and flavors of chamomile, yarrow, and other aromatic taxa.

Inulin: A soluble polysaccharide sugar storage compound synthesized by many Asteraceae (e.g., Jerusalem artichoke, chicory root). Inulin is not digestible by human enzymes and serves as a prebiotic, promoting beneficial gut bacteria.

Sesquiterpene lactones: Oxygenated triterpenoids with antimicrobial and immunomodulatory properties; present in species used medicinally.

¶Phylogeny and Internal Diversity

Asteraceae belongs to order Asterales (APG IV). The family’s internal phylogeny is well-studied and reveals robust clades. Some major groups include:

Subfamily Asteroideae (~17,000 species): The largest subfamily, including most familiar daisy-like plants. Contains major tribes such as Anthemideae (chamomile, yarrow, feverfew), Heliantheae (sunflower, coreopsis), Astereae (asters, goldenrod), Senecioneae (ragwort, groundsel), Cardueae (thistles, knapweed).

Subfamily Cichorioideae (~3,000 species): Characterized by all florets being ray florets (no disc florets). Includes Taraxacum (dandelion), Lactuca (lettuce), Cichorium (chicory), Tragopogon (salsify), and Cynara (artichoke, thistle).

Subfamily Carduoideae (~3,000 species): Includes thistles and related taxa; florets are tubular, typically pink, purple, or red.

Other smaller subfamilies complete the diversity.

¶Ecological Distribution and Pollination

Asteraceae occupy nearly every terrestrial habitat: grasslands, forests, deserts, wetlands, high mountains, and disturbed sites. They range from annuals to long-lived perennials, woody shrubs, and (rarely) trees. The family is particularly abundant in Mediterranean scrubland, temperate grasslands, and tropical savannas.

Pollination is primarily by insects (bees, flies, butterflies, beetles) attracted by the visual display of the capitulum and rewarded with abundant pollen and nectar from disc florets. Some genera are wind-pollinated (ragweed, Ambrosia, is a major wind-pollinated allergen). Self-pollination is common in some genera (e.g., Taraxacum).

Seed dispersal varies: wind dispersal via pappus (dandelion, thistles, milkweed), animal ingestion and scat dispersal (fruits eaten by birds and mammals), and human dispersal (seeds clinging to fur or clothing).

¶Economic and Ecological Importance

Food crops:

  • Helianthus annuus (sunflower) — seeds, oil
  • Lactuca sativa (lettuce) — primary leaf vegetable worldwide
  • Cynara cardunculus var. scolymus (artichoke) — edible flower buds
  • Cichorium intybus (chicory) — root vegetable, chicory coffee
  • Helianthus tuberosus (Jerusalem artichoke) — tubers

Culinary herbs and spices:

  • Matricaria chamomilla (chamomile) — herbal tea
  • Achillea millefolium (yarrow) — traditional medicinal herb
  • Tagetes spp. (marigold) — flowers as edible garnish and in traditional medicine

Ornamentals: Dahlias, chrysanthemums, sunflowers, zinnias, cosmos, rudbeckia, echinacea, and countless others.

Medicinal plants: Echinacea (coneflower) for immune support; Silybum marianum (milk thistle) for liver support; Artemisia annua (sweet wormwood) source of artemisinin antimalarial.

Invasive species: Ragweed (Ambrosia spp.), Canada thistle (Cirsium arvense), and spotted knapweed (Centaurea stoebe) are major agricultural and ecological pests.

Allergens: Ragweed pollen is a leading cause of autumn allergic rhinitis in North America.
