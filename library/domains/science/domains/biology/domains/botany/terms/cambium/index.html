Cambium

Cambium: a lateral meristem — a thin, active layer of cells responsible for secondary (lateral) growth, the process by which stems and roots increase in diameter. A cambium is a perpetually juvenile tissue that remains meristematic throughout the plant’s life, continuously dividing to produce new secondary xylem and phloem.

The vascular cambium is the principal cambium in woody plants. It is a single-celled-thick cylindrical sheet positioned between the primary xylem (toward the center of the stem) and the primary phloem (toward the periphery). When a vascular cambium cell divides, one daughter cell differentiates into xylem (adding to the inner surface), while the other remains meristematic or differentiates into phloem (adding to the outer surface). This asymmetrical division layer by layer accumulates wood — the secondary xylem. The wood production is often seasonal: in temperate and continental climates, faster growth in spring (when water is abundant and day length is increasing) produces large xylem cells; slower growth in late summer produces smaller cells with thicker walls. This variation in cell size creates the visible annual growth rings seen in a cross-section of a tree trunk.

The cork cambium (also called the phellogen) appears later in development and replaces the epidermis as the outer protective layer. It produces cork cells outward (forming the bark) and the phelloderm inward. Cork cells are dead at maturity, with suberin-impregnated walls that are highly impermeable to water, providing excellent insulation and protection against desiccation and frost. Over time, successive layers of cork cambium may form at deeper levels in the stem, producing the thick, furrowed bark of old trees.

The vascular and cork cambia are absent or rudimentary in monocots, which have scattered vascular bundles and lack the ring anatomy necessary for a functional vascular cambium. As a result, monocots lack secondary growth and cannot form woody trees — with few exceptions (palms achieve height through primary growth). Most woody trees and shrubs are eudicots or gymnosperms with robust vascular cambia.

The cambium’s capacity to produce wood for decades or centuries is a fundamental feature of tree physiology and is the basis of forestry, timber production, and the carbon storage capacity of forests. The rate of cambial activity is responsive to environmental cues (day length, temperature, water availability) and to hormonal signals from the shoot and root — auxin and gibberellins promote cambial activity, while cytokinins enhance xylem cell differentiation.
