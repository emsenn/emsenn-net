Translocation

2026-04-02
Defines Translocation, translocation, phloem translocation, pressure-flow hypothesis, Münch hypothesis

Translocation: the movement of organic solutes (primarily sucrose, the main transport sugar in phloem) through the phloem from source tissues to sink tissues. Unlike the transpiration stream, which moves passively from roots to shoots driven by evaporative tension, phloem translocation is active, energy-dependent, and bidirectional. Sugar moves from photosynthetic leaves (sources) downward to roots, storage organs, and developing tissues (sinks), and also moves upward from storage organs mobilizing reserves during growth or reproduction. Translocation velocities are rapid — 50 to 100 centimeters per hour — much faster than diffusion alone could achieve.

The driving mechanism for translocation is the Münch pressure-flow hypothesis (also called the mass flow hypothesis), proposed in 1927 and now well-supported by experimental evidence. The hypothesis operates as follows: At a source (e.g., a photosynthetic leaf), sucrose is actively transported (via H⁺-ATPase-powered co-transporters) into phloem sieve elements by their associated companion cells. This loading increases the solute concentration in the sieve element, lowering the water potential. Water follows by osmosis, increasing turgor pressure. At a sink (e.g., a developing root or fruit), sucrose is unloaded from the phloem and either stored or consumed in growth and respiration. This unloading decreases turgor pressure in the sieve element. The difference in turgor pressure between source and sink creates a pressure gradient that drives bulk flow of solution from source to sink. The solution moves as a viscous fluid through sieve tubes, carrying all solutes along — a mechanism analogous to water flowing through a pipe under pressure difference.

Translocation is far more selective than transpiration. While the xylem delivers primarily water and minerals, the phloem delivers organic molecules of plant synthesis: sugars (carbohydrates for energy and building), amino acids (protein synthesis), lipids (membrane synthesis and energy storage), hormones (developmental signals), nucleotides (DNA/RNA synthesis), and secondary metabolites (signaling and defense compounds). Some of these molecules are also proteins and nucleic acids, whose movement through phloem indicates that sieve tubes are living, interconnected cells capable of selective transport.

The source-sink relationship is plastic and responds to developmental and environmental cues. Young leaves are sinks (import sugar for growth). Mature, fully expanded leaves are sources (export sugar from photosynthesis). Developing fruits are strong sinks (import large quantities of sugar for growth). Roots are perpetual sinks (import sugar for respiration and storage). During winter dormancy or drought, storage organs (roots, tubers) become sources, mobilizing starch and other reserves to sustain basal metabolism and support spring growth.

The coupling of source tissues (leaves), translocation pathway (phloem), and sink tissues (roots, meristems, fruits) is a fundamental integration system in plants, coordinating carbon allocation across the plant body and linking photosynthetic production to growth demands.
