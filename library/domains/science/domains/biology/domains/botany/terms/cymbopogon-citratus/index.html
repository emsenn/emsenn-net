Cymbopogon citratus

2026-04-02 Tropical perennial grass native to South Asia and Southeast Asia, with elongated leaf blades containing 0.5-1.5 percent essential oil dominated by citral, foundation of Thai, Vietnamese, Indonesian, and Southeast Asian cuisines.

Cymbopogon citratus (DC.) Stapf, commonly called lemongrass, is a tropical perennial grass in the family Poaceae (grass family), native to South Asia (particularly India and Sri Lanka) and Southeast Asia (Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia, Vietnam). It is distinguished by long, linear leaf blades arising from a central pseudostem, reaching 1-1.5 m tall, with a distinctive lemon aroma produced by essential oils comprising 0.5-1.5 percent of fresh leaf weight, dominated by the monoterpene aldehydes citral (geranial and neral isomers). The plant is freeze-intolerant and is cultivated as an annual in temperate climates or as a container plant.

¶Taxonomy and Morphology

Cymbopogon citratus is one of approximately 56 Cymbopogon species, distinguished from related species by its intense lemony aroma and use as a culinary herb rather than as a perfumery source (though the essential oil is used in cosmetics). The plant forms dense clumps of long, linear, keeled leaf blades, 40-100 cm long and 6-12 mm wide, with a prominent midvein. Leaves are arranged in two ranks and are gray-green in color.

The plant does not flower readily in cultivation outside tropical regions with consistent photoperiod and warmth. In tropical conditions with extended daylength and warmth, inflorescences develop as branched, compound racemes with small, inconspicuous florets.

¶Chemistry

The essential oil is dominated by citral (60-90 percent), comprising the cis isomer neral (30-50 percent) and the trans isomer geranial (35-50 percent), with the ratio varying by environmental conditions and cultivar. Secondary components include myrcene (5-15 percent), limonene (2-10 percent), and geraniol (1-5 percent). The combination of geranial and neral produces the bright lemon aroma. Citral has documented antimicrobial properties against Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, and antifungal activity against Candida species.

¶Ecology and Cultivation History

Cymbopogon citratus is native to India and Sri Lanka, where it grows in warm, moist, sunny habitats at elevations from sea level to 2,000 m. The species is pantropical and is now cultivated throughout warm regions of the world. It requires consistent warmth (>15°C) and is killed by freezing; growth is negligible below 10°C. The plant prefers moist, fertile soil and benefits from regular irrigation in dry climates. Propagation is exclusively vegetative via division of clumps; the plant does not produce viable seed in most cultivated regions.

¶Culinary and Regional Roles

Lemongrass is foundational to Thai, Vietnamese, Cambodian, Indonesian, Malaysian, Singaporean, and Sri Lankan cuisines. It is used fresh, dried, or as an infused oil or juice, providing the characteristic flavor profile in tom yum (spicy soup), tom kha gai (coconut curry soup), laksa (spiced noodle soup), and satay dipping sauce. The plant is used in curries, stir-fries, soups, beverages, and salads. Only the tender inner portion (approximately the basal 15 cm of the pseudostem and innermost leaf layers) is typically used; outer, fibrous portions are removed.

Lemongrass is also used in traditional medicine across Southeast Asia and South Asia for digestive complaints, fevers, and antiseptic purposes. Global annual production exceeds 200,000 tonnes, with major cultivation in India, Indonesia, Thailand, Guatemala, and Brazil. Essential oil extraction yields 0.3-0.8 percent of fresh material, used in perfumery, cosmetics, soaps, detergents, and pharmaceuticals.

¶Container Cultivation in Cold Climates

In temperate zones (USDA zones 9-11), lemongrass is grown outdoors as a warm-season annual, planted after last frost and harvested before first frost. In colder climates (zones 3-8), the plant is grown in containers, overwintered indoors in a warm, bright location (15-20°C minimum), and moved outdoors after all frost danger has passed.
