Double Fertilization

2026-04-02

Double fertilization: the defining reproductive innovation of angiosperms, in which a single pollen tube delivers two sperm cells to the ovule. One sperm fuses with the egg cell to form the diploid zygote that will develop into the embryo; the other fuses with the central cell’s two polar nuclei to form the triploid endosperm. This two-for-one event is unique to flowering plants and ensures that endosperm is produced only when fertilization occurs, eliminating wasted provisioning of unfertilized ovules.

¶The process

When a pollen grain germinates on the flower’s receptive stigma, the vegetative cell extends a pollen tube that grows through the style toward the ovule. This growth is guided by chemotactic signals (peptides, calcium gradients, lipids) emanating from the synergid cells flanking the egg cell. The pollen tube can traverse the entire style — sometimes several centimeters — in a matter of hours, growing through the pollen tube is a single, continuous extension of the vegetative cell’s cytoplasm.

During pollen tube growth, the generative cell (initially present as a second nucleus in the mature pollen grain) divides mitotically, producing two sperm nuclei. When the pollen tube penetrates the integument at the micropyle and enters the embryo sac, the tube ruptures, releasing the two sperm into the female gametophyte’s cytoplasm. The two sperm are typically unequal in size and swim through the viscous cytoplasm to their targets.

First fertilization event. One sperm fuses with the egg cell, forming a diploid (2n) zygote. This zygote is totipotent — it will undergo a series of mitotic divisions to form the embryo, which develops through specific stages: globular, heart, torpedo, and mature cotyledonary stages in dicots. The embryo proper (epiblast in grasses) and the suspensor (a temporary tissue anchoring the embryo during development) are derived from this zygote.

Second fertilization event. The second sperm fuses with the central cell, which typically contains two polar nuclei (or, less commonly, a secondary nucleus from a previous fusion of two polar nuclei). The fusion of this sperm with the central cell creates the primary endosperm nucleus, which is triploid (3n, containing one set of chromosomes from each parent sperm and two from the female). This nucleus undergoes multiple rounds of mitosis without cytokinesis (in most species), creating a syncytium — a cell with many nuclei. Cellularization (formation of cell walls around nuclei) follows, producing endosperm tissue.

¶The endosperm: structure and function

The endosperm develops rapidly after fertilization and becomes the dominant tissue in the developing seed. In many species, the endosperm is absorbed by the growing embryo before maturity (e.g., legumes, where the embryo fills the seed). In cereals and other species, the endosperm persists as a substantial tissue in the mature seed.

Composition. Endosperm tissue is predominantly starch (in cereals), oils (in sunflower and canola), or protein (in legumes). The triploid composition matters: genes showing parent-of-origin-dependent expression (imprinting) produce dosage effects. For instance, if both sets of endosperm genome are maternal, the endosperm may develop abnormally — too small or with deficient cell division.

Function. The endosperm feeds the embryo during development and, in many seeds, remains as a food reserve for the seedling after germination. Wheat flour is almost entirely endosperm (the bran is the outer layers, the germ is the embryo). Coconut water is the liquid endosperm; coconut meat is solid endosperm. The barley seed used in brewing is valued specifically for its starch-rich endosperm.

¶Energetic significance

Double fertilization is not a random duplication but an elegant solution to a developmental problem. In gymnosperms (which use only single fertilization), the endosperm-equivalent tissue (megagametophyte) is produced even in unfertilized ovules. This is wasteful: if pollination fails, the plant has expended resources building nutritive tissue that will never support an embryo. Angiosperms avoid this through double fertilization: endosperm is produced only when fertilization occurs, coupled to successful reproduction.

The triploid endosperm has an additional advantage: by containing two maternal and one paternal genome set, it creates an endosperm that is “more maternal” than the embryo (which is half paternal, half maternal). This biases endosperm development toward supporting the seed rather than potentially conflicting parental interests. The paternal genome in the endosperm is “held back” numerically, reducing its influence over growth, a phenomenon interpreted through the lens of genomic imprinting and parent-of-origin effects.

¶Evolutionary innovations enabling double fertilization

The evolution of double fertilization required several innovations in angiosperm reproduction:

  • Development of a robust pollen tube mechanism, allowing sperm delivery through a solid tissue
  • Evolution of the embryo sac, particularly the synergid cells that guide pollen tube entry
  • Development of mechanisms for the two sperm to separate and reach their targets
  • Evolution of triploid-viability mechanisms (in gymnosperms, polyploid endosperm is typically lethal)

These features appear to have evolved together in the basal angiosperms 130-140 million years ago, coinciding with the dramatic diversification of flowering plants.
