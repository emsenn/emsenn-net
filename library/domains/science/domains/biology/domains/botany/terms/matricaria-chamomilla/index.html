Matricaria chamomilla

2026-04-02 Annual herb in the daisy family with finely divided feathery foliage and white ray flowers with yellow central disc, flowers rich in bisabolol and chamazulene, widely used in traditional medicine.

Matricaria chamomilla L. (synonym: Matricaria recutita L.), commonly called German chamomile, is an annual herbaceous plant in the family Asteraceae (daisy family), native to the Mediterranean basin and southwestern Asia. It is distinguished by finely divided, feathery, bipinnate foliage; small, daisy-like flowers with white ray florets and a hollow, yellow central disc; and volatile essential oils in flowers that comprise 0.4-1.5 percent by dry weight, dominated by bisabolol and chamazulene.

¶Taxonomy and Morphology

German chamomile is distinguished from Roman chamomile (Chamaemelum nobile, Anthemis nobilis), a perennial Asteraceae species, by its annual life history, lack of hair on stems and leaves, and slightly different essential oil composition. Both are colloquially called chamomile and have been used interchangeably in traditional medicine, though German chamomile is more widely cultivated commercially.

The plant grows 20-60 cm tall with a branching, bushy habit. Leaves are 2-3 cm long, finely divided into filiform segments, creating a feathery appearance and sweet aroma when crushed. Inflorescences are capitula (flower heads) 8-15 mm diameter, with 10-20 white ray flowers and a dome-shaped, hollow yellow receptacle bearing 30-80 disc florets. The receptacle is notably concave and expanded apically. Seeds are small achenes, 0.8-1 mm long, smooth, yellowish-brown.

¶Chemistry

The essential oil is dominated by (−)-α-bisabolol (20-50 percent) and its derivatives, bisabolone, and bisaboloxide. Chamomile blue (azulene or chamazulene, 1-15 percent) develops during drying and storage as the sesquiterpene lactone matricine is converted to chamazulene, imparting a blue tint to dried flowers. Secondary constituents include flavonoids (apigenin, luteolin), coumarin (umbelliferone), and polyphenols. The compounds accumulate principally in florets and receptacle, with minimal concentration in leaves.

¶Ecology and Cultivation History

Matricaria chamomilla is native to temperate and Mediterranean regions from the Mediterranean eastward through southwestern Asia. It has been cultivated for medicinal purposes for at least 2,000 years, with evidence in Egyptian papyri, Roman herbals, and medieval European texts. Domestication involved selection for larger flowers and more compact growth. The plant self-seeds readily, with seeds germinating in autumn in Mediterranean climates or spring in temperate zones.

Chamomile prefers cool-season growing conditions, tolerates light frost, and grows well in disturbed, nutrient-poor soils. It is drought-tolerant once established and attracts diverse beneficial insects, particularly hoverflies and parasitic wasps.

¶Medicinal and Ethnobotanical Significance

German chamomile is the most widely consumed herbal tea in the world, with annual consumption exceeding 1 million tonnes of dried flowers. It is central to German herbal medicine (Phytopharmaka), where it is approved by the German Commission E for internal use to treat gastrointestinal discomfort, and topically for inflammation of mucous membranes. The essential oil compounds, particularly bisabolol, have documented anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and sedative properties in biochemical assays. Global commercial cultivation exceeds 30,000 hectares, primarily in Hungary, Egypt, and India, with annual production of 100,000-150,000 tonnes of dried flowers.
