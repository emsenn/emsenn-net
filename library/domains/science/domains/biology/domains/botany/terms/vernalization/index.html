vernalization

2026-04-02 The requirement for a prolonged cold period to induce flowering in winter annuals and biennials, mediated by epigenetic silencing of the FLC gene

Vernalization is the phenomenon in which a prolonged exposure to cold temperatures is required to induce the transition from vegetative to reproductive growth (flowering) in certain plants. The term derives from vernalis, Latin for “spring,” reflecting the plant’s dependence on winter cold to enable spring flowering. This process is widespread among biennials and winter annuals in temperate and boreal regions.

¶Typical Cold Requirements

Standard vernalization conditions range from 1–7°C (34–45°F) applied for 4–12 weeks, though duration and temperature vary by species and genotype. Winter wheat requires vernalization after germination as a young seedling; winter rye requires similar treatment. Biennial crops including parsley, leek, beet, cabbage, and broccoli must experience a winter cold period between germination and the onset of their second growing season before flowering can proceed.

¶The FLC Mechanism

The molecular basis of vernalization has been elucidated in Arabidopsis thaliana and involves the FLOWERING LOCUS C (FLC) gene, a transcriptional repressor that actively blocks flowering. Winter annual and biennial plants typically carry an active FRIGIDA (FRI) allele that promotes FLC expression, maintaining high levels of FLC protein that suppress genes required for floral development.

Cold exposure induces the expression of VERNALIZATION INSENSITIVE 3 (VIN3), a gene encoding a PHD-domain protein. VIN3 protein recruits the VERNALIZATION 2 (VRN2) polycomb-like complex, which catalyzes trimethylation of histone H3 at lysine 27 (H3K27me3)—a repressive chromatin mark. This histone modification is laid down progressively during the cold period over the FLC locus, progressively silencing FLC transcription.

¶Epigenetic Memory

Once cold has been experienced, FLC silencing persists through the remainder of the plant’s life. This is achieved through epigenetic memory: the H3K27me3 modification remains on FLC chromatin through cell divisions and tissue growth, maintaining repression even as temperatures warm in spring. However, this memory is reset each generation during seed development; seeds produced by vernalized plants return to high FLC expression after germination and require re-vernalization to flower.

This regeneration of FLC expression in each generation is thought to be adaptive, preventing biennial plants from flowering prematurely in warm fall conditions following seed maturation.

¶Ecological and Agricultural Significance

Vernalization serves an ecological function by synchronizing flowering with actual spring arrival rather than early warm spells in late winter. This prevents reproductive investment during unsuitable conditions and reduces frost damage to flowers and developing seeds.

Agriculturally, this distinction is exploited:

  • Winter wheat is sown in autumn and vernalized by winter; it flowers the following spring at the plant’s natural time.
  • Spring wheat is an alternative cultivar lacking FRI (and thus lacking vernalization requirement) that can be sown in spring and flowers within a single growing season.

The choice between winter and spring crops determines planting date, growing season, and harvest timing, making the vernalization requirement a key agronomic trait.

¶Related Phenomena

Premature flowering in late-winter or early-spring warm spells—called bolting in crops—occurs in biennials if they are exposed to cold while very young (even as seeds) and then warmed; this causes inappropriate FLC silencing outside the normal vernalization window. Conversely, insufficient winter cold can prevent biennial flowering entirely, keeping plants vegetative an extra year.
