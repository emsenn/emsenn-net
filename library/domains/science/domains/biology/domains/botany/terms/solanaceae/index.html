Solanaceae

Solanaceae (the nightshade family) is a major angiosperm family comprising approximately 103 genera and 2,700 species distributed across temperate and tropical zones worldwide, with primary diversity in South and Central America. The family includes staple food crops, ornamental plants, botanical medicines, and some of the most lethal poisons known.

Diagnostic Morphology

The defining characteristics of Solanaceae are shared across nearly all members:

Flowers are pentamerous (occasionally tetramerous), actinomorphic, with petals fused into a corolla tube or rotate form. The stamens adhere to the corolla tube and are usually equal in number to petals. The ovary is superior, typically bilocular.

Fruit is either a berry (soft, fleshy, developing from a superior ovary) or a capsule (dry, dehiscent), depending on genus. The fruit typically contains numerous small seeds embedded in pulp or in locules.

Leaves are alternate or opposite, simple to compound, entire or lobed. Stems often bear trichomes (hairs), some glandular and producing volatile compounds.

Phylogeny and Subfamilies

Solanaceae belongs to order Solanales (APG IV). The family is divided into six subfamilies based on molecular phylogenetics: Cestroideae, Goetzeoideae, Nicotianoideae, Petunioideae, Schizanthoideae, and Schwenkioideae. However, nearly 80% of Solanaceae diversity falls within the large genus Solanum (approximately 2,000 species), which itself contains multiple tribes and informal groups. This uneven distribution makes subfamily classification unstable and ongoing work continues to refine internal structure.

Chemistry: Alkaloids and Secondary Metabolism

Solanaceae are defined by their accumulation of alkaloids—nitrogen-containing secondary metabolites with potent biological activity. The family’s alkaloid diversity is extraordinary:

Tropane alkaloids (cocaine, ecgonine): Found in Erythroxylum and some Solanum species; act as dopamine reuptake inhibitors.

Steroidal alkaloids (solanine, tomatine, capsaicinoids): The most widespread in the family. Solanine accumulates in potato (Solanum tuberosum) tubers, particularly in green or damaged tissues as a defense against pathogens and herbivores. Tomatine is present in tomato leaves and unripe fruit, degrading as the fruit ripens. Capsaicinoids (capsaicin, dihydrocapsaicin) are unique to Capsicum peppers, binding to vanilloid receptors (TRPV1) in mammalian sensory neurons and triggering heat sensation.

Atropane alkaloids (atropine, hyoscyamine, scopolamine): Found in Atropa belladonna (deadly nightshade), Datura species, and Hyoscyamus; act as competitive antagonists of acetylcholine at muscarinic receptors. Atropine has medical uses (pupil dilation, antispasmodic) but is lethal in small doses.

Nicotine: The alkaloid defining Nicotiana tabaci (tobacco), acting as a competitive agonist of nicotinic acetylcholine receptors. Nicotine serves as an insecticide in the plant’s tissues.

These alkaloids typically accumulate in all tissues (roots, stems, leaves, flowers, fruit) but often at highest concentrations in unripe fruit and leaves—the tissues that require greatest defense against herbivory. Ripe fruits often show alkaloid depletion, allowing seed dispersal by frugivores.

Ecological Role and Distribution

In their native range (primarily Mesoamerica, the Andes, and lowland South America), Solanaceae are pollinated by bees, hummingbirds, and specialized buzz-pollinating insects. The family occupies diverse habitats: montane cloud forests (Solanum diversity center in the Andes), tropical lowlands, disturbed secondary growth, and arid scrublands. Many species are pioneer plants, colonizing recently disturbed sites.

Seed dispersal is primarily by frugivorous birds and mammals, which consume berries and disperse seeds via scat. This mutualism requires that ripe fruit be attractive (sweet, colorful, nutritious) and safe—hence the alkaloid depletion that occurs during ripening.

Economic and Medicinal Importance

Food crops: Solanum lycopersicum (tomato) is the world’s most consumed vegetable crop. Solanum tuberosum (potato) is a staple starch source in temperate and tropical highlands. Capsicum peppers (chili, bell pepper) are fundamental to cuisines across Mesoamerica, Asia, and Africa. Physalis philadelphica (tomatillo) and other Physalis species are cultivated for their edible, tart berries.

Medicinal and psychoactive plants: Atropa belladonna (deadly nightshade) has been used medically for pupil dilation and as a treatment for asthma, though the margin between therapeutic and lethal dose is extremely narrow. Datura species contain variable mixtures of tropane alkaloids and have been used ritually and medicinally across cultures, with high risk of fatal overdose. Nicotiana tabacum (tobacco) is cultivated globally as a recreational drug and nicotine source.

Ornamentals: Petunia hybrids and Solanum species are widely grown for flowers.
