Japanese Knotweed

2026-04-02 A rhizomatous perennial in Polygonaceae, native to East Asia, among the world's most aggressive invasive plants — also one of the richest natural sources of resveratrol and a major herb in Traditional Chinese Medicine

Japanese knotweed (Reynoutria japonica Houtt.) is a large, rhizomatous herbaceous perennial in the buckwheat family (Polygonaceae), native to Japan, Korea, Taiwan, and northern China. It is among the most aggressive invasive plant species in Europe and North America, capable of penetrating asphalt, concrete foundations, and flood defenses — and simultaneously one of the most pharmacologically significant plants in East Asian medicine, where it has been used for over two thousand years under the name Hu Zhang (虎杖, “tiger stick”).

Principal synonyms: Fallopia japonica (Houtt.) Ronse Decr. (1988); Polygonum cuspidatum Siebold & Zucc. (1846). The species has 35 documented synonyms reflecting its complex taxonomic history.

Taxonomic history

The plant’s nomenclatural history is unusually tangled, reflecting repeated reclassification across three genera. It was first described by Maarten Houttuyn in 1777 as Reynoutria japonica. Carl Peter Thunberg independently described it as Polygonum cuspidatum in 1784, and this name dominated pharmacological and medical literature for over two centuries. In the late twentieth century, molecular phylogenetics moved it into Fallopia (as Fallopia japonica (Houtt.) Ronse Decraene), and many ecological papers use this name. Current consensus, following Plants of the World Online and the Flora of China, restores the original Reynoutria japonica [beerling1994].

All three names appear actively in the literature. Polygonum cuspidatum dominates pharmacological and TCM references. Fallopia japonica dominates ecological and invasive species literature from the 1990s–2010s. Reynoutria japonica is the currently accepted name.

The genus Reynoutria includes two other species of ecological significance: giant knotweed (R. sachalinensis), which is larger but less invasive, and Bohemian knotweed (R. × bohemica), the fertile hybrid between Japanese and giant knotweed. The hybrid is increasingly common in invaded ranges and may be more aggressive than either parent, combining the vigor of Japanese knotweed with the greater cold tolerance of giant knotweed.

Morphology

Japanese knotweed is a deciduous herbaceous perennial that dies back to ground level each winter and regenerates from its rhizome network each spring. Aboveground growth is rapid and substantial:

Stems are hollow, bamboo-like, erect, up to 3 meters tall (occasionally 4 meters in favorable conditions), with distinctive raised nodes and a characteristic zigzag pattern between nodes. Young spring shoots are reddish-purple, resembling asparagus — these are the edible stage. Mature stems are green, speckled with purple, and become woody and brown by autumn. Dead canes persist through winter and can remain standing for several years.

Leaves are broadly ovate to triangular, 7–14 cm long, with a truncate (flat-cut) base that distinguishes them from the cordate (heart-shaped) bases of giant knotweed and the intermediate bases of the Bohemian hybrid. Leaf arrangement is alternate. The leaf tip is acuminate (tapering to a point). Leaves are glabrous on the upper surface.

Flowers appear in late summer (August–October in the Northern Hemisphere) as small, creamy-white, in axillary panicles up to 15 cm long. The plant is functionally dioecious — individual plants are either male-sterile (producing only female flowers) or produce both male and female flowers. In the British Isles, virtually all plants are female clones of a single introduction, meaning they cannot produce viable seed without pollen from a compatible male — but they hybridize readily with R. sachalinensis to produce the Bohemian hybrid [bailey2009].

Fruit is a three-angled achene, approximately 3 mm, enclosed in persistent winged perianth segments (8–9 mm long, three-winged). Seed production varies by region: in both the British Isles and North America, virtually all established Japanese knotweed plants are functionally female clones that produce no pollen. Viable seed production requires cross-pollination from R. sachalinensis or the Bohemian hybrid R. × bohemica, which do produce pollen. Where these species co-occur, hybridization produces seed with high germination rates (84–93% under optimal conditions). Climate warming is extending the zone where seed can mature before frost, raising the prospect of sexual reproduction becoming a significant spread mechanism at higher latitudes.

The rhizome network

The rhizome system is the plant’s most ecologically consequential feature. Rhizomes extend laterally up to 7 meters from the parent stem and penetrate to depths of 3 meters or more. They are dark brown externally and bright orange when freshly cut — this orange interior is a reliable field identification feature. Rhizome segments as small as 0.7 grams (approximately 1 cm) can regenerate into new plants under experimental conditions [beerling1994].

The rhizome network constitutes the bulk of the plant’s biomass. Aboveground tissue — the stems, leaves, and flowers that are visible — represents roughly one-third of total biomass. The remaining two-thirds is underground, stored as a massive carbohydrate reserve that funds the rapid spring growth (stems can extend 8 cm per day under favorable conditions, with some records of 30 cm per day; plants can reach 1 meter in three weeks) and makes eradication by cutting alone futile. The plant simply resprouts from stored reserves. Rhizomes survive temperatures down to −35°C, explaining the species’ establishment in cold-winter regions across northern Europe and Canada.

Rhizomes exploit weaknesses in built structures. They grow through cracks in concrete, asphalt, drainage pipes, and building foundations — not by exerting unusual mechanical force, but by finding existing fissures and expanding as they grow. This capacity for infrastructure damage is the primary reason Japanese knotweed has economic and legal significance far beyond its ecological impact.

Ecology and invasion

Japanese knotweed is native to disturbed volcanic landscapes in Japan, where it is one of the first colonizers of fresh lava flows and volcanic ash deposits. This adaptation to primary succession on bare mineral substrate — an environment with intense light, minimal competition, and no established soil biome — explains much of its invasive success in human-disturbed landscapes.

In its native range, the plant is controlled by a complex of specialist herbivores and pathogens (over 180 species of insects and 40 fungal pathogens attack it in Japan) that limit its growth. In invaded ranges, the absence of these natural enemies allows unchecked growth. This enemy release, combined with the plant’s vegetative reproduction, rapid growth rate, and massive rhizome reserves, produces the dense monocultures that characterize established infestations.

Ecological damage is substantial. Dense knotweed stands shade out native vegetation (reducing light penetration by over 90%), reduce plant species diversity by up to 80% in invaded sites, and alter soil chemistry through allelopathy — the plant releases over 100 identified secondary metabolites (including resveratrol, catechins, and anthraquinones) into the rhizosphere that inhibit germination and growth of neighboring species and suppress mycorrhizal colonization. Riparian knotweed stands are particularly damaging: they displace native riverbank vegetation, increase bank erosion (knotweed’s shallow-rooted aboveground growth provides less soil stabilization than the deep-rooted native plants it replaces), and reduce invertebrate diversity in adjacent waterways.

History of introduction

The plant was introduced to Europe by Philipp Franz von Siebold, a Bavarian physician and naturalist who spent years in Japan during the 1820s–1840s collecting botanical specimens. He brought Japanese knotweed to his nursery in Leiden, Netherlands, around 1850. The plant was initially celebrated — it received a gold medal from the Society of Agriculture and Horticulture at Utrecht in 1847 as “the most interesting ornamental plant of the year.” By the 1850s, it was distributed across European botanical gardens and private estates as an ornamental and as livestock fodder [bailey2009].

The plant spread through vegetative fragmentation — pieces of rhizome carried in soil, garden waste, and river-transported debris. By the early twentieth century, it was naturalized across Britain. By the late twentieth century, it was recognized as one of the most destructive invasive plant species in Europe. The International Union for Conservation of Nature lists it among the world’s 100 worst invasive alien species.

In North America, both male and female plants were introduced (unlike the single-clone British introduction), allowing sexual reproduction and greater genetic diversity in the invasive population.

Medicinal significance

Japanese knotweed is one of the most pharmacologically studied plants in the Polygonaceae. Its root and rhizome are the source of the Traditional Chinese Medicine herb Hu Zhang (虎杖), used for over two millennia for conditions involving heat, dampness, pain, and stasis. See Hu Zhang for the TCM perspective.

The plant is among the richest known natural sources of resveratrol (trans-3,5,4’-trihydroxystilbene) and its glycoside polydatin (piceid). Most commercial resveratrol supplements are extracted from Japanese knotweed rhizome rather than from grape skins, because the plant produces resveratrol at far higher concentrations. The rhizome also contains emodin (an anthraquinone with laxative and anti-inflammatory properties), catechins, and proanthocyanidins.

See Japanese Knotweed as Medicine for a comprehensive treatment of the plant’s pharmacology, traditional uses, and modern clinical research.

Edibility

Young shoots harvested in spring when 15–30 cm tall are edible and have a tart, lemony-green flavor due to oxalic acid content — more accurately compared to sorrel than to rhubarb, despite the common comparison. In Japan, the shoots are called itadori (イタドリ, literally “removes pain” — reflecting the plant’s analgesic reputation) and are eaten as a wild vegetable, typically peeled, soaked or parboiled to reduce sourness, then served boiled, steamed, or chilled. Regional names vary: tonkiba in Yamagata, itazuiko in Nagano and Mie, sukanpo in other regions — the diversity of local names indicating deep integration into regional food cultures. The shoots are also salted for preservation (10 days in salt), made into soups, pies, jams, and wines. In the UK and North America, foraging Japanese knotweed is increasingly promoted as a way to engage the public in invasive species management — eating the enemy.
