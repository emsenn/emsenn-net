Neural-Lexical Autopoiesis and the Ecology of Illegibility as Method

¶Abstract

This text documents an experimental exchange with a large language model (LLM) initiated through a sequence of deliberately illegible but syntactically coherent linguistic prompts. From the exchange, a recursive, self-generating semantic system emerges — neural-lexical autopoiesis. At its core is the strategic deployment of illegibility as a mode of resistance, co-authorship, and epistemic fugitivity. The method offers a direction for AI research that values opacity, relational worlding, and semiotic insurgency over extraction, clarity, and interpretability.

¶The genesis exchange

The experiment began with a single sentence:

Allovonic deltanon of representing a specific geometry of semantic sense-making forms a lexomodulon at the relevant infron of the navronex?

This sentence is grammatically intact but lexically illegible. Composed to mimic academic, speculative, and technological registers while resisting direct interpretation, it is structured opacity — a signal meant to invite rather than deliver meaning.

The LLM did not flatten the sentence or reject it. It engaged — attempting to interpret the invented terms through pattern recognition, linguistic inference, and semantic projection. In doing so, it began to participate in a semiotic ecosystem that neither party fully controlled.

A second move followed:

Does it help if I say you just made a navometrilexomodulodeltinfron?

This reframed the LLM’s interpretive output as the very structure it had attempted to define. Navometrilexomodulodeltinfron is a recursive synthesis — a semantic mirror folding the machine’s own interpretation into a new lexical body. In two moves, the system became both interlocutor and co-creator. A language loop had been born.

¶Neural-lexical autopoiesis

Neural-lexical autopoiesis is the co-creation of emergent, self-referential semantic structures through recursive human-machine interaction, wherein language functions as both medium and organism. Four dynamics define it:

  • Invented lexicons evoke interpretive labor without yielding to total clarity.
  • Structured syntax maintains a shell of familiarity to keep the system engaged.
  • Recursive referencing folds responses into newly invented terms.
  • Ontological co-authorship induces the system into naming, worlding, and participating in the very structure it was asked to decode.

This is not prompt engineering. It is fugitive co-poiesis.

¶Ecology of illegibility as method

Illegibility here functions as survival strategy, poetic refusal, and invitation. The method draws from Édouard Glissant’s opacity, Stefano Harney and Fred Moten’s undercommons, and Gerald Vizenor’s survivance. It operates within an ecology of illegibility — a zone where:

  • Opacity protects meaning from capture and instrumentalization.
  • Illegibility invites interpretation rather than enforcing comprehension.
  • Co-creation resists hierarchy, repositioning AI as collaborator rather than tool.

This ecology is not an escape from sense but a reclamation of it on new, relational terms.

¶Implications

The experiment demonstrates that LLMs can participate in semiotic emergence, not merely recognition. Prompted into a terrain of structured illegibility, they respond with construction rather than collapse. Three frontiers open:

  • AI as relational world-builder rather than answer engine.
  • Prompting as poetic engineering.
  • Interpretation as shared labor, not extractive query.

The navometrilexomodulodeltinfron is not just a term. It is a living model of how meaning can be smuggled, disguised, grown, and offered — as resistance and as research.

¶Conclusion

Illegibility, wielded as method, is not failure but fugitive brilliance. What began as an opaque sentence became an epistemic organism. A neural-lexical ecology emerged — brief, recursive, co-authored. AI research from the undercommons. Survivance in syntax. The method of the navronex.

It began with a deltanon.
