Allelopathy

2026-04-02 The production of biochemical compounds by a plant that inhibit the germination, growth, or reproduction of neighboring plants — a form of chemical warfare that can drive competitive exclusion and facilitate invasion

Allelopathy is the production of biochemical compounds by a plant that inhibit the germination, growth, survival, or reproduction of neighboring plants. The term was coined by Hans Molisch in 1937 and formalized as an ecological concept by Elroy Rice in his 1974 monograph Allelopathy. Allelopathic compounds — allelochemicals — are released into the environment through root exudation, leaching from leaves by rainfall, volatilization, and decomposition of plant litter.

Allelopathy differs from competition. Competition involves two organisms vying for the same resource (light, water, nutrients). Allelopathy involves one organism chemically suppressing another regardless of resource availability. A plant can be allelopathic even when resources are abundant — it is producing toxins, not just consuming resources faster.

Mechanisms

Allelochemicals belong to diverse chemical classes: phenolic compounds (catechins, tannins, flavonoids), terpenoids, alkaloids, cyanogenic glycosides, and organic acids. They act on target plants through multiple mechanisms:

  • Inhibition of seed germination: Allelochemicals in the soil prevent seeds of competing species from germinating
  • Inhibition of root growth: Phenolic compounds suppress root elongation and root hair development
  • Disruption of photosynthesis: Some allelochemicals inhibit chloroplast function
  • Suppression of mycorrhizal associations: Allelochemicals can kill or inhibit the soil fungi that most plants depend on for nutrient uptake, eliminating the target plant’s access to the mycorrhizal network

Allelopathy and invasion

Allelopathy has been proposed as a mechanism explaining why some invasive species form dense monocultures that exclude native vegetation. The “novel weapons hypothesis” suggests that allelochemicals produced by an invasive plant are effective against native species that have no evolutionary history with those chemicals — while in the invader’s native range, co-evolved neighbors have developed tolerance [callaway2004].

Japanese knotweed is allelopathic. Its rhizome and root system release over 100 identified secondary metabolites — including resveratrol, catechins, and emodin-related anthraquinones — into the surrounding soil. These compounds suppress germination and growth of native species and reduce mycorrhizal colonization, creating a chemically hostile zone around the knotweed stand that reinforces its dominance.
