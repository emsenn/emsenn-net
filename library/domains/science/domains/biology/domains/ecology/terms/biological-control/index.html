Biological Control

2026-04-02 The use of living organisms — predators, parasites, pathogens, or competitors — to suppress populations of pest species, most commonly by reuniting an invasive organism with a specialist natural enemy from its native range

Biological control (biocontrol) is the deliberate use of living organisms to suppress populations of pest species. In the context of invasive species management, classical biological control involves importing a specialist natural enemy — typically an insect herbivore, parasitoid, or pathogen — from the invasive species’ native range and releasing it in the invaded range to establish a permanent, self-sustaining check on the invader’s population.

The logic is straightforward: an invasive species is often invasive precisely because it has escaped from the natural enemies that constrain it in its native range (the enemy release hypothesis). Classical biocontrol reverses that escape by reuniting the organism with an enemy specifically adapted to exploit it.

¶How it works

Classical biocontrol follows a structured process that typically takes 10–20 years from initial research to measurable field impact:

1. Target selection: Identify an invasive species that is causing sufficient damage to justify the investment and where other control methods are inadequate. Good biocontrol targets are widespread, ecologically or economically damaging, and not closely related to valued native or crop species.

2. Foreign exploration: Survey the pest’s native range for natural enemies. Collect candidate agents — insects, mites, fungi, or other organisms that attack the target species. Document their biology, host range, and impact on the target in the native range.

3. Host specificity testing: The critical safety step. Each candidate agent is tested against a panel of non-target species (native plants, crops, species related to the target) to determine whether it will attack anything other than the intended target. Only agents with a sufficiently narrow host range proceed. This testing typically takes 3–10 years and is conducted in quarantine facilities.

4. Risk assessment and regulatory approval: Submit test results to regulatory authorities (in the UK, DEFRA; in the US, USDA-APHIS and the Technical Advisory Group for Biological Control Agents of Weeds) for review. Approval requires demonstration that the agent poses negligible risk to non-target species and the environment.

5. Release and establishment: Release approved agents at selected field sites. Monitor survival, reproduction, and population growth. Many agents fail to establish on the first attempt — environmental conditions in the invaded range may not match the agent’s requirements. Multiple releases with different strains or at different sites may be needed.

6. Impact monitoring: Track the agent’s population growth and its effect on the target species over years to decades. Successful biocontrol is rarely dramatic — it is a gradual reduction in the target’s vigor, density, and competitive dominance, typically becoming apparent 5–15 years after successful establishment.

¶What biocontrol does not do

Biocontrol is not eradication. A successful biocontrol agent reduces the pest population to a lower equilibrium level — it does not eliminate it. The agent and its host reach a dynamic balance where the agent’s population is limited by the host’s reduced density, and the host’s population is limited by the agent’s predation. This equilibrium is the desired outcome: the invasive species persists but at a density where its ecological and economic impact is tolerable and where native species can compete.

This is a strength, not a limitation. Unlike chemical or mechanical control, which require repeated application and ongoing expenditure, successful biocontrol is self-sustaining and permanent. Once established, the agent maintains its suppressive effect indefinitely without further human intervention or cost.

¶Risks and controversies

The history of biocontrol includes failures that inform modern practice:

Non-target effects: Early biocontrol programs (before modern host-specificity protocols) sometimes released generalist predators that attacked non-target species. The cane toad (Rhinella marina), released in Australia in 1935 to control sugar cane beetles, is the most notorious example — it failed to control its target and became a devastating invasive species itself. Modern programs use strict host-specificity testing to prevent such outcomes.

Indirect effects: Even host-specific agents can have indirect effects — for example, by reducing an invasive plant that native species have come to depend on (as habitat or food source) in the decades since the invasion. These second-order effects are harder to predict and test for.

Irreversibility: Once a biocontrol agent is released and establishes, it cannot be recalled. This is both the method’s advantage (permanent control without ongoing cost) and its risk (permanent consequences if effects are undesirable). The irreversibility demands rigorous pre-release testing and conservative approval decisions.

¶Case study: Aphalara itadori and Japanese knotweed

The UK’s program to control Japanese knotweed with the psyllid Aphalara itadori is the most prominent contemporary biocontrol program in Europe. After over a decade of host-specificity testing by CABI, the UK government approved the first field release in 2010 — making it the first deliberate release of a biocontrol agent against a weed in the European Union [shaw2009].

The psyllid feeds on knotweed sap and causes leaf curling and stunted growth, reducing the plant’s photosynthetic capacity and competitive vigor. Initial field releases in 2011 were disappointing — the psyllid struggled to establish persistent populations due to predation by native insects and poor survival on older foliage. A second strain (the Murakami line, from a climate more similar to the UK’s) was approved in 2020–2021 and has shown improved survival. Meanwhile, the program has expanded internationally: Canada released psyllids in 2014, the US approved releases in 2020, and Pennsylvania conducted its first operational release in summer 2025 using a strain selected specifically for Bohemian knotweed control.

A second biocontrol approach is under development: the leaf-spot fungus Mycosphaerella polygoni-cuspidati, a pathogen from knotweed’s native range. Field trials at CABI UK (2019–2021) showed it could cause necrotic leaf spots and reduce growth. It has not been approved as a classical biocontrol agent due to potential non-target effects, but is being repositioned as a mycoherbicide — a biological product applied like a herbicide rather than released as a self-sustaining population.

The program reflects the reality that biocontrol operates on timescales of decades, not years.
