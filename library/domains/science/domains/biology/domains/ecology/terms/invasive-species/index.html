Invasive Species

2026-04-02 An organism introduced outside its native range that establishes self-sustaining populations and causes ecological, economic, or health damage — distinguished from merely non-native species by its capacity for harm

An invasive species is an organism that has been introduced — deliberately or accidentally — to a region outside its native range, has established self-sustaining populations there, and causes measurable harm to the ecology, economy, or human health of the invaded region. Not every non-native species is invasive. Most introduced species fail to establish. Of those that establish, most remain ecologically minor. A small fraction — estimated at roughly 1% of introductions, the “tens rule” — become invasive, and these are the ones that cause disproportionate damage.

The distinction matters because the word “invasive” carries regulatory and management consequences. A non-native species that naturalizes without measurable harm (many garden plants, some agricultural crops) is alien but not invasive. A species that causes ecological disruption, infrastructure damage, or health impacts is invasive and typically subject to legal controls on import, trade, and management.

What makes a species invasive

Charles Elton’s The Ecology of Invasions by Animals and Plants (1958), the founding text of invasion biology, framed the problem as one of “ecological explosions” — rapid population growth and geographic spread following introduction to a new environment [elton1958]. Subsequent research has identified several mechanisms that explain why some species become invasive while most do not:

Enemy release: In its native range, an organism’s population is constrained by co-evolved predators, parasites, pathogens, and competitors. When introduced to a new region without these natural enemies, the organism’s population can grow unchecked. This is the primary mechanism behind the invasiveness of Japanese knotweed — controlled by over 180 insect species and 40 fungal pathogens in Japan, it has no specialist enemies in Europe or North America.

Propagule pressure: The probability of establishment increases with the number of individuals introduced and the frequency of introduction events. A single accidental introduction rarely succeeds. Repeated introductions through trade, horticulture, or transport increase the chance that a founder population reaches the critical size needed for persistence.

Disturbance synergy: Invasive species disproportionately thrive in disturbed habitats — roadsides, construction sites, agricultural margins, urban areas. Human activity creates the disturbed habitats that invasive species are pre-adapted to exploit. Urbanization and climate change are both increasing the availability of disturbed habitat globally, expanding the invasion opportunity.

Novel weapons: Some invasive species bring biochemical capabilities that native species have no defense against. Allelopathic plants release compounds that inhibit native species’ growth. Invasive pathogens encounter hosts with no evolved resistance. The “novel weapon” is effective precisely because the invaded community has no evolutionary history with it.

Rapid evolution: Invasive populations can evolve rapidly in their new range, adapting to local conditions, hybridizing with related species (as in the knotweed complex, where Reynoutria japonica × R. sachalinensis produces the aggressively invasive Bohemian knotweed), or evolving increased competitive ability when freed from the cost of defense against native enemies.

Ecological impacts

Invasive species are the second leading cause of species extinction worldwide (after habitat destruction) and a primary driver of biotic homogenization — the process by which distinct regional biotas are replaced by a smaller set of widespread, human-associated species.

Impacts operate at multiple scales:

Population level: Invasive predators drive prey species to extinction (brown tree snake on Guam). Invasive competitors displace native species from their habitat (grey squirrel replacing red squirrel in Britain). Invasive pathogens devastate naive host populations (chestnut blight, sudden oak death).

Community level: Dense invasive monocultures replace diverse native plant communities, reducing species richness and altering food web structure. Invertebrate and bird diversity typically declines in invaded habitats.

Ecosystem level: Invasive species can alter nutrient cycling, hydrology, fire regimes, and soil microbial communities. Nitrogen-fixing invasive trees (e.g., Myrica faya in Hawaii) fundamentally change soil chemistry. Invasive grasses promote more frequent fire, which kills native trees and promotes more grass — a positive feedback loop that converts forest to grassland.

Management frameworks

Invasive species management follows a hierarchy of intervention:

  1. Prevention: Stop introductions before they happen. Border biosecurity, trade regulations, risk assessment of species proposed for import. Prevention is orders of magnitude cheaper than control after establishment.

  2. Early detection and rapid response: Find and eradicate new populations before they establish. Requires monitoring, reporting systems, and rapid-deployment capacity. Eradication is feasible only when populations are small and localized.

  3. Containment: Prevent established populations from spreading to new areas. Barrier management, buffer zones, transport pathway management.

  4. Long-term management: For established, widespread invasions where eradication is not feasible, reduce populations to levels where their ecological and economic impact is tolerable. Chemical control, biological control, mechanical removal, and integrated approaches.

  5. Adaptation: Accept that some invasions are irreversible and manage the altered ecosystem for the best achievable outcomes.
