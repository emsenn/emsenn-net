Nitrogen Cycle

2026-04-02 The biogeochemical cycle by which nitrogen moves between the atmosphere, soil, water, and living organisms through fixation, nitrification, assimilation, ammonification, and denitrification

The nitrogen cycle is the set of biogeochemical processes by which nitrogen moves between its atmospheric, terrestrial, and aquatic reservoirs. Nitrogen is essential to all living organisms — it is a core component of proteins, nucleic acids, and chlorophyll — yet most of Earth’s nitrogen (78% of the atmosphere) is locked in inert molecular form (N₂) that most organisms cannot use. The nitrogen cycle describes how this unavailable nitrogen becomes biologically available and how it cycles through life and back to the atmosphere.

Atmospheric Reservoir

Molecular nitrogen (N₂) comprises the bulk of Earth’s atmosphere and is effectively inert to most organisms. The largest and most stable nitrogen reservoir is therefore separated from the biosphere by a barrier: very few organisms possess the enzyme nitrogenase, which can break the triple bond in N₂ and reduce it to reactive forms.

Key Transformations

Nitrogen Fixation: Certain bacteria and archaea reduce atmospheric N₂ to ammonia (NH₃), making nitrogen available to plants and other organisms. This occurs through two main pathways:

  • Symbiotic fixation: Rhizobium bacteria live in root nodules of legumes (peas, beans, alfalfa, clover) and fix nitrogen in exchange for carbohydrates from the plant.
  • Free-living fixation: Azotobacter, Cyanobacteria, and other diazotrophs fix nitrogen without a host plant, contributing 5–10 billion kg of fixed nitrogen globally per year. Lightning-induced fixation adds additional atmospheric nitrogen, though in much smaller quantities.

Nitrification: Soil bacteria oxidize ammonia stepwise to nitrite and then nitrate:

NH₃ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻

Autotrophic nitrifiers (chemoautotrophic bacteria of the genera Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter, Nitrosospira, and Nitrosococcus) carry out this process, obtaining energy from oxidation of nitrogen compounds. This transformation is essential: plants prefer to absorb nitrogen as nitrate (NO₃⁻) or ammonium (NH₄⁺), and nitrification makes ammonia available in forms plants can readily use.

Assimilation: Plants absorb nitrate and ammonium from soil solution and incorporate nitrogen into proteins, nucleic acids, and other organic molecules. Heterotrophic organisms obtain nitrogen by consuming plants or other organisms.

Ammonification: When organisms die or excrete waste, decomposer bacteria break down organic nitrogen compounds and release ammonia back to the soil. This step returns nitrogen tied up in living tissue to forms that can be mobilized by plants or further transformed.

Denitrification: Under anaerobic conditions (waterlogged soil, sediments, oxygen-depleted water), denitrifying bacteria (genera Bacillus, Paracoccus, Pseudomonas) reduce nitrate back to molecular nitrogen (N₂), returning it to the atmosphere. This is the main pathway by which fixed nitrogen leaves the biosphere and returns to the atmospheric reservoir.

Agricultural Significance

Nitrogen is the most frequently limiting nutrient in agriculture. Industrial agriculture addresses this through the Haber-Bosch process, which fixes atmospheric nitrogen industrially via high temperature and pressure, consuming approximately 1–2% of global energy production. This input supports global food production but creates dependency on fossil fuel energy.

Alternative nitrogen sources include:

  • Legume crop rotation: Rotating nitrogen-demanding crops with legumes replaces soil nitrogen through biological fixation. A well-managed legume crop can add 100–300 lb/acre of biologically fixed nitrogen.
  • Manure and compost: Organic matter from animal sources provides nitrogen in forms that slowly mineralize, but without additional microbial fixation.
  • Cover crops: Legume cover crops (alfalfa, clover) increase soil nitrogen during off-season fallow periods.

The nitrogen cycle is thus central to both ecosystem function and agricultural practice. Understanding it is essential to designing sustainable food systems that do not depend entirely on industrial fixation.
