Sensitive species and obscured locations

2025-12-30

Observations can expose vulnerable species to harm. iNaturalist provides geoprivacy settings, but the observer must decide when to use them.

Practice guidance

  • Use obscured or private locations for threatened or collectible species.
  • Consider local regulations and community guidance for sensitive taxa.
  • Document the reason for geoprivacy in observation notes when possible.

Scientific tradeoffs

  • Conservation value vs. risk exposure: precise data can help management but also enable harm.
  • Spatial resolution: obscured records reduce location precision; compensate with detailed habitat notes.

Cultural sensitivity and access

Some species and locations have cultural significance beyond conservation status. If local guidance indicates sensitivity, prioritize protection over precision.

What goes wrong if you do this poorly

Precise locations can lead to poaching, habitat disturbance, or over-collection. Harm can occur even when observers have benign intent.

Sources

  • iNaturalist geoprivacy help: https://www.inaturalist.org/pages/help#geoprivacy
  • Tulloch et al. (2013) on sensitive species data: https://doi.org/10.1111/1365-2664.12119
  • CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance: https://www.gida-global.org/care

Geoprivacy decision path

  1. Is the taxon known to be sensitive or collectible? If yes, obscure or private.
  2. Is the location culturally sensitive? If yes, obscure and avoid detailed notes.
  3. Is the observation part of a managed project with safeguards? Follow project guidance.
  4. Otherwise: default to open locations with honest accuracy radius.

Coordination guidance

  • Check local conservation agency guidance for sensitive taxa.
  • If participating in a monitoring program, align with its data-sharing rules.
