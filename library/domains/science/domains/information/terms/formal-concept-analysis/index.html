2026-03-30 A mathematical framework for deriving conceptual hierarchies from data tables. A formal context of objects and attributes generates a concept lattice via a Galois connection.

Formal concept analysis (Ganter & Wille 1999) derives conceptual structure from data. The input is a formal context (G,M,I)(G, M, I): objects GG, attributes MM, and an incidence relation II recording which objects have which attributes.

The derivation operators form a Galois connection between subsets of objects and subsets of attributes. The closed elements — pairs (A,B)(A, B) with A′=BA' = B and B′=AB' = A — are the formal concepts. AA is the extent (the objects); BB is the intent (the attributes). The formal concepts form a complete concept lattice.

FCA applies to any data expressible as objects with attributes: database tables, key-value records, survey responses, text corpora. A repository of files with frontmatter is a formal context where files are objects and frontmatter keys are attributes.

Belohlavek (2004) generalized FCA to many-valued contexts where I:G×M→LI: G \times M \to L for a residuated lattice LL. The concept lattice inherits LL’s residuated structure. Wurm (2012) proved such concept lattices give complete semantics for the Lambek calculus.
