2026-03-31 The set of attributes that define a formal concept — the 'why' of the concept. Paired with extent (the 'what').

The intent of a formal concept is the set of attributes that define it.

In formal concept analysis, a formal concept is a pair (A,B)(A, B) where AA is the extent (objects) and BB is the intent (attributes). The intent contains exactly the attributes shared by all objects in the extent, and the extent contains exactly the objects that have all attributes in the intent.

If the concept is “papers in the repository,” the intent is {type: paper} — the attribute that defines membership. If the concept is “authored, published papers,” the intent is {type: paper, authors, status: published} — a richer defining attribute set.

In a concept lattice, concepts are ordered by reverse intent inclusion: a concept with a larger intent (more defining attributes, fewer objects) is more specific and sits lower in the lattice.

The term comes from Ganter & Wille (1999). In the Galois connection on a formal context, the intent is the output of the objects-to-attributes map A′A'.

In the extends/restricts framework: the intent is on the restricts side of the adjunction — it is what constrains which objects can belong.
