Requisite variety

2026-03-03 Ashby's law: a system can regulate its environment only if its internal variety is at least equal to the variety of disturbances arriving from that environment. Equivalently, only variety can absorb variety. Stability requires that the regulating system match the complexity of what it regulates.

Requisite variety is the principle, formulated by W. Ross Ashby in An Introduction to Cybernetics (1956), that a system can regulate its environment only if its internal variety is at least equal to the variety of disturbances arriving from that environment. Equivalently: only variety can absorb variety. The law has a one-line statement and substantial consequences.

¶Etymology and formal context

The word variety in Ashby’s usage carries a specific information-theoretic meaning, building on Shannon (1948). The variety of a set is the number of distinguishable states it can take. A system with two distinguishable states has variety 2; one with sixteen has variety 16. Requisite means required: the requisite variety is the variety the regulating system must have in order to do its regulating work. The law specifies the lower bound.

The formal statement comes through Ashby’s variety equation. Let VDV_D be the variety of disturbances, VRV_R be the variety of regulator-responses, and VOV_O be the variety of outcomes. Then VO≥VD/VRV_O \geq V_D / V_R. The variety of outcomes (the spread of possible end-states) is at least the variety of disturbances divided by the variety of responses. To minimize VOV_O — to keep the system stable — one must maximize VRV_R. If VR≥VDV_R \geq V_D, the regulator can in principle bring VOV_O to its minimum.

¶Definitional structure

Three features mark a regulating system as having (or lacking) requisite variety.

Match between regulator-variety and disturbance-variety. The regulating system must have at least as many distinguishable response-states as the environment has distinguishable disturbance-states. A thermostat with two settings (heat-on, heat-off) has variety 2; an environment that produces variety 2 in temperature outcomes can be regulated by it; an environment that produces variety 32 cannot.

The regulator embodies a model of the regulated. Conant and Ashby (1970) proved formally what is implicit in the law: a good regulator is necessarily a model of the system it regulates. The regulator’s variety must map onto the disturbance-variety, and that mapping is what models do. This means there is no purely external regulation; the regulating system is internally complex enough to mirror the regulated system.

Variety can be amplified or attenuated, not created from nothing. Stafford Beer’s elaboration (1972, 1979) introduces attenuators (mechanisms that reduce environmental variety to manageable signal) and amplifiers (mechanisms that extend the regulator’s capacity to act). Together, attenuators and amplifiers can keep the variety equation balanced even when raw variety differences are large. But neither can manufacture variety; variety is a conserved quantity in this sense.

¶Examples

A thermostat regulating room temperature. Disturbances arrive in a continuum (any temperature is possible). The thermostat attenuates this to a binary signal (above or below the threshold) and responds with a binary action (heat-on or heat-off). The system is regulated only over a narrow range; outside that range, the thermostat lacks requisite variety and the temperature wanders.

A central bank attempting to regulate inflation. Inflation is produced by many sources of disturbance (wage-price dynamics, oil shocks, currency movements, asset-bubble cycles). The central bank’s principal regulator (interest-rate adjustment) has limited variety. Its operationally-effective response space is narrow relative to the variety of inflation-causing disturbances. When the disturbance-variety expands beyond what interest-rate variety can absorb, the central bank’s regulation fails until it adds amplifiers (forward guidance, asset purchases, lending facilities).

A health system absorbing pandemic disturbance. Routine variations in disease incidence are absorbed by routine variety (provider scheduling, deferral of elective procedures, resource reallocation across departments). A pandemic surge produces disturbance-variety that exceeds the routine regulator-variety. The system can extend by adding amplifiers (field hospitals, telemedicine, expanded staffing) and attenuators (triage protocols, public-behavior change), but if the additions cannot match the disturbance-variety, the system’s regulation fails.

A managerial control structure. Beer’s organizational analysis treats firms as variety-regulating systems. A firm with a low-variety control structure (rigid hierarchy, single-channel communication) can regulate only low-variety environments. A firm operating in a high-variety environment (rapid technical change, competitive disruption, regulatory uncertainty) requires distributed variety (autonomous teams, multiple decision-channels, recursive structure) to remain viable.

¶Adjacent concepts

Requisite variety is not the same as resource-sufficiency. A system can have abundant resources and still lack requisite variety if its response-space is structurally constrained. Variety is about distinguishable response-states, not about how much of each response is available.

Requisite variety is not the same as resilience in Holling’s (1973) ecological sense, though the two are related. Resilience is the system’s ability to remain in its operating regime despite perturbations; requisite variety is the precondition for resilience to obtain. A system with insufficient variety cannot be resilient; a system with sufficient variety may or may not be resilient depending on how the variety is configured.

Requisite variety is not the same as redundancy. Redundancy is the duplication of capacity (multiple instances of the same response-type). Variety is the differentiation of capacity (multiple distinct response-types). Redundancy without variety produces robustness within a narrow regime but not adaptation across regimes.

¶Open questions

How is variety to be measured for systems with continuous state-spaces or with state-spaces too large to enumerate? Information theory provides tools (entropy, Kolmogorov complexity), but the practical measurement of variety in social systems is contested. Beer’s variety attenuators and amplifiers remain useful as design heuristics even when the variety equation cannot be solved quantitatively.

What happens to a system that maintains requisite variety only by continuously expanding its regulator? Such systems are stable in the short run but accumulate complexity that may itself become a source of disturbance — a regulator-of-the-regulator problem. Beer’s recursive viable-system structure (1979) is one analytical response.

How does Ashby’s law interact with second-order cybernetics, in which the observer is part of the regulated system? Heinz von Foerster’s (2003) extension of cybernetics to observer-included systems suggests the variety equation must include the observer’s variety as well. The framework here gestures at the question without resolving it.
