Directory organization for knowledge systems: a best-practices synthesis

2026-03-09
Abstract

Synthesis of research on knowledge organization, intellectual differentiation, epistemological structure, and process ontology into best practices for directory organization in knowledge repositories.

A knowledge repository needs a directory structure that can hold the full range of what knowledge work involves. The literature surveyed in the supporting research texts [@asr-research-knowledge-organization-systems; @asr-research-intellectual-differentiation; @asr-research-epistemological-structure; @asr-research-process-ontology; @asr-research-social-learning] does not converge on a single canonical schema. It does converge on a set of functional distinctions that any adequate schema must represent. This text synthesizes those distinctions into four organizing groups and their constituent directory types.

1. Grounding principle: categories as activities

Process philosophy establishes that categories are better understood as ways of tracking patterns of becoming than as bins for finished things [asr-research-process-ontology]. This permits — and the relational commitment of this project requires — that directory names take gerund form: they name activities, not substances. A methods/ directory holds how-knowledge-is-produced, not an inert category called “methods.” The form is functional.

Peircean semiotics reinforces this. Peirce’s triadic structure of Firstness (quality/possibility), Secondness (reaction/encounter), and Thirdness (mediation/habit) describes how meaning moves rather than where it sits [asr-research-process-ontology]. Trinities of directory types within each group can be read as covering these three moments without being mechanically derived from them.

2. Four organizing groups

2.1 Governing: methods, practices, standards

Philosophy of science establishes that methods, practices, and standards are analytically distinct though intertwined [asr-research-epistemological-structure]:

  • Methods are generalized inquiry forms — how knowledge is supposed to be produced in a given domain. They are prescriptive and transportable across situations.
  • Practices are situated enactments — how knowledge work is actually done in context. They include tacit know-how, material arrangements, and local routines. Lave and Wenger establish that practices are not downstream from knowledge but among the primary sites where knowing is formed and transmitted [asr-research-social-learning].
  • Standards are normative constraints — what counts as adequate evidence, responsible conduct, or correct form. They tell practitioners what to meet, not only how to proceed. The lived account adds that standards often first materialize as interface problems: what must I produce for this to be legible to others? [asr-research-lived-experience]

These three are not reducible to each other. A field can share standards while disagreeing on method. Methods can be formally specified while practices diverge. The directory types mark these real distinctions.

2.2 Engaging: learning, researching, applying

These are the three modes of knowledge engagement — the ways a practitioner or community transacts with a body of knowledge:

  • Researching is knowledge intake from external sources: literature engagement, source analysis, citation, evidence gathering. The emsemioverse research cadence makes this the first stage of any new domain entry.
  • Learning is knowledge output in accessible form: exposition, practical guidance, curricula, and materials that transfer knowledge to others. Wenger’s situated learning establishes that this is not merely distribution of content but a form of participation [asr-research-social-learning]. Shulman’s signature pedagogies show that how a field teaches encodes its methods, practices, and standards simultaneously [asr-research-social-learning].
  • Applying is knowledge use in concrete situations: case analyses, implementations, worked examples, treatments of specific problems. This is where theory meets the world.

The cycle among these is iterative: researching fills gaps that applying reveals; learning structures what researching produces; applying tests what learning transmits.

2.3 Situating: disciplines, schools, lineages

A field subdivides along at least three independent axes [@asr-research-intellectual-differentiation; @asr-research-knowledge-organization-systems]:

  • Disciplines are institutional and jurisdictional subdivisions — defined by what subject domain is studied and claimed (Abbott’s system of professions). A discipline is not primarily a style but a scope: what falls within versus outside this field’s work.
  • Schools are epistemic and methodological subdivisions — defined by how inquiry is conducted, what counts as evidence, what exemplars structure normal work (Kuhn’s disciplinary matrix, Fleck’s thought styles, Lakatos’s research programmes). Two researchers can share a discipline while belonging to different schools.
  • Lineages are transmissive subdivisions — defined by intellectual descent and derivational chains. What was learned from whom, what frameworks are inherited, what derivations carry forward the commitments of a prior tradition. Fleck’s analysis of knowledge carried through apprenticeship and Wenger’s account of communities of practice as transmission structures both point here. In the emsemioverse, lineages are especially salient because original contributions (neuroreflexivity, semiotic-markdown) are explicitly located by derivational origin rather than by conventional disciplinary address.

The distinction among these three matters for directory placement: the same content may belong to different locations depending on whether its primary relationship is subject (discipline), approach (school), or derivation (lineage).

2.4 Defining: topics, concepts, terms

Knowledge-organization theory and terminology science establish three levels of conceptual specificity [@asr-research-epistemological-structure; @asr-research-knowledge-organization-systems]:

  • Terms are specific referents: the linguistic handles that stabilize a single encountered thing. Dahlberg’s concept theory grounds terms as the denominative face of concepts — they make concepts communicable. In practice, terms arise first, from concrete encounter or observation.
  • Concepts are units of knowledge: patterns recognized across terms, emergent from inferential and theoretical relations. The philosophical literature treats concepts as roles in wider inferential systems, not merely compressed referents [asr-research-epistemological-structure]. They gather and sharpen patterns that terms point at individually.
  • Topics are aboutness-classes: broad regions of inquiry or discussion that gather many concepts and terms under one heading (Hjorland on subjects). They name areas of concern rather than discrete units of meaning.

The lived-experience account confirms the experiential sequence: encounter something concrete → assign a term → recognize the pattern across instances → name the concept → locate it within a region of concern (topic). The progression is bottom-up, not deductive.

3. The structure as a whole

governing:   methods/    practices/   standards/
engaging:    learning/   researching/ applying/
situating:   disciplines/ schools/    lineages/
defining:    topics/     concepts/    terms/

Each of these twelve directory types is a cross-cutting subdirectory that may appear within any Thing in a knowledge repository. Their presence or absence in a given directory signals which aspects of that Thing’s knowledge work have been developed.

The four groups are not parallel in the same way: governing and engaging are activity-oriented (they name what is DONE), while situating and defining are structure-oriented (they name what IS CREATED by those activities). But all twelve names are gerunds or process-nouns, consistent with the process-ontological commitment that structural categories name recurrent patterns of activity, not inert bins.

4. What this synthesis does not settle

  • Whether the twelve types exhaust the necessary cross-cutting directories, or whether domain-specific types (e.g., proofs/ in mathematics, formalization/ in formal systems) require additional cases.
  • How these directory types interact with the texts/ directory that already exists widely in the repository. Research argues that researching/ and learning/ absorb most of what texts/ currently holds, but migration is a separate question from naming.
  • Whether lineages/ is already present implicitly in any existing directories, or is a genuinely new type.
  • The relationship between practices/ (a directory type for situated doing) and practices/specification.md (the normative face of a Thing). These share a name for good reason — both are on the interface/practice side of the structuration-interface dual — but their scope differs.
