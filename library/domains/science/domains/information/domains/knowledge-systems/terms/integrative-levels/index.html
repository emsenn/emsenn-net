Integrative Levels

2026-03-31 Successive forms of order in a scale of complexity, where each level organizes the level below and adds emergent qualities.

Integrative levels are successive forms of order arranged in a scale of complexity: atoms, molecules, cells, organisms, minds, societies, cultures. Joseph Needham coined the term in 1937, drawing on earlier work in emergentist philosophy. The core idea is that matter organizes itself into increasingly complex structures, each level integrating the components of the level below while producing qualities that did not exist at that lower level.

James Feibleman (1954) formalized the concept into twelve laws. The central ones: each level organizes entities from the level below into new wholes. Each level has at least one emergent quality not present at lower levels. Higher levels depend on lower levels for their existence. Higher levels cannot be fully explained by (reduced to) lower levels. The laws run in both directions — higher levels also exert constraints on lower levels (downward causation), and disruption at any level propagates both up and down.

Douglas Foskett applied integrative levels to library classification in the 1960s, arguing that the natural order of knowledge follows the sequence of integrative levels rather than the administrative structure of universities. This principle underlies the Bliss Bibliographic Classification, Second Edition (BC2), which arranges its main classes from physics through chemistry, biology, psychology, and the social sciences. The CRG (Classification Research Group) in London adopted integrative levels as the theoretical basis for a new general classification scheme, though the full scheme was never completed.

Integrative levels provide a rationale for faceted classification that is independent of any particular domain. Where faceted classification analyzes subjects into orthogonal dimensions, integrative levels supply the vertical axis — the ordering principle that determines which domains come before which. Together they offer a two-dimensional framework: levels provide the main sequence, facets provide the internal structure within each level.
