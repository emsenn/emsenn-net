Documentation Theory: A Survey of Documents, Signs, and Records

2026-04-03 Survey of documentation theory from Otlet and Briet through archival science and Buckland. Examines what documents are, how they differ from knowledge, the principles of archival preservation, and the threefold nature of information and documentation.

¶The Question at the Threshold

What is a document? The question seems simple until you try to answer it. A book is clearly a document. What about a photograph? A map? A museum specimen? A contract? A birth certificate? A text message? Where does documentation end and non-documentation begin?

This is not a trivial question. How we answer it determines what we preserve, what we organize, what we make retrievable to future inquirers. It determines which traces of human activity count as evidence and which fade into loss. Documentation theory is the philosophical examination of the nature of documents, the principles governing their preservation and organization, and the relationship between documents and the knowledge they carry.

¶From Otlet to Briet: The Birth of Documentation as Philosophy

Paul Otlet (1934) begins with a bold premise: all manifestations of human knowledge can be organized and made accessible. His Traité de Documentation is a comprehensive attempt to develop a science of documentation — a systematic way of thinking about what documents are and how they should be organized.

Otlet argued that documentation should not be confined to books and traditional scholarly texts. Documentation encompasses everything that carries information: printed materials, images, manuscripts, maps, artifacts, and objects. The goal of documentation is to extract useful information from these diverse materials and integrate them into a unified system of knowledge accessible to any inquirer.

This was revolutionary. It expanded the scope of what counts as a document from literary and scholarly texts to all recorded information. It also established documentation as an intellectual discipline with its own principles and methods. Documentation was not merely librarianship (the practical management of libraries) but a theoretical framework for understanding how knowledge is recorded, preserved, and made accessible.

Suzanne Briet (1951), working in the framework Otlet established, offers a precise definition: A document is any physical or symbolic sign, preserved or recorded, for the purpose of representing, reconstructing, or proving a phenomenon.

This definition is deceptively simple. Unpack it and it reveals philosophy:

A document must be preserved or recorded. It is not ephemeral. It has been deliberately kept, organized into a collection, made to persist. The events of a day — conversations, observations, transactions — are not documents until they are recorded.

A document is a physical or symbolic sign. It can be a book, a photograph, a record, an object, an image, a sound, a digital file — anything that stands for something beyond itself. The sign need not be textual; it can be a three-dimensional object.

A document exists for the purpose of representing, reconstructing, or proving. This is the intentional dimension. A document is deliberately shaped or preserved to carry information. A fragment of pottery is not a document until someone has preserved it as evidence of an ancient culture. An antelope in the wild is not a document; an antelope in a zoo is a document because the zoo has deliberately preserved it as a representation of a species.

Briet’s famous example makes this concrete: An antelope in the wild is not a document. An antelope in a zoo is a document. The difference is not the antelope itself but the intentional preservation and organization that transforms it from a living thing into a sign set apart for the purpose of making something known.

This means that documentation is fundamentally about intention and preservation. A log file on a computer is not a document unless someone has deliberately preserved it as evidence. A photograph is a document not simply because it is a recording but because it has been kept, filed, made part of a collection intended to preserve information for future reference.

¶The Threefold Nature of Information and Documents

Michael Buckland (1991) articulates a distinction that clarifies the relationship between knowledge, information, and documents. There are three senses of “information” that we often confuse:

Information-as-process: becoming informed; the experience of learning. When someone reads a document and understands something new, information-as-process is occurring. It is the subjective experience of coming to know.

Information-as-knowledge: what is known; the content of understanding. The body of knowledge about a subject, the accumulated insights and facts. This is what we mean when we say “medical information” or “historical information.”

Information-as-thing: objects regarded as informative — documents, records, artifacts, signs. Physical or symbolic objects that carry information, that can be organized, stored, retrieved, transmitted. This is information as a material thing.

These are not separate phenomena but three angles on a single phenomenon. A patient’s medical file is information-as-thing (a physical record organized and stored). Reading the file is information-as-process (the doctor becoming informed). What the doctor learns is information-as-knowledge (the body of facts about the patient’s condition).

The three aspects cannot be separated without loss. Information-as-thing without anyone to read it is just objects. Information-as-process without objects to carry it cannot occur. Information-as-knowledge without things to preserve it is lost when the knower dies. A robust system of documentation requires all three: material preservation, the capacity for processing, and the achievement of knowledge.

Most documentation theory has neglected information-as-thing — the physical properties of documents themselves. Buckland argues that this is a fundamental error. The form of a document matters. Its organization, its location, its material condition, its archival context — these are not merely practical concerns. They shape what the document can communicate and to whom.

¶The Principles of Archival Science

Archival science developed over centuries through the work of custodians, historians, and archivists who had to preserve records and make them useful to future inquirers. It established three foundational principles:

Provenance: A document’s meaning and value lie in understanding where it came from, how it was accumulated, and what institution or person created it. The provenance — the history of custody and use — is part of the document’s meaning. A letter gains significance when you know it was created by a particular person under particular conditions, was preserved by a particular successor, was eventually donated to an archive. Strip away provenance and you strip away essential information.

Original Order: Reorganizing documents destroys evidence of the logic of their creation. The way documents were originally arranged — filed, ordered, organized — tells you something about how the institution or person who created them thought. It reveals the structure of their work and concerns. An archive that rearranges documents according to a librarian’s schema destroys that evidence.

Respect des Fonds: Documents should be kept in the context of the fonds — the body of records that produced them. A single letter has different meaning if you read it in isolation than if you read it in the context of all the letters in a collection, all the documents from that institution, all the records of that period. The fonds is the web of relationships that gives individual documents meaning.

From these principles, archival science developed practices: cataloging documents with their provenance clearly stated, preserving the original arrangement when possible, organizing collections to make relationships visible. The goal is to make documents accessible while preserving the context that gives them meaning.

An archivist is not a neutral curator. Every choice about what to preserve, what to discard, how to organize — these are interpretive choices. Which documents are deemed significant enough to preserve? Which uses of the collection are prioritized? How is the collection organized for discovery? The archivist’s work shapes what future inquirers will be able to know.

¶Document-as-Thing vs. Document-as-Sign

Two conceptually distinct frameworks have emerged in documentation theory, representing two ways of understanding what a document is:

Document-as-thing (Briet, Buckland’s information-as-thing, archival science): A document is a material or symbolic object deliberately preserved to carry information. It is studied as a thing — what it is, what it contains, where it comes from, how it is organized. Importance lies in physical properties, provenance, original order. The document is valuable because it carries evidence of the past, because it has been preserved intentionally and can be recovered by future inquirers.

This framework emphasizes preservation, authenticity, and the material basis of knowledge. It asks: How do we preserve documents? How do we authenticate them? What is the chain of custody? How does physical organization affect meaning?

Document-as-sign (Peirce’s semiotics, discourse theory): A document is a sign that refers to something beyond itself — a concept, an event, a state of affairs. It is studied as a relation — what it refers to, how it is interpreted, what it enables. Importance lies in its function in systems of meaning-making. The document is valuable because it creates meaning in the act of being read, because it participates in discourse.

This framework emphasizes interpretation, context, and the role of the reader. It asks: What does the document refer to? How is it interpreted in different contexts? What does the document enable someone to do or think?

These frameworks are not opposed; they are complementary. A contract is a thing (a physical document preserved with its provenance) and a sign (it refers to agreements and rights, it functions in a legal system). An archive is a collection of things (preserved objects in original order) and a web of signs (each document refers beyond itself, meanings emerge from relationships). Understanding documents requires holding both frameworks.

¶Nils Lund: The Semantic and Pragmatic Dimensions

Nils Windfeld Lund (2009) offers a comprehensive theory of documents that integrates previous work. A document, for Lund, has four defining characteristics:

It is recorded — preserved in some medium that allows persistence beyond the moment of creation.

It is intended to communicate — created with the purpose of conveying information to someone.

It is contextualized — located in a specific time, place, and social context.

It mediates social action — it functions to enable coordination, documentation of decisions, evidence of responsibility.

Lund argues that documentation is fundamentally social. Documents exist to mediate between people across time and space. A contract enables two parties to coordinate their actions. A medical record enables a doctor to understand a patient’s history. A memo documents a decision and creates accountability. A scientific publication enables researchers in different places to learn from each other.

This means that documents are always situated in practices. A spreadsheet is a document because someone created it to communicate information to someone else for a purpose. A personal diary is a document if (as Lund argues) the writer intends to communicate with a future self or a future reader. A private note that is never shown to anyone is information but not quite a document because it lacks the communication intention.

The implication is that documentation is never neutral. Every document embodies choices about what to record, what to leave out, how to organize information, what audience to address. These choices are interpretive. Two people documenting the same event might create very different documents depending on their purposes and perspectives.

¶Bernd Frohmann: Facts and the Modernist Vision

Bernd Frohmann (2008) examines Paul Otlet’s Traité de Documentation through a historical lens, asking what vision Otlet had for documentation and what it tells us about documentation theory itself.

Otlet was a modernist who believed that all knowledge could be extracted from documents, organized systematically, and made universally accessible. Knowledge was to be broken into atomic facts, organized in a standardized way, retrieved through a universal index. Documentation was the technology that would make this possible.

Frohmann argues that this vision, while influential, rests on assumptions that are worth examining. Otlet assumed that knowledge could be reduced to facts, that facts could be extracted from documents and organized independently, that a universal system of organization could be created. Frohmann suggests that these assumptions are historically specific — products of Otlet’s time and culture, not universal truths about documentation.

This is important because it suggests that how we think about documentation — what we preserve, how we organize it, what we expect it to do — is not determined by the nature of documents themselves. It is shaped by historical, cultural, and philosophical assumptions. Different eras and cultures have understood documents differently.

The question for any documentation system is: What are we assuming about how knowledge relates to documents? Are we treating documents as containers of facts that can be extracted and reorganized? Are we treating documents as signs whose meaning emerges in relationships? Are we attending to the material and institutional context that gives documents meaning?

¶The Relationship Between Documents and Knowledge

Documentation theory reveals a paradox: documents are essential to the preservation and transmission of knowledge, yet they cannot fully capture knowledge itself.

From epistemology (the subject of the companion survey), we know that knowledge is structured, warranted through process, practical or relational, and situated. Documents can record information — facts, propositions, procedures. But they cannot fully capture the process that produced knowledge, the practical skill of applying it, or the relational context in which it was created and is used.

A manual cannot teach you to diagnose an illness; you need apprenticeship. A specification cannot teach you to build a bridge well; you need experience and mentorship. A historical document cannot teach you to understand the past; you need to engage with the document in context, knowing something of the assumptions and practices of its time.

Yet documents are indispensable for making knowledge public, preserving it across generations, submitting it to criticism and refinement. A knowledge claim that exists only in someone’s mind is vulnerable — it dies with the knower. A knowledge claim that is documented can be examined, tested, improved by a community of inquirers.

The solution is not to document everything or to treat documents as complete knowledge. The solution is to understand documents for what they are: important technologies for recording information, preserving traces of past thinking and action, and enabling communication across time and space. They are essential but limited. They enable knowledge to be shared and tested but do not themselves constitute knowledge.

¶What Counts as a Document

From documentation theory, several principles emerge for determining what counts as a document:

Intentionality: Was it deliberately created or preserved to carry information?

Preservation: Has it been kept, organized, made to persist?

Signification: Does it refer beyond itself to something — a concept, an event, a state of affairs?

Addressivity: Was it created with some audience in mind, even if that audience is the creator’s own future self?

Social embededness: Does it function in some social, institutional, or communicative context?

These are not strict criteria but dimensions that help us understand what documents do. A private journal might be a document if the writer intends to preserve their thoughts for a future self. A text message might be a document if it is archived and made accessible to inquirers studying a particular communication. A museum specimen is a document because it has been preserved and organized to represent something to visitors.

What matters is understanding that documentation is not a natural or inevitable process. It is a social technology with specific affordances and limitations. What we document shapes what can be known in the future. What we fail to document is lost.

¶The Archive as Epistemic Space

An archive is not merely a collection of documents. It is an epistemic space — a place where knowledge can be produced through careful reading, comparison, and connection of documents. The archive embodies the principles of documentation theory in practice:

  • Documents are organized according to principles (provenance, original order, respect des fonds) that make their context visible
  • The material condition and authenticity of documents are preserved
  • Access is structured to enable different kinds of inquiry (scholarly research, genealogy, institutional history, etc.)
  • The limitations of what is documented are acknowledged

An archive is also an institutional space. It is staffed by archivists who make decisions about what to preserve, how to organize it, what metadata to record. These decisions shape what future inquirers will be able to discover and know. An archive that preserves only the records of powerful institutions will tell a different story than an archive that actively collects the materials of marginalized communities.

Documentation theory thus has a political dimension. It asks: Who decides what is worth preserving? Whose knowledge is documented and whose is lost? What voices are recorded and what voices are silenced? These are not merely practical questions about archive management. They are fundamental questions about what will be known and what will be forgotten.

¶Documentation and Memory

Ultimately, documentation is a technology of memory. It preserves the traces of past action and thought so they can be recovered in the future. But memory is selective. We remember some things and forget others. We organize memories in ways that may distort or clarify the past.

Documentation systems have the same character. They select what is worth preserving. They organize it in ways that enable some retrievals and make others difficult. They inevitably shape what can be remembered and how.

This is neither a flaw nor a cause for despair. It is the nature of documentation. The goal is to practice documentation with awareness of what is preserved and what is lost, to organize documents in ways that preserve the context that gives them meaning, and to remain open to new interpretations and uses of documented materials.

Documents are the artifacts through which communities transmit knowledge across time. They are imperfect, partial, always incomplete. But they make possible the continuity of knowledge, the testing of claims against past work, the accumulation of understanding across generations. Without documentation, each generation would begin again from nothing. With documentation — practiced with care and awareness — we can build on what has been learned.
