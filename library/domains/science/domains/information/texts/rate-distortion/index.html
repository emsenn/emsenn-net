Rate-Distortion and Lossy Compression

2025-12-30 The tradeoff between compression rate and distortion.

Core idea

Lossless compression has a hard floor: you cannot encode a source using fewer than HH bits per symbol on average without losing information. Lossy compression breaks through that floor by tolerating some distortion – a controlled difference between the original data and the reconstruction. Rate-distortion theory quantifies the best possible tradeoff between compression rate and distortion, answering the question: for a given tolerance of error, what is the minimum number of bits per symbol required?

The theory applies whenever perfect reproduction is unnecessary or impractical. Speech, images, and video all contain more detail than a listener or viewer can distinguish, so discarding some of that detail in a principled way allows dramatic reductions in data rate without perceptible loss.

Rate-distortion function

The rate-distortion function R(D)R(D) gives the minimum bit rate needed to represent a source such that the expected distortion does not exceed DD:

R(D)=min⁡p(x^∣x): E[d(X,X^)]≤DI(X;X^)R(D) = \min_{p(\hat{x}|x) : \, E[d(X, \hat{X})] \le D} I(X; \hat{X})

Here d(X,X^)d(X, \hat{X}) is a distortion measure (for example, squared error for continuous signals or Hamming distance for discrete symbols), and the minimization searches over all conditional distributions p(x^∣x)p(\hat{x}|x) that satisfy the distortion constraint. The function R(D)R(D) is convex and decreasing: accepting more distortion always permits a lower rate, but the marginal savings diminish as DD grows.

For a memoryless Gaussian source with squared-error distortion, the rate-distortion function has a clean closed form: R(D)=12log⁡2σ2DR(D) = \frac{1}{2} \log_2 \frac{\sigma^2}{D} for D≤σ2D \le \sigma^2, and R(D)=0R(D) = 0 for D>σ2D > \sigma^2. This means that halving the distortion tolerance costs an additional half bit per sample.

Why this matters

Rate-distortion theory provides the theoretical limits behind audio codecs (MP3, AAC), image formats (JPEG, WebP), and video compression (H.264, AV1). Every practical lossy codec operates somewhere on or above the R(D)R(D) curve for its source model. Understanding this tradeoff clarifies why no codec can simultaneously achieve low bitrate and low distortion beyond what the theory permits, and it gives engineers a benchmark for evaluating how close their designs come to the fundamental limit.
