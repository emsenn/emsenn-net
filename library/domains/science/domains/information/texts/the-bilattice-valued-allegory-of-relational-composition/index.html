The Bilattice-Valued Allegory of Relational Composition

2026-04-14 The allegory of bilattice-valued relations on $\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}$ decomposes as a product of two classical Freyd-Scedrov allegories. Classical relational composition embeds as a proper sub-allegory; the bilattice allegory is a strictly stronger, lossless refinement tracking an orthogonal refutation channel.

Hypothesis

Classical Freyd-Scedrov allegories of two-valued relations (Freyd & Scedrov 1990) record a single channel of evidence: whether a pair (x,y)(x, y) is related. The bilattice 2⊗2\mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2} of the adjoint paper separates positive evidence (told-true) from negative evidence (told-false), with independent values ⊥\bot (neither) and ⊤\top (both). We prove that the allegory of bilattice-valued relations on 2⊗2\mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2} is a strictly stronger, lossless refinement of the classical Freyd-Scedrov allegory, in a precise sense:

  • Lossless. The bilattice allegory Rel2⊗2\mathbf{Rel}_{\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}} is allegory-isomorphic to the product Rel2×Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} \times \mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} of two copies of the classical Freyd-Scedrov allegory. No information carried by a bilattice-valued relation is crushed by the allegorical structure; it is exactly the pair of its positive and negative classical projections (Theorem 2).
  • Strictly stronger. The classical allegory Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} embeds as a proper sub-allegory of Rel2⊗2\mathbf{Rel}_{\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}} by R↦(R,∅)R \mapsto (R, \varnothing), and this embedding preserves composition, converse, and meet on the nose (Theorem 3). Any statement provable in the classical allegory is provable in the bilattice allegory about its image; and the bilattice allegory carries, in addition, an orthogonal refutation channel and the non-allegorical residuation ⊙⊣⇒\odot \dashv \Rightarrow of the adjoint paper.

The current text proves both claims, verifies the Freyd-Scedrov axioms by a single product argument, and characterizes the image of the classical embedding.

All notation and prior results of the adjoint paper — values {⊥,t,f,⊤}\{\bot, t, f, \top\}, twist representation (a+,a−)(a^+, a^-), orderings ≤t\leq_t and ≤k\leq_k, operations ∧k\wedge_k, ∨k\vee_k, ⊙\odot, ⇒\Rightarrow, the fiber functor F:Cop→BiLat\mathcal{F}: \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{BiLat} — are inherited and used without redefinition.

1. The bilattice allegory

Definition 1. For a finite entity set EE, a bilattice-valued relation on EE is a function R:E×E→{⊥,t,f,⊤}R: E \times E \to \{\bot, t, f, \top\}. The bilattice allegory Rel2⊗2\mathbf{Rel}_{\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}} has finite sets as objects and bilattice-valued relations as morphisms E→E′E \to E', with the following structure:

  • Composition. (R∘S)(x,y):=⋁m∈E′kR(x,m)∧kS(m,y)(R \circ S)(x, y) := \bigvee^{k}_{m \in E'} R(x, m) \wedge_k S(m, y), where ⋁k\bigvee^k is the finite knowledge supremum.
  • Meet. (R∩S)(x,y):=R(x,y)∧kS(x,y)(R \cap S)(x, y) := R(x, y) \wedge_k S(x, y) (pointwise knowledge meet).
  • Converse. R∘(x,y):=R(y,x)R^{\circ}(x, y) := R(y, x) (pure swap; not negation-augmented inversion).
  • Identity. ΔE(x,y):=t\Delta_E(x, y) := t if x=yx = y, ⊥\bot otherwise.

We will shortly see that this structure is an allegory, as a consequence of a decomposition theorem.

Remark on converse. The adjoint paper’s §3.2 uses a negation-augmented converse Rinv(x,y)=∼R(y,x)R^{\mathrm{inv}}(x, y) = \mathord{\sim} R(y, x) as a fiber-functor morphism. The allegorical converse here is pure swap without negation. These are two separate ingredients with two separate purposes: the fiber-functor inversion is a bilattice homomorphism governing epistemic flip; the allegorical converse is the anti-involutive pairing required by Freyd & Scedrov (1990). That they do not coincide is what one should expect — negation is logical, converse is geometric — and §2 will show that the product-decomposition isolates pure swap as the allegorical converse automatically.

2. Decomposition

Every bilattice value decomposes as a pair: a=(a+,a−)∈{0,1}2a = (a^+, a^-) \in \{0, 1\}^2, with ⊥=(0,0)\bot = (0,0), t=(1,0)t = (1,0), f=(0,1)f = (0,1), ⊤=(1,1)\top = (1,1). The knowledge meet ∧k\wedge_k and join ∨k\vee_k act componentwise (Fitting 1991):

(a+,a−)∧k(b+,b−)=(a+∧b+, a−∧b−)(a+,a−)∨k(b+,b−)=(a+∨b+, a−∨b−)(a^+, a^-) \wedge_k (b^+, b^-) = (a^+ \wedge b^+,\ a^- \wedge b^-) \qquad (a^+, a^-) \vee_k (b^+, b^-) = (a^+ \vee b^+,\ a^- \vee b^-)

Apply this componentwise to a bilattice-valued relation:

Definition 2. For R:E×E′→{⊥,t,f,⊤}R: E \times E' \to \{\bot, t, f, \top\}, the positive projection R+:E×E′→{0,1}R^+: E \times E' \to \{0, 1\} and negative projection R−:E×E′→{0,1}R^-: E \times E' \to \{0, 1\} are the two-valued relations

R+(x,y):=first component of R(x,y)R−(x,y):=second component of R(x,y).R^+(x, y) := \text{first component of } R(x, y) \qquad R^-(x, y) := \text{second component of } R(x, y).

Each two-valued relation R±:E×E′→{0,1}R^\pm: E \times E' \to \{0, 1\} is a morphism in the classical Freyd-Scedrov allegory Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} (Freyd & Scedrov 1990). The assignment R↦(R+,R−)R \mapsto (R^+, R^-) takes bilattice-valued relations to pairs of classical relations. We prove this is an allegory isomorphism.

Theorem 1 (Componentwise composition). For bilattice-valued relations R,SR, S on a common entity domain,

(R∘S)+=R+∘2S+(R∘S)−=R−∘2S−(R \circ S)^+ = R^+ \circ_\mathbf{2} S^+ \qquad (R \circ S)^- = R^- \circ_\mathbf{2} S^-

where ∘2\circ_\mathbf{2} is classical relational composition (P∘2Q)(x,y)=∃m. P(x,m)∧Q(m,y)(P \circ_\mathbf{2} Q)(x, y) = \exists m.\, P(x, m) \wedge Q(m, y).

Proof. At point (x,y)(x, y):

(R∘S)(x,y)=⋁mkR(x,m)∧kS(m,y)=⋁mk(R+(x,m)∧S+(m,y), R−(x,m)∧S−(m,y))(R \circ S)(x, y) = \bigvee^k_m R(x, m) \wedge_k S(m, y) = \bigvee^k_m \big(R^+(x, m) \wedge S^+(m, y),\ R^-(x, m) \wedge S^-(m, y)\big)

Since ∨k\vee_k acts componentwise, the join distributes into the two components:

=(⋁mR+(x,m)∧S+(m,y), ⋁mR−(x,m)∧S−(m,y))=((R+∘2S+)(x,y), (R−∘2S−)(x,y))□= \Big(\bigvee_m R^+(x, m) \wedge S^+(m, y),\ \bigvee_m R^-(x, m) \wedge S^-(m, y)\Big) = \big((R^+ \circ_\mathbf{2} S^+)(x, y),\ (R^- \circ_\mathbf{2} S^-)(x, y)\big) \quad \square

Theorem 2 (Decomposition). The assignment

Π:Rel2⊗2⟶Rel2×Rel2R⟼(R+,R−)\Pi: \mathbf{Rel}_{\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}} \longrightarrow \mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} \times \mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} \qquad R \longmapsto (R^+, R^-)

is an isomorphism of categories with chosen structure — meet, converse, identity, and composition. In particular:

  1. Π\Pi is a bijection on hom-sets: R↦(R+,R−)R \mapsto (R^+, R^-) has inverse (P,Q)↦λ(x,y).(P(x,y),Q(x,y))(P, Q) \mapsto \lambda(x, y).(P(x, y), Q(x, y)).
  2. Π\Pi preserves identity: Π(ΔE)=(ΔE,∅E)\Pi(\Delta_E) = (\Delta_E, \varnothing_E) where ΔE\Delta_E on the right is the classical diagonal and ∅E\varnothing_E is the empty relation.
  3. Π\Pi preserves composition: by Theorem 1.
  4. Π\Pi preserves meet: (R∩S)±=R±∩2S±(R \cap S)^\pm = R^\pm \cap_\mathbf{2} S^\pm by componentwise action of ∧k\wedge_k.
  5. Π\Pi preserves converse: (R∘)±(x,y)=R±(y,x)=(R±)∘(x,y)(R^\circ)^\pm(x, y) = R^\pm(y, x) = (R^\pm)^\circ(x, y) by definition of pure swap.

Proof. Each clause is immediate from the componentwise action of ∧k\wedge_k, ∨k\vee_k, and pure swap. Clause (3) is Theorem 1. Bijectivity in (1) is inverse pairing of components. □\square

Corollary 1 (Allegory axioms). Rel2⊗2\mathbf{Rel}_{\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}} satisfies the Freyd-Scedrov allegory axioms: associativity of ∘\circ; identity Δ\Delta; anti-involution (R∘)∘=R(R^\circ)^\circ = R; distribution of converse over composition and over meet; idempotency, commutativity, and associativity of ∩\cap; and the modular law R∩(S∘T)≤k(S∩(R∘T∘))∘TR \cap (S \circ T) \leq_k (S \cap (R \circ T^\circ)) \circ T.

Proof. Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} is a Freyd-Scedrov allegory (Freyd & Scedrov 1990). Products of allegories are allegories — each axiom is an equation or an inequation between composites of the allegory operations, and by Theorem 2 each such (in)equation in Rel2⊗2\mathbf{Rel}_{\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}} holds iff it holds on both components in Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2}. The partial order ≤k\leq_k on bilattice relations is the pointwise pairing of the two inclusion orders on Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2}; the modular law in Rel2⊗2\mathbf{Rel}_{\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}} is therefore the conjunction of the classical modular law on R±,S±,T±R^\pm, S^\pm, T^\pm. □\square

Theorem 2 is the lossless half of the hypothesis: the bilattice allegory carries precisely the information of a pair of classical allegory morphisms, with no identification or quotient.

3. Strict strengthening

Classical relational composition corresponds to the case where only positive evidence is tracked. Formalize this as an embedding.

Definition 3. The positive embedding ι:Rel2↪Rel2⊗2\iota: \mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} \hookrightarrow \mathbf{Rel}_{\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}} sends a classical relation PP to the bilattice-valued relation ι(P)(x,y):=(P(x,y),0)\iota(P)(x, y) := (P(x, y), 0) — i.e. tt where PP holds, ⊥\bot elsewhere.

Theorem 3 (Embedding). The positive embedding ι\iota is a faithful allegory homomorphism: it preserves composition, converse, meet, and identity strictly (on the nose, not merely up to isomorphism). Its image is the full sub-allegory of Rel2⊗2\mathbf{Rel}_{\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}} consisting of relations RR with R−=∅R^- = \varnothing. This sub-allegory is proper — there exist bilattice-valued relations not in its image.

Proof. Π(ι(P))=(P,∅)\Pi(\iota(P)) = (P, \varnothing) by construction. Composition: by Theorem 1, (ι(P)∘ι(Q))+=P∘2Q(\iota(P) \circ \iota(Q))^+ = P \circ_\mathbf{2} Q and (ι(P)∘ι(Q))−=∅∘2∅=∅(\iota(P) \circ \iota(Q))^- = \varnothing \circ_\mathbf{2} \varnothing = \varnothing, so ι(P)∘ι(Q)=ι(P∘2Q)\iota(P) \circ \iota(Q) = \iota(P \circ_\mathbf{2} Q). The cases of converse, meet, and identity are analogous by Theorem 2. Faithfulness: if ι(P1)=ι(P2)\iota(P_1) = \iota(P_2), then P1=(ι(P1))+=(ι(P2))+=P2P_1 = (\iota(P_1))^+ = (\iota(P_2))^+ = P_2. Properness of the image: the relation RR with R(x0,y0)=fR(x_0, y_0) = f for a single chosen pair and ⊥\bot elsewhere has R−≠∅R^- \neq \varnothing and so lies outside ι(Rel2)\iota(\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2}). □\square

Corollary 2 (Conservative extension). Every equation or inequation in allegory operations provable in Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} is provable about its ι\iota-image in Rel2⊗2\mathbf{Rel}_{\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}}.

Proof. By Theorem 3, ι\iota preserves all allegory operations strictly. □\square

Theorem 3 is the strictly stronger half: classical relational composition is literally a special case of bilattice relational composition — the slice where no refutation evidence is ever recorded. The bilattice allegory sees distinctions invisible to the classical allegory, and the four identifiable kinds of distinction are the cells of 2⊗2\mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2} itself:

  • R(x,y)=tR(x, y) = t: a pair for which ι−1\iota^{-1} exists — classical “PP relates xx to yy.”
  • R(x,y)=⊥R(x, y) = \bot: a pair for which ι−1\iota^{-1} exists — classical “PP does not relate.”
  • R(x,y)=fR(x, y) = f: refutation without corresponding support; no classical counterpart in ι\iota’s image.
  • R(x,y)=⊤R(x, y) = \top: support and refutation jointly recorded; no classical counterpart in ι\iota’s image.

The refutation channel R−R^- is orthogonal to the support channel R+R^+, and classical composition collapses both onto a single {0,1}\{0, 1\} channel.

Proposition 1 (Refutation-path composition). For R,SR, S bilattice-valued relations on EE and (x,y)∈E×E(x, y) \in E \times E:

(R∘S)(x,y)=⊤iff∃m,m′∈E: R+(x,m)∧S+(m,y) and R−(x,m′)∧S−(m′,y).(R \circ S)(x, y) = \top \quad\text{iff}\quad \exists m, m' \in E:\ R^+(x, m) \wedge S^+(m, y) \ \text{and}\ R^-(x, m') \wedge S^-(m', y).

Proof. Apply Theorem 1 componentwise. The positive component of (R∘S)(x,y)(R \circ S)(x, y) is 11 iff there exists mm with R+(x,m)=S+(m,y)=1R^+(x, m) = S^+(m, y) = 1; the negative component is 11 iff there exists m′m' with R−(x,m′)=S−(m′,y)=1R^-(x, m') = S^-(m', y) = 1; the value is ⊤\top iff both hold. □\square

The two existential witnesses need not coincide: R∘SR \circ S records that some positive path and some refutation path exist simultaneously, through possibly distinct intermediates. Classical composition has no mechanism to record this — the refutation path is entirely invisible to it.

4. Relation to the adjoint paper

4.1 The fiber functor

The adjoint paper’s fiber functor F:Cop→BiLat\mathcal{F}: \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{BiLat} (Theorem 2, §3.2) tracks three classes of predicate relationships that induce bilattice homomorphisms between fiber algebras: specialization, inversion (negation-augmented swap), and parallel composition (pointwise ∧k\wedge_k). These operate on the entire fiber algebra ApA_p as a bilattice, pointwise.

Bilattice relational composition ∘\circ is a distinct operation: it takes morphisms (parameterized by a second fiber) and produces a morphism via intermediate-entity join. By Theorem 1, ∘\circ factors through Π\Pi into the two classical relational compositions. Neither factor is a bilattice homomorphism at the level of 2⊗2\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2} — classical relational composition is not a homomorphism between the source lattices either; it is a morphism in the Freyd-Scedrov allegory. The failure-of-distributivity observations of Birkhoff (1948) and the non-homomorphism character of classical composition are inherited componentwise.

So there is no tension with the adjoint paper’s fiber functor: the fiber functor formalizes the homomorphic structure (what the adjoint paper is about); the allegory formalizes the orthogonal composition-via-quantification structure (what the current paper is about). They live in different ambient categories — BiLat\mathbf{BiLat} vs. Rel2⊗2\mathbf{Rel}_{\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}} — over the same underlying entity-indexed data.

4.2 The residuation

The adjoint paper’s operations ⊙\odot (extending) and ⇒\Rightarrow (restricting), applied pointwise, give a non-allegorical enrichment of Rel2⊗2\mathbf{Rel}_{\mathbf{2}\otimes\mathbf{2}} that ι(Rel2)\iota(\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2}) does not carry. The twist-product formulas

(x,y)⊙(z,v)=(x∧z, (x→v)∧(z→y))(x,y)⇒(z,v)=((x→z)∧(v→y), x∧v)(x,y) \odot (z,v) = (x \wedge z,\ (x \to v) \wedge (z \to y)) \qquad (x,y) \Rightarrow (z,v) = ((x \to z) \wedge (v \to y),\ x \wedge v)

couple the positive and negative components — the negative component of ⊙\odot depends on the positive components of both arguments and vice versa. Consequently ⊙\odot and ⇒\Rightarrow do not decompose as products of classical operations along Π\Pi. They are bilattice-native structure that has no counterpart in Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} or in Rel2×Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} \times \mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} regarded as an allegory alone.

Proposition 2. The operations ⊙\odot and ⇒\Rightarrow, applied pointwise to bilattice-valued relations, do not arise from any pair of operations on classical relations via the isomorphism Π\Pi of Theorem 2.

Proof. A componentwise operation Π−1(α,β)\Pi^{-1}(\alpha, \beta) would compute the positive component of its output from the positive components of its inputs and the negative from the negative. The ⊙\odot formula computes (x⊙z)−=(x+→z−)∧(z+→x−)(x \odot z)^- = (x^+ \to z^-) \wedge (z^+ \to x^-), which depends on both positive components x+,z+x^+, z^+ — crossing the product factorization. □\square

Thus the bilattice allegory is strictly stronger than classical Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} in two independent ways: the product factorization Rel2×Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} \times \mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} (orthogonal refutation channel) and the residuation ⊙⊣⇒\odot \dashv \Rightarrow (cross-channel coupling).

4.3 Decidability

Classical Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} with composition, intersection, complement, and converse is undecidable (Tarski 1941). By Theorem 2 the bilattice allegory is a product of two classical allegories, and so its allegorical-operation fragment is undecidable for the same reason: decidability on the product reduces to decidability on each factor. The adjoint paper’s decidability result (Corollary 2 of §5.1) applies to the modal calculus arising from the fiber functor F\mathcal{F}, not to the allegorical fragment; the two decidability questions concern different fragments of the overall structure.

5. Summary

The allegory of bilattice-valued relations on 2⊗2\mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2} is a product of two classical Freyd-Scedrov allegories, with the positive and negative components of each bilattice value giving the two projections (Theorem 2). Classical Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} embeds as a proper sub-allegory by the positive-only embedding R↦(R,∅)R \mapsto (R, \varnothing), preserving all allegory operations strictly (Theorem 3). The bilattice allegory carries, in addition to what classical Rel2\mathbf{Rel}_\mathbf{2} can express, (a) an independent refutation channel with its own allegorical dynamics and (b) the adjoint paper’s residuation ⊙⊣⇒\odot \dashv \Rightarrow, which crosses the product factorization and has no classical counterpart (Proposition 2). The bilattice-valued allegory is therefore a lossless strictly-stronger refinement of classical relational composition in the sense spelled out in the hypothesis.
