Heteroglossia

2026-04-02 The coexistence of multiple social languages within any living community — not just dialects but entire registers, worldviews, and evaluative stances. Bakhtin's term for what genring destroys.

Heteroglossia (raznorečie) is Mikhail Bakhtin’s term for the coexistence of multiple social languages within any living community. Not merely dialects or accents, but entire registers, worldviews, evaluative stances, and ways of being. A community contains farmers and poets, traditionalists and radicals, the pious and the profane, the ambitious and the content. These voices are not reconciled into a single perspective. They coexist in tension, answering and contesting one another. This multi-voicedness is the normal condition of any living culture.

Heteroglossia is the social expression of dialogism — the principle that every utterance is oriented toward other utterances and that meaning is constituted through encounter between voices. Where dialogism names the constitutive condition of language, heteroglossia names the social reality that produces it: a world in which multiple groups speak from multiple positions, and no single voice can claim to speak for all.

For Bakhtin, the novel is the literary form that orchestrates heteroglossia — giving voice to a doctor’s discourse, a merchant’s discourse, a priest’s discourse, a peasant’s discourse without reducing them to a single authorial position. Monologue, by contrast, is the socially enforced reduction of heteroglossia: one voice claiming authority, suppressing or absorbing the others. Bakhtin considered monologue not the absence of dialogue but its political suppression.

The concept has become central to the analysis of genring — the reduction of living peoples to fixed literary types. Genring collapses heteroglossia into monologue. The multiple voices of a living people are replaced by a single authorized character. The Lakota community of the 1870s contained warriors and peace advocates, hunters and farmers, ceremonial specialists and political strategists. Karl May’s Winnetou retains one voice: the Noble Savage. Kurdish civilization contains Kurmanji and Sorani speakers, Sunni and Yezidi believers, tribal conservatives and anarchist municipalists, dengbej storytellers and Marxist intellectuals. The genre “Mountain Kurd” retains one voice: the fierce tribesman. Everything heteroglossia contains — internal debate, generational conflict, competing visions, the ordinary mess of people who share a territory and disagree about how to inhabit it — is silenced. Not by censorship. By illegibility. The genre provides no character for the other voices.
