Sign

¶What This Is

A sign is something that stands for something else to an interpreter. The formulation is older than its modern theorizations — Augustine, the Stoics, the medieval scholastics each developed versions of it — but two twentieth-century traditions give the contemporary discipline its working apparatus: Peirce’s triadic semiotic and Saussure’s dyadic semiology. Each is a complete account; they are not interchangeable, and the choice between them carries consequences for what the analysis can see.

The minimal stake: a sign is not the same as a stimulus. A stimulus produces a response causally; a sign produces a response through standing for something else. Smoke can be a stimulus that makes you cough; smoke is a sign when it stands for fire to someone who reads it that way. The mediating relation — what stands for what to whom — is what semiotics studies.

¶Formal Definition: The Peircean Triad

In Peirce’s account, a sign is a triadic relation:

Sign=(R,  O,  I)\text{Sign} = (R,\; O,\; I)

where:

  • RR is the representamen — the sign-vehicle, the perceptible thing (a mark, a sound, a gesture) that does the standing-for.
  • OO is the object — what RR stands for. The object can be a particular thing in the world, a fictional entity, a state of affairs, another sign, or anything an interpreter can take RR to be about.
  • II is the interpretant — the meaning produced in (or by) the interpreter, the functional effect RR has when read as standing for OO.

The triadic structure is irreducible. A two-place relation between RR and OO alone (a mark and a thing) does nothing without an interpreter to take it as standing-for. A two-place relation between RR and II alone is just a private association. The sign is only the three-place relation — and the relation, not its components, is what semiotics studies.

The interpretant is the crucial third term and the source of semiosis’s dynamism. The interpretant is itself a sign — a thought, a behavior, another representamen — that stands for the same object. That interpretant has its own interpretant, and so on. Semiosis is the open-ended chain of interpretants, with no last sign.

Three subdivisions of the interpretant. Peirce distinguishes the immediate interpretant (the meaning the sign carries internally, before being grasped), the dynamical interpretant (the actual effect the sign has on a particular interpreter), and the final interpretant (the effect the sign would produce in a sufficiently informed interpreter, the meaning toward which interpretation tends).

¶Peirce’s Trichotomies

Peirce classifies signs along three intersecting axes, each derived from his categories of Firstness, Secondness, and Thirdness. The three trichotomies yield, in principle, 3×3×3=273 \times 3 \times 3 = 27 classes; in practice Peirce constrained these to ten through inclusion rules.

Trichotomy 1 — by the sign’s mode of being (the representamen itself):

  • Qualisign: a quality functioning as a sign (a particular shade of red, a specific pitch).
  • Sinsign: a singular event or thing functioning as a sign (this footprint, this weather-vane reading).
  • Legisign: a general law or convention functioning as a sign (a word-type, a traffic-signal class).

Trichotomy 2 — by the relation between sign and object:

  • Icon: the sign resembles its object (a portrait, a map, a diagram, a metaphor).
  • Index: the sign is causally or spatially connected to its object (smoke indexing fire, a weathervane indexing wind, a finger pointing).
  • Symbol: the sign is connected to its object by convention or rule (most words, traffic signs, currency).

Trichotomy 3 — by the kind of interpretant the sign produces:

  • Rheme (term): a sign of qualitative possibility (a noun-like sign).
  • Dicisign (proposition): a sign of actual existence (an assertion, a proposition).
  • Argument: a sign of law or reasoning (a syllogism, a proof).

A given sign is classified across all three axes simultaneously. A spoken word like “red” is, in standard use, a legisign-symbol-rheme. A specific weathercock at a specific moment is a sinsign-index-dicisign. The classifications do important analytical work in distinguishing how different signs operate, and they prevent the easy collapse of all signs into the symbol-paradigm that bedevils less careful semiotics.

¶Saussure’s Dyadic Alternative

Ferdinand de Saussure’s Course in General Linguistics (1916, posthumous) develops a different framework, oriented to language as a system rather than to signs in general. A sign in Saussure’s account is dyadic:

Sign=(signifier,  signified)\text{Sign} = (\text{signifier},\; \text{signified})

The signifier is the sound-image (the perceptible side); the signified is the concept (the mental side). The two are bound together inseparably — like the two sides of a sheet of paper — and neither has any reality independent of the other.

Two structural claims follow:

  1. The arbitrariness of the sign. There is no natural connection between signifier and signified. The fact that English uses the sound-image /dog/ for the concept dog is a fact about the conventions of English, not a fact about dogs. Different languages bind different signifiers to similar signifieds; there is no necessary correspondence.

  2. The system as condition for the sign. A signifier signifies what it does only by being differentiated from other signifiers in the system. The English word “blue” means what it means partly because it is differentiated from “green,” and what counts as blue in English is not what counts as the corresponding term in languages that divide the color spectrum differently. The sign has its identity from its place in the differential system, not from any intrinsic property.

The structuralist tradition (Lévi-Strauss, early Barthes, Lacan, Eco’s earlier work) extends this dyadic-systemic framework into anthropology, literary criticism, psychoanalysis, and cultural analysis.

¶Comparing the Two Traditions

The Peircean triad and the Saussurean dyad are not equivalent. The reduction of the third term (the object, in Peirce; the world, in Saussure) is what distinguishes them.

Peirce includes the object as a constitutive term. The sign is about something; that something is part of the sign-relation. This makes Peircean semiotics a theory of reference as well as of meaning. The object can be missing or fictional, but its position in the triad is structural — the sign’s standing-for is standing-for-an-object, even if the object is the empty class or an imaginary one.

Saussure brackets the object. The signified is the concept, not the world. Saussure’s semiology is not a theory of how language refers to the world; it is a theory of how the linguistic system internally differentiates concepts. Reference, in this framework, is a problem to be handled separately, often through Frege-style sense-and-reference distinctions that the Saussurean dyad does not itself supply.

The contemporary practical effect: Peircean semiotics is more useful for investigating signs in non-linguistic registers (animal communication, photography, cinema, scientific notation, medical symptoms) where the object’s role is salient. Saussurean semiology is more useful for analyzing differential structures (the lexicon of a language, the structure of a mythology, the system of fashion). Most contemporary semioticians work with both, choosing the framework that suits the analytical question.

¶Eco: Sign Production and the Encyclopedia

Umberto Eco (A Theory of Semiotics, 1976) develops a synthesis that takes the Peircean triad seriously while incorporating structuralist resources. Eco argues that the sign is not a static three-place relation but a production: the sign-function is continuously instituted by the interpreter’s deployment of the cultural-encyclopedic resources available to them.

The encyclopedia, in Eco’s account, is the open, contradictory, never-fully-systematic body of cultural knowledge that interpreters bring to any sign. A dictionary attempts to fix meanings; the encyclopedia is what people actually use to interpret. Encyclopedic interpretation is rule-governed but not closed — there is always more to know about any sign, and the interpreter’s knowledge changes what the sign means to them.

This re-introduces the dynamism of semiosis at a different register from Peirce’s: not the chain of interpretants extending into the future, but the cultural-cognitive resources brought to bear in any given interpretive act.

¶Barthes: Connotation and the Cultural Sign

Roland Barthes (Mythologies, 1957; Elements of Semiology, 1964) extends Saussure’s framework into cultural analysis through the distinction between denotation and connotation.

A photograph of a young Black soldier saluting the French flag (Barthes’s example from Mythologies) denotes, at the first level, that specific scene — that person, that gesture, that flag. At the second level, it connotes “French imperialism is good and inclusive,” “the empire is loved by its colonized subjects,” “France is a multiethnic civic nation.” The denotation is the literal sign-relation; the connotation is the cultural-mythical relation that takes the entire denotative sign as its signifier and attaches to it a higher-order signified.

Mythologies, on Barthes’s reading, are second-order semiological systems that naturalize particular ideological positions by routing them through ordinary signs. The political work of cultural critique is to denaturalize the second-order sign — to show that what seems to be a transparent description is in fact carrying a cultural-political payload.

¶Lotman: The Sign Within the Semiosphere

Yuri Lotman (Universe of the Mind, 1990) reframes the question by asking: what is the space in which signs exist? Lotman’s answer is the semiosphere — the totality of sign-systems and communicative processes that constitutes a culture’s conditions of intelligibility. A sign is not a free-floating semantic object; it operates only within a semiosphere that bounds, conditions, and translates it.

The semiosphere has internal heterogeneity: multiple sub-systems with different codes, different translation-rules between them, and a boundary that distinguishes the semiosphere’s inside (intelligible) from its outside (not-yet-or-no-longer intelligible). Cultural change occurs at the boundary, where untranslated material enters and is partially encoded, generating semantic novelty.

Lotman’s contribution to the question of “what is a sign?” is the structural insistence that signs are always within a semiosphere. The sign-as-isolated-object is an analytical convenience; the sign-as-element-of-a-living-cultural-system is the substance.

¶Adjacent Distinctions

A sign is not the same as a mark. A mark is the perceptible thing — the ink on paper, the sound-wave, the gesture. A mark becomes a sign only when an interpreter takes it as standing-for something. The mark is the condition; the sign is the relation.

A sign is not the same as a symptom in the strict medical sense. A symptom is a causal-indexical sign (in Peirce’s classification) — fever indexes infection because the infection causes the fever. The symptom can function as a sign for a competent interpreter, but its sign-function depends on the interpreter, not on the causal connection alone.

A sign is not the same as a code. A code is the rule-system that pairs signs with their interpretive frames; the sign is the individual instance the code governs. Saussure’s langue is the code; parole is the actualized signs in use. Eco’s encyclopedia is what the dictionary’s code-function gestures at without exhausting.

A sign is not the same as a representation in the broader cognitive-philosophy sense. Representations include unconscious mental states, sub-personal information-bearing structures, and computational tokens that may not function as signs for any interpreter. Signs are a subset: representations that operate triadically (Peirce) or dyadically (Saussure) within an interpretive practice.

¶Open Questions

Are there signs without interpreters? The standard answer is no — Peirce’s triad collapses without II, Saussure’s dyad presupposes the linguistic community. But biosemiotic and ecosemiotic accounts (Sebeok, Hoffmeyer, Kull) have argued that non-human organisms read signs in their environments, and that the sign-relation extends below the human and below the conscious. Whether these are signs in the strict sense or analogues by metaphor is contested.

How do signs operate under conditions of saturated digital production, where the sign’s interpretant chain is continuously routed through algorithmic processing rather than human reading? Platform conditions extend the chain of semiosis into machine-readable encodings whose interpretive work no human directly performs; whether this is still semiosis or has crossed into something else is open.

What is the relation between sign and ideology? Barthes’s myth-as-second-order-sign, the structuralist analyses of cultural systems, the contemporary discourse-analysis traditions all work this question; the answers are not unified. The minimal claim that signs carry cultural-political content beyond their literal denotation is uncontroversial; the structural account of how this happens, and what it implies for political analysis, is contested.
