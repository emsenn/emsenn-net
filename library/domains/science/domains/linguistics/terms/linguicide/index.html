Linguicide

2026-04-02 The deliberate killing of a language by state policy — not natural language death but the systematic suppression of a language through bans, educational exclusion, and institutional erasure. Coined by Amir Hassanpour.

Linguicide is the deliberate killing of a language by state policy. The term, coined by Amir Hassanpour (1992), distinguishes state-driven language destruction from natural language death (where a language loses speakers through demographic change) and language shift (where speakers voluntarily adopt a dominant language). Linguicide names a specific political operation: the institutions of a state — its schools, courts, media, military, civil administration — act as instruments of language killing.

Hassanpour developed the concept through his study of Kurdish language suppression across Turkey, Iran, and Syria. In each case, the state did not merely neglect Kurdish but actively prohibited it: banning Kurdish-language education, publications, broadcasts, music, and public speech. Turkey imposed fines on every word of Kurdish spoken in public. Iran classified Kurdish as a “rustic and primitive dialect” unsuitable for schools. Syria stripped Kurdish-speaking citizens of their nationality. These are not cases of a language fading. They are cases of a language being killed — its speakers punished, its written forms confiscated, its institutional supports dismantled.

The concept is analytically important because it names the agent. Languages do not die of natural causes when a state bans their use. They are killed, and the killer has an address. Linguicide connects language suppression to the broader apparatus of nationalist legibility production: the state requires a single national language to administer its population, and languages that compete with this requirement are not merely inconvenient but threatening to the state’s claim to coherent sovereignty.

Linguicide is also an attack on heteroglossia. A language carries not just vocabulary and grammar but entire worldviews, kinship structures, relational categories, and ways of being that may be inexpressible in the dominant language. When the boarding school forbids Lakhotiyapi, when the Pahlavi state bans Kurdish, when the Turkish republic declares Kurdish nonexistent, they are not merely silencing speakers. They are destroying the grammar in which alternative ways of life are expressible.
