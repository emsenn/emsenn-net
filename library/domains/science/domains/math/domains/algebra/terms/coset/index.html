Coset

2026-03-05
Defines Coset, coset

A coset is a “shifted copy” of a subgroup within a larger group. Given a subgroup HH of a group GG and an element g∈Gg \in G, the left coset of HH by gg is gH={g⋅h:h∈H}gH = \{g \cdot h : h \in H\} — you take every element of HH and multiply it on the left by gg. The right coset is Hg={h⋅g:h∈H}Hg = \{h \cdot g : h \in H\} — you multiply on the right instead.

Consider the integers Z\mathbb{Z} with addition as the group operation, and the even integers 2Z2\mathbb{Z} as the subgroup. The cosets of 2Z2\mathbb{Z} are the even integers themselves (0+2Z={…,−4,−2,0,2,4,…}0 + 2\mathbb{Z} = \{\ldots, -4, -2, 0, 2, 4, \ldots\}) and the odd integers (1+2Z={…,−3,−1,1,3,5,…}1 + 2\mathbb{Z} = \{\ldots, -3, -1, 1, 3, 5, \ldots\}). Every integer is in exactly one of these two cosets. The cosets partition Z\mathbb{Z} into two disjoint pieces of “equal size.”

This partitioning property holds in general: the distinct left cosets of HH in GG are pairwise disjoint and cover all of GG. Every element of GG belongs to exactly one coset. Two cosets gHgH and g′Hg'H are either identical (when g−1g′∈Hg^{-1}g' \in H) or completely disjoint (when g−1g′∉Hg^{-1}g' \notin H). Moreover, all cosets have the same size as HH — the map h↦ghh \mapsto gh is a bijection from HH to gHgH.

This partition is the engine behind Lagrange’s theorem: if GG is finite, then ∣G∣=∣G:H∣⋅∣H∣|G| = |G:H| \cdot |H|, where ∣G:H∣|G:H| is the number of distinct cosets (called the index of HH in GG). Since GG splits into ∣G:H∣|G:H| disjoint pieces of size ∣H∣|H|, the order of HH must divide the order of GG. Joseph-Louis Lagrange proved this in the context of permutation groups in Réflexions sur la résolution algébrique des équations (1770–1771).

Cosets also determine when a subgroup allows a quotient construction. When the left and right cosets coincide — gH=HggH = Hg for all g∈Gg \in G — the subgroup is normal, and the set of cosets inherits a well-defined group operation: (gH)(hH)=(gh)H(gH)(hH) = (gh)H. The resulting structure is the quotient group G/HG/H. When left and right cosets differ, this multiplication is ambiguous and no quotient group exists.
