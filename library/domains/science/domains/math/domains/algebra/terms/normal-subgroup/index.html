Normal Subgroup

2026-03-05

A normal subgroup is a subgroup NN of a group GG that is “symmetric” with respect to the rest of the group — it looks the same from every vantage point within GG. Formally, NN is normal (written N⊴GN \trianglelefteq G) if gNg−1=NgNg^{-1} = N for every g∈Gg \in G. This condition says that conjugating NN by any element of GG — moving to a different “perspective” within the group — gives NN back again. Equivalently, the left and right cosets of NN coincide: gN=NggN = Ng for all gg.

Why does this condition matter? Because normal subgroups are exactly the subgroups you can “divide out” to form a well-defined quotient group. When you try to define multiplication on cosets — (gN)(hN)=(gh)N(gN)(hN) = (gh)N — you need the result to be independent of which representatives gg and hh you choose from each coset. This works if and only if NN is normal. Without normality, the coset multiplication is ambiguous and the quotient construction breaks down.

Normal subgroups are also exactly the kernels of group homomorphisms. If ϕ:G→H\phi: G \to H is a homomorphism, then ker⁡(ϕ)={g∈G:ϕ(g)=eH}\ker(\phi) = \{g \in G : \phi(g) = e_H\} is always a normal subgroup of GG. Conversely, every normal subgroup is the kernel of the canonical projection π:G→G/N\pi: G \to G/N defined by π(g)=gN\pi(g) = gN. This connection between normal subgroups, quotient groups, and homomorphisms is codified in the first isomorphism theorem: for any homomorphism ϕ:G→H\phi: G \to H, the quotient G/ker⁡(ϕ)G/\ker(\phi) is isomorphic to the image of ϕ\phi.

In an abelian group — where the group operation is commutative — every subgroup is automatically normal, because gNg−1=gg−1N=NgNg^{-1} = gg^{-1}N = N for all gg. In non-abelian groups, normality is a genuine constraint that must be checked. The symmetric group S3S_3, for example, has subgroups of order 2 that are not normal: conjugating them by different elements of S3S_3 moves them around.

Normal subgroups play the same role in group theory that ideals play in ring theory: they are the substructures that enable quotient constructions. Évariste Galois implicitly used normal subgroups in his work on the solvability of polynomial equations by radicals (1832, published posthumously 1846) — a polynomial equation is solvable by radicals if and only if its Galois group has a composition series with abelian quotients, a condition that depends entirely on the normal subgroup structure.
