2026-03-30 The property that order does not matter: a · b = b · a. Distinguishes commutative algebra from non-commutative, and linear logic from the Lambek calculus.

A binary operation ∗\ast is commutative if order does not matter:

a∗b=b∗aa \ast b = b \ast a

Addition is commutative: 3+5=5+33 + 5 = 5 + 3. Multiplication is commutative: 3×5=5×33 \times 5 = 5 \times 3. Function composition is not: g∘f≠f∘gg \circ f \neq f \circ g in general. Matrix multiplication is not.

Commutativity distinguishes algebraic families:

  • A commutative group is an abelian group.
  • A commutative ring has ab=baab = ba for all elements.
  • A commutative residuated lattice has a⋅b=b⋅aa \cdot b = b \cdot a, so the two residuals (// and \\backslash) coincide into a single implication →\to.

In substructural logic, commutativity corresponds to the exchange rule: the order of hypotheses does not matter. Dropping exchange gives the Lambek calculus, where the two residuals are genuinely different (forward application // vs. backward application \\backslash). Keeping exchange gives commutative linear logic, where a single implication suffices.

The four-valued bilattice 2⊗2\mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2} is a commutative residuated lattice (Busaniche & Cignoli 2014): a⊙b=b⊙aa \odot b = b \odot a. The two sides of the Lambek residuation collapse, and extending/restricting become two readings of a single adjunction.
