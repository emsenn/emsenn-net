2026-03-31 The property that one operation spreads over another: a · (b + c) = (a · b) + (a · c). The condition that makes meet and join, or product and sum, interact coherently.

A binary operation ⋅\cdot distributes over another operation ++ if:

a⋅(b+c)=(a⋅b)+(a⋅c)a \cdot (b + c) = (a \cdot b) + (a \cdot c)

Multiplication distributes over addition: 3×(4+5)=3×4+3×5=273 \times (4 + 5) = 3 \times 4 + 3 \times 5 = 27. This is why you can expand brackets in arithmetic.

In a lattice, distributivity means meet distributes over join:

a∧(b∨c)=(a∧b)∨(a∧c)a \wedge (b \vee c) = (a \wedge b) \vee (a \wedge c)

A distributive lattice is one where this holds. In a distributive lattice, either distributive law implies the other (meet over join and join over meet). Most lattices encountered in practice are distributive: power sets, chains, the lattice of open sets of a topological space.

Distributivity matters because it ensures the two operations interact predictably. Without it, meet and join can behave in surprising ways — there exist non-distributive lattices where a∧(b∨c)≠(a∧b)∨(a∧c)a \wedge (b \vee c) \neq (a \wedge b) \vee (a \wedge c).

In a ring, multiplication distributes over addition. In a bilattice, the interlacing conditions require each of the four operations (truth-meet, truth-join, knowledge-meet, knowledge-join) to be monotone with respect to both orderings — a strong form of cross-distributivity. The four-valued bilattice 2⊗2\mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2} is distributive in all relevant senses (Fitting 2002).
