2026-03-31 An element ¬a such that a ∧ ¬a = ⊥ and a ∨ ¬a = ⊤. Every element of a Boolean algebra has a complement; in a Heyting algebra, not every element does.

In a bounded lattice, the complement of an element aa is an element ¬a\neg a satisfying:

a∧¬a=⊥a∨¬a=⊤a \wedge \neg a = \bot \qquad a \vee \neg a = \top

The meet gives non-contradiction: aa and its complement have nothing in common. The join gives excluded middle: between them, aa and ¬a\neg a cover everything.

In a Boolean algebra, every element has a unique complement, and ¬¬a=a\neg\neg a = a. In a Heyting algebra, the pseudo-complement ¬a=a→⊥\neg a = a \to \bot exists but may not satisfy excluded middle: a∨¬a=⊤a \vee \neg a = \top can fail. This is the algebraic gap between classical and constructive logic.

Complementation is different from negation on a bilattice. Negation ∼\mathord{\sim} on the four-valued bilattice swaps t↔ft \leftrightarrow f and fixes ⊥,⊤\bot, \top. It is an involution (∼∼a=a\mathord{\sim}\mathord{\sim} a = a) but it is not a complement: t∧t∼t=t∧tf=f≠⊥t \wedge_t \mathord{\sim} t = t \wedge_t f = f \neq \bot. Bilattice negation reverses truth; it does not produce the lattice-theoretic complement.
