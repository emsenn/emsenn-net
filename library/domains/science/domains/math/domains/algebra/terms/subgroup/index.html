Subgroup

2026-03-05
Defines Subgroup, subgroups

A subgroup is a group that lives inside a larger group — a subset that is itself a group under the same operation. The even integers form a subgroup of the integers under addition: they are closed under addition (even plus even is even), they contain the identity element 0, and every even integer has an additive inverse that is also even. The odd integers do not form a subgroup: odd plus odd is even, so they are not closed under addition.

Formally, a subset HH of a group GG is a subgroup if it satisfies three conditions: HH is non-empty; if aa and bb are in HH, then abab is in HH (closure under the group operation); and if aa is in HH, then a−1a^{-1} is in HH (closure under inverses). These three conditions together guarantee that HH inherits all the group axioms from GG — associativity comes for free because HH uses the same operation as GG.

Every group has at least two subgroups: the trivial subgroup {e}\{e\} containing only the identity element, and the group GG itself. Any subgroup other than these two is called a proper subgroup. Finding and classifying the subgroups of a group is one of the central problems in group theory, because the subgroups reveal the internal structure of the group.

Lagrange’s theorem connects the size of a subgroup to the size of the group: if GG is finite and HH is a subgroup, then ∣H∣|H| divides ∣G∣|G|. Joseph-Louis Lagrange proved this in Réflexions sur la résolution algébrique des équations (1770–1771), in the context of studying permutation groups associated with polynomial equations. The proof works through cosets: the distinct left cosets of HH partition GG into non-overlapping pieces of equal size ∣H∣|H|, so ∣G∣=∣G:H∣⋅∣H∣|G| = |G:H| \cdot |H|, where ∣G:H∣|G:H| is the number of cosets (the index of HH in GG). This immediately implies, for instance, that a group of order 12 can have subgroups of order 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, or 12, but not of order 5, 7, 8, 9, 10, or 11.

The subgroups of a group form a lattice ordered by inclusion. The meet of two subgroups is their intersection (which is always a subgroup). The join is the subgroup generated by their union — the smallest subgroup containing both. This lattice of subgroups is one of the primary tools for understanding a group’s structure.
