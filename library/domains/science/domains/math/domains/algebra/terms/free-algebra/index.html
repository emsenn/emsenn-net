2026-03-31 The algebra generated by a set of elements with no relations beyond the axioms of the algebraic theory. The 'most general' algebra on those generators.

The free algebra on a set of generators is the algebra you get by combining the generators using the operations of the theory, imposing only the axioms and nothing more. It is the “most general” algebra on those generators — any other algebra on the same generators is a quotient of it.

The free group on one generator is the integers under addition: {...,−2,−1,0,1,2,...}\{..., -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, ...\}, where the generator is 11 and the group operation is ++. No relation holds between the powers of the generator except what the group axioms force.

The free monoid on an alphabet Σ\Sigma is the set of all strings over Σ\Sigma, with concatenation as the operation and the empty string as identity. Every string is a product of generators, and no two distinct strings are equal. This is why strings are fundamental to computation — they are the free monoid.

Fitting (2002) proved the four-valued bilattice {⊥,t,f,⊤}\{\bot, t, f, \top\} is the free bounded distributive bilattice on one generator. This means: it is the most general bilattice you can build from a single element, imposing only the bilattice axioms. Any bounded distributive bilattice generated by one element is a quotient of {⊥,t,f,⊤}\{\bot, t, f, \top\}.

This freeness result is why 2⊗2\mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2} is the canonical bilattice. It is not an arbitrary choice — it is the universal one. Any bilattice-valued predicate system factors through it.

Free algebras are connected to adjunctions: the free construction (generators →\to free algebra) is left adjoint to the forgetful functor (algebra →\to underlying set). Freeness IS left adjointness.
