2026-03-30 A set with two lattice orderings (truth and knowledge), plus negation and conflation involutions, interacting via interlacing conditions.

A bilattice is a set LL equipped with two bounded lattice structures:

  • A truth lattice (L,∧t,∨t,f,t)(L, \wedge_t, \vee_t, f, t) with bottom ff and top tt
  • A knowledge lattice (L,∧k,∨k,⊥,⊤)(L, \wedge_k, \vee_k, \bot, \top) with bottom ⊥\bot and top ⊤\top

together with two involutions:

  • Negation ∼\sim: reverses the truth ordering, preserves the knowledge ordering
  • Conflation −-: preserves the truth ordering, reverses the knowledge ordering

Ginsberg (1988) introduced bilattices for AI reasoning. Fitting (1991, 2002) developed their theory. The canonical example is the four-valued bilattice {⊥,t,f,⊤}\{\bot, t, f, \top\} defined by Belnap (1977) for reasoning under incomplete and contradictory information. Fitting (2002) proved this is the free bounded distributive bilattice on one generator.

A bilattice is distributive if both lattice structures are distributive and the interlacing conditions hold: each of the four operations (∧t,∨t,∧k,∨k\wedge_t, \vee_t, \wedge_k, \vee_k) is monotone with respect to both orderings. A bilattice is interlaced if all four operations are monotone with respect to both orderings.

The four-valued bilattice is isomorphic to the twist product 2⊗2\mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2} of the two-element Boolean algebra with itself (Kalman 1958, Fitting 1991). In this representation, each value is a pair (a,b)∈{0,1}2(a, b) \in \{0,1\}^2: ⊥=(0,0)\bot = (0,0), t=(1,0)t = (1,0), f=(0,1)f = (0,1), ⊤=(1,1)\top = (1,1). The first component tracks positive evidence; the second tracks negative evidence.

Negation and conflation together generate Z2×Z2\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2 (the Klein four-group), the symmetry group of the bilattice viewed as a square.

The bilattice carries a residuated lattice structure via the Busaniche-Cignoli (2014) operations on the twist product.
