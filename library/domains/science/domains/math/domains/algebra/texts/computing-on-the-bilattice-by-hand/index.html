Computing on the Bilattice by Hand

2026-03-31 A tutorial: take a small set of assertions with epistemic states, compute extends (⊙) and restricts (⇒) step by step, and see what the adjunction does.

This tutorial walks through computing with the four-valued bilattice by hand. You will apply the extending operation (⊙\odot) and the restricting operation (⇒\Rightarrow) to concrete epistemic states and verify the adjunction on specific examples.

¶The four values

Each value is a pair of binary components — positive evidence and negative evidence:

Value Pair Meaning
⊥\bot (0, 0) No information from any source
tt (1, 0) Affirmed: positive evidence, no negative
ff (0, 1) Denied: negative evidence, no positive
⊤\top (1, 1) Contested: both positive and negative evidence

¶The operations

The base lattice is {0,1}\{0, 1\} with multiplication x⋅y=min⁡(x,y)x \cdot y = \min(x, y) and implication x→y=0x \to y = 0 if x>yx > y, else 11.

Extending (⊙\odot): (x,y)⊙(z,v)=(x⋅z, (x→v)∧(z→y))(x, y) \odot (z, v) = (x \cdot z,\ (x \to v) \wedge (z \to y))

Restricting (⇒\Rightarrow): (x,y)⇒(z,v)=((x→z)∧(v→y), x⋅v)(x, y) \Rightarrow (z, v) = ((x \to z) \wedge (v \to y),\ x \cdot v)

¶Worked examples

¶Example 1: Extending an affirmation by an affirmation

t⊙t=(1,0)⊙(1,0)t \odot t = (1, 0) \odot (1, 0)

First component: 1⋅1=11 \cdot 1 = 1 Second component: (1→0)∧(1→0)=0∧0=0(1 \to 0) \wedge (1 \to 0) = 0 \wedge 0 = 0

Result: (1,0)=t(1, 0) = t.

Affirming something that is already affirmed gives affirmation. Extending does not create contradiction from agreement.

¶Example 2: Extending an affirmation by a denial

t⊙f=(1,0)⊙(0,1)t \odot f = (1, 0) \odot (0, 1)

First component: 1⋅0=01 \cdot 0 = 0 Second component: (1→1)∧(0→0)=1∧1=1(1 \to 1) \wedge (0 \to 0) = 1 \wedge 1 = 1

Result: (0,1)=f(0, 1) = f.

Extending an affirmation by a denial yields denial. The denial wins — it absorbs the affirmation.

¶Example 3: What about ⊥ ⊙ ⊥?

⊥⊙⊥=(0,0)⊙(0,0)\bot \odot \bot = (0, 0) \odot (0, 0)

First component: 0⋅0=00 \cdot 0 = 0 Second component: (0→0)∧(0→0)=1∧1=1(0 \to 0) \wedge (0 \to 0) = 1 \wedge 1 = 1

Result: (0,1)=f(0, 1) = f.

Extending nothing by nothing gives denial. This is the most surprising result in the table: the product of two unknowns is not unknown but denied. Two absences of evidence combine into a negative result.

¶Example 4: Restricting an affirmation by itself

t⇒t=(1,0)⇒(1,0)t \Rightarrow t = (1, 0) \Rightarrow (1, 0)

First component: (1→1)∧(0→0)=1∧1=1(1 \to 1) \wedge (0 \to 0) = 1 \wedge 1 = 1 Second component: 1⋅0=01 \cdot 0 = 0

Result: (1,0)=t(1, 0) = t.

Restricting something by itself affirms it. Self-restriction is self-confirmation.

¶Example 5: Does the adjunction hold?

Check the triple a=ta = t, p=⊥p = \bot, q=tq = t:

Left side: t⊙⊥=⊥t \odot \bot = \bot (computed from the table). Is ⊥≤tt\bot \leq_t t? The truth ordering has ⊥≤tt\bot \leq_t t. Yes. Left side: TRUE.

Right side: t⇒t=tt \Rightarrow t = t (computed above). Is ⊥≤tt\bot \leq_t t? Yes. Right side: TRUE.

Both true. The adjunction holds for this triple.

Check a=fa = f, p=tp = t, q=⊥q = \bot:

Left side: f⊙t=ff \odot t = f. Is f≤t⊥f \leq_t \bot? The truth ordering has f≤t⊥f \leq_t \bot. Yes. Left side: TRUE.

Right side: f⇒⊥=tf \Rightarrow \bot = t. Is t≤ttt \leq_t t? Yes. Right side: TRUE.

Both true. Holds again.

¶The full tables

For reference, the complete operation tables (verify any entry using the formulas above):

⊙  |  ⊥   t   f   ⊤        ⇒  |  ⊥   t   f   ⊤
---|----------------        ---|-------------
---
⊥  |  f   ⊥   f   ⊥        ⊥  |  t   t   ⊥   ⊥
t  |  ⊥   t   f   t        t  |  ⊥   t   f   f
f  |  f   f   f   f        f  |  t   t   t   t
⊤  |  ⊥   t   f   ⊤        ⊤  |  ⊥   t   f   ⊤

Patterns to notice:

  • The ⊤\top row and column of ⊙\odot reproduce the other argument: ⊤⊙a=a\top \odot a = a. Extending by the maximally-informed state does nothing.
  • The ff row of ⊙\odot is all ff: extending by denial always yields denial.
  • The ff row of ⇒\Rightarrow is all tt: denial restricts everything to affirmation. (If the premise is denied, the implication is vacuously true.)
  • The diagonal of ⇒\Rightarrow is tt except for ⊤⇒⊤=⊤\top \Rightarrow \top = \top: self-restriction affirms, unless you are already contested.
