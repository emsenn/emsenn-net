Population

2026-01-01

A population is the complete set of individuals, items, or observations that a statistical study aims to describe. It is the thing you actually want to know about — the sample is just the means by which you learn. If a medical researcher wants to know whether a drug lowers blood pressure in adults, the population is all adults (or perhaps all adults with hypertension, depending on the research question). If a manufacturer wants to know the defect rate of its products, the population is every item produced.

Populations can be finite and concrete: all 1,200 students at a particular school, all 50 states in the United States, all light bulbs produced in a factory this month. They can also be conceptually infinite: all possible rolls of a die (the die could be rolled forever), all adults who could ever take the drug (including future patients). The distinction matters because infinite populations cannot be observed in full even in principle, making sampling and inference unavoidable.

Population parameters are numerical summaries of the entire population: the population mean μ\mu, the population variance σ2\sigma^2, the population proportion pp, and so on. These parameters are typically unknown — if you knew the mean blood pressure of all adults, you would not need a study. The goal of statistical inference is to estimate population parameters from sample statistics (the sample mean xˉ\bar{x}, sample variance s2s^2, etc.) and to quantify the uncertainty of those estimates through confidence intervals, p-values, or posterior distributions.

A well-defined population requires clear membership criteria: who or what is included, and who or what is excluded. This sounds trivial but is often the hardest part of a study. “All adults” includes people of every age, nationality, and health status — is that really who you mean? “All adults with hypertension” requires a definition of hypertension (what blood pressure threshold?). Ambiguity about the population leads directly to ambiguity about what the study’s conclusions mean and to whom they apply. The generalizability of a study — whether its results extend beyond the specific sample — depends on the match between the sample and the population it is intended to represent.

The mathematical relationship between population and sample is formalized through probability theory. Each observation in the sample is modeled as an independent random draw from the population (or more precisely, from the population’s probability distribution). The independence assumption is what justifies the mathematical machinery of inference: the law of large numbers, the central limit theorem, and the formulas for standard errors all depend on it. When independence fails — as it does in clustered data, time series, or spatially correlated observations — the standard formulas can give misleading results, and more sophisticated methods are needed.
