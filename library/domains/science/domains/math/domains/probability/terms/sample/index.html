Sample

2026-01-01
Defines Sample, samples

A sample is a subset of a population selected for observation. Statistics exists because populations are usually too large, too expensive, or too impractical to observe in full. You cannot measure the blood pressure of every human alive, but you can measure the blood pressure of 500 people and use the results to draw conclusions about the whole. The 500 people are the sample; the conclusions about all humans are the inference.

The sample’s value depends entirely on how it is selected. A random sample — one where each member of the population has a known probability of being chosen — is the gold standard. Simple random sampling gives every member equal probability of selection: draw names from a hat, use a random number generator, or assign each member a number and select numbers at random. Stratified sampling divides the population into subgroups (strata) and samples within each stratum, ensuring that minority groups are adequately represented. Cluster sampling selects entire groups (schools, neighborhoods) and observes everyone within the selected groups. Systematic sampling selects every kkth member from a list.

What all these methods share is that the selection mechanism is known and probabilistic, which means the relationship between the sample and the population can be quantified mathematically. A biased sample — one that systematically over- or under-represents parts of the population — undermines this relationship. A survey conducted only by telephone misses people without phones. A medical study that recruits only from one hospital may not generalize to other settings. The most famous example of sampling bias is the 1936 Literary Digest presidential poll, which predicted a landslide for Alf Landon based on 2.4 million responses — from a sample drawn disproportionately from automobile and telephone owners, who were wealthier and more Republican than the general population. Franklin Delano Roosevelt won in a landslide.

Sample statistics — the sample mean xˉ\bar{x}, sample variance s2s^2, sample median — are numbers computed from the observed data and used to estimate the corresponding population parameters μ\mu, σ2\sigma^2, and so on. These estimates are not exact, but they improve with sample size. The law of large numbers guarantees that sample statistics converge to population parameters as the number of observations grows. The central limit theorem guarantees that the sampling distribution of the mean (the distribution of xˉ\bar{x} across many hypothetical samples) approaches a normal distribution regardless of the population’s shape, provided the sample is large enough. These two theorems are the mathematical justification for all of frequentist statistics: they make it possible to quantify how much a sample can tell you about a population.
