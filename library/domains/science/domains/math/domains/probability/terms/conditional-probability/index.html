Conditional Probability

2026-01-01

Conditional probability is the probability of one event given that another event has already occurred or is known to be true. It answers questions like: what is the probability it will rain tomorrow, given that the barometric pressure is falling? What is the probability a patient has a disease, given that their test came back positive? The “given” clause changes the calculation by restricting attention to a subset of the possible outcomes.

The formal definition is straightforward: the conditional probability of event AA given event BB is P(A∣B)=P(A∩B)/P(B)P(A \mid B) = P(A \cap B) / P(B), provided P(B)>0P(B) > 0. This formula says: look at the outcomes where both AA and BB occur, and measure their probability relative to BB alone. Conditioning on BB is equivalent to shrinking the sample space to BB and rescaling all probabilities so they sum to 1 within that restricted space.

A concrete example makes this clear. Suppose you draw one card from a standard deck. The probability of drawing an ace is 4/52=1/134/52 = 1/13. But if someone tells you the card is a spade, the sample space shrinks from 52 cards to 13. There are still only 1 ace of spades among those 13, so P(ace∣spade)=1/13P(\text{ace} \mid \text{spade}) = 1/13 — in this case, knowing the suit happens not to change anything. But if someone tells you the card is a face card (12 cards), then P(ace∣face card)=0P(\text{ace} \mid \text{face card}) = 0 — aces are not face cards. The conditioning information can increase, decrease, or leave unchanged the probability of an event.

The most important consequence of conditional probability is Bayes’ theorem: P(A∣B)=P(B∣A)⋅P(A)/P(B)P(A \mid B) = P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A) / P(B). This relates the probability of a hypothesis given evidence to the probability of evidence given the hypothesis. Thomas Bayes derived this result in An Essay towards solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances (published posthumously in 1763), and Pierre-Simon Laplace independently developed and generalized it in Théorie analytique des probabilités (1812).

Bayes’ theorem is the engine of Bayesian inference. The prior P(A)P(A) represents initial belief about hypothesis AA. The likelihood P(B∣A)P(B \mid A) measures how well AA explains the observed evidence BB. The posterior P(A∣B)P(A \mid B) is the updated belief after seeing the evidence. The denominator P(B)P(B) ensures the result is a valid probability. The theorem formalizes a natural process: you start with a belief, you observe something, and you update.

Two events are independent when conditioning changes nothing: P(A∣B)=P(A)P(A \mid B) = P(A), equivalently P(A∩B)=P(A)⋅P(B)P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B). Independence means that knowing BB occurred gives no information about AA. When events are not independent, conditioning is what captures the dependency.

For sequences of events, conditional probabilities chain: P(A∩B∩C)=P(A)⋅P(B∣A)⋅P(C∣A∩B)P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A) \cdot P(C \mid A \cap B). This chain rule decomposes the joint probability of multiple events into a product of conditional probabilities, each conditioned on everything that came before. This decomposition is the foundation of probabilistic graphical models (Bayesian networks), Markov chains, and sequential decision-making under uncertainty.
