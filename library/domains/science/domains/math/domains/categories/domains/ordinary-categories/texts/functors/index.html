Functors

2025-12-26

Entry conditions

Use functors only when you already have:

  • Two categories with explicit objects and morphisms.
  • A way to map objects to objects and morphisms to morphisms.
  • Proof that identities and composition are preserved.

Definitions

  • A functor F:C→DF:C \to D maps objects of CC to objects of DD and morphisms of CC to morphisms of DD, preserving identities and composition.

Vocabulary (plain language)

  • Preserve identity: F(idA)=idF(A)F(\mathrm{id}_A) = \mathrm{id}_{F(A)}.
  • Preserve composition: F(g∘f)=F(g)∘F(f)F(g \circ f) = F(g) \circ F(f).

Symbols used

  • F:C→DF:C \to D: a functor from category CC to category DD.
  • F(f)F(f): the image of a morphism ff under FF.

Intuition

A functor is a translation that respects structure. It moves both things and processes between things, without changing how composition works.

Worked example

Let U:Grp→SetU:\mathbf{Grp} \to \mathbf{Set} send each group to its underlying set and each group homomorphism to the same function. This preserves identities and composition, so UU is a functor.

How to recognize the structure

  • You have explicit object and morphism mappings.
  • Identities are preserved.
  • Composition is preserved.

Common mistakes

  • Mapping objects but not morphisms.
  • Failing to check preservation of composition.

Minimal data

  • Two categories.
  • A mapping of objects and morphisms.
  • Proofs of identity and composition preservation.
