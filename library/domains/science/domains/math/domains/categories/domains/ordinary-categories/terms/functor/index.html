2025-12-26 A structure-preserving map between categories — sends objects to objects and morphisms to morphisms, respecting composition and identity.

A functor F:C→DF : C \to D is a map from one category to another that preserves the structure. It sends objects to objects and morphisms to morphisms. It does not break composition or identities: if ff and gg compose to hh in C, then F(f)F(f) and F(g)F(g) compose to F(h)F(h) in D. If idA\mathrm{id}_A is an identity morphism, then F(idA)=idF(A)F(\mathrm{id}_A) = \mathrm{id}_{F(A)}.

That is the whole definition. A functor maps things and arrows between things, and it respects how arrows chain together. A functor is to categories what a homomorphism is to groups or a continuous map is to topological spaces — a structure-preserving translation between structured worlds.

A covariant functor preserves the direction of morphisms. A contravariant functor reverses direction: if ff goes from A to B, its image F(f)F(f) goes from F(B)F(B) to F(A)F(A). Contravariant functors from C to D are the same thing as covariant functors from the opposite category CopC^{\mathrm{op}} to D. The opposite category has the same objects but every morphism is reversed.

A forgetful functor strips structure away. The functor from groups to sets sends each group to its underlying set and forgets the group operation. A free functor adds structure. The functor from sets to groups sends each set to the free group generated by it. These two functors are related by an adjunction — the free functor is left adjoint to the forgetful functor. Adjunctions formalize the idea that two constructions are complementary: one adds structure, the other forgets it, and they are linked by a universal property.

A presheaf on a category C is a contravariant functor from C to Set. It assigns a set of data to each object and a function (restriction map) to each morphism. Presheaves are the basic building blocks of sheaf theory.

Functors between two categories form their own category, called the functor category [C,D][C, D]. The objects are the functors. The morphisms are natural transformations. This is the sense in which category theory is self-referential — categories of functors are themselves categories, and the same definitions apply at every level.
