2026-03-30 A functor from a base category of contexts to a fiber category of propositions, formalizing how predicates vary over contexts — Lawvere's categorical model of predicate logic.

A hyperdoctrine (Lawvere 1969, 1970) is a functor from a base category of contexts to a fiber category of propositions:

P:Cop→LatP: \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Lat}

For each context cc in C\mathcal{C}, the fiber P(c)P(c) is a lattice of propositions that can be stated in context cc. For each morphism f:c→df: c \to d (a substitution or change of context), the reindexing map P(f):P(d)→P(c)P(f): P(d) \to P(c) pulls back propositions from context dd to context cc.

The reindexing maps have adjoint pairs: left adjoints ∃f\exists_f (existential quantification along ff) and right adjoints ∀f\forall_f (universal quantification along ff):

∃f⊣f∗⊣∀f\exists_f \dashv f^* \dashv \forall_f

∃f\exists_f extends a predicate to a larger context by asserting existence. ∀f\forall_f restricts a predicate to where it holds universally. This is Lawvere’s insight: the logical quantifiers ∃\exists and ∀\forall are not primitive connectives but adjoint functors.

In classical logic, the fibers P(c)P(c) are Boolean algebras. In intuitionistic logic, Heyting algebras. In bilattice-valued logic, the fibers are distributive bilattices — giving a bilattice-valued hyperdoctrine where each predicate generates a fiber carrying the full bilattice algebra.

Maruyama (2021) generalized the framework to fibered algebraic semantics for non-classical logics: any non-classical logic with an algebraic semantics can be fibered over a base category. The bilattice-valued fiber functor is an instance of Maruyama’s framework for the specific case of the four-valued bilattice over predicate-structured data.
