A quasi-category is a simplicial set in which every inner horn has a filler: for every map Λⁿₖ → X with 0 < k < n, there exists an extension Δⁿ → X. This condition ensures that composites of morphisms exist but are not required to be unique — they are unique only up to a contractible space of choices.

Quasi-categories are the most widely used combinatorial model of ∞-categories. Every ordinary category embeds into quasi-categories via its nerve (the simplicial set whose n-simplices are chains of n composable morphisms), and the inner horn condition generalizes the fact that composites exist in a category. A quasi-category where all horns (not just inner ones) have fillers is a Kan complex — the model of an ∞-groupoid, where every morphism is invertible up to homotopy.

The theory of quasi-categories, developed extensively by André Joyal and Jacob Lurie, provides the combinatorial foundation for (∞,1)-categories and (∞,1)-topoi. Within this framework, the classical concepts of category theory — functors, natural transformations, adjunctions, limits — all have well-defined ∞-categorical analogues.
