Intersection

2025-12-26 The intersection of two [sets](./set.md) is the set of everything that belongs to both. If $A = {1, 2, 3, 4}$ and $B = {3, 4, 5, 6}$, then $A cap B = {3, 4}$ — only the [elements](./element.md) that appear in $A$ *and* in $B$. Intersection is the "and" operation on sets: $x in A cap B$ means $x in A$ and $x in B$.

The intersection of two sets is the set of everything that belongs to both. If A={1,2,3,4}A = \{1, 2, 3, 4\} and B={3,4,5,6}B = \{3, 4, 5, 6\}, then A∩B={3,4}A \cap B = \{3, 4\} — only the elements that appear in AA and in BB. Intersection is the “and” operation on sets: x∈A∩Bx \in A \cap B means x∈Ax \in A and x∈Bx \in B.

If two sets share no elements, their intersection is the empty set: {1,2}∩{3,4}=∅\{1, 2\} \cap \{3, 4\} = \emptyset. Such sets are called disjoint. If one set is contained in another (A⊆BA \subseteq B), their intersection is the smaller set: A∩B=AA \cap B = A.

More generally, the intersection of any family of sets {Ai}i∈I\{A_i\}_{i \in I} is ⋂i∈IAi={x:x∈Ai for all i∈I}\bigcap_{i \in I} A_i = \{x : x \in A_i \text{ for all } i \in I\} — the collection of elements that belong to every set in the family. The intersection gets smaller (or stays the same) as you add more sets to the family, because each additional set imposes another membership requirement.

Intersection is commutative (A∩B=B∩AA \cap B = B \cap A), associative ((A∩B)∩C=A∩(B∩C)(A \cap B) \cap C = A \cap (B \cap C)), and idempotent (A∩A=AA \cap A = A). It distributes over union: A∩(B∪C)=(A∩B)∪(A∩C)A \cap (B \cup C) = (A \cap B) \cup (A \cap C). De Morgan’s laws relate intersection to complement: (A∩B)c=Ac∪Bc(A \cap B)^c = A^c \cup B^c — failing to be in both sets means failing to be in at least one. Together with union and complement, intersection gives the power set of any set the structure of a Boolean algebra.

In a lattice, intersection corresponds to meet — the greatest lower bound. In the lattice of subsets ordered by inclusion, the meet of AA and BB is A∩BA \cap B: the largest set contained in both.

In the open sets of a topological space, intersection behaves asymmetrically compared to union. Arbitrary unions of open sets are always open, but only finite intersections are guaranteed open. The intersection of infinitely many open sets can fail to be open: ⋂n=1∞(−1/n,1/n)={0}\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} (-1/n, 1/n) = \{0\}, a single point, which is not open in R\mathbb{R}. This asymmetry — unrestricted unions, restricted intersections — is what makes the open-set lattice a frame rather than a Boolean algebra, and it is the structural signature of topology.

In the Heyting algebra HH, meet (a∧ba \wedge b) is the semantic analogue of intersection: the strongest value entailed by both aa and bb. The Heyting implication is defined through meet by the residuation law: c≤(a→b)c \leq (a \to b) if and only if c∧a≤bc \wedge a \leq b. This adjunction between meet and implication is the algebraic heart of intuitionistic logic.
