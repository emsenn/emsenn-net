Union

2025-12-26

The union of two sets is the set of everything that belongs to either one. If A={1,2,3}A = \{1, 2, 3\} and B={2,3,4,5}B = \{2, 3, 4, 5\}, then A∪B={1,2,3,4,5}A \cup B = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\} — everything that is in AA, in BB, or in both. The elements that appear in both sets are not counted twice; they simply appear once in the union. Union is the “or” operation on sets: x∈A∪Bx \in A \cup B means x∈Ax \in A or x∈Bx \in B (or both).

More generally, you can take the union of any family of sets. Given sets {Ai}i∈I\{A_i\}_{i \in I} indexed by some set II, the union ⋃i∈IAi={x:x∈Ai for some i∈I}\bigcup_{i \in I} A_i = \{x : x \in A_i \text{ for some } i \in I\} collects everything that belongs to at least one set in the family. The family can be finite, countably infinite, or uncountably infinite — the definition works regardless.

Union satisfies several clean algebraic properties. It is commutative: A∪B=B∪AA \cup B = B \cup A. It is associative: (A∪B)∪C=A∪(B∪C)(A \cup B) \cup C = A \cup (B \cup C). It is idempotent: A∪A=AA \cup A = A (taking the union of a set with itself changes nothing). The empty set is the identity element: A∪∅=AA \cup \emptyset = A (adding nothing changes nothing). These properties make union an operation in the algebraic sense, and together with intersection and complement, union gives the power set of any set the structure of a Boolean algebra.

Union interacts with intersection through the distributive law: A∩(B∪C)=(A∩B)∪(A∩C)A \cap (B \cup C) = (A \cap B) \cup (A \cap C), and with complement through De Morgan’s laws: (A∪B)c=Ac∩Bc(A \cup B)^c = A^c \cap B^c. The complement of a union is the intersection of the complements — an element fails to be in either set if and only if it fails to be in the first and fails to be in the second.

In a lattice, union corresponds to join — the least upper bound. In the lattice of subsets of a set, ordered by inclusion, the join of AA and BB is A∪BA \cup B: the smallest set containing both AA and BB. In the open sets of a topological space, arbitrary unions of open sets are open — this is one of the three topology axioms — and the join of open sets is their union. This unrestricted closure under union (contrasted with only finite closure under intersection) is what makes the open-set lattice a frame rather than a Boolean algebra.

In the Heyting algebra HH, join (a∨ba \vee b) is the semantic analogue of union: the weakest value that is at least as strong as both aa and bb. In intuitionistic logic, disjunction (∨\vee) is interpreted as join — asserting “aa or bb” requires producing evidence for at least one of the disjuncts.
