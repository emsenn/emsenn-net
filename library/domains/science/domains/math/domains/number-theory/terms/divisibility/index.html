Divisibility

2026-03-05

Divisibility is the fundamental relation of multiplicative number theory: it asks whether one integer fits evenly into another. We say that aa divides bb (written a∣ba \mid b) if there exists an integer kk such that b=akb = ak — that is, bb is a multiple of aa, and aa is a divisor (or factor) of bb. The number 3 divides 12 because 12=3×412 = 3 \times 4. The number 5 does not divide 12 because there is no integer kk with 12=5k12 = 5k.

Divisibility gives the positive integers a natural ordering — not the usual ordering by size, but an ordering by “fits into.” The number 1 divides everything and sits at the bottom. The primes sit just above 1: each is divisible only by 1 and itself. Composite numbers sit higher, divisible by more factors. This ordering is a partial order: it is reflexive (a∣aa \mid a for all aa), antisymmetric (if a∣ba \mid b and b∣ab \mid a then a=ba = b for positive integers), and transitive (if a∣ba \mid b and b∣cb \mid c then a∣ca \mid c, since if b=akb = ak and c=bjc = bj then c=akjc = akj). It is only a partial order, not a total one: 4 does not divide 6, and 6 does not divide 4 — the two are incomparable.

Under the divisibility ordering, the positive integers form a lattice. The meet of two numbers — their greatest lower bound under divisibility — is the greatest common divisor. The join — their least upper bound — is the least common multiple. The meet of 12 and 18 is 6 (the largest number dividing both), and the join is 36 (the smallest number both divide into). The fundamental theorem of arithmetic ensures that this lattice is well-behaved: every positive integer has a unique prime factorization, and the GCD and LCM can be read off by taking the minimum and maximum exponent of each prime, respectively.

Divisibility is the starting point for modular arithmetic, ring theory, and algebraic number theory. The concept of an ideal in a ring generalizes divisibility from integers to abstract algebraic structures, and the failure of divisibility to behave as expected in certain number rings — the failure of unique factorization — motivated the entire theory of ideals.
