Congruence

2026-03-05

Congruence is a way of saying that two integers leave the same remainder when divided by a given number. The numbers 17 and 5 are congruent modulo 12 because both leave a remainder of 5 when divided by 12 — or equivalently, because their difference 17−5=1217 - 5 = 12 is divisible by 12. Clock arithmetic is the most familiar example: 3 o’clock and 15 o’clock are the “same” time on a 12-hour clock, because 15≡3(mod12)15 \equiv 3 \pmod{12}.

Formally, two integers aa and bb are congruent modulo nn (written a≡b(modn)a \equiv b \pmod{n}) if nn divides a−ba - b. This relation was introduced by Carl Friedrich Gauss in Disquisitiones Arithmeticae (1801) — a book he published at the age of 24 that reshaped number theory. Gauss chose the ≡\equiv symbol and the “mod” notation, which remain standard today. Before Gauss, mathematicians worked with remainders and divisibility ad hoc; after him, congruence became a systematic tool.

Congruence modulo nn is an equivalence relation on Z\mathbb{Z}: it is reflexive (a≡aa \equiv a), symmetric (if a≡ba \equiv b then b≡ab \equiv a), and transitive (if a≡ba \equiv b and b≡cb \equiv c then a≡ca \equiv c). It partitions the integers into nn equivalence classes — the residue classes [0],[1],…,[n−1][0], [1], \ldots, [n-1], where [r][r] is the set of all integers with remainder rr when divided by nn.

What makes congruence powerful is its compatibility with arithmetic. If a≡a′(modn)a \equiv a' \pmod{n} and b≡b′(modn)b \equiv b' \pmod{n}, then a+b≡a′+b′(modn)a + b \equiv a' + b' \pmod{n} and ab≡a′b′(modn)ab \equiv a'b' \pmod{n}. You can add and multiply congruences freely, just as you add and multiply equations. This compatibility is not merely convenient — it is the structural fact that makes the residue classes into a ring. The quotient group Z/nZ\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} (the integers modulo nn) inherits well-defined addition and multiplication from Z\mathbb{Z}, forming a commutative ring. When nn is prime, this ring is a field — every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse — because a prime cannot divide a product without dividing one of the factors.

Modular arithmetic has applications far beyond pure number theory. Cryptography relies on it — the RSA algorithm works in Z/nZ\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} where nn is a product of two large primes. Computer science uses it for hash functions, error-correcting codes, and pseudorandom number generation. Calendar calculations, ISBN check digits, and bank routing number verification all use congruence arithmetic.
