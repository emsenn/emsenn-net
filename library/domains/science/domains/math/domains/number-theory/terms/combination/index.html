Combination

2026-03-05

A combination is an unordered selection of objects from a collection — a choice of which objects to include, without regard to the order in which they are chosen. From the letters A, B, C, D, the 3-letter combinations are {A,B,C}, {A,B,D}, {A,C,D}, and {B,C,D} — four in total. The arrangement ABC and the arrangement CBA are different permutations but the same combination, because they involve the same three letters.

The number of ways to choose kk objects from nn is the binomial coefficient (nk)=n!k!(n−k)!\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}. The formula comes from a simple overcounting argument: the number of kk-permutations of nn objects is n!(n−k)!\frac{n!}{(n-k)!} (you choose kk objects and arrange them). But each combination corresponds to k!k! permutations (all possible orderings of the same kk objects). Dividing the number of permutations by k!k! eliminates the duplicate orderings and gives the number of combinations.

The distinction between combinations and permutations is the distinction between “does order matter?” In a committee of three people, it does not — the committee {Alice, Bob, Carol} is the same regardless of the order you list them. That is a combination problem. In a president-vice-president-secretary assignment, it does — Alice as president is different from Alice as secretary. That is a permutation problem. Recognizing which type of problem you face is the first step in any counting argument.

Combinations arise throughout probability and statistics. The number of ways to get exactly 3 heads in 10 coin flips is (103)=120\binom{10}{3} = 120 — you are choosing which 3 of the 10 flips come up heads, without caring about the order. The binomial distribution, which gives the probability of exactly kk successes in nn independent trials, uses (nk)\binom{n}{k} as its combinatorial factor.

Blaise Pascal developed the systematic theory of combinations in Traité du Triangle Arithmétique (published posthumously, 1665), building on his correspondence with Pierre de Fermat about games of chance (1654). The arithmetic triangle of binomial coefficients was known much earlier — to Yang Hui in China (c. 1261), al-Karaji in Persia (c. 1000 CE), and Bhaskara II in India (Lilavati, 1150 CE) — but Pascal was the first to develop its combinatorial and probabilistic implications systematically.
