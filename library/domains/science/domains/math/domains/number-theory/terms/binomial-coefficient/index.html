Binomial Coefficient

2026-03-05 The binomial coefficient $inom{n}{k}$ (read "$n$ choose $k$") counts the number of ways to choose $k$ objects from $n$ without regard to order — the number of [combinations](./combination.md). It is defined as $inom{n}{k} = rac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$ for $0 leq k leq n$. For example, $inom{5}{2} = rac{5!}{2! cdot 3!} = rac{120}{2 cdot 6} = 10$: there are 10 ways to choose 2 objects from 5.

The binomial coefficient (nk)\binom{n}{k} (read “nn choose kk”) counts the number of ways to choose kk objects from nn without regard to order — the number of combinations. It is defined as (nk)=n!k!(n−k)!\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} for 0≤k≤n0 \leq k \leq n. For example, (52)=5!2!⋅3!=1202⋅6=10\binom{5}{2} = \frac{5!}{2! \cdot 3!} = \frac{120}{2 \cdot 6} = 10: there are 10 ways to choose 2 objects from 5.

The binomial coefficients have a beautiful recursive structure: (nk)=(n−1k−1)+(n−1k)\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k}. This says that each element either is or is not in the chosen subset — if it is in, you need to choose k−1k-1 more from the remaining n−1n-1; if it is not, you need to choose all kk from the remaining n−1n-1. This recurrence generates Pascal’s triangle, where each entry is the sum of the two entries above it:

1111211331\begin{array}{ccccccc} & & & 1 & & & \\ & & 1 & & 1 & & \\ & 1 & & 2 & & 1 & \\ 1 & & 3 & & 3 & & 1 \\ \end{array}

The triangle was known to Yang Hui in China (c. 1261), al-Karaji in Persia (c. 1000 CE), and Bhaskara II in India (c. 1150 CE), centuries before Blaise Pascal studied its properties systematically in Traité du Triangle Arithmétique (published posthumously, 1665). Pascal’s contribution was not the triangle itself but the depth of his analysis: he proved the recurrence, derived combinatorial identities, and connected the triangle to problems in probability.

The binomial coefficients earn their name from the binomial theorem: (x+y)n=∑k=0n(nk)xkyn−k(x + y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^k y^{n-k}. Expanding (x+y)3(x + y)^3, for instance, gives x3+3x2y+3xy2+y3x^3 + 3x^2y + 3xy^2 + y^3, with coefficients 1, 3, 3, 1 — the fourth row of Pascal’s triangle. The binomial theorem was known in special cases for centuries (Isaac Newton generalized it to non-integer exponents in 1665), and the binomial coefficients are its structural backbone.

The symmetry (nk)=(nn−k)\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k} has a clean combinatorial meaning: choosing kk elements to include is the same as choosing n−kn-k elements to exclude. The identity ∑k=0n(nk)=2n\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} = 2^n says that the total number of subsets of an nn-element set is 2n2^n — each element is independently in or out, giving 2 choices per element.

Binomial coefficients appear throughout mathematics and its applications: in the binomial distribution of probability theory, in the expansion of polynomials, in counting lattice paths, and in the combinatorial identities that underpin discrete mathematics.
