Graph

2026-03-05

A graph is a mathematical structure consisting of objects (called vertices or nodes) and connections between them (called edges). Draw a dot for each vertex and a line for each edge, and you have a picture of a graph. Graphs formalize any situation where things are related to other things: cities connected by roads, people who know each other, web pages linked by hyperlinks, atoms bonded in a molecule.

Formally, a graph G=(V,E)G = (V, E) consists of a set VV of vertices and a set EE of edges. In an undirected graph, each edge is an unordered pair {u,v}\{u, v\} — the connection between uu and vv runs both ways. In a directed graph (or digraph), each edge is an ordered pair (u,v)(u, v) — the connection runs from uu to vv but not necessarily back. A graph with no multiple edges between the same pair of vertices and no edges from a vertex to itself is called simple.

Graph theory was born from a recreational puzzle. In 1736, Leonhard Euler published “Solutio problematis ad geometriam situs pertinentis,” analyzing the Seven Bridges of Königsberg: is it possible to walk through the city crossing each of its seven bridges exactly once? Euler proved it is not, by reducing the problem to its essential structure — four land masses (vertices) connected by seven bridges (edges) — and showing that such a walk requires each vertex to have an even number of edges, which Königsberg’s graph does not satisfy. This paper founded graph theory and, more broadly, the field of topology (which Euler called “geometria situs,” the geometry of position).

Graphs formalize relations between discrete objects. A symmetric binary relation on a set corresponds exactly to an undirected graph; a general binary relation corresponds to a directed graph. This correspondence makes graph theory the natural language for networks of all kinds: social networks, communication networks, transportation networks, dependency graphs in software, and metabolic pathways in biology.

In category theory, a directed graph (also called a quiver) is a pair of functions s,t:E→Vs, t: E \to V assigning a source and target vertex to each edge. A category can be seen as a graph enriched with composition (a way to chain edges) and identity edges (a self-loop at every vertex). This perspective makes the graph the simplest precursor to a category — a category is a graph where you can compose paths.
