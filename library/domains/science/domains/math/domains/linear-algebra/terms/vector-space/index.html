Vector Space

2026-03-05

A vector space is a collection of objects — called vectors — that can be added together and scaled by numbers from a field, in a way that behaves the way addition and scaling ought to behave. The field supplies the scalars: these might be the real numbers, the complex numbers, or any other field. The vectors themselves might be arrows in space, columns of numbers, functions, polynomials, or any of a hundred other things. What makes them a vector space is not what they are, but how they combine.

The formal requirements are straightforward. The set VV must form an abelian group under addition: addition is associative and commutative, there is a zero vector 0\mathbf{0}, and every vector has an additive inverse. Scalar multiplication F×V→VF \times V \to V must distribute over both vector addition and field addition, must be compatible with field multiplication (meaning a(bv)=(ab)va(b\mathbf{v}) = (ab)\mathbf{v}), and must satisfy 1⋅v=v1 \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}. These eight axioms are all it takes.

The most familiar example is Rn\mathbb{R}^n — the space of ordered nn-tuples of real numbers, with componentwise addition and scaling. When n=2n = 2, this is the plane; when n=3n = 3, ordinary three-dimensional space. But the concept reaches far beyond geometry. The set of all polynomials with real coefficients forms an infinite-dimensional real vector space. The set of all continuous functions from [0,1][0, 1] to R\mathbb{R} forms another. These examples are what give the abstraction its power: by identifying the shared structure, results proved once for “any vector space” apply to arrows, number columns, polynomials, and function spaces simultaneously.

The central structural fact about vector spaces is that every vector space has a basis — a set of vectors from which every other vector can be built by taking linear combinations (adding scaled copies). The number of vectors in any basis is the same, regardless of which basis you choose. This number is the dimension of the space. Dimension completely determines a finite-dimensional vector space up to isomorphism: any two vector spaces over the same field with the same dimension are structurally identical. R3\mathbb{R}^3 and the space of real polynomials of degree at most 2 are both three-dimensional real vector spaces, and are therefore isomorphic — they have exactly the same algebraic structure, just wearing different clothes.

Hermann Grassmann first described the core ideas in Die lineale Ausdehnungslehre (1844), working with what he called “extensive magnitudes.” His work was largely ignored for decades. Giuseppe Peano gave the first explicitly axiomatic definition in Calcolo geometrico secondo l’Ausdehnungslehre di H. Grassmann (1888), listing the axioms we still use today and crediting Grassmann directly. The definition became standard through the work of Hermann Weyl, Stefan Banach, and Bartel Leendert van der Waerden in the 1920s and 1930s.

Linear maps — functions between vector spaces that respect addition and scaling — are the natural structure-preserving maps. Every linear map from a finite-dimensional space to another can be represented by a matrix, and composition of linear maps corresponds to matrix multiplication. This correspondence is why linear algebra is simultaneously the theory of vector spaces and the theory of systems of linear equations: solving a system Ax=bA\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} is the same as asking which vectors map to b\mathbf{b} under the linear map represented by AA.

When the scalars come from a ring rather than a field, the resulting structure is called a module. Modules are more general and significantly less well-behaved — they need not have bases, and their structure theory is much more complicated.
