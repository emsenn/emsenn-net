Eigenvalue

2026-03-05

Most linear maps scramble directions — they stretch space one way, compress it another, and rotate things sideways. But along certain special directions, a linear map does something simple: it just scales. An eigenvalue is the scaling factor along one of these special directions, and the direction itself is called an eigenvector.

Formally, if T:V→VT: V \to V is a linear map on a vector space VV, then a scalar λ\lambda is an eigenvalue of TT if there exists a nonzero vector v\mathbf{v} such that T(v)=λvT(\mathbf{v}) = \lambda \mathbf{v}. The vector v\mathbf{v} is an eigenvector corresponding to λ\lambda. The equation says that TT acts on v\mathbf{v} by pure scaling — it sends v\mathbf{v} to λ\lambda times itself, without changing its direction. When λ>1\lambda > 1, the map stretches along that direction. When 0<λ<10 < \lambda < 1, it compresses. When λ<0\lambda < 0, it reverses direction (and scales). When λ=0\lambda = 0, the map collapses that direction entirely.

Consider a concrete example. A 2×22 \times 2 matrix represents a linear map on R2\mathbb{R}^2. The matrix (2103)\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix} has eigenvalues 2 and 3. Along the eigenvector (1,0)(1, 0), it stretches by a factor of 2. Along the eigenvector (1,1)(1, 1), it stretches by a factor of 3. Every other vector gets both stretched and rotated — but these two directions are the “grain” of the transformation, the natural axes along which it acts simply.

To find eigenvalues, you solve the characteristic polynomial det⁡(A−λI)=0\det(A - \lambda I) = 0, where AA is the matrix of the linear map and II is the identity matrix. This is a polynomial of degree nn (where nn is the dimension of the space), so there are at most nn eigenvalues counted with multiplicity. The characteristic polynomial connects eigenvalues to the determinant — the determinant of AA is the product of all eigenvalues, and the trace (sum of diagonal entries) is their sum.

The concept traces to Joseph-Louis Lagrange’s analysis of rigid body rotation in Mécanique Analytique (1788) and Augustin-Louis Cauchy’s work on quadratic forms in the 1820s. The term “Eigenwert” (own value, characteristic value) was coined by David Hilbert in Grundzüge einer allgemeinen Theorie der linearen Integralgleichungen (1904–1910), where he studied the spectral properties of integral operators. John William Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh, connected eigenvalues to physical vibrating systems in The Theory of Sound (1877) — the natural frequencies of a vibrating string or drumhead are eigenvalues of a differential operator.

This physical connection is why eigenvalues matter far beyond pure mathematics. In quantum mechanics, the observable values of a physical quantity are eigenvalues of the corresponding operator. In data science, principal component analysis finds the eigenvectors of a covariance matrix to identify the directions of greatest variation. In engineering, the eigenvalues of a system matrix determine whether the system is stable, oscillatory, or divergent. The eigenvectors are the natural coordinates of the system, and the eigenvalues tell you what happens along each one.
