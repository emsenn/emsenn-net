Vector

2026-03-05

A vector is an element of a vector space — anything that can be added to others of its kind and scaled by numbers from a field. The word brings to mind arrows in space: a direction and a magnitude, drawn as a line segment with a pointed tip. That image is useful but narrow. Arrows in two- or three-dimensional space are vectors, but so are columns of numbers, polynomials, continuous functions, and sequences of real numbers. What makes something a vector is not what it looks like, but what you can do with it.

In Rn\mathbb{R}^n — the most concrete setting — a vector is an ordered nn-tuple of real numbers: (3,−1,4)(3, -1, 4) is a vector in R3\mathbb{R}^3. Addition is componentwise: (3,−1,4)+(1,2,−1)=(4,1,3)(3, -1, 4) + (1, 2, -1) = (4, 1, 3). Scalar multiplication scales every component: 2⋅(3,−1,4)=(6,−2,8)2 \cdot (3, -1, 4) = (6, -2, 8). You can visualize these as arrows from the origin — adding two vectors places one arrow at the tip of the other (the parallelogram rule), and scaling a vector stretches or shrinks the arrow.

But the same operations work for polynomials. The polynomials 3x2+x−13x^2 + x - 1 and x2−2x+5x^2 - 2x + 5 can be added: 4x2−x+44x^2 - x + 4. You can scale by a real number: 2(3x2+x−1)=6x2+2x−22(3x^2 + x - 1) = 6x^2 + 2x - 2. These operations satisfy all the vector space axioms, so polynomials are vectors in the vector space of polynomials. Similarly, continuous functions from [0,1][0, 1] to R\mathbb{R} form a vector space — you add functions pointwise and scale them pointwise — making each function a vector.

The distinction between “vector” and “scalar” is relational, not intrinsic. A real number is a scalar when it multiplies a vector, but the real numbers themselves form a one-dimensional vector space over R\mathbb{R}, in which they are vectors. A 2×22 \times 2 matrix is a vector in the space of all 2×22 \times 2 matrices (you can add matrices and scale them), and simultaneously a representation of a linear map that acts on other vectors. Context determines which role an object plays.

The term “vector” comes from the Latin vehere (to carry), introduced in its mathematical sense by William Rowan Hamilton in the 1840s as part of his quaternion theory. Hamilton distinguished between the “scalar part” and the “vector part” of a quaternion. Josiah Willard Gibbs and Oliver Heaviside extracted the vector concept from quaternions in the 1880s and 1890s, developing the three-dimensional vector analysis still used in physics and engineering. The modern abstract sense — a vector as any element of any vector space — emerged through the axiomatization work of Giuseppe Peano and Hermann Grassmann, and became standard in the early twentieth century.
