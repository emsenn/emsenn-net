A matrix is a rectangular grid of numbers (or more generally, elements of a field), arranged in rows and columns. An m×nm \times n matrix has mm rows and nn columns. You write the entry in the iith row and jjth column as aija_{ij}. A 2×32 \times 3 matrix looks like (123456)\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix} — two rows, three columns, six entries.

Matrices are useful because they represent linear maps. Once you choose a basis for each vector space involved, any linear map T:V→WT: V \to W between finite-dimensional spaces can be written as a matrix — the columns of the matrix are the images of the basis vectors, expressed in coordinates. This representation converts the abstract question “what does this linear map do?” into the concrete question “what numbers are in this grid?” Applying the map to a vector becomes multiplying the matrix by a column of numbers.

Matrix multiplication encodes composition of linear maps. If AA represents the map TT and BB represents SS, then ABAB represents T∘ST \circ S (apply SS first, then TT). The rule for computing ABAB — the (i,j)(i, j) entry is the dot product of the iith row of AA with the jjth column of BB — follows directly from this correspondence. Matrix multiplication is associative (because composition is) but generally not commutative: AB≠BAAB \neq BA in most cases, because applying TT then SS is not the same as applying SS then TT.

A square matrix (where m=nm = n) represents a linear map from a space to itself. The set of all n×nn \times n matrices over a field FF forms a ring under addition and multiplication — one of the most important non-commutative rings in mathematics. A square matrix is invertible (has a two-sided inverse) if and only if its determinant is nonzero, which is equivalent to saying the corresponding linear map is bijective.

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz used rectangular arrays of coefficients to study systems of linear equations in letters to Guillaume de l’Hôpital in 1693, and Gabriel Cramer published his rule for solving such systems using determinants in Introduction à l’analyse des lignes courbes algébriques (1750). But the matrix as an algebraic object in its own right — something you could add, multiply, and reason about abstractly — was the work of James Joseph Sylvester, who coined the word “matrix” in 1850, and Arthur Cayley, who developed matrix algebra systematically in A Memoir on the Theory of Matrices (1858). Cayley proved what is now called the Cayley-Hamilton theorem: every square matrix satisfies its own characteristic polynomial.

Matrices are everywhere. In computer graphics, rotations, reflections, and projections are matrix multiplications. In statistics, the covariance structure of a dataset is a matrix. In physics, quantum states evolve by multiplication by unitary matrices. In economics, input-output models represent an entire economy as a matrix of production coefficients. The power of the matrix concept is that all these different domains share the same underlying algebra — once you know how matrices behave, you can reason about any system they model.
