What Is a Lattice?

2026-03-30
Abstract

An introduction to lattices for readers who understand 'greater than' and 'less than' but haven't seen the algebraic structure.

A partial order lets you compare things. A lattice guarantees that comparisons always produce results.

Given two elements aa and bb in a partial order, you can ask: what is the most you can say that is true of both? And: what is the least you can say that covers both? In a lattice, these questions always have answers.

The meet of aa and bb (written a∧ba \wedge b) is their greatest lower bound: the largest element that is below both aa and bb. Think of it as “what aa and bb agree on.”

The join of aa and bb (written a∨ba \vee b) is their least upper bound: the smallest element that is above both. Think of it as “the least thing that accounts for both aa and bb.”

A lattice is a partial order where every pair of elements has a meet and a join.

Consider sets ordered by inclusion again. The meet of {1,2,3}\{1, 2, 3\} and {2,3,4}\{2, 3, 4\} is their intersection {2,3}\{2, 3\} — the largest set contained in both. The join is their union {1,2,3,4}\{1, 2, 3, 4\} — the smallest set containing both. Every pair of sets has an intersection and a union, so sets under inclusion form a lattice.

A lattice has a top element ⊤\top if there is something above everything: a≤⊤a \leq \top for all aa. And a bottom element ⊥\bot if there is something below everything: ⊥≤a\bot \leq a for all aa. A lattice with both is bounded.

A lattice is distributive if meet and join interact nicely: a∧(b∨c)=(a∧b)∨(a∧c)a \wedge (b \vee c) = (a \wedge b) \vee (a \wedge c). This says: taking the meet with aa distributes over a join, the same way multiplication distributes over addition in arithmetic. Most lattices you encounter in practice are distributive.

A lattice is complete if meets and joins exist not just for pairs but for ALL subsets, including infinite ones. The power set of any set (all subsets, ordered by inclusion) is a complete lattice.

Lattices appear everywhere: types in a programming language ordered by subtyping form a lattice. Propositions ordered by entailment form a lattice. Concepts ordered by generality form a lattice. Any time you have a collection of things with a natural “more specific / more general” ordering, and the ordering behaves well enough that “most specific common generalization” and “most general common specialization” always exist, you have a lattice.

The step from a lattice to a residuated lattice adds a product operation — a way to COMBINE elements, not just compare them — and that step is where the algebra becomes capable of modeling predication.
