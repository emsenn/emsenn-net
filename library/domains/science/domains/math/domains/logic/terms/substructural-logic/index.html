2026-03-30 A family of logics that weaken or drop structural rules (contraction, weakening, exchange) of classical logic. Includes linear logic, relevance logic, and the Lambek calculus.

A substructural logic is a logic that restricts one or more of the structural rules assumed in classical logic:

  • Contraction (A,A⊢BA, A \vdash B implies A⊢BA \vdash B): a hypothesis can be used more than once. Dropping this gives resource sensitivity.
  • Weakening (A⊢BA \vdash B implies A,C⊢BA, C \vdash B): unused hypotheses are harmless. Dropping this gives relevance.
  • Exchange (A,B⊢CA, B \vdash C implies B,A⊢CB, A \vdash C): order doesn’t matter. Dropping this gives non-commutativity.

Linear logic drops contraction and weakening: each hypothesis is used exactly once. The Lambek calculus drops all three: hypotheses are ordered, used exactly once, and cannot be reordered.

Galatos, Jipsen, Kowalski, & Ono (2007) proved that the algebraic semantics of substructural logics are residuated lattices. Each structural rule corresponds to an algebraic condition: contraction to idempotence of the product, weakening to integrality, exchange to commutativity.
