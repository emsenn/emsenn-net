Connective

2025-12-26

A connective is a logical operation that builds compound propositions from simpler ones. “It is raining and it is cold” combines two propositions using the connective and. “If it rains, then the ground is wet” uses the connective if-then. “It is not raining” uses negation. Connectives are the glue of logical reasoning — they determine how the truth of a compound statement depends on the truth of its parts.

The standard connectives are five:

  • Conjunction (∧\wedge, “and”): a∧ba \wedge b is true when both aa and bb are true.
  • Disjunction (∨\vee, “or”): a∨ba \vee b is true when at least one of aa or bb is true.
  • Implication (→\to, “if…then”): a→ba \to b is true when aa being true guarantees bb is true.
  • Negation (¬\neg, “not”): ¬a\neg a is true when aa is false.
  • Biconditional (↔\leftrightarrow, “if and only if”): a↔ba \leftrightarrow b is true when aa and bb have the same truth value.

George Boole was the first to treat these operations algebraically, representing propositions as elements of an algebra and connectives as algebraic operations in An Investigation of the Laws of Thought (1854). Augustus De Morgan established the laws that bear his name, relating negation to conjunction and disjunction: ¬(a∧b)=¬a∨¬b\neg(a \wedge b) = \neg a \vee \neg b and ¬(a∨b)=¬a∧¬b\neg(a \vee b) = \neg a \wedge \neg b. Gottlob Frege introduced a fully formalized notation for connectives, including the conditional, in Begriffsschrift (1879) — the first formal logical language capable of expressing all of mathematics.

In a Heyting algebra HH, the connectives have precise algebraic definitions. Conjunction is the binary meet: a∧b=inf⁡{a,b}a \wedge b = \inf\{a, b\}. Disjunction is the binary join: a∨b=sup⁡{a,b}a \vee b = \sup\{a, b\}. Implication is defined as a→b=max⁡{c∈H:a∧c≤b}a \to b = \max\{c \in H : a \wedge c \leq b\} — the largest element that, combined with aa, does not exceed bb. Negation is ¬a=a→⊥\neg a = a \to \bot. The biconditional is (a→b)∧(b→a)(a \to b) \wedge (b \to a).

The central structural fact is that conjunction and implication form an adjunction: c≤(a→b)c \leq (a \to b) if and only if a∧c≤ba \wedge c \leq b. This residuation property is what defines the Heyting implication and governs how logical deduction works within the algebra.

In classical logic — where the underlying algebra is Boolean and ¬¬a=a\neg\neg a = a for all aa — implication simplifies to a→b=¬a∨ba \to b = \neg a \vee b, and the law of excluded middle a∨¬a=⊤a \vee \neg a = \top holds universally. But in intuitionistic logic, where the algebra may be non-Boolean, double negation does not collapse and excluded middle can fail. The connectives take on a constructive reading: a proof of a∧ba \wedge b is a pair of proofs, a proof of a∨ba \vee b is a proof of one specific disjunct (you must know which), and a proof of a→ba \to b is a method that transforms any proof of aa into a proof of bb.

In the open-set lattice O(X)\mathcal{O}(X) of a topological space XX, the connectives become geometric operations: U∧V=U∩VU \wedge V = U \cap V, U∨V=U∪VU \vee V = U \cup V, U→V=int(Uc∪V)U \to V = \mathrm{int}(U^c \cup V), and ¬U=int(Uc)\neg U = \mathrm{int}(U^c). This is the concrete setting where intuitionistic logic lives inside topology.
