2026-03-30 A strong implication on bilattices incorporating both direct and contrapositive reasoning: x ⇒_N y = (x ⊃ y) ∧ (∼y ⊃ ∼x).

The Nelson implication is an implication operation on bilattices and twist products that incorporates both direct and contrapositive reasoning simultaneously:

x⇒Ny=(x⊃y)∧(∼y⊃∼x)x \Rightarrow_N y = (x \supset y) \wedge (\mathord{\sim} y \supset \mathord{\sim} x)

where ⊃\supset is the weak (Heyting) implication and ∼\sim is negation.

Chajda & Langer (2021) established the Nelson implication as a second adjoint pair on twist products, alongside the Busaniche-Cignoli residuation. It satisfies the Nelson identity:

(x⇒(x⇒y))∧k(∼y⇒(∼y⇒∼x))=x⇒y(x \Rightarrow (x \Rightarrow y)) \wedge_k (\mathord{\sim} y \Rightarrow (\mathord{\sim} y \Rightarrow \mathord{\sim} x)) = x \Rightarrow y

On the four-valued bilattice 2⊗2\mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2}, the simple contrapositive fails: ∼(a⊙b)≠∼b⇒∼a\mathord{\sim}(a \odot b) \neq \mathord{\sim} b \Rightarrow \mathord{\sim} a in general. The Nelson form — which tests both the direct implication and the contrapositive at once — is sound. Any modal calculus on bilattice-valued fibers must use the Nelson implication for negation-aware reasoning, not the naive contrapositive.
