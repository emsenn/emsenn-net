2026-03-30 A substructural logic formalizing how predicates combine with arguments, equivalent to the logic of residuated lattices.

The Lambek calculus (Lambek 1958) formalizes how a functor — a predicate, a verb, any expression that takes arguments — combines with its arguments to produce a complete expression.

A product operation ⋅\cdot combines two expressions. The calculus introduces two residuals:

a⋅b≤ciffb≤a\ciffa≤c/ba \cdot b \leq c \quad\text{iff}\quad b \leq a \backslash c \quad\text{iff}\quad a \leq c / b

The left residual a\ca \backslash c asks: what must combine with aa on the right to yield something below cc? The right residual c/bc / b asks: what must combine with bb on the left? These two residuals exhaust the modes of functor-argument interaction. The product has two argument positions, so there are exactly two residuals.

Galatos, Jipsen, Kowalski, & Ono (2007) proved the Lambek calculus is the logic of residuated lattices. The // and \\backslash of categorial grammar are residuals in the algebraic sense, and the full theory of residuated lattices applies to predicate-argument combination.

In the commutative case (a⋅b=b⋅aa \cdot b = b \cdot a), the two residuals coincide into a single implication →\to. The adjunction retains two sides: the product side (combining, generating) and the implication side (testing, constraining).

Lawvere (1969) identified the same adjunction in categorical logic: existential quantification ∃\exists (left adjoint to substitution) and universal quantification ∀\forall (right adjoint) are the categorical form of the two residuals.

Wurm (2012) proved that syntactic concept lattices — concept lattices arising from distributional data — give complete semantics for the full Lambek calculus.

Pentus (1993) proved Lambek grammars generate exactly the context-free languages.
