Necessity

2025-12-26

Necessity is the modality of what must be the case — what holds not just as things happen to be, but in every relevant way things could be. “2 + 2 = 4” is necessarily true: there is no possible world in which it fails. “It is raining in London” is contingently true (or false): it depends on circumstances that could have been otherwise. The concept of necessity, and its dual possibility, are the central subjects of modal logic.

Aristotle discussed necessity and contingency in De Interpretatione, most famously in the sea-battle problem: is it necessarily true or necessarily false now that there will be a sea battle tomorrow? Aristotle argued that future contingents are neither, raising questions about the scope of necessity that philosophers have debated ever since. The modern formal treatment begins with Clarence Irving Lewis, who introduced the strict conditional and the hierarchy of modal systems S1 through S5 in Symbolic Logic (1932, with Cooper Harold Langford). But Lewis’s systems lacked a clear semantics — you could prove things in them without knowing exactly what the symbols meant. Saul Aaron Kripke provided that semantics in 1963.

In a Kripke model (W,R,V)(W, R, V), necessity has a precise definition: □φ\Box\varphi (read “necessarily φ\varphi”) holds at a world ww if and only if φ\varphi holds at every world accessible from ww:

M,w⊨□φiff∀v (wRv⇒M,v⊨φ).M, w \models \Box\varphi \quad \text{iff} \quad \forall v\,(wRv \Rightarrow M, v \models \varphi).

The accessibility relation RR determines what “every relevant alternative” means. If RR is reflexive, then □φ→φ\Box\varphi \to \varphi holds (the T axiom): if something is necessary, it is actually true, because the current world is among the accessible ones. In the system S5, where RR is an equivalence relation, □φ\Box\varphi holds at ww if and only if φ\varphi holds throughout ww’s entire equivalence class — necessity is absolute within each cluster of worlds.

Necessity and possibility are duals: □φ=¬◊¬φ\Box\varphi = \neg\Diamond\neg\varphi. A proposition is necessary if and only if its negation is not possible. This duality mirrors the relationship between universal and existential quantification — □\Box is “for all accessible worlds” and ◊\Diamond is “there exists an accessible world.”

In the algebraic setting, necessity corresponds to a closure operator j:H→Hj: H \to H on a Heyting algebra HH (extensive, monotone, and idempotent). A proposition aa is necessary when j(a)=aj(a) = a — it is already jj-closed and necessitation adds nothing to it. The set of necessary elements Hj={a∈H:j(a)=a}H^j = \{a \in H : j(a) = a\} is closed under conjunction and contains ⊤\top. When jj is a nucleus (meaning j(a∧b)=j(a)∧j(b)j(a \wedge b) = j(a) \wedge j(b)), the necessary elements themselves form a Heyting algebra with implication a⇒jb=j(a→b)a \Rightarrow_j b = j(a \to b).

This algebraic perspective connects modal logic to topology. On the open-set lattice O(X)\mathcal{O}(X) of a topological space, a Lawvere-Tierney topology jj defines a nontrivial modal system on the subobject classifier — the necessity operator becomes a topological closure operation, and the necessary propositions are those that survive closure.
