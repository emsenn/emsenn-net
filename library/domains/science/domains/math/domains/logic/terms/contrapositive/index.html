2026-03-30 The logical equivalence p → q ≡ ¬q → ¬p. Holds in classical logic; fails on bilattices without the Nelson form.

The contrapositive of an implication p→qp \to q is ¬q→¬p\neg q \to \neg p. In classical logic, these are equivalent: proving p→qp \to q is the same as proving ¬q→¬p\neg q \to \neg p.

On the four-valued bilattice, the simple contrapositive fails: ∼(a⊙b)≠∼b⇒∼a\mathord{\sim}(a \odot b) \neq \mathord{\sim} b \Rightarrow \mathord{\sim} a in general. Negation does not commute with the residuated operations.

The Nelson implication repairs this by incorporating both the direct and contrapositive simultaneously: x⇒Ny=(x⊃y)∧(∼y⊃∼x)x \Rightarrow_N y = (x \supset y) \wedge (\mathord{\sim} y \supset \mathord{\sim} x). The Nelson identity holds on the bilattice even though the simple contrapositive does not. Negation-aware iterated implication is sound; naive contrapositive is not.
