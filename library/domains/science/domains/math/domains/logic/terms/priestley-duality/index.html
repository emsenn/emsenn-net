2026-03-30 A dual equivalence between distributive lattices and ordered compact topological spaces (Priestley spaces), giving every lattice a geometric dual.

Priestley duality (Priestley 1970) is a dual equivalence between the category of bounded distributive lattices and the category of Priestley spaces — compact topological spaces equipped with a compatible partial order.

Every bounded distributive lattice LL has a Priestley dual: the space of prime filters of LL, topologized and ordered. Every Priestley space determines a lattice (its clopen upsets). These constructions are inverse to each other up to isomorphism.

The duality gives every lattice a geometric dual. Algebraic properties of the lattice translate to topological properties of the space and vice versa.

For bilattices: the Priestley dual of a distributive bilattice is the coproduct (disjoint union) of the Priestley duals of its two lattice components (Mobasher, Pigozzi, Slutzki, & Voutsadakis 2000). For the four-valued bilattice 2⊗2\mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2}, both components are the two-element chain, whose dual is a single point. The Priestley dual of 2⊗2\mathbf{2} \otimes \mathbf{2} is a discrete two-point space.

Jung & Rivieccio (2012) refined the dual for bilattices with negation: the dual space carries an order-reversing homeomorphism encoding negation as a topological involution. The two points are connected by the negation swap.
