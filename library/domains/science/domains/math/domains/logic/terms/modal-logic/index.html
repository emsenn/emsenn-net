2026-03-30 A family of logics extending classical logic with operators for necessity (□) and possibility (◇), interpreted over relational structures.

Modal logic extends classical logic with two operators: necessity □\Box (“it must be the case that”) and possibility ◊\Diamond (“it could be the case that”). These are dual: □p=¬◊¬p\Box p = \neg \Diamond \neg p.

The standard semantics is Kripke semantics (Kripke 1963): a model consists of a set of worlds WW, an accessibility relation RR on WW, and a valuation assigning truth values to propositions at each world. □p\Box p holds at world ww iff pp holds at every world accessible from ww. ◊p\Diamond p holds at ww iff pp holds at some accessible world.

Different conditions on RR yield different modal logics. Reflexive RR gives the logic T. Reflexive and transitive gives S4. Equivalence relation gives S5. These are all well-studied with known axiomatizations and completeness theorems.

On the four-valued bilattice, Rivieccio, Jung, & Jansana (2017) proved that modal operators decompose into pairs of classical operators on the twist product components:

□(a+,a−)=(□1(a+), ◊2(a−))\Box(a^+, a^-) = (\Box_1(a^+),\ \Diamond_2(a^-))

◊(a+,a−)=(◊1(a+), □2(a−))\Diamond(a^+, a^-) = (\Diamond_1(a^+),\ \Box_2(a^-))

The resulting four-valued modal logic has the finite model property and is decidable.

A logic is decidable if there exists an algorithm that determines, in finite time, whether any given formula is valid. Decidability means the logic can be mechanized.
