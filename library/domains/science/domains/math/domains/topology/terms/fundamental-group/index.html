Fundamental Group

2025-12-26

The fundamental group is an algebraic snapshot of the “holes” in a topological space. It answers a basic geometric question: if you walk in a loop, starting and ending at the same point, can you continuously shrink that loop down to a single point, or does the shape of the space force the loop to wrap around something? Loops that can be shrunk to a point are trivial. Loops that cannot are evidence of a hole. The fundamental group collects and organizes all the essentially different loops through a point.

Henri Poincaré introduced the fundamental group in Analysis Situs (1895), making it the first algebraic invariant of a topological space and launching the field of algebraic topology. His insight was that topology — the study of shape — could be translated into algebra, where computation and classification become possible.

Formally, fix a topological space XX and a basepoint x0∈Xx_0 \in X. A loop at x0x_0 is a continuous path γ:[0,1]→X\gamma: [0,1] \to X with γ(0)=γ(1)=x0\gamma(0) = \gamma(1) = x_0 — it starts and ends at the same point. Two loops are considered “the same” if one can be continuously deformed into the other while keeping the endpoints fixed — that is, if they are homotopic relative to {0,1}\{0, 1\}. The homotopy class [γ][\gamma] represents all loops that can be deformed into γ\gamma.

The fundamental group π1(X,x0)\pi_1(X, x_0) is the set of all such homotopy classes, with the group operation being concatenation: [γ]⋅[δ]=[γ∗δ][\gamma] \cdot [\delta] = [\gamma * \delta], where you walk the loop γ\gamma in the first half of the time interval and δ\delta in the second half. The identity element is the constant loop (stay at x0x_0 forever), and the inverse of [γ][\gamma] is [γˉ][\bar{\gamma}] — walk the same loop backward. Associativity holds up to homotopy: (γ∗δ)∗ϵ(\gamma * \delta) * \epsilon and γ∗(δ∗ϵ)\gamma * (\delta * \epsilon) traverse the same three loops in the same order, just at different speeds, and speed differences can be smoothed out continuously.

The most illuminating example is the circle S1S^1. A loop on the circle can wind around zero times (contractible to a point), once, twice, or any number of times, in either direction. Two loops that wind the same number of times in the same direction can be deformed into each other; loops with different winding numbers cannot. The fundamental group of the circle is therefore Z\mathbb{Z} — the integers — with the winding number as the group element. Addition of integers corresponds to concatenation of loops: a loop that winds twice followed by one that winds three times gives a loop that winds five times.

The torus T2T^2 (the surface of a donut) has fundamental group Z×Z\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}: there are two independent generating loops, one going “around the hole” and one going “through the hole,” and every loop can be expressed as a combination of these. The figure-eight space S1∨S1S^1 \vee S^1 has fundamental group F2F_2, the free group on two generators — here the two loops do not commute, so the order in which you traverse them matters. A sphere SnS^n with n≥2n \geq 2 has trivial fundamental group: every loop on a sphere can be contracted to a point, because there are no one-dimensional holes. A space with trivial fundamental group is called simply connected.

If XX is path-connected, the choice of basepoint does not matter: π1(X,x0)\pi_1(X, x_0) and π1(X,x1)\pi_1(X, x_1) are isomorphic for any two basepoints, via conjugation by a path between them. Moreover, any continuous map f:X→Yf: X \to Y sending x0x_0 to y0y_0 induces a group homomorphism f∗:π1(X,x0)→π1(Y,y0)f_*: \pi_1(X, x_0) \to \pi_1(Y, y_0), and this assignment is functorial: it respects composition and identities. This functoriality is what makes the fundamental group a tool for proving that spaces are not homeomorphic — if their fundamental groups are different, the spaces cannot be topologically the same.

The fundamental group is the case n=1n = 1 of the homotopy groups πn(X,x0)\pi_n(X, x_0), which detect higher-dimensional holes using maps from spheres rather than circles.
