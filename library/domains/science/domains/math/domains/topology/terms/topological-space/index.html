Topological Space

A topological space is a set equipped with a notion of “nearness” — a way of saying which points are close to which, without necessarily measuring exact distances. The idea is to keep the parts of geometry that matter for continuity (can you deform one shape into another without tearing?) while throwing away the parts that don’t (how far apart are these two points, exactly?).

In a metric space — a set where you can measure distances — you already have a notion of nearness: a point is near a set if it can be reached with arbitrarily small steps. But many important spaces (function spaces, quotient spaces, spaces arising in algebraic geometry) don’t come with a natural distance function, and different distance functions on the same set can give the same notion of nearness. The topological space axioms capture nearness directly, without going through distance.

The formal definition works by specifying which subsets count as open sets. A topology on a set XX is a collection τ⊆P(X)\tau \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X) satisfying three axioms: (1) the empty set ∅\emptyset and the whole space XX belong to τ\tau; (2) any union of sets in τ\tau belongs to τ\tau; and (3) any finite intersection of sets in τ\tau belongs to τ\tau. The pair (X,τ)(X, \tau) is a topological space. An open set is one that belongs to τ\tau — a set is open because the topology says it is, not because of any intrinsic property.

This may sound circular, but it is precisely the point. The axioms do not tell you which subsets are open — that is a choice. What the axioms constrain is how open sets must relate to each other: you can always take unions and finite intersections of open sets and get open sets back. Different choices of τ\tau on the same underlying set XX give genuinely different topological spaces with different notions of continuity, convergence, and connectedness.

The most familiar example is the standard topology on R\mathbb{R}, where the open sets are unions of open intervals (a,b)(a, b). This is the metric topology generated by the distance d(x,y)=∣x−y∣d(x, y) = |x - y|: a set UU is open if every point in UU has some room around it — formally, for every x∈Ux \in U, some open ball B(x,ε)={y:∣x−y∣<ε}B(x, \varepsilon) = \{y : |x - y| < \varepsilon\} fits entirely inside UU. Every metric space generates a topology this way.

At the extremes, the discrete topology declares every subset open (so every function out of the space is continuous), while the indiscrete topology declares only ∅\emptyset and XX open (so every function into the space is continuous). Between these extremes lie topologies of real mathematical interest: the cofinite topology (open sets are those whose complements are finite), Zariski topologies in algebraic geometry, and the weak topologies of functional analysis.

Felix Hausdorff gave the first systematic axiomatization in Grundzüge der Mengenlehre (1914), using neighborhood axioms that included a separation condition (now called the Hausdorff or T2 condition). Kazimierz Kuratowski offered an alternative via closure-operator axioms in 1922. The modern open-set formulation — the one given above — was consolidated by the Bourbaki group in Topologie Générale (1940) and is now standard.

The collection τ\tau of open sets, ordered by inclusion, forms a frame: a complete lattice in which finite meets (intersections) distribute over arbitrary joins (unions). Equivalently, τ\tau is a complete Heyting algebra. This algebraic structure is the starting point of pointless topology, where one studies frames (called locales) directly, without reference to an underlying set of points.

Topological spaces and continuous maps between them form the category Top\mathbf{Top}, which admits products, coproducts, subspaces, and quotients. Viewing the open sets of a space as a category (with inclusions as morphisms) is the starting point of sheaf theory: a presheaf assigns data to each open set, and a sheaf is a presheaf whose local data glue uniquely.
