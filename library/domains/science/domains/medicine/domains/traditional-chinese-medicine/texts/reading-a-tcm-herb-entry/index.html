Reading a TCM Herb Entry

2026-04-02
Abstract

A tutorial for readers with no TCM background — how to understand the nature, taste, channel entry, actions, and contraindications that define a Chinese medicinal herb, using Hu Zhang as a worked example

This text teaches you to read and understand a Traditional Chinese Medicine herb entry — the kind of structured description found in the Chinese materia medica and in this repository’s TCM terms. You do not need any prior knowledge of TCM. By the end, you will be able to read the Hu Zhang entry and understand what every field means.

The structure of a TCM herb entry

Every herb in the Chinese materia medica is described by a standard set of characteristics. These are not arbitrary labels — each characteristic answers a specific clinical question:

Characteristic Question it answers
Name What plant is this?
Nature (thermal character) Does this herb warm or cool the body?
Taste What functional effect does this herb have?
Channel entry Where in the body does this herb direct its effects?
Actions What does this herb do?
Indications What conditions is it used for?
Dosage How much is used?
Contraindications When should it NOT be used?

Let’s work through each one using Hu Zhang (虎杖, Japanese knotweed root) as our example.

Nature: slightly cold

Every herb has a thermal nature — an inherent tendency to warm or cool the body. There are five grades:

Hot → Warm → Neutral → Cool → Cold

Hu Zhang is slightly cold (微寒). This means it has a mild cooling effect on the body. It is appropriate for conditions with heat signs (inflammation, redness, fever, burning sensations) and inappropriate for conditions with cold signs (chills, pale complexion, cold limbs, watery discharge).

The practical implication: if you already feel cold and depleted, a cold herb will make you feel worse. If you feel hot and inflamed, a cold herb provides relief. TCM prescribing matches the thermal nature of the herb to the thermal character of the condition — cold herbs for hot patterns, hot herbs for cold patterns.

Taste: bitter

TCM classifies herbs by taste, but “taste” here is a functional category, not a flavor description (though the two often correlate). Each taste has a characteristic therapeutic action:

Taste What it does Think of it as
Pungent Disperses, moves, opens Getting things moving outward
Sweet Tonifies, nourishes, harmonizes Building up what’s depleted
Sour Astringes, consolidates Holding things in
Bitter Drains, dries, descends Moving things down and out
Salty Softens, purges Breaking up hardness

Hu Zhang is bitter. Bitter herbs drain excess, dry dampness, and promote downward movement. This is consistent with Hu Zhang’s use as a mild laxative (downward movement through the bowel) and as a damp-heat clearing herb (draining heat and drying dampness from the Liver/Gallbladder system).

The five tastes correspond to the Five Phases: bitter corresponds to Fire, pungent to Metal, sweet to Earth, sour to Wood, salty to Water. This correspondence is part of TCM’s systematic mapping of phenomena onto the Five Phases framework.

Channel entry: Liver, Gallbladder, Lung

Channel entry tells you where the herb directs its effects. TCM divides the body into organ-function systems connected by meridians (channels). When an herb “enters” a channel, it means the herb’s therapeutic action preferentially targets that organ system.

Hu Zhang enters three channels:

  • Liver: Governs smooth flow of Qi, stores blood, governs tendons, opens to the eyes. Hu Zhang’s blood-invigorating action targets Liver-related conditions — menstrual pain, rib-side pain, eye disorders.
  • Gallbladder: Stores and secretes bile, governs decision-making. Hu Zhang’s damp-heat-clearing action targets the Gallbladder — jaundice, gallstones, bitter taste in the mouth.
  • Lung: Governs Qi and respiration, distributes defensive Qi, regulates water passages. Hu Zhang’s phlegm-resolving action targets the Lung — cough with thick yellow sputum.

If you see a different herb that enters the Kidney and Bladder channels, you know its effects are directed toward the lower body, the urinary system, and the functions TCM associates with the Kidney (reproduction, bone health, aging).

Actions: what the herb does

TCM describes what an herb does through standardized action phrases. Each phrase combines a verb (the type of action) with an object (what the action targets). Hu Zhang has four main actions:

“Invigorates blood and dispels stasis” (活血祛瘀): Restores movement to blood that has become stagnant. Blood stasis is a specific TCM pathological pattern — fixed sharp pain, dark complexion, purple tongue. Hu Zhang gets stagnant blood moving again.

“Clears heat and resolves dampness” (清热利湿): Removes the compound pathological pattern of damp-heat — the sticky, stubborn combination of inflammation (heat) and fluid stagnation (dampness). This is the action that makes Hu Zhang specific for jaundice.

“Clears heat and relieves toxicity” (清热解毒): Addresses acute inflammatory or infectious conditions. Applied topically for burns, sores, and skin infections.

“Resolves phlegm and stops cough” (化痰止咳): Clears thick, hot phlegm from the Lung. Used for cough with yellow sputum.

Each action phrase is a diagnosis in miniature — it implies the pattern the herb treats. “Invigorates blood and dispels stasis” implies the patient has blood stasis. “Clears heat and resolves dampness” implies the patient has damp-heat. The herb is selected when the patient’s pattern matches the herb’s actions.

Contraindications: when NOT to use it

Contraindications are as important as indications. Hu Zhang’s contraindications are:

  • Pregnancy: Blood-moving herbs risk miscarriage. This is not theoretical caution — it reflects centuries of clinical observation.
  • Heavy menstrual bleeding: The herb moves blood and could worsen hemorrhage.
  • Spleen-Stomach cold deficiency: The herb is cold and bitter — it will further damage an already cold, weak digestive system. A patient with chronic diarrhea, cold abdomen, and poor appetite should not take a cold, draining herb without warming herbs to counterbalance.

The contraindications follow logically from the nature, taste, and actions. A cold herb is contraindicated in cold conditions. A blood-moving herb is contraindicated where blood movement could be dangerous.

Putting it together

Now read the full Hu Zhang entry. Every field should make sense:

  • Nature: slightly cold → it cools; use it for heat conditions, not cold ones
  • Taste: bitter → it drains downward and dries; use it for excess and dampness, not deficiency
  • Channel entry: Liver, Gallbladder, Lung → its effects target these organ systems specifically
  • Actions → four specific things it does, each implying a pattern it treats
  • Contraindications → follow logically from its properties

This is how TCM practitioners select herbs: by matching the herb’s profile (nature + taste + channel entry + actions) to the patient’s pattern. The herb is not selected for a disease name. It is selected for a pattern of disharmony — and the materia medica entry tells you exactly which patterns each herb fits.
