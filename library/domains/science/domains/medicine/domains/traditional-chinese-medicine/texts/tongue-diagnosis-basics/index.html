Tongue Diagnosis Basics

2026-04-02 How to read a tongue in TCM — what the body color, shape, coating color, and coating texture reveal about the body's internal state, with a visual reference for the most common findings

Tongue diagnosis is one of TCM’s most distinctive and reliable clinical tools. The tongue is the only internal organ visible from outside the body, and TCM holds that it reflects the state of the internal organ systems, the balance of Qi and Blood, and the presence of pathological factors with a specificity that has no equivalent in Western clinical examination. This guide teaches you the basics — enough to read the common findings that appear in TCM term definitions throughout this repository.

¶How to examine a tongue

Examine the tongue in natural daylight if possible. Artificial lighting alters color perception. Ask the person to open their mouth and extend the tongue naturally — not forced out as far as possible (which distorts shape and color) and not held out too long (which changes the color).

Look at four things in this order:

  1. Body color — the color of the tongue tissue itself
  2. Body shape — the size, thickness, and texture of the tongue
  3. Coating color — the color of the film on the tongue surface
  4. Coating texture — thin or thick, dry or moist, greasy or not

¶Tongue body color

The body color reflects the state of Blood, Qi, and the Yin-Yang balance:

Body color What it indicates
Pale pink Normal. Adequate Qi and Blood, balanced Yin and Yang
Pale / light Qi deficiency or Blood deficiency. Insufficient Blood to fill the tongue
Red Heat. Either excess heat (full, strong redness) or Yin deficiency heat (red with no coating)
Deep red / crimson Severe heat. Heat at the Blood level — a serious pattern
Purple Blood stasis. Blood is not flowing properly. If purple and moist: cold-type stasis. If purple and dry: heat-type stasis
Blue-purple Severe cold or severe stasis. Blood circulation critically impaired

¶Tongue body shape

Shape What it indicates
Normal thickness Normal
Swollen / puffy Dampness or Phlegm accumulation. The retained fluids swell the tongue tissue
Tooth-marked margins The swollen tongue presses against the teeth. Confirms dampness / Spleen Qi deficiency
Thin Blood or Yin deficiency. Insufficient fluid to fill the tongue
Stiff / rigid Heat or Wind affecting the interior. Can indicate stroke risk
Trembling Qi deficiency (too weak to hold the tongue still) or internal Wind
Deviated (points to one side) Internal Wind. Can indicate stroke or pre-stroke

¶Tongue coating color

The coating (tai 苔) reflects the state of the digestive system and the presence of pathogenic factors:

Coating color What it indicates
Thin white Normal. Healthy Stomach Qi
Thick white Cold pattern or early-stage external invasion. Pathogenic factor present but not yet transformed to heat
Yellow Heat. The darker the yellow, the more intense the heat
Gray or black Either extreme cold or extreme heat, depending on moisture. Gray-black and moist: extreme cold. Gray-black and dry: extreme heat
No coating (peeled, mirror-like) Stomach Yin deficiency or severe Yin depletion. The Yin that produces the coating has been consumed

¶Tongue coating texture

Texture What it indicates
Thin Normal, or pathogenic factor is mild / at the surface
Thick Pathogenic factor is strong, or dampness / Phlegm / food stagnation is present
Moist Normal, or fluids adequate
Dry Heat consuming fluids, or Yin deficiency
Greasy / slimy (ni 腻) Dampness or Phlegm. The coating has a sticky, slimy quality that cannot be scraped off. This is the single most reliable sign of dampness
Thick yellow greasy Damp-heat. The combination of yellow (heat) + thick greasy (dampness) is the hallmark

¶Tongue regions

Different areas of the tongue correspond to different organ systems:

  • Tip: Heart and Lung
  • Center: Spleen and Stomach
  • Sides: Liver and Gallbladder
  • Root (back): Kidney, Bladder, Intestines

A red tip with a normal body elsewhere suggests Heart heat (anxiety, insomnia). A thick greasy coating at the root suggests damp-heat in the lower burner (urinary issues). Red sides suggest Liver heat.

¶Common tongue presentations

The “Hu Zhang tongue”: Red body, thick yellow greasy coating — damp-heat. This is the tongue presentation that indicates the pattern Hu Zhang treats.

The stressed professional: Normal or slightly red body, wiry (tense) sublingual veins, thin coating — Qi stagnation. The tension is visible in the veins under the tongue.

The exhausted parent: Pale, thin body, tooth-marked, thin white coating — Qi and Blood deficiency. The body lacks the substance to fill the tongue and the force to hold its shape.

The menopausal patient: Red body with no coating, especially red at the sides — Yin deficiency with Liver heat. The lost Yin has taken the coating with it; the relative heat shows in the redness.

¶Limitations

Tongue diagnosis is powerful but not infallible. Food and drink color the tongue temporarily (coffee, beets, Pepto-Bismol). Medications affect the coating (antibiotics can strip it; proton pump inhibitors can change it). Smoking yellows the coating. Always consider these factors before interpreting.
