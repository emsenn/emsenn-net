Common TCM Patterns: A Reference

2026-04-02 A concise reference of the most frequently encountered TCM patterns — their key signs, tongue and pulse findings, and treatment principles — organized by organ system and pathological type

This is a quick-reference guide to the most commonly encountered patterns in TCM clinical practice. Each entry gives the pattern name, its key signs, the diagnostic tongue and pulse findings, and the treatment principle. For full understanding of how patterns work, see Understanding TCM Patterns.

¶Qi patterns

¶Qi deficiency (qi xu 气虚)

Key signs: Fatigue, shortness of breath on exertion, weak voice, spontaneous sweating, poor appetite Tongue: Pale, possibly with tooth marks Pulse: Weak (xu) or empty (kong) Treatment: Tonify Qi. Primary herb: Huang Qi (Astragalus)

¶Qi stagnation (qi zhi 气滞)

Key signs: Distension and fullness (chest, ribs, abdomen), mood swings, irritability, sighing, symptoms worse with stress Tongue: May be normal or slightly purple at sides Pulse: Wiry (xian) Treatment: Regulate Qi, soothe the Liver. Primary herb: Chai Hu (Bupleurum). Formula: Xiao Yao San

¶Qi sinking (qi xian 气陷)

Key signs: Chronic diarrhea, organ prolapse (uterine, rectal, gastric), bearing-down sensation, extreme fatigue Tongue: Pale Pulse: Weak, especially at the right Guan position Treatment: Tonify and raise Qi. Formula: Bu Zhong Yi Qi Tang

¶Blood patterns

¶Blood deficiency (xue xu 血虚)

Key signs: Pale face, dizziness, blurred vision, insomnia, scanty menstruation, dry skin, brittle nails, numbness Tongue: Pale, thin Pulse: Thin (xi), choppy (se) Treatment: Nourish Blood. Primary herbs: Dang Gui (Angelica), Shu Di Huang (Rehmannia). Formula: Si Wu Tang

¶Blood stasis (xue yu 血瘀)

Key signs: Fixed stabbing pain, dark complexion, masses/lumps, dark menstrual blood with clots Tongue: Purple or dark with petechiae Pulse: Choppy (se) or wiry Treatment: Invigorate Blood, dispel stasis. Herbs include Hu Zhang, Dan Shen, Chuan Xiong. Formula: Xue Fu Zhu Yu Tang

¶Blood heat (xue re 血热)

Key signs: Reckless bleeding (nosebleeds, heavy periods, blood in stool), skin eruptions, agitation, feeling of heat Tongue: Deep red Pulse: Rapid, possibly overflowing Treatment: Cool Blood, stop bleeding. Primary herbs: Sheng Di (raw Rehmannia), Mu Dan Pi (Moutan bark)

¶Yin and Yang patterns

¶Yin deficiency (yin xu 阴虚)

Key signs: Night sweats, five-palm heat, afternoon fever, dry mouth/throat, malar flush Tongue: Red, little or no coating Pulse: Thin and rapid Treatment: Nourish Yin. Formula: Liu Wei Di Huang Wan (Kidney Yin); Mai Men Dong Tang (Lung Yin)

¶Yang deficiency (yang xu 阳虚)

Key signs: Cold limbs, pale face, fatigue (worse mornings), edema, copious clear urine, loose stools, low back pain Tongue: Pale, swollen, tooth-marked, white moist coating Pulse: Deep and slow Treatment: Warm Yang. Formula: Jin Gui Shen Qi Wan (Kidney Yang); Li Zhong Wan (Spleen Yang)

¶Combined and compound patterns

¶Liver Qi stagnation with Spleen deficiency

Key signs: Rib-side distension + abdominal bloating, irritability + fatigue, alternating constipation and loose stools, symptoms worse with stress and improved with rest Tongue: Pale body with slightly red sides Pulse: Wiry at left Guan, weak at right Guan Treatment: Soothe Liver, tonify Spleen. Formula: Xiao Yao San

¶Damp-heat in the Liver/Gallbladder

Key signs: Jaundice, bitter taste, nausea, rib-side fullness, dark urine, irritability Tongue: Red with thick yellow greasy coating Pulse: Wiry, slippery, rapid Treatment: Clear heat, resolve dampness. Herbs include Hu Zhang, Yin Chen Hao, Zhi Zi. Formula: Yin Chen Hao Tang

¶Phlegm misting the Heart

Key signs: Mental confusion, muddled thinking, incoherent speech, possible loss of consciousness, rattling sound in throat Tongue: Swollen with thick greasy coating Pulse: Slippery Treatment: Resolve Phlegm, open the orifices. Emergency herbs: Shi Chang Pu (Acorus), Yuan Zhi (Polygala)

¶Kidney Yin and Yang both deficient

Key signs: Low back pain, weak knees, tinnitus, PLUS cold limbs and nocturia — signs of both Yin AND Yang depletion. Common in advanced aging and severe chronic illness Tongue: Pale-red, thin, with partial coating Pulse: Deep, thin, may be slow or rapid depending on which deficiency predominates Treatment: Nourish Yin and warm Yang simultaneously. Formula: modified Jin Gui Shen Qi Wan

¶How to use this reference

  1. Identify the patient’s dominant symptoms
  2. Match to the pattern whose key signs best fit
  3. Check tongue and pulse for confirmation
  4. Apply the treatment principle

In practice, patients often present with compound patterns (two or more simultaneous patterns). The skilled practitioner identifies the primary pattern and any secondary patterns, then designs treatment that addresses both — treating the root while managing the branch.
