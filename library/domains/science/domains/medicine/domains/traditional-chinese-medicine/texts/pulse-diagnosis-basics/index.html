Pulse Diagnosis Basics

2026-04-02 How TCM reads the pulse — not just rate and rhythm but depth, width, strength, shape, and quality at three positions on each wrist, each corresponding to specific organ systems

Pulse diagnosis is one of TCM’s two signature diagnostic methods (alongside tongue diagnosis). Where Western medicine takes the pulse to measure rate and rhythm, TCM reads the pulse as a complex signal carrying information about the state of Qi, Blood, the organ-function systems, and the nature of any pathological pattern present. A skilled TCM practitioner can identify the Eight Principles character (interior/exterior, hot/cold, excess/deficient) and narrow the organ-system involvement from the pulse alone.

This guide introduces the basic framework — enough to understand pulse references in TCM term definitions.

Where to take the pulse

TCM pulse diagnosis uses the radial artery at the wrist — the same artery Western medicine uses, but assessed at three adjacent positions on each wrist:

Position Name Organ correspondence (left wrist) Organ correspondence (right wrist)
Cun (寸, “inch”) — closest to the hand Distal Heart / Small Intestine Lung / Large Intestine
Guan (关, “gate”) — at the radial styloid Middle Liver / Gallbladder Spleen / Stomach
Chi (尺, “foot”) — closest to the elbow Proximal Kidney (Yin) / Bladder Kidney (Yang) / Ming Men

The practitioner places three fingers on the three positions simultaneously and assesses each position independently. This allows organ-specific assessment: a weak pulse at the right Guan position suggests Spleen Qi deficiency; a wiry pulse at the left Guan position suggests Liver Qi stagnation.

What the practitioner feels for

TCM pulse assessment evaluates multiple qualities simultaneously:

Depth — how much pressure is needed to feel the pulse:

  • Floating (fu 浮): Felt with light pressure, disappears with heavy pressure → exterior pattern, or Yin deficiency (Yin cannot anchor the pulse)
  • Deep (chen 沉): Felt only with heavy pressure → interior pattern, or Yang deficiency

Rate — beats per respiration (the traditional unit) or per minute:

  • Rapid (shuo 数): >5 beats per respiration (~90+ bpm) → heat
  • Slow (chi 迟): <4 beats per respiration (~60 bpm or less) → cold

Width and force:

  • Thin/thready (xi 细): Narrow and weak → Blood or Yin deficiency (insufficient fluid volume)
  • Full/large (da 大): Wide and forceful → excess heat
  • Weak (ruo 弱): Deep, thin, and soft → Qi and Blood deficiency
  • Forceful (you li 有力): Strong at all depths → excess pattern

Shape and quality — the distinctive feel:

  • Wiry (xian 弦): Taut like a guitar string → Liver involvement, Qi stagnation, pain
  • Slippery (hua 滑): Round, smooth, rolling like beads → Phlegm, dampness, food stagnation, or pregnancy
  • Choppy (se 涩): Rough, catching, uneven → Blood stasis
  • Soggy (ru 濡): Floating, thin, and soft → dampness with deficiency
  • Tight (jin 紧): Taut and vibrating like a twisted rope → cold, pain

The most common clinical pulses

Wiry and thin → Liver Qi stagnation with Blood deficiency. The classic “stressed and depleted” pulse.

Slippery and rapid → Damp-heat. The dampness makes the pulse slippery; the heat makes it rapid. This is the pulse that accompanies the thick yellow greasy tongue in damp-heat patterns.

Deep, slow, and weak → Yang deficiency. The pulse retreats, slows, and loses force — the body lacks warming, activating power.

Thin and rapid → Yin deficiency. The pulse is thin (insufficient fluid) and rapid (relative heat from depleted cooling).

Choppy → Blood stasis. The pulse catches and stutters, reflecting impeded blood flow.

Learning to read the pulse

Pulse diagnosis is traditionally considered the most difficult TCM skill to learn — requiring years of supervised practice to develop sensitivity and reliability. The descriptions above are conceptual frameworks; the actual tactile experience of a wiry versus a slippery pulse can only be learned through practice on real patients under guidance.

This is not a limitation of the method — it is a recognition that pulse diagnosis is a skilled perception, like wine tasting or musical ear training. The information is in the signal; the challenge is developing the sensitivity to read it.
