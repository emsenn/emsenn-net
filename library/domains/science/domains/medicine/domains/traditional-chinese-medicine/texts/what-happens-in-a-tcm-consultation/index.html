What Happens in a TCM Consultation

2026-04-02 What to expect when you visit a Traditional Chinese Medicine practitioner — the four examinations, how the practitioner arrives at a pattern diagnosis, and what the treatment recommendations mean

This tutorial describes what happens during a consultation with a Traditional Chinese Medicine practitioner, so you know what to expect and understand why they do what they do. No prior knowledge of TCM is required.

¶The four examinations

A TCM consultation uses four methods of assessment, called the four examinations (si zhen 四诊). Every consultation involves all four, though the emphasis varies.

¶1. Looking (wang 望)

The practitioner observes you — your complexion, posture, movements, and energy level — from the moment you walk in. They notice things a Western doctor might also note (pallor, posture, gait) and things they probably would not (the brightness of your eyes, the quality of your skin’s luster, whether your overall bearing suggests vitality or depletion).

The most detailed observation is tongue diagnosis. The practitioner will ask you to open your mouth and extend your tongue. They are examining the color of the tongue body, its shape and size, and the color, thickness, and texture of the coating. See Tongue Diagnosis Basics for what they are looking for. This typically takes 10–15 seconds.

¶2. Listening and smelling (wen 聞)

The practitioner listens to your voice (strength, tone, clarity), your breathing (wheezing, sighing, coughing), and any other sounds. A loud, forceful voice suggests excess; a weak, low voice suggests deficiency. Frequent sighing suggests Qi stagnation. The practitioner may also note body odor and breath — different patterns have characteristic smells in TCM theory, though this is less emphasized in modern practice.

¶3. Asking (wen 問)

The clinical interview. This is usually the longest part of the consultation. The practitioner asks about your chief complaint and then systematically inquires about areas that reveal your underlying pattern:

  • Temperature: Do you tend to feel hot or cold? Do you prefer warm or cool environments?
  • Sweating: Do you sweat easily? At night? With minimal exertion?
  • Sleep: Do you fall asleep easily? Stay asleep? Dream a lot? Feel rested on waking?
  • Digestion: Appetite, bowel habits, bloating, nausea, taste in the mouth
  • Urination: Frequency, color, urgency
  • Pain: Location, character (sharp, dull, burning, heavy), timing, what makes it better or worse
  • Emotions: Predominant emotional state, stress response, irritability, anxiety, sadness
  • Menstruation (if applicable): Cycle regularity, flow, color, clots, pain
  • Energy: Overall energy level, when it is best and worst in the day

Many of these questions would be familiar in a Western medical history. The difference is what the practitioner does with the answers — they are mapping your responses onto the Eight Principles framework and building a pattern diagnosis, not looking for a disease diagnosis.

¶4. Touching (qie 切)

Primarily pulse diagnosis. The practitioner places three fingers on the radial artery at your wrist and feels the pulse at three positions on each wrist. They are assessing depth, rate, width, strength, and quality — not just “how fast is the heart beating” but “what is the quality of Qi and Blood flow through the entire system.” See Pulse Diagnosis Basics for detail.

The practitioner may also palpate your abdomen (checking for tenderness, temperature, tension) and any areas of pain.

¶Arriving at a diagnosis

After the four examinations, the practitioner synthesizes the findings into a pattern diagnosis (see Understanding TCM Patterns). This is not a disease name — it is a description of how your body is currently out of balance.

For example, after examining a patient with rib-side distension, irritability, sighing, irregular periods with clots, a wiry pulse, and a slightly purple tongue, the practitioner might diagnose: Liver Qi stagnation with Blood stasis. This pattern names what is happening (Qi is stuck, Blood is stagnant), where it is happening (Liver system), and implies the treatment strategy (move Qi, invigorate Blood).

The practitioner may explain the pattern in TCM terms, in Western terms, or both, depending on their practice style and your preferences. Don’t hesitate to ask for explanation in language you understand.

¶Treatment recommendations

Based on the pattern diagnosis, the practitioner recommends treatment. Common modalities include:

Herbal medicine: A formula of 4–15 herbs tailored to your specific pattern. See How TCM Classifies Herbs and Reading a TCM Herb Entry for how formulas work. The formula may be prescribed as raw herbs (to decoct at home), granules (to dissolve in hot water), or pills/capsules.

Acupuncture: Insertion of fine needles at specific points along the meridians to regulate Qi flow. Often combined with herbal medicine.

Dietary advice: Foods are classified by the same system as herbs (nature, taste, channel entry). The practitioner may recommend foods that support the treatment and advise against foods that aggravate the pattern.

Lifestyle recommendations: Exercise, sleep, stress management, and emotional awareness — all framed in terms of how they affect the pattern.

¶What TCM cannot do

TCM is not a replacement for emergency medicine, surgery, or the treatment of acute life-threatening conditions. It is not appropriate as sole treatment for cancer, acute infections requiring antibiotics, or conditions requiring surgical intervention. Many patients use TCM alongside Western medicine — the two systems can complement each other when practitioners on both sides communicate.

¶Follow-up

TCM patterns change over time, especially with treatment. Expect to return for reassessment after 2–4 weeks. The practitioner will re-examine your tongue and pulse, ask about symptom changes, and adjust the formula or treatment plan as the pattern evolves.
