Understanding TCM Patterns

2026-04-02 A beginner's tutorial on pattern diagnosis — why TCM treats patterns rather than diseases, what a pattern is, how the Eight Principles work, and how to read a pattern name like 'Liver Qi stagnation with Spleen deficiency'

This tutorial teaches you to understand TCM pattern names — the phrases like “Liver Qi stagnation,” “Kidney Yang deficiency,” or “damp-heat in the Lower Burner” that appear throughout TCM herb entries, clinical discussions, and this repository’s TCM content. You do not need any prior knowledge. By the end, you will be able to parse any pattern name and understand what it describes.

¶Why patterns, not diseases

Western biomedicine diagnoses diseases — specific pathological entities with defined mechanisms. “Type 2 diabetes” names a disease: insulin resistance and beta-cell dysfunction, measurable by blood glucose and HbA1c, treated by targeting those mechanisms.

TCM diagnoses patterns of disharmony — configurations of signs and symptoms that reveal how a particular body is out of balance. The same person that biomedicine diagnoses with “type 2 diabetes” might present in TCM as “Spleen Qi deficiency with Dampness accumulation” — a description not of a disease entity but of a functional state: the digestive system (Spleen) lacks operational force (Qi deficiency), causing fluids to accumulate rather than transform (Dampness).

The practical consequence: ten patients with the same biomedical diagnosis may have ten different TCM patterns and receive ten different treatments. Two patients with different biomedical diagnoses may have the same TCM pattern and receive the same treatment. The herb Hu Zhang is not prescribed for “hepatitis” (a disease name). It is prescribed for “damp-heat in the Liver and Gallbladder” (a pattern) — which may manifest as hepatitis, or as gallstones, or as chronic cholecystitis, or as something else entirely.

¶The Eight Principles: the coordinate system

TCM organizes clinical findings using four pairs of opposites called the Eight Principles (ba gang 八纲). These are the coordinate axes of diagnosis:

¶Interior vs. Exterior

  • Exterior: The problem is on the surface — skin, muscles, the body’s outer layer. Think: catching a cold. Symptoms are acute, recent, and affect the surface (fever, chills, body aches, runny nose).
  • Interior: The problem is deep inside — the organ-function systems. Think: chronic digestive problems, liver dysfunction, long-standing pain. Symptoms are often chronic and organ-specific.

¶Hot vs. Cold

  • Hot: The body shows signs of excess heat — inflammation, redness, thirst for cold drinks, dark urine, fast pulse, red tongue with yellow coating.
  • Cold: The body shows signs of insufficient warmth — chills, pale face, preference for warm drinks, clear urine, slow pulse, pale tongue with white coating.

¶Excess vs. Deficiency

  • Excess: Something is present that shouldn’t be — a pathogenic factor (infection, accumulation, stagnation). Symptoms are strong, forceful, acute. Pain is sharp.
  • Deficiency: Something is missing that should be there — the body lacks Qi, Blood, Yin, or Yang. Symptoms are weak, dull, chronic. Pain is aching.

¶Yin vs. Yang

The master pair that summarizes the others:

  • Yin patterns: interior + cold + deficient
  • Yang patterns: exterior + hot + excess

¶How to read a pattern name

TCM pattern names are constructed from modular components. Once you know the components, you can parse any pattern name:

¶Component 1: The organ system

The first word usually names the affected organ-function system: Liver, Heart, Spleen, Lung, Kidney. Remember, these are functional systems, not anatomical organs — the TCM “Liver” governs the smooth flow of Qi, stores blood, and relates to emotional flexibility, which is a broader concept than the biomedical liver.

¶Component 2: The substance affected

The second element names what substance is disordered: Qi, Blood, Yin, or Yang.

¶Component 3: The type of disorder

The third element names what has gone wrong: deficiency (not enough), stagnation (stuck), excess (too much), heat (inflamed), cold (depleted warmth).

¶Putting it together

Pattern name Organ Substance Disorder What it means
Liver Qi stagnation Liver Qi Stagnation The Liver’s Qi flow is stuck — irritability, rib-side pain, sighing
Kidney Yang deficiency Kidney Yang Deficiency The Kidney’s warming force is depleted — cold limbs, low back pain, fatigue
Heart Blood deficiency Heart Blood Deficiency The Heart lacks Blood to house the Shen — insomnia, anxiety, poor memory
Spleen Qi deficiency Spleen Qi Deficiency The Spleen lacks force to transform food — fatigue, bloating, loose stools
Lung Yin deficiency Lung Yin Deficiency The Lung’s moistening substance is depleted — dry cough, dry throat

¶Compound patterns

Real clinical presentations often involve multiple simultaneous patterns. These are named by combining components:

“Liver Qi stagnation with Spleen Qi deficiency”: The Liver’s Qi is stuck (causing irritability, rib-side pain) AND the Spleen’s Qi is weak (causing fatigue, poor digestion). The two are connected: the stagnant Liver “overacts” on the weak Spleen, disrupting digestion. This is the single most common combined pattern in clinical practice.

"Damp-heat in the Liver and Gallbladder": The Liver/Gallbladder system has accumulated both dampness (heaviness, turbid fluids) and heat (inflammation, yellow discoloration). This is the pattern Hu Zhang specifically treats — manifesting as jaundice, bitter taste, nausea, dark urine.

"Blood stasis with Qi stagnation": Blood has become stagnant (fixed sharp pain, dark tongue) because the Qi that moves it has also stagnated. The treatment must move both Qi and Blood.

¶Patterns are not permanent

A pattern is a snapshot of how the body is functioning right now, not a permanent label. Patterns change as conditions change — as the seasons shift, as treatment progresses, as the patient’s life circumstances evolve. A patient with Liver Qi stagnation who receives effective treatment may present next month with Spleen Qi deficiency (the stagnation is resolved but the underlying weakness remains). The treatment changes accordingly.

This is why TCM practitioners reassess the pattern at each visit. The formula that was correct last month may need modification this month because the pattern has shifted.
