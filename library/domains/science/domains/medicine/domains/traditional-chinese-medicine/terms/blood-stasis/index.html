Blood Stasis

2026-04-02 血瘀 (xue yu) — a pathological condition in which blood flow is impeded, stagnant, or obstructed, producing fixed sharp pain, dark complexion, and purple tongue — one of the most clinically significant pathological products in TCM

Blood stasis (xue yu 血瘀) is a pathological condition in Traditional Chinese Medicine in which blood flow is impeded, slowed, or obstructed. It is not merely “poor circulation” in the colloquial sense — it is a specific diagnostic category with characteristic signs, defined causes, predictable complications, and targeted treatment principles. Blood stasis is one of the most clinically important pathological products in TCM because it both results from and causes further disease, creating self-reinforcing cycles of obstruction and dysfunction.

Diagnosis

Blood stasis has a distinctive clinical presentation. The cardinal signs are:

Pain: Fixed, stabbing, sharp, and well-localized — as opposed to the wandering pain of Wind or the dull pain of deficiency. The pain is typically worse at night and worse with pressure. The TCM axiom is: “where there is no flow, there is pain” (bu tong ze tong 不通则痛). Blood stasis is the paradigmatic “no flow” condition.

Complexion: Dark, dusky, or purple. The area of the body affected by stasis may show dark discoloration. Facial complexion in chronic blood stasis is characteristically dark and lusterless.

Tongue: Purple or dark-red tongue body, often with dark spots or petechiae. The sublingual veins (visible when the tongue is curled upward) may be engorged, dark, and tortuous. Tongue diagnosis is the single most reliable clinical indicator of blood stasis.

Pulse: Choppy (se 涩) — a pulse that feels rough, uneven, and “catching,” as if the blood flow encounters resistance. The choppy pulse reflects the impeded flow that defines the condition.

Other signs: Fixed masses or lumps (abdominal masses, uterine fibroids), varicose veins, spider angiomata, dark or clotted menstrual blood, bleeding that is dark in color, skin that bruises easily and heals slowly.

Causes

Blood stasis does not arise spontaneously. It is always secondary to another pathological process:

Qi stagnation: The most common cause. Qi moves blood — it is the motive force behind circulation. When Qi stagnates (from emotional constraint, particularly frustration and unexpressed anger), blood movement slows and eventually stagnates. Liver Qi stagnation leading to blood stasis is an extremely common clinical progression, particularly in gynecological conditions.

Cold congealing blood: External or internal cold causes blood to congeal, just as cold solidifies liquids. Cold-type blood stasis produces severe cramping pain that is relieved by warmth — the classic presentation of dysmenorrhea (painful menstruation) from cold in the uterus.

Heat scorching blood: Extreme heat can damage blood vessels and cause blood to extravasate (leave the vessels) or become thick and viscous. This is blood stasis from heat — less intuitive than cold-type stasis, but clinically significant in febrile diseases and hemorrhagic conditions.

Trauma: Physical injury directly damages blood vessels and produces localized blood stasis — the bruise, the swelling, the hematoma. Traumatic blood stasis is the most straightforward type and the easiest to understand in biomedical terms.

Chronic deficiency: In long-standing Qi deficiency, the motive force driving blood movement weakens progressively. Blood stasis from deficiency is characteristic of elderly patients, the chronically ill, and postpartum women — all states where the body’s operational force is depleted.

Treatment principle: invigorate blood, dispel stasis

The treatment of blood stasis involves herbs that invigorate blood (huo xue 活血) — literally “enliven the blood,” restore its movement — and dispel stasis (qu yu 祛瘀) — break up the stagnant accumulation. Hu Zhang (Japanese knotweed root) belongs to this category. Other major blood-invigorating herbs include:

  • Dang Gui (Angelica sinensis) — nourishes and moves blood simultaneously; the most widely used blood herb in the TCM materia medica
  • Chuan Xiong (Ligusticum chuanxiong) — moves blood and Qi; reaches the head (treats headaches from blood stasis)
  • Tao Ren (peach kernel) — breaks blood stasis; used for severe fixed masses and amenorrhea
  • Hong Hua (safflower) — invigorates blood, dispels stasis, alleviates pain
  • Dan Shen (Salvia miltiorrhiza) — invigorates blood, cools blood, calms the spirit; widely studied for cardiovascular applications

The choice of herb depends on the type and location of stasis. Hu Zhang is selected when blood stasis co-occurs with damp-heat (as in jaundice or hepatobiliary disorders), because it addresses both pathologies simultaneously — a dual classification that most blood-invigorating herbs do not share.

Blood stasis and modern medicine

Blood stasis is the TCM concept with perhaps the strongest correspondence to modern biomedical understanding. The clinical presentation — fixed pain, dark discoloration, masses, abnormal bleeding, choppy pulse — maps onto conditions characterized by:

  • Microcirculatory dysfunction: Impaired blood flow through capillary beds, measurable by laser Doppler flowmetry and nail-fold capillaroscopy
  • Hypercoagulability: Elevated fibrinogen, increased platelet aggregation, elevated D-dimer — laboratory markers that correlate with TCM blood stasis diagnoses
  • Chronic inflammation: Elevated CRP, IL-6, TNF-α in patients diagnosed with blood stasis patterns
  • Endothelial dysfunction: Impaired nitric oxide production, increased endothelin — the vascular endothelium’s failure to maintain normal blood flow

Recent research has identified a particularly striking structural correlate: fibrinaloid microclots — anomalous, dense clots resistant to fibrinolysis that block microcapillaries and reduce oxygen transfer. These microclots occur in chronic inflammatory and vascular diseases and are now understood to be mechanistically responsible for many of the symptoms TCM has attributed to blood stasis for centuries. The parallel with Virchow’s triad (vessel wall damage, blood flow stasis, hypercoagulable state) is direct.

Research programs in China, Japan, and integrative medicine centers have established statistically significant correlations between TCM blood stasis diagnosis and these biomedical markers [chen2004]. The classical formula Xue Fu Zhu Yu Tang (血府逐瘀汤, “Drive Out Stasis from the Mansion of Blood”), developed by Wang Qing-Ren in the 1830s, has been the subject of randomized controlled trials and is available as an FDA-approved capsule in China (since 2002) for coronary heart disease and hyperlipidemia — a rare example of a traditional formula validated through modern clinical trial methodology.

This convergence does not mean blood stasis “is” microcirculatory dysfunction — the concepts operate at different levels of description — but it validates that TCM’s clinical observation identified a real pathological phenomenon through functional assessment that biomedicine later described through molecular mechanisms.

The pharmacology of blood-invigorating herbs aligns with this correspondence. Hu Zhang’s resveratrol inhibits platelet aggregation and improves endothelial function. Dan Shen’s tanshinones are anticoagulant and anti-inflammatory. Chuan Xiong’s tetramethylpyrazine improves microcirculation. The herbs that TCM selected over centuries for “invigorating blood” turn out to contain compounds that address the biomedical correlates of impaired circulation.
