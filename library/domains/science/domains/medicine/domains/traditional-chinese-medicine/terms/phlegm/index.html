Phlegm

2026-04-02 痰 (tan) — a pathological product of impaired fluid metabolism in TCM, ranging from visible mucus to invisible obstructions that produce nodules, dizziness, mental fog, and masses — 'the source of a hundred diseases'

Phlegm (tan 痰) is a pathological product of impaired fluid metabolism in Traditional Chinese Medicine. TCM’s concept of phlegm is far broader than the biomedical meaning of respiratory mucus. It encompasses any abnormal accumulation of thick, turbid fluid in the body — from visible sputum in the lungs to invisible obstructions that produce nodules, masses, dizziness, mental fog, numbness, and psychiatric symptoms. TCM calls phlegm “the source of a hundred diseases” (bai bing duo you tan zuo sui 百病多由痰作祟) — a recognition of its role in an extraordinary range of conditions.

¶Visible and invisible phlegm

Visible phlegm (you xing zhi tan 有形之痰): Phlegm you can see or hear — sputum coughed from the lungs, mucus in the throat, nasal discharge, vomited mucoid fluid. This corresponds roughly to the biomedical concept of mucus and exudate.

Invisible phlegm (wu xing zhi tan 无形之痰): Phlegm that cannot be directly seen but manifests through its effects — subcutaneous nodules, goiter, lipomas, atherosclerotic plaque, gallstones, kidney stones, joint swelling, a feeling of obstruction in the throat or chest, dizziness with a heavy head, and mental-emotional symptoms (confusion, mania, epilepsy-like episodes). This is the distinctively TCM dimension of the concept — the idea that thick, turbid, congealed fluid can obstruct any system in the body, not just the respiratory tract.

¶How phlegm forms

Phlegm arises when body fluids are not properly transformed and transported. The primary organ responsible is the Spleen (which governs fluid transformation), with contributions from the Lung (which distributes fluids) and the Kidney (which governs water metabolism). The TCM axiom is: “the Spleen is the source of phlegm; the Lung is the storehouse of phlegm” (pi wei sheng tan zhi yuan, fei wei chu tan zhi qi 脾为生痰之源，肺为储痰之器).

Common pathways to phlegm formation:

  • Spleen Qi deficiency → fluids not transformed → accumulation → phlegm
  • Qi stagnation → impaired fluid movement → accumulation → phlegm
  • Heat → condenses fluids into thick, sticky phlegm (phlegm-heat: yellow, thick, difficult to expectorate)
  • Cold → congeals fluids into watery, copious phlegm (phlegm-cold: white, thin, easily expectorated)

¶Phlegm and the mind

One of TCM’s most distinctive claims about phlegm is that it can “mist the Heart orifices” (tan mi xin qiao 痰迷心窍), obstructing Shen and producing mental-emotional symptoms: confusion, muddled thinking, mania, incoherent speech, loss of consciousness. This “phlegm misting the mind” pattern is TCM’s framework for understanding certain acute psychiatric presentations, epilepsy, and post-stroke cognitive impairment.

The concept is not as foreign to biomedicine as it might appear. Neuroinflammation, cerebrovascular disease, and metabolic encephalopathy all involve the accumulation of abnormal substances (inflammatory mediators, lipids, metabolic waste) that impair brain function — the biomedical version of “turbid obstruction affecting the mind.”

¶Treatment

Phlegm-resolving herbs (hua tan yao 化痰药) are classified by the type of phlegm they address:

  • Cold-phlegm: Warm, drying herbs — Ban Xia (Pinellia), Bai Jie Zi (white mustard seed)
  • Heat-phlegm: Cool, clearing herbs — Gua Lou (Trichosanthes), Zhu Ru (bamboo shavings), Bei Mu (Fritillaria)
  • Wind-phlegm (causing dizziness, tremor): Tian Ma (Gastrodia), Gou Teng (Uncaria)

Hu Zhang addresses phlegm-heat in the Lung through its bitter, cold properties.
