Body Fluids (Jin Ye)

2026-04-02 津液 — the normal physiological fluids of the body in TCM, comprising thin clear jin (sweat, tears, saliva) and thick nourishing ye (joint fluid, marrow, cerebrospinal fluid) — a fundamental substance alongside Qi, Blood, and Jing

Body fluids (jin ye 津液) are the normal physiological fluids of the body in Traditional Chinese Medicine — all fluids other than Blood. Together with Qi, Blood, and Jing, they constitute the fundamental substances that the body needs to function. Jin Ye moisten the skin, lubricate the joints, nourish the brain, fill the eyes and ears, and maintain the suppleness of all tissues.

Jin and Ye

TCM distinguishes two subtypes:

Jin (津, “thin fluids”): Light, clear, watery, and mobile. Jin circulates on the body’s surface and in the muscles, moistening the skin and filling the orifices. Sweat, tears, saliva, nasal mucus, and the fluid component of urine are manifestations of Jin. Jin moves with Wei Qi (defensive Qi) on the surface.

Ye (液, “thick fluids”): Dense, heavy, viscous, and slow-moving. Ye circulates in the interior, lubricating joints, moistening the brain and spinal cord, nourishing bone marrow, and filling the sensory organs. Synovial fluid, cerebrospinal fluid, and the vitreous humor of the eye are manifestations of Ye. Ye is closely related to Jing and Blood — all three are Yin substances that nourish in depth.

Metabolism of body fluids

The production, distribution, and excretion of body fluids involves three organ systems:

  • Spleen: Transforms ingested fluids from food and drink into usable Jin Ye (the “source of fluids”)
  • Lung: Distributes fluids to the surface (skin, pores) and directs excess fluids downward to the Kidney
  • Kidney: Governs water metabolism, separating clear (recycled) from turbid (excreted as urine). The Kidney’s Yang provides the metabolic fire that drives fluid transformation

When this system functions normally, fluids are produced, distributed, and excreted in balance. When it fails — through Spleen deficiency, Lung dysfunction, or Kidney Yang depletion — fluids accumulate pathologically as dampness, edema, or Phlegm.

Fluid pathology

Fluid deficiency (jin ye bu zu 津液不足): Insufficient fluids. Dry skin, dry mouth, dry eyes, constipation, scanty concentrated urine, thirst. Overlaps with Yin deficiency — Jin Ye are Yin substances, and their depletion is one expression of Yin depletion.

Fluid accumulation: When fluids are not transformed and excreted, they accumulate as:

  • Dampness (shi 湿) — diffuse, heavy, soggy
  • Phlegm (tan 痰) — congealed, thick, obstructing
  • Edema (shui zhong 水肿) — visible fluid accumulation in tissues
  • Fluid retention (yin 饮) — fluid accumulation in body cavities

The progression — normal fluids → dampness → phlegm — represents increasing density and pathological severity of accumulated fluid.
