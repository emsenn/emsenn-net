Blood (Xue)

2026-04-02 血 — the dense, nourishing fluid in TCM that moistens tissues, anchors Shen, and circulates through the meridians — functionally distinct from the biomedical concept of blood, though anatomically overlapping

Blood (xue 血) in Traditional Chinese Medicine is the dense, nourishing fluid that circulates through the meridians and blood vessels, moistening and nourishing the tissues, organs, skin, hair, and sense organs. It is functionally richer than the biomedical concept of blood — TCM Blood includes not just the red fluid in vessels but the nourishing, moistening, and consciousness-anchoring functions that the fluid performs.

¶How Blood differs from the biomedical concept

The TCM concept overlaps with but is not identical to the biomedical concept. Biomedical blood is a tissue: red cells, white cells, platelets, plasma, measurable by CBC and serology. TCM Blood is a functional category: the body’s capacity to nourish, moisten, and anchor awareness. A patient can have a normal CBC and still present with TCM Blood deficiency — pale face, dry skin, poor memory, insomnia, scanty menstruation — because the functional capacity the concept names is broader than the laboratory values.

¶Production

Blood is produced through the collaboration of multiple organ-function systems:

  • The Spleen transforms food into Food Qi (gu qi), the raw material for Blood
  • The Heart transforms Food Qi into Blood proper through its warming, circulating function
  • Jing (stored in the Kidney) contributes to Blood production — TCM states “Jing and Blood share a common source”

This production chain explains why Spleen deficiency (poor digestion, poor absorption) leads to Blood deficiency over time: if the raw material is inadequate, the final product is insufficient.

¶Functions

Nourishing: Blood moistens and nourishes every tissue. Muscles, tendons, skin, hair, nails, and the sense organs all depend on Blood for their vitality. Blood deficiency produces dryness, brittleness, and loss of luster — dry skin, brittle nails, blurred vision, muscle cramps.

Moistening: Where Qi warms and moves, Blood cools and moistens. The Qi-Blood pair is another expression of the Yang-Yin dynamic: Qi is the active, warming, moving principle; Blood is the nourishing, cooling, anchoring principle. Each depends on the other — Qi moves Blood (without Qi, Blood stagnates), Blood nourishes Qi (without Blood, Qi has no substance to animate).

Anchoring Shen: The Heart “stores” Shen (awareness, coherent consciousness), and Blood is the medium in which Shen resides. Adequate Heart Blood provides the substrate for settled, coherent awareness. When Heart Blood is deficient, Shen becomes unanchored — producing insomnia, anxiety, restlessness, poor memory, dream-disturbed sleep, and a sense of vague unease.

¶Blood pathology

Blood deficiency (xue xu 血虚): Insufficient Blood. Pale complexion, dizziness, blurred vision, insomnia, scanty or absent menstruation, pale tongue, thin pulse. Common in women (menstruation depletes Blood), the elderly, and the chronically ill.

Blood stasis (xue yu 血瘀): Blood that has stopped flowing properly — stagnant, obstructed, or extravasated. Fixed sharp pain, dark complexion, purple tongue, choppy pulse. Treated by blood-invigorating herbs including Hu Zhang.

Blood heat (xue re 血热): Heat entering the Blood level, causing reckless bleeding, skin eruptions, agitation. The Blood is “boiling” — moving chaotically rather than in its proper channels.
