Yang Deficiency

2026-04-02 阳虚 (yang xu) — the depletion of the body's warming, activating, protecting force, producing cold signs, fatigue, fluid accumulation, and a deep slow pulse — the pattern of insufficient metabolic fire

Yang deficiency (yang xu 阳虚) is the depletion of the body’s Yang — the warming, activating, transforming, protecting aspect of the Yin-Yang dynamic. When Yang is deficient, the body lacks the metabolic fire to warm itself, transform nutrients, circulate fluids, and protect against invasion. Cold signs predominate — not because an external cold pathogen is present, but because the body’s internal warmth is insufficient.

Presentation

The signs of Yang deficiency reflect the loss of Yang’s qualities — warmth, activity, transformation:

  • Cold: Feeling cold, especially in the extremities (cold hands and feet), the low back, and the abdomen. The patient seeks warmth — warm rooms, warm drinks, warm clothing
  • Fatigue: Profound tiredness, worse in the morning, improved somewhat by warmth and gentle activity. The body lacks the activating force to sustain effort
  • Pale complexion: A bright, white pallor — not the dull paleness of Blood deficiency, but the whiteness of insufficient warming
  • Fluid accumulation: Yang transforms and moves fluids. When Yang is deficient, fluids accumulate — edema (especially in the legs), copious clear urination, loose watery stools, clear watery nasal discharge
  • Tongue: Pale, swollen (puffy), often with tooth marks at the margins (the swollen tongue presses against the teeth). Coating is white and moist. The moisture and pallor reflect cold and fluid accumulation
  • Pulse: Deep (chen 沉) and slow (chi 迟). The pulse retreats from the surface (insufficient Yang to push it outward) and slows (insufficient fire to drive circulation)

Yang deficiency by organ system

Kidney Yang deficiency: The most fundamental. The Kidney is the root of Yang for the whole body — the “gate of vitality” (ming men 命门). Kidney Yang deficiency produces: cold low back and knees, frequent copious clear urination (especially at night — nocturia), impotence or diminished libido, infertility, edema in the legs, dawn diarrhea (wu geng xie 五更泻 — diarrhea at 5 AM, when Yang should be rising but cannot). This pattern describes the metabolic slowdown of advanced aging in TCM terms.

Spleen Yang deficiency: The Spleen transforms food and fluids. When Spleen Yang is deficient, transformation fails — producing poor appetite, abdominal bloating after eating, loose stools with undigested food, a heavy feeling in the limbs, and edema. The Spleen “cannot hold” blood in the vessels, so bruising, heavy menstruation, or blood in the stool may occur. This is the pattern underlying many chronic digestive disorders.

Heart Yang deficiency: The Heart circulates Blood and houses Shen. Heart Yang deficiency produces: palpitations, chest oppression, cold sweats, a feeling of emptiness in the chest, and in severe cases, cyanosis (blue-purple lips and nails). This pattern corresponds to congestive heart failure in biomedical terms.

Causes

Yang depletes through:

  • Aging: Yang naturally declines with age, typically after Yin has begun declining. The progressive loss of warmth, vitality, and metabolic vigor in old age is Yang deficiency
  • Chronic illness: Prolonged disease consumes Yang resources
  • Overexposure to cold: Living in cold environments, consuming excessive cold or raw foods, cold-water swimming without adequate recovery
  • Overwork: Physical overexertion (as opposed to the mental overwork that depletes Yin) exhausts Yang
  • Constitutional weakness: Some individuals are born with weaker Yang — a TCM framework for understanding constitutional variation

Treatment principle: warm Yang, tonify the Kidney

Yang-tonifying (bu yang 补阳) herbs are warm or hot, often with sweet and pungent tastes. They add warmth and metabolic fire to the depleted system:

  • Fu Zi (prepared aconite root) — the strongest Yang-warming herb; “rescues devastated Yang.” Toxic if improperly prepared; used only in processed form
  • Rou Gui (cinnamon bark) — warms the Kidney, strengthens Ming Men fire
  • Du Zhong (Eucommia bark) — tonifies Kidney Yang, strengthens low back and knees
  • Bu Gu Zhi (Psoralea fruit) — warms Kidney Yang, stops diarrhea
  • Gan Jiang (dried ginger) — warms the middle burner, rescues Spleen Yang

The classical formula is Jin Gui Shen Qi Wan (金匮肾气丸, “Kidney Qi Pill from the Golden Cabinet”) — Liu Wei Di Huang Wan (the Yin-nourishing base) with added Fu Zi and Rou Gui to warm Yang. This elegant structure — nourishing Yin while warming Yang — reflects the principle that Yang cannot be strengthened without a Yin foundation.
