Damp-Heat

2026-04-02 湿热 (shi re) — a compound pathological pattern combining the heaviness and stickiness of dampness with the inflammation and urgency of heat, characteristically affecting the Liver/Gallbladder, Bladder, and digestive systems

Damp-heat (shi re 湿热) is a compound pathological pattern in Traditional Chinese Medicine that combines two distinct pathogenic qualities — dampness and heat — into a condition that is characteristically stubborn, difficult to resolve, and clinically significant. It is one of the most commonly encountered combined patterns in TCM practice, particularly in conditions affecting the hepatobiliary system, urinary tract, skin, and digestive organs. It is the pattern for which Hu Zhang (Japanese knotweed root) is most specifically indicated.

¶The two components

To understand damp-heat, you must understand each component:

Dampness (shi 湿) is heavy, turbid, sticky, and downward-sinking. It obstructs Qi flow through viscosity rather than constriction. It impairs the Spleen’s transformative function (the Spleen governs fluid transformation; dampness overwhelms it). Dampness alone produces: heaviness in the limbs and head, poor appetite, nausea, loose stools, a feeling of being “bogged down,” a thick greasy tongue coating, and a slippery pulse. Dampness is slow to develop and slow to resolve — its stickiness is literal in clinical terms, meaning it resists treatment and tends to linger.

Heat (re 热) is ascending, dispersing, rapid, and consuming. It produces inflammation, redness, urgency, thirst, and agitation. Heat alone is relatively straightforward to treat — clear the heat, and the condition resolves.

¶Why the combination is worse than either alone

When dampness and heat combine, each quality modifies the other in ways that make the resulting pattern harder to treat than either alone:

  • Dampness traps heat: the sticky, heavy quality of dampness prevents heat from being vented or dispersed. The heat cannot rise and dissipate as it normally would, because the dampness holds it in place.
  • Heat thickens dampness: heat condenses fluids and makes the dampness more viscous and harder to drain. The normal treatment for dampness — drying, draining, and transforming — is impeded because heat has concentrated the dampness.
  • The two reinforce each other: dampness generates heat (stagnant fluids ferment), and heat generates dampness (inflammation produces exudates). This creates a self-reinforcing cycle.

The clinical consequence is a condition that is “greasy” — hard to get hold of, resistant to simple strategies, and prone to relapse. TCM practitioners describe treating damp-heat as trying to dry something that is simultaneously wet and hot: drying herbs risk making the heat worse; cooling herbs risk making the dampness worse. Effective treatment must address both simultaneously, which is why damp-heat-clearing herbs (like Hu Zhang) are valued for their dual action.

¶Where damp-heat settles

Damp-heat has affinities for specific organ systems based on the body’s terrain and the source of the pathology:

¶Liver/Gallbladder damp-heat

The most common location for damp-heat relevant to Hu Zhang. Presents as:

  • Jaundice (yellow sclera, yellow skin — the hallmark of hepatobiliary damp-heat)
  • Bitter taste in the mouth
  • Nausea, poor appetite
  • Fullness and pain in the hypochondrium (rib-side area, the Liver/Gallbladder region)
  • Dark, concentrated urine
  • Irritability
  • Tongue: red body with thick yellow greasy coating
  • Pulse: wiry and slippery

Biomedical correlates include hepatitis, cholecystitis, gallstones, and other hepatobiliary inflammatory conditions. Hu Zhang’s traditional indication for jaundice with damp-heat corresponds precisely to this pattern.

¶Bladder damp-heat

Damp-heat in the lower burner (xia jiao), affecting the urinary system:

  • Frequent, urgent urination
  • Burning or painful urination (dysuria)
  • Dark, turbid urine, possibly with blood
  • Lower abdominal distension
  • Tongue: red with yellow greasy coating, especially at the root
  • Pulse: rapid and slippery

Biomedical correlate: urinary tract infection. The burning, urgency, and turbid urine of a UTI is a near-exact description of Bladder damp-heat.

¶Spleen/Stomach damp-heat

Damp-heat in the middle burner (zhong jiao), affecting digestion:

  • Epigastric fullness and distension
  • Nausea, poor appetite
  • Loose stools with urgency, or diarrhea with burning
  • Thirst without desire to drink (the dampness impairs fluid metabolism; the heat creates thirst; the result is a paradoxical sensation)
  • Thick yellow greasy tongue coating
  • Slippery rapid pulse

Biomedical correlates include gastroenteritis, inflammatory bowel disease, and dysentery.

¶Skin damp-heat

Damp-heat expressed through the skin:

  • Red, swollen, weeping skin lesions (eczema, dermatitis)
  • Pustular acne
  • Boils, carbuncles
  • Itching that is worse with heat and moisture
  • Yellow crusting

The “weeping” quality — lesions that produce fluid rather than being dry and scaly — is the signature of dampness in skin conditions. The redness and inflammation are the heat component.

¶Causes

Damp-heat arises from several mechanisms:

External damp-heat: Living or working in hot, humid environments. The climate directly invades the body through the skin and meridians. Common in tropical and subtropical regions.

Dietary causes: Excessive consumption of greasy, rich, sweet, or spicy foods; excessive alcohol (which TCM classifies as damp and hot). These overwhelm the Spleen’s transformative capacity, generating internal dampness that combines with dietary heat.

Spleen deficiency generating dampness: The most common internal pathway. A weakened Spleen fails to transform fluids properly, allowing dampness to accumulate internally. Dampness that lingers gradually transforms into heat — a progression from deficiency to excess that is the primary mechanism for internally generated damp-heat. If the person’s constitution tends toward heat, or if they consume heating foods or alcohol, the transformation is accelerated. This Spleen Qi deficiency → internal dampness accumulation → heat transformation pathway explains why damp-heat conditions are so common in patients with chronically impaired digestion.

Modern microbiome research has found distinct gut microbial signatures in patients diagnosed with damp-heat patterns, suggesting that TCM’s observation of the Spleen’s role in damp-heat may reflect real alterations in gut ecology — the “Spleen’s transformative function” mapping onto the microbiome’s metabolic activity.

Progression from external invasion: An external damp or damp-cold pathogen that is not resolved can transform into damp-heat over time, as the body’s defensive response generates heat in its attempt to expel the dampness.

¶Treatment principles

Treating damp-heat requires simultaneously:

  1. Clearing heat (qing re 清热) — using bitter, cold herbs that drain heat
  2. Resolving dampness (li shi 利湿) — using aromatic or bland herbs that transform and drain dampness
  3. Supporting the Spleen — because a weakened Spleen generated or perpetuated the dampness, and because damp-heat-clearing herbs are cold and bitter (which can further weaken the Spleen if used without support)

Key damp-heat-clearing herbs include:

  • Hu Zhang (Japanese knotweed root) — bitter, slightly cold; clears heat, resolves dampness, invigorates blood. Its dual classification as a blood-invigorating AND damp-heat-clearing herb makes it uniquely useful when damp-heat and blood stasis co-occur (as in chronic hepatitis with jaundice and pain)
  • Yin Chen Hao (Artemisia capillaris) — the primary herb for jaundice; bitter, slightly cold; clears damp-heat specifically from the Liver/Gallbladder
  • Huang Qin (Scutellaria baicalensis) — bitter, cold; clears heat and dries dampness, especially in the Lung and upper body
  • Huang Lian (Coptis chinensis) — very bitter, very cold; clears heat and dries dampness, especially from the Heart and Stomach
  • Zhi Zi (Gardenia jasminoides) — bitter, cold; clears heat and drains dampness through the urine

¶The greasy tongue coating

The thick yellow greasy tongue coating is the most reliable diagnostic sign of damp-heat. It combines indicators of both components:

  • Thick: indicates a pathogenic factor (excess pattern)
  • Yellow: indicates heat
  • Greasy (ni 腻): indicates dampness — the coating has a slimy, sticky quality that cannot be scraped off

A thick white greasy coating indicates damp-cold (dampness without heat). A thin yellow coating indicates heat without dampness. The thick yellow greasy coating specifically indicates the combination. Tongue diagnosis is the primary method for distinguishing damp-heat from heat alone or dampness alone.
