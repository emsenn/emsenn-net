Wei Qi

2026-04-02 卫气 (wei qi) — defensive Qi, the protective force that circulates on the body's surface, warms the skin, regulates pore opening, and resists invasion by external pathogenic factors

Wei Qi (卫气, “defensive Qi” or “protective Qi”) is the aspect of Qi that circulates on the body’s surface, between the skin and muscles, protecting against invasion by external pathogenic factors. It is TCM’s concept of the body’s first line of defense — analogous to what biomedicine calls innate immunity and barrier function, though broader in scope.

Functions

Warming the skin and muscles: Wei Qi maintains surface temperature. When Wei Qi is weak, the patient feels cold and is easily chilled — not from internal Yang deficiency but from insufficient surface protection.

Regulating pore opening and closing: The opening and closing of the pores (cou li 腠理) controls sweating. Wei Qi governs this regulation. When Wei Qi is strong, pores open and close appropriately — sweating when the body needs to cool, closing when the body needs to retain heat. When Wei Qi is weak, pores fail to close properly, producing spontaneous sweating (zi han 自汗) — sweating without exertion or heat.

Resisting external pathogenic factors: Wei Qi is the shield against the six external causes (wind, cold, heat, dampness, dryness, summer heat). When Wei Qi is sufficient, the body surface is sealed and pathogens cannot penetrate. When Wei Qi is weak, the body surface is porous and vulnerable — the patient catches colds easily, is sensitive to drafts, and recovers slowly from upper respiratory infections.

Production and circulation

Wei Qi is produced by the Spleen (from food Qi) and distributed by the Lung (which governs the body surface). During the day, Wei Qi circulates on the body’s exterior (yang regions) — this is why people are more alert and resistant during waking hours. At night, Wei Qi retreats to the interior (yin regions) — this is why the body surface is more vulnerable during sleep and why fevers often spike at night.

This day-night circulation also explains why spontaneous sweating (daytime, Wei Qi failing on the surface) differs from night sweats (nighttime, Yin deficiency failing to anchor fluids) — they are different pathologies despite both involving sweat.

Clinical significance

Wei Qi weakness is the TCM framework for understanding recurrent upper respiratory infections, allergic rhinitis, chronic sinusitis, and the general vulnerability to environmental influences that some patients exhibit. A person who “catches everything” and is “always getting sick” has, in TCM terms, weak Wei Qi.

The primary tonifying herb for Wei Qi is Huang Qi (Astragalus membranaceus) — sweet, slightly warm, it tonifies Qi with a specific affinity for the body surface. Modern research on astragalus has demonstrated immunomodulatory effects — increased natural killer cell activity, enhanced macrophage function — that correspond to the traditional claim of strengthened surface defense.
