Yin Deficiency

2026-04-02 阴虚 (yin xu) — the depletion of the body's cooling, moistening, anchoring substance, producing heat signs from insufficient cooling rather than excess heat — night sweats, dry mouth, five-palm heat, and a thin rapid pulse

Yin deficiency (yin xu 阴虚) is the depletion of the body’s Yin — the cooling, moistening, nourishing, anchoring aspect of the Yin-Yang dynamic. When Yin is deficient, its complementary opposite (Yang — the warming, activating, ascending principle) is relatively unopposed, producing signs of heat. But this is empty heat (xu re 虚热) — heat from insufficient cooling, not from excess fire. The distinction is critical because the treatment is completely different: empty heat is treated by nourishing Yin (replenishing the cooling substrate), not by clearing heat (which would further deplete an already deficient system).

Presentation

The signs of Yin deficiency reflect the loss of Yin’s qualities — moisture, coolness, anchoring:

  • Night sweats: The body loses its ability to contain fluids, particularly at night when Yin predominates. Sweating during sleep that soaks the sheets is a hallmark
  • Five-palm heat (wu xin fan re 五心烦热): Heat sensation in the palms of the hands, soles of the feet, and the chest. The extremities radiate heat that the patient describes as “burning from inside”
  • Afternoon/evening fever: Low-grade temperature elevation in the afternoon or evening, when Yin should be ascending. This tidal fever (chao re 潮热) is characteristic
  • Dry mouth and throat: Particularly at night. The patient may sip water frequently but without quenching thirst
  • Malar flush: A reddish flush on the cheekbones — not the full facial redness of excess heat, but a localized, dusky redness
  • Tongue: Red, with little or no coating. The coating is Yin — when Yin is deficient, the coating thins or disappears, leaving a bare, red tongue body. This is the most reliable diagnostic sign
  • Pulse: Thin (xi 细) and rapid (shuo 数). The thinness reflects Yin depletion (less fluid volume); the rapidity reflects relative heat

Yin deficiency by organ system

Each Zang-Fu system has its own Yin deficiency presentation:

Kidney Yin deficiency: The archetype. The Kidney stores Jing and is the root of Yin for the whole body. Kidney Yin deficiency produces: low back ache, weak knees, tinnitus, diminished hearing, premature graying, poor memory, night sweats, nocturnal emissions, scanty dark urine. It is the pattern of aging — Yin naturally declines with age, and Kidney Yin is the first to show it.

Liver Yin deficiency: The Liver stores Blood, and Blood is a Yin substance. Liver Yin deficiency produces: dry eyes, blurred vision, dizziness, numbness or tingling in the limbs, brittle nails, scanty menstruation, irritability from heat. When severe, it progresses to Liver Yang rising — headaches, hypertension, tinnitus.

Lung Yin deficiency: The Lung governs moisture distribution. Lung Yin deficiency produces: dry cough (possibly with scanty, sticky, blood-streaked sputum), dry throat, hoarse voice, dry skin. This is the pattern of chronic dry cough that worsens at night.

Stomach Yin deficiency: The Stomach needs fluids to digest. Stomach Yin deficiency produces: dry mouth, thirst with desire to sip, poor appetite despite hunger, dry stools, a peeled (mirror-like) tongue coating in the center. This pattern is common after febrile illness that has consumed fluids.

Causes

Yin depletes through:

  • Chronic illness: Prolonged disease consumes the body’s resources
  • Overwork without rest: Yin is restored through rest; chronic overwork without adequate recovery depletes it. TCM specifically identifies excessive mental work (overthinking) as Yin-depleting
  • Aging: Natural Yin decline is a TCM description of biological aging
  • Blood loss: Blood is a Yin substance; significant blood loss depletes Yin
  • Unresolved heat disease: Febrile illness that lingers consumes Yin — “heat scorches Yin”
  • Emotional exhaustion: Prolonged anxiety, grief, or fear depletes Yin

Treatment principle: nourish Yin

Yin-nourishing (zi yin 滋阴) herbs are sweet, cool, and moistening. They are the opposite of the bitter, cold, draining herbs used for excess heat — they replenish rather than drain. Key herbs include:

  • Shu Di Huang (prepared Rehmannia) — the primary Kidney Yin tonic
  • Mai Men Dong (Ophiopogon) — nourishes Lung and Stomach Yin
  • Gou Qi Zi (Lycium / goji berry) — nourishes Liver and Kidney Yin
  • Nu Zhen Zi (Ligustrum) — nourishes Liver and Kidney Yin gently
  • Bai Shao (white peony) — nourishes Liver Blood and Yin

The classical formula is Liu Wei Di Huang Wan (六味地黄丸, “Six-Ingredient Pill with Rehmannia”) — the foundational Kidney Yin tonic formula, consisting of three nourishing herbs and three draining herbs in elegant balance.
