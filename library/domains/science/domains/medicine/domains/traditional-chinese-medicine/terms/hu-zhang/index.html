Hu Zhang

2026-04-02 虎杖 — 'tiger stick,' the dried root and rhizome of Japanese knotweed used in TCM to invigorate blood, dispel stasis, clear heat, resolve dampness, and relieve toxicity

Hu Zhang (虎杖, “tiger stick”) is the dried root and rhizome of Japanese knotweed (Reynoutria japonica Houtt., syn. Polygonum cuspidatum), used in Traditional Chinese Medicine for over two thousand years. The name refers to the plant’s stem markings, which resemble the striped pattern of a tiger’s skin, and to the stout, cane-like stems that recall a walking stick.

Classification

In the TCM materia medica classification system, Hu Zhang belongs to the category of herbs that invigorate blood and dispel stasis (huó xuè qū yū 活血祛瘀). It is simultaneously classified among herbs that clear heat and resolve dampness — an unusual dual classification that reflects the herb’s broad clinical utility.

Nature: Slightly cold (wēi hán 微寒) Taste: Bitter (kǔ 苦) Channel entry: Liver, Gallbladder, Lung

The bitter taste and cold nature place Hu Zhang among herbs that drain, descend, and clear heat — consistent with its anthraquinone content (emodin and related compounds) which produces purgative and anti-inflammatory effects. The channel entry to Liver and Gallbladder indicates its primary clinical sphere: conditions involving blood stasis, pain, and damp-heat in the hepatobiliary system.

Historical sources

Hu Zhang appears in the Shennong Bencao Jing (Divine Farmer’s Classic of Materia Medica), the foundational pharmacopoeia of Chinese medicine compiled around 200 CE, though drawing on older oral tradition. The Shennong Bencao Jing classifies it among the middle-grade herbs — those that address disease and may have some toxicity with prolonged use, as distinct from superior-grade herbs (tonics taken long-term) and inferior-grade herbs (potent agents used short-term for acute conditions).

Li Shizhen’s Bencao Gangmu (Compendium of Materia Medica, 1596) expanded the herb’s description, documenting its use for:

  • Painful conditions of the joints and muscles (bì zhèng 痹证)
  • Jaundice and hepatobiliary damp-heat
  • Amenorrhea and postpartum blood stasis
  • Cough with phlegm-heat in the Lung
  • Burns and skin lesions (topical application)
  • Snake and insect bites

Clinical actions

The Chinese Pharmacopoeia recognizes the following actions for Hu Zhang [bensky2004]:

Invigorates blood and dispels stasis (huó xuè qū yū 活血祛瘀): Hu Zhang moves blood and breaks up stasis. It is used for amenorrhea (absence of menstruation due to blood stasis), dysmenorrhea (painful menstruation), postpartum abdominal pain with retained lochia, and traumatic injuries with swelling and pain. The blood-moving action is attributed pharmacologically to resveratrol and polydatin, which inhibit platelet aggregation and improve microcirculation.

Clears heat and resolves dampness (qīng rè lì shī 清热利湿): Hu Zhang drains damp-heat from the Liver and Gallbladder. It is used for jaundice, gallstones, urinary tract infections (lin syndrome with damp-heat), and vaginal discharge with damp-heat signs. The bitter, cold nature and Liver/Gallbladder channel entry make it specific for hepatobiliary damp-heat — a pattern characterized by bitter taste, nausea, abdominal distension, dark urine, and icteric sclera.

Clears heat and relieves toxicity (qīng rè jiě dú 清热解毒): Hu Zhang is applied topically as a poultice or wash for burns, skin sores, and snake bites. The antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties of emodin and resveratrol provide a pharmacological basis for this traditional use.

Resolves phlegm and stops cough (huà tán zhǐ ké 化痰止咳): Hu Zhang clears phlegm-heat from the Lung. It is used for cough with thick yellow sputum, a presentation corresponding to the phlegm-heat pattern.

Promotes bowel movement (tōng biàn 通便): Through its emodin content, Hu Zhang acts as a mild laxative. This action is weaker than that of Da Huang (rhubarb root), which contains higher anthraquinone concentrations, but is clinically useful as a secondary benefit in formulas addressing constipation with heat signs.

Dosage and preparation

Standard dosage: 9–15 grams of dried root/rhizome per day, decocted in water. Higher doses (up to 30 g) are sometimes used for acute conditions under close supervision. The herb is typically combined with other herbs in formula, not used as a single-herb prescription. Common formula pairings include:

  • With Dang Gui (Angelica sinensis) and Chuan Xiong (Ligusticum chuanxiong) for blood stasis with pain
  • With Yin Chen (Artemisia capillaris) and Zhi Zi (Gardenia jasminoides) for jaundice with damp-heat
  • With Bei Mu (Fritillaria) for phlegm-heat cough

Contraindications

Hu Zhang is contraindicated in pregnancy (blood-moving herbs risk miscarriage) and during heavy menstrual bleeding (it could exacerbate hemorrhage). Its cold nature makes it inappropriate for patients with Spleen-Stomach cold deficiency (manifesting as chronic diarrhea, cold abdomen, poor appetite) without warming herbs to counterbalance. Prolonged high-dose use should be avoided due to the hepatotoxic potential of emodin at sustained high concentrations.

Modern pharmacological validation

The traditional actions of Hu Zhang map remarkably well onto its known pharmacology:

TCM action Pharmacological correlate
Invigorates blood, dispels stasis Resveratrol inhibits platelet aggregation; polydatin improves microcirculation
Clears heat Anti-inflammatory: resveratrol inhibits NF-κB, COX-2; emodin inhibits NLRP3 inflammasome
Resolves dampness Diuretic and choleretic effects; emodin promotes bile secretion
Relieves toxicity Antimicrobial: emodin active against S. aureus, H. pylori; resveratrol antiviral
Promotes bowel movement Emodin stimulates colonic secretion and motility

This convergence does not prove that TCM practitioners understood the molecular mechanisms. It demonstrates that centuries of clinical observation can identify therapeutically relevant effects that modern pharmacology later explains at the molecular level.
