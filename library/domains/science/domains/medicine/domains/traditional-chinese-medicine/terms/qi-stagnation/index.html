Qi Stagnation

2026-04-02 气滞 (qi zhi) — the condition where Qi flow is impeded or stuck, producing distension, pain that moves, emotional frustration, and sighing — the most common excess pattern in clinical TCM practice

Qi stagnation (qi zhi 气滞) is the condition where Qi flow through the body’s meridians and organ-function systems is impeded, slowed, or stuck. It is the most common excess pattern in clinical TCM practice — the default pathological response to emotional constraint, chronic stress, and sedentary modern life.

The concept follows directly from TCM’s understanding of Qi as circulating operational force. Qi should flow: smoothly, in the correct direction, through every organ system and meridian. When flow is impeded, the Qi “stagnates” — it accumulates where it should pass through, creating pressure, distension, and dysfunction.

¶The Liver connection

Qi stagnation is most commonly associated with the Liver system, because the Liver’s primary function in TCM is to ensure the smooth flow of Qi throughout the body (shu xie 疏泄). When the Liver’s flow-regulating function is impaired — most often by emotional constraint, unexpressed anger, or chronic frustration — Qi flow stagnates throughout the body, producing the pattern known as Liver Qi stagnation (gan qi yu jie 肝气郁结).

Liver Qi stagnation is arguably the single most common pattern in modern TCM practice. The characteristic presentation:

  • Distension and fullness: A feeling of pressure in the chest, hypochondrium (rib-side area), epigastrium, or abdomen — not a sharp pain, but a bloated, pressurized sensation that fluctuates with emotional state
  • Emotional frustration: Irritability, mood swings, depression, a feeling of being “stuck” or unable to express oneself. The emotional quality and the physical quality mirror each other — the Qi is stuck, and the person feels stuck
  • Sighing: Spontaneous deep sighing is diagnostic. The sigh is the body’s attempt to move the stagnant Qi in the chest
  • Distension that moves: Unlike blood stasis pain (fixed) or dampness heaviness (settling), Qi stagnation distension can shift location
  • Wiry pulse (xian 弦): The most reliable pulse finding — the pulse feels taut, like a guitar string, reflecting the tension in the system
  • Symptoms worsen with stress: The hallmark temporal pattern. The distension, pain, and emotional symptoms increase with emotional stress and decrease with relaxation, exercise, or emotional expression

¶The cascade: what Qi stagnation produces

Qi stagnation is clinically significant not just for its own symptoms but because it is the upstream cause of several other pathological patterns:

Qi stagnation → Blood stasis: Qi moves blood. When Qi stagnates, blood movement slows and eventually stagnates. This is the most common progression — Liver Qi stagnation producing menstrual pain, fixed masses, or cardiovascular dysfunction over time.

Qi stagnation → Heat: Stagnant Qi generates heat, the way friction generates warmth. Prolonged Qi stagnation transforms into Liver Fire — the irritability intensifies into outbursts of anger, the face flushes red, headaches become throbbing and temporal.

Qi stagnation → Phlegm: When Qi stagnation impairs the Spleen’s transformative function, fluids accumulate and congeal into Phlegm. The classic presentation is “plum pit Qi” (mei he qi 梅核气) — a sensation of something stuck in the throat that can neither be swallowed nor expelled. This is TCM’s description of what biomedicine calls globus hystericus.

Qi stagnation → Damp-heat: Stagnant Qi impairs fluid metabolism (generating dampness) while simultaneously generating heat. The combined result is damp-heat — commonly settling in the Liver/Gallbladder system.

¶Treatment principle: soothe the Liver, regulate Qi

The treatment of Qi stagnation involves herbs that move and regulate Qi flow:

  • Chai Hu (Bupleurum chinense) — the primary Liver Qi-regulating herb; spreads Liver Qi, relieves constraint
  • Xiang Fu (Cyperus rotundus) — moves Qi, alleviates pain; called “the general of Qi regulation”
  • Chen Pi (aged tangerine peel) — regulates Qi in the middle burner, resolves Phlegm
  • Bo He (peppermint) — disperses Liver Qi, clears the head
  • Yu Jin (Curcuma wenyujin) — moves Qi and blood, particularly for Liver constraint with blood stasis

The classical formula is Xiao Yao San (逍遥散, “Free and Easy Wanderer”) — detailed in Herbal Medicine — which soothes Liver Qi stagnation while simultaneously nourishing Blood and supporting the Spleen.

¶Qi stagnation and modern life

TCM practitioners note that Qi stagnation has become the background pattern of modern urban life. Sedentary work, emotional suppression in professional settings, chronic screen use, and the mismatch between the body’s need for movement and the reality of desk-bound existence create conditions that the TCM framework identifies as systemic Qi stagnation. This is not a claim about modernity’s pathology — it is an observation that the pattern TCM calls Qi stagnation maps onto the symptom clusters modern medicine sees in stress-related functional disorders: irritable bowel syndrome, tension headaches, non-cardiac chest pain, and functional dyspepsia.
