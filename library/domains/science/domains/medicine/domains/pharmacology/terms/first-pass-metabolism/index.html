First-Pass Metabolism

2026-04-02 The phenomenon by which an orally administered drug is extensively metabolized by the liver and intestinal wall before reaching systemic circulation, dramatically reducing the fraction of active drug available

First-pass metabolism (the first-pass effect) is the metabolic degradation of an orally administered drug as it passes through the intestinal wall and liver before reaching systemic circulation. After absorption from the gut, all blood drains into the portal vein and passes through the liver before entering the general circulation. The liver’s metabolic enzymes — particularly the cytochrome P450 family — can convert a large fraction of the drug into inactive metabolites during this first passage, dramatically reducing the drug’s bioavailability.

First-pass metabolism is the primary reason that many drugs require much higher oral doses than intravenous doses to achieve the same plasma concentration. It is also the primary reason that resveratrol — despite dramatic effects in cell culture — has disappointed in clinical trials: oral resveratrol is rapidly conjugated by glucuronidation and sulfation in the intestinal wall and liver, leaving peak plasma concentrations of free resveratrol orders of magnitude below the concentrations used in vitro.

¶The metabolic gauntlet

An orally administered molecule must survive three sequential metabolic barriers:

  1. Intestinal lumen: Gastric acid, digestive enzymes, and gut microbiota can degrade the compound before absorption
  2. Intestinal wall: Enterocytes (intestinal epithelial cells) contain CYP3A4 and other metabolic enzymes, plus efflux transporters (P-glycoprotein) that pump drug molecules back into the lumen
  3. Liver: Hepatocytes contain the full complement of CYP enzymes, glucuronosyltransferases (UGTs), sulfotransferases (SULTs), and other conjugation enzymes

The net result is that for some compounds, less than 1% of the ingested dose reaches systemic circulation in active form.

¶Clinical significance

First-pass metabolism explains why:

  • Some drugs cannot be given orally (nitroglycerin, lidocaine) — they are almost entirely destroyed on first pass
  • Sublingual, transdermal, rectal, and inhaled routes bypass the liver and achieve higher bioavailability
  • Grapefruit juice interacts with many drugs — furanocoumarins in grapefruit inhibit intestinal CYP3A4, reducing first-pass metabolism and increasing drug levels
  • Polydatin (the glucoside of resveratrol) has better bioavailability than free resveratrol — the glucose moiety allows active transport via SGLT1, bypassing passive diffusion limitations
