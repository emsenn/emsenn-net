Polyphenol

2026-04-02 A large class of plant-produced organic compounds characterized by multiple phenol rings — including flavonoids, stilbenoids, tannins, and phenolic acids — with antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and cardioprotective properties
Defines Polyphenol, polyphenols

Polyphenols are a large and structurally diverse class of organic compounds produced by plants, characterized by the presence of multiple phenol groups — aromatic rings bearing hydroxyl (-OH) substituents. Over 8,000 polyphenolic compounds have been identified in plants. They are the most abundant antioxidants in the human diet, present in fruits, vegetables, tea, coffee, wine, chocolate, and medicinal herbs.

Plants produce polyphenols primarily as defensive compounds — protecting against ultraviolet radiation, pathogens, and herbivores. This defensive function is why stressed or damaged plants often produce higher concentrations of polyphenols than unstressed plants, and why phytoalexins (stress-induced defensive compounds) are frequently polyphenolic.

Major classes

Flavonoids (~6,000 known compounds): The largest subclass. Includes quercetin, catechins, epicatechin, kaempferol, anthocyanins. Found in virtually all fruits, vegetables, and medicinal herbs. Responsible for many of the colors of flowers and fruits.

Stilbenoids: Includes resveratrol and polydatin (piceid). Found in grapes, peanuts, and most abundantly in Japanese knotweed. Stilbenoids are produced as phytoalexins — stress-response compounds.

Tannins: Large polyphenolic molecules that bind proteins. Responsible for the astringent taste of tea, red wine, and unripe fruit. Divided into condensed tannins (proanthocyanidins) and hydrolyzable tannins (gallotannins and ellagitannins).

Phenolic acids: Simple phenol derivatives including gallic acid, caffeic acid, and chlorogenic acid. Abundant in coffee, berries, and whole grains.

Anthraquinones: Include emodin and related compounds. Found in rhubarb, senna, aloe, and Japanese knotweed. Distinguished by their laxative activity.

Pharmacological significance

Polyphenols as a class share several pharmacological properties, though the potency and specificity of individual compounds varies enormously:

Antioxidant activity: Polyphenols scavenge reactive oxygen species (free radicals) through their phenol hydroxyl groups. This is their most widely promoted property, though the clinical significance of dietary antioxidant supplementation remains debated — the in vitro antioxidant activity of a compound does not predict its in vivo therapeutic benefit, because bioavailability, metabolism, and the complexity of in vivo redox biology intervene.

Anti-inflammatory activity: Many polyphenols inhibit NF-κB, COX-2, and pro-inflammatory cytokine production. This activity is more consistently demonstrated in vivo than pure antioxidant effects and likely accounts for much of the health benefit associated with polyphenol-rich diets.

Cardiovascular effects: Epidemiological evidence consistently associates polyphenol-rich diets with reduced cardiovascular risk. Mechanisms include improved endothelial function, reduced platelet aggregation, and anti-inflammatory effects.

The bioavailability challenge

Most dietary polyphenols have low oral bioavailability. They are extensively metabolized by intestinal and hepatic enzymes and by the gut microbiome. The compounds that reach systemic circulation are often metabolites rather than the parent compound. This means the therapeutic effects of polyphenol-rich foods and herbs may be mediated by metabolites rather than the compounds measured in vitro — a major challenge for translational research.
