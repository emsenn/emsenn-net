Resveratrol

2026-04-02 A stilbenoid phytoalexin produced by plants under stress, studied extensively for anti-inflammatory, cardioprotective, and antioxidant properties — most commercially sourced from Japanese knotweed rhizome

Resveratrol (trans-3,5,4’-trihydroxystilbene) is a polyphenolic stilbenoid produced by plants as a phytoalexin — a defensive compound synthesized in response to injury, ultraviolet radiation, or fungal infection. It is found in grape skins, peanuts, blueberries, and most abundantly in the root and rhizome of Japanese knotweed (Reynoutria japonica), which produces resveratrol at concentrations 50–100 times higher than grape skins and is the primary commercial source for dietary supplements.

¶The French paradox

Resveratrol entered Western biomedical consciousness through the “French paradox” — the epidemiological observation, reported by Renaud and de Lorgeril in The Lancet in 1992, that French populations had lower rates of coronary heart disease than predicted by their saturated fat intake [renaud1992]. Red wine consumption was proposed as the protective factor, and resveratrol — present in grape skins and therefore concentrated in red wine — was identified as a candidate molecule responsible for the cardioprotective effect. Whether resveratrol at the concentrations present in wine is sufficient to explain the epidemiological observation remains contested. The paradox may reflect dietary pattern effects (the Mediterranean diet), reporting artifacts, or multiple interacting factors rather than a single compound.

¶Molecular targets

Resveratrol interacts with a broad array of molecular targets, which is both its pharmacological interest and the source of skepticism about its specificity:

SIRT1 (Sirtuin 1): Howitz et al. (2003) reported that resveratrol activates SIRT1, a NAD+-dependent protein deacetylase involved in cellular stress response, metabolism, and aging [howitz2003]. This finding launched a wave of aging research and commercial interest. Baur and Sinclair (2006) showed that resveratrol-fed mice on high-calorie diets had improved health markers and extended survival compared to controls [baur2006]. However, subsequent work revealed that the original SIRT1 activation assay used a fluorescent substrate that produced artifactual results — resveratrol’s activation of SIRT1 is indirect (via AMPK) rather than direct. The downstream metabolic effects are real; the mechanism is more complex than initially proposed.

NF-κB pathway: Resveratrol inhibits NF-κB (nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells), a transcription factor that drives inflammatory gene expression. This inhibition underlies many of resveratrol’s anti-inflammatory effects in cell culture and animal models.

COX-2 (cyclooxygenase-2): Resveratrol inhibits COX-2, the same enzyme targeted by NSAIDs like ibuprofen. This provides a molecular basis for the anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties attributed to Japanese knotweed in traditional medicine.

AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase): Resveratrol activates AMPK, a master regulator of cellular energy homeostasis. AMPK activation increases fatty acid oxidation, glucose uptake, and mitochondrial biogenesis — effects that mimic caloric restriction.

¶Bioavailability problem

The central limitation of resveratrol pharmacology is bioavailability. Oral resveratrol is rapidly absorbed but extensively metabolized by first-pass metabolism in the intestine and liver, producing glucuronide and sulfate conjugates. Peak plasma concentration of free resveratrol after a 25 mg oral dose is approximately 1–5 ng/mL — orders of magnitude below the concentrations used in most cell culture studies (typically 10–100 μM). This disconnect between in vitro activity and achievable in vivo concentrations is the fundamental challenge for translating resveratrol research into clinical therapeutics.

Several strategies address this: higher dosing (clinical trials have used up to 5 g/day), formulation with absorption enhancers (piperine from black pepper inhibits glucuronidation), nanoparticle delivery, and the use of more bioavailable analogues like pterostilbene (a dimethylated resveratrol derivative found in blueberries).

¶Polydatin

Polydatin (piceid) is the glucoside form of resveratrol — resveratrol bonded to a glucose molecule. It is the predominant form in Japanese knotweed rhizome, where it occurs at higher concentrations than free resveratrol. Polydatin achieves serum concentrations 3–4 times higher than free resveratrol at the same oral dose, because it uses the active SGLT1 (sodium-glucose linked transporter 1) in the intestine rather than relying on passive diffusion. This dramatically better oral bioavailability, combined with greater water solubility and metabolic stability, has led some researchers to argue that polydatin, not free resveratrol, is the therapeutically relevant form in traditional herbal preparations — and that the bioavailability problem afflicting resveratrol research may partly reflect studying the wrong compound [burns2002].

¶Clinical status

Despite thousands of preclinical studies, resveratrol has not yet produced a breakthrough clinical application. Phase I–III trials have been conducted for cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, Alzheimer’s disease, and various cancers. Results are mixed: some trials show modest improvements in inflammatory markers, insulin sensitivity, or endothelial function; others show no significant effect. No major regulatory body has approved resveratrol as a drug. In the United States, it is sold as a dietary supplement under DSHEA (Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act). In the United Kingdom, trans-resveratrol is authorized as a novel food with a maximum daily intake of 150 mg for adults. It is typically sold as purified extract from Japanese knotweed rhizome, in capsule form at doses of 100–500 mg/day.

The gap between resveratrol’s dramatic effects in cell culture, its promising effects in animal models, and its modest effects in human trials is a case study in translational pharmacology — and in the difference between a compound’s biological activity and its therapeutic utility.
