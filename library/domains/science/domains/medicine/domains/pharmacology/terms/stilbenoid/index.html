Stilbenoid

2026-04-02 A class of polyphenolic compounds based on a 1,2-diphenylethylene backbone, produced by plants as phytoalexins — including resveratrol, the compound that made Japanese knotweed pharmacologically significant
Defines Stilbenoid, stilbenoids

Stilbenoids are a class of polyphenolic compounds based on a 1,2-diphenylethylene (stilbene) backbone — two phenyl rings connected by a two-carbon ethylene bridge. They are produced by plants primarily as phytoalexins — defensive compounds synthesized in response to pathogen attack, UV radiation, or tissue injury. Stilbenoids are synthesized via the phenylpropanoid pathway from phenylalanine, with stilbene synthase catalyzing the final cyclization step.

Key stilbenoid compounds

Resveratrol (trans-3,5,4’-trihydroxystilbene): The most studied stilbenoid. Produced in large quantities by Japanese knotweed rhizome, grape skins, peanuts, and blueberries. Inhibits platelet aggregation, NF-κB, COX-2, and activates AMPK and SIRT1 pathways.

Polydatin (piceid): The glucoside form of resveratrol — resveratrol bonded to a glucose molecule. The predominant stilbenoid in Japanese knotweed rhizome. Better oral bioavailability than free resveratrol due to active intestinal transport via SGLT1.

Piceatannol: A hydroxylated derivative of resveratrol with stronger antioxidant and immunomodulatory activity. Found in grapes, passion fruit, and knotweed.

Pterostilbene: A dimethylated analogue of resveratrol found primarily in blueberries. Higher oral bioavailability than resveratrol due to reduced susceptibility to glucuronidation.

Distribution

Stilbenoid production is concentrated in a few plant families: Vitaceae (grapes), Polygonaceae (knotweeds, rhubarb), Fabaceae (peanuts), and Dipterocarpaceae (tropical timber trees). Japanese knotweed produces stilbenoids at concentrations 50–100 times higher than grape skins, making it the primary commercial source for resveratrol supplements.
