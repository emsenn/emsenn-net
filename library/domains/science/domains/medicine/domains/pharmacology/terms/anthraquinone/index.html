Anthraquinone

2026-04-02 A class of aromatic organic compounds with a tricyclic ring structure, found in rhubarb, senna, aloe, and Japanese knotweed — characterized by laxative, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial activity

Anthraquinones are a class of aromatic organic compounds based on the 9,10-anthracenedione skeleton — a tricyclic structure consisting of three fused six-membered rings with two ketone groups. They are produced by several plant families, most notably Polygonaceae (knotweed, rhubarb), Fabaceae (senna), Asphodelaceae (aloe), and Rhamnaceae (cascara sagrada).

Anthraquinones are the chemical basis of the oldest known class of laxative drugs. Senna, rhubarb root, cascara, and aloe have been used as purgatives across multiple medical traditions for millennia. The laxative mechanism is well characterized: anthraquinone glycosides pass through the small intestine unabsorbed, are hydrolyzed by colonic bacteria to release the active aglycone, which then stimulates colonic motility and inhibits water reabsorption — producing the cathartic effect.

¶Key anthraquinone compounds

Emodin (1,3,8-trihydroxy-6-methylanthraquinone): Found in Japanese knotweed, rhubarb, and senna. The most pharmacologically studied anthraquinone, with anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and anticancer activity beyond its laxative effect.

Chrysophanol: Found in rhubarb and senna. Laxative and hepatoprotective.

Rhein: Found in rhubarb. Anti-inflammatory and nephroprotective in animal studies.

Aloe-emodin: Found in aloe. Laxative and potentially anticancer; classified by IARC as possibly carcinogenic to humans (Group 2B).

Physcion: Found in Japanese knotweed. Hepatoprotective and antifungal.

¶Safety

Chronic use of anthraquinone laxatives can cause electrolyte depletion (particularly potassium), melanosis coli (darkening of the colonic mucosa), and potential hepatotoxicity and nephrotoxicity at high doses. Traditional formularies limit treatment duration and combine anthraquinone-containing herbs with harmonizing agents to buffer their drastic action — a practice that modern toxicology confirms as prudent.
