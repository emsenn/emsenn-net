Cytochrome P450

2026-04-02 A superfamily of heme-containing enzymes — primarily in the liver — that metabolize the majority of pharmaceutical drugs, dietary compounds, and environmental toxins through oxidation reactions

The cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzymes are a superfamily of heme-containing monooxygenases found primarily in the endoplasmic reticulum of hepatocytes (liver cells) and enterocytes (intestinal cells). They catalyze the oxidation of a vast range of substrates — pharmaceutical drugs, dietary compounds, endogenous hormones, and environmental toxins — and are responsible for the metabolism of approximately 75% of all clinically used drugs.

The name reflects their biochemistry: “cytochrome” (colored cellular protein), “P450” (the protein absorbs light at 450 nm wavelength when bound to carbon monoxide). Humans have 57 CYP genes organized into 18 families and 44 subfamilies.

The major drug-metabolizing CYPs

Five CYP enzymes account for the metabolism of most drugs:

Enzyme % of drug metabolism Key substrates Notable inhibitors
CYP3A4 ~50% Statins, calcium channel blockers, immunosuppressants, many antibiotics Grapefruit juice, ketoconazole, resveratrol
CYP2D6 ~25% Codeine, tamoxifen, many antidepressants, beta-blockers Fluoxetine, paroxetine
CYP2C9 ~10% Warfarin, NSAIDs, phenytoin Fluconazole
CYP2C19 ~5% Proton pump inhibitors, clopidogrel, diazepam Omeprazole
CYP1A2 ~5% Caffeine, theophylline, some antipsychotics Fluvoxamine, resveratrol

Drug interactions

CYP enzymes are the primary mechanism of pharmacokinetic drug interactions. A compound that inhibits a CYP enzyme slows the metabolism of all other drugs that enzyme processes, effectively increasing their blood levels — sometimes to toxic concentrations. A compound that induces a CYP enzyme accelerates metabolism of other drugs, reducing their efficacy.

This is clinically relevant for herbal-drug interactions. Resveratrol inhibits CYP3A4 and CYP1A2, meaning Japanese knotweed preparations can increase blood levels of drugs metabolized by these enzymes. Emodin also affects CYP-mediated metabolism. These interactions are the pharmacological basis for the contraindication of Hu Zhang with certain pharmaceutical medications.

Genetic variation

CYP genes are highly polymorphic — different individuals carry different versions of CYP genes, producing enzymes with different metabolic capacity. CYP2D6 polymorphism is particularly well studied: “poor metabolizers” (lacking functional CYP2D6) accumulate drugs that the enzyme normally clears, while “ultra-rapid metabolizers” (with duplicated CYP2D6 genes) clear drugs so quickly they may not achieve therapeutic levels at standard doses.
