Accelerating Expansion

2026-03-31 The accelerating expansion is the observed fact that the [expansion of the universe](./cosmic-expansion.md) is speeding up rather than slowing down. It was discovered in 1998 and is caused by [dark energy](../../structure/terms/dark-energy.md).

The accelerating expansion is the observed fact that the expansion of the universe is speeding up rather than slowing down. It was discovered in 1998 and is caused by dark energy.

Before 1998, cosmologists expected the expansion to be decelerating. Gravity attracts, so the mutual gravitational pull of all the matter in the universe should slow the expansion over time, like a ball thrown upward. The only question seemed to be whether the expansion would eventually halt and reverse (a “Big Crunch”) or merely slow toward a coast.

Two independent teams – the Supernova Cosmology Project (Saul Perlmutter) and the High-z Supernova Search Team (Brian Schmidt and Adam Riess) – measured distances to distant Type Ia supernovae. These supernovae were fainter than expected, meaning farther away, meaning the expansion has been accelerating. The result earned Perlmutter, Schmidt, and Riess the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics.

In terms of the Friedmann equations, accelerating expansion (a¨>0\ddot{a} > 0) requires a component with sufficiently negative pressure. The cosmological constant Λ\Lambda satisfies this condition. In the Lambda-CDM model, the universe’s expansion decelerated during the matter-dominated era (when matter’s gravitational pull dominated) and began accelerating about 5 billion years ago, when dark energy’s repulsive effect overtook matter’s attractive effect. The expansion will continue to accelerate indefinitely unless the nature of dark energy changes.
