Recombination

Recombination is the epoch approximately 380,000 years after the Big Bang when the universe cooled to about 3,000 kelvin – cool enough for free electrons to combine with atomic nuclei to form neutral atoms. This made the universe transparent to light for the first time.

The name is somewhat misleading: electrons and nuclei had never been combined before, so “combination” would be more accurate. The name persists for historical reasons.

Before recombination, the universe was a hot plasma of free electrons, protons, and helium nuclei, bathed in intense radiation. Photons scattered constantly off free electrons (Thomson scattering), making the universe opaque – no photon could travel far before being deflected. Matter and radiation were tightly coupled.

At recombination, free electrons were captured into atoms. With no free electrons to scatter off, photons could stream freely through space. This moment is called “decoupling” – the moment radiation decoupled from matter. The photons released at decoupling have been traveling through the universe ever since, redshifting as space expands. They are observed today as the cosmic microwave background.

Recombination marks a sharp boundary in observational cosmology. We cannot see anything earlier using electromagnetic radiation (the universe was opaque). The CMB is, literally, the oldest light we can observe. Everything we know about the universe before recombination comes from indirect evidence: the primordial element abundances from nucleosynthesis and, potentially, primordial gravitational waves.
