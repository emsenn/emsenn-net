Cosmic Expansion

2026-03-31

Cosmic expansion is the observed increase in distances between gravitationally unbound objects in the universe, described mathematically by the scale factor a(t)a(t).

Expansion does not mean galaxies are flying apart through space like shrapnel from an explosion. It means space itself is growing. The distances between galaxies (and between galaxy clusters) increase over time because the metric of spacetime is changing. Objects gravitationally bound to each other – stars within a galaxy, planets within a solar system, atoms within a molecule – do not expand, because local gravitational and electromagnetic forces overwhelm the expansion.

Edwin Hubble provided the first observational evidence in 1929, showing that distant galaxies are receding from us with velocities proportional to their distance: v=H0dv = H_0 d. This relation – Hubble’s law – follows directly from uniform expansion. If all distances grow by the same factor over time, then the recession velocity of any object is proportional to its distance.

The dynamics of expansion are governed by the Friedmann equations, which relate the expansion rate to the energy content of the universe. Whether expansion decelerates or accelerates depends on the balance between matter (which decelerates) and dark energy (which accelerates). Current observations show the expansion is accelerating.
