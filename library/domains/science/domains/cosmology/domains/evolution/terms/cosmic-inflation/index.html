Cosmic Inflation

2026-03-31
Defines Cosmic Inflation, cosmic inflation, inflation
Requires
  • big-bang
  • cosmological-principle

Cosmic inflation is a hypothesized period of exponentially rapid expansion in the first fraction of a second after the Big Bang, proposed to explain several otherwise puzzling features of the observed universe.

Alan Guth proposed inflation in 1981 to solve three problems:

The horizon problem. The cosmic microwave background has nearly the same temperature in every direction, even in regions so far apart that light has not had time to travel between them since the Big Bang. How did they reach the same temperature without being in causal contact? Inflation solves this: those regions were in causal contact before inflation, which then stretched them far apart.

The flatness problem. The universe is observed to be spatially flat (or very nearly so). In standard Big Bang cosmology without inflation, spatial flatness is an unstable condition – any tiny deviation from flatness in the early universe would have been amplified over time. Inflation drives the geometry toward flatness by stretching space so dramatically that any initial curvature becomes undetectable, like the surface of a balloon inflated to enormous size.

The monopole problem. Certain grand unified theories predict the production of magnetic monopoles in the early universe. None have been observed. Inflation dilutes any pre-existing monopoles to undetectable densities.

Inflation also provides a natural origin for the tiny density fluctuations visible in the CMB: quantum fluctuations in the inflaton field (the hypothetical field driving inflation) were stretched to macroscopic scales and seeded the large-scale structure of the universe.

Inflation is widely accepted as the best available solution to these problems, but the inflaton field has not been directly detected, and the mechanism that started and ended inflation remains speculative. It is a well-motivated hypothesis, not a confirmed theory.
