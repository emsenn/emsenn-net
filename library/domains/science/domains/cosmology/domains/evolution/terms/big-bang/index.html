Big Bang

2026-03-31

The Big Bang is the event approximately 13.8 billion years ago from which the observable universe expanded from an extremely hot, dense state. It is not an explosion in space. It is the beginning of the expansion of spacetime itself.

The Big Bang model follows from general relativity applied to an expanding universe. If the universe is expanding now, it was smaller and denser in the past. Extrapolate backward and you reach a state of arbitrarily high density and temperature. The model does not describe what happened at the very first instant (the “singularity” at t=0t = 0 is a sign that general relativity breaks down at extreme densities), but it makes specific, testable predictions about what happened shortly after:

  1. The universe was hot enough for nuclear fusion in its first few minutes, producing hydrogen, helium, and lithium in precise ratios that match observation.
  2. The universe transitioned from opaque plasma to transparent gas about 380,000 years later, releasing the cosmic microwave background.
  3. The universe has been expanding ever since, and the expansion rate is described by the Hubble parameter.

The name “Big Bang” was coined by Fred Hoyle in 1949 as a derisive label for a theory he opposed. It stuck. The name is misleading – there was no “bang” and no pre-existing space for it to happen in – but it is universally used.
