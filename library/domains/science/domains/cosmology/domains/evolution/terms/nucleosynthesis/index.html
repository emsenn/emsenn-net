Nucleosynthesis is the formation of atomic nuclei. Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) refers specifically to the formation of light elements – hydrogen, helium, and trace amounts of lithium and deuterium – in the first few minutes after the Big Bang, when the entire universe was hot and dense enough for nuclear fusion.

At about one second after the Big Bang, the temperature was roughly 10 billion kelvin. Protons and neutrons were in thermal equilibrium. As the universe expanded and cooled, neutrons began converting to protons (neutrons are slightly heavier and decay into protons via the weak nuclear force). By about three minutes, the temperature dropped to about one billion kelvin – still hot enough for fusion. Protons and neutrons combined to form deuterium, then helium-4, with small amounts of helium-3, lithium-7, and other light nuclei.

By about 20 minutes, the universe had cooled too much for further fusion. The result: roughly 75% hydrogen and 25% helium by mass, with trace amounts of deuterium and lithium. These ratios are precise predictions of the Big Bang model, and they match the observed primordial abundances measured in old, chemically unprocessed environments.

BBN is one of the three pillars of evidence for the Big Bang (along with the cosmic microwave background and the observed expansion). The predicted deuterium abundance is particularly constraining: it depends sensitively on the density of baryonic matter, providing an independent measurement of Ωb\Omega_b that agrees with CMB observations.

All elements heavier than lithium were produced later, inside stars – a distinct process called stellar nucleosynthesis.
