Hubble Parameter

2026-03-31

The Hubble parameter H(t)H(t) is the rate of cosmic expansion at a given time, defined as the ratio of the rate of change of the scale factor to the scale factor itself:

H(t)=a˙(t)a(t)H(t) = \frac{\dot{a}(t)}{a(t)}

Its present-day value, H0H_0, is called the Hubble constant, though it is constant only in the sense that it is evaluated at the current epoch – the Hubble parameter changes over cosmic time.

H0H_0 sets the relationship between a galaxy’s distance and its recession velocity: v=H0dv = H_0 d. This is Hubble’s law. The current best measurements place H0H_0 at approximately 67-73 km/s/Mpc (kilometers per second per megaparsec), meaning that for every megaparsec (about 3.26 million light-years) of distance, the recession velocity increases by 67-73 km/s.

The range in the quoted value reflects the “Hubble tension” – a persistent disagreement between the value measured from the cosmic microwave background (Planck, ~67.4 km/s/Mpc) and the value measured from local distance ladder methods using supernovae and Cepheid variables (~73 km/s/Mpc). Whether this tension reflects systematic measurement errors or new physics is an active area of research.

The inverse of the Hubble constant, 1/H01/H_0, gives a rough estimate of the age of the universe (approximately 14 billion years), though the actual age depends on the full expansion history.
