The First Three Minutes

2026-03-31 Run the expansion backward. The universe gets hotter and denser. At some point, it was hot enough for nuclear fusion -- not inside stars, but everywhere, all at once. This text traces the first few minutes of the universe, when the light elements were forged.

Run the expansion backward. The universe gets hotter and denser. At some point, it was hot enough for nuclear fusion – not inside stars, but everywhere, all at once. This text traces the first few minutes of the universe, when the light elements were forged.

¶Before nucleosynthesis

The standard Big Bang model describes the universe as beginning in an extremely hot, dense state. The model does not describe the very first instant – the “singularity” at t=0t = 0 is a signal that general relativity breaks down. But from about 10−610^{-6} seconds onward, the physics is well understood.

At one microsecond: the universe is a quark-gluon plasma at trillions of kelvin. Quarks combine into protons and neutrons as the universe cools. At one second: the temperature is about 10 billion kelvin. Protons and neutrons exist in roughly a 7:1 ratio (protons are lighter and therefore favored). Neutrinos decouple from matter – they stream freely through the universe from this point on.

¶Nucleosynthesis: minutes 1-20

At about one minute, the temperature drops to about 1 billion kelvin. Now conditions allow nuclear fusion. Protons and neutrons begin combining:

  1. A proton and a neutron form deuterium (heavy hydrogen).
  2. Two deuterium nuclei fuse to form helium-4.
  3. Small amounts of helium-3, lithium-7, and beryllium-7 are produced as side reactions.

By about 20 minutes, the universe has cooled below the threshold for fusion. The nuclear reactions freeze out. The result: roughly 75% hydrogen, 25% helium-4 by mass, with trace amounts of deuterium (0.003%) and lithium-7 (10−1010^{-10}).

These are the primordial abundances. Every element heavier than lithium was produced later, inside stars.

¶Why the predictions matter

Nucleosynthesis is one of the most precise tests of the Big Bang model. The predicted abundances depend on a single parameter: the density of baryonic matter. The observed primordial deuterium abundance – measured in high-redshift gas clouds that have undergone minimal chemical processing – matches the prediction. The helium-4 abundance matches. The lithium abundance is slightly discrepant (the “cosmological lithium problem”), but the overall agreement is strong.

The baryon density inferred from nucleosynthesis (Ωb≈0.049\Omega_b \approx 0.049) agrees with the completely independent measurement from the cosmic microwave background. Two different windows into the early universe – one from the first minutes, one from 380,000 years later – give the same answer.

¶Why this matters

The title is borrowed from Steven Weinberg’s 1977 book of the same name. Weinberg’s point was that the first three minutes are the most consequential: the nuclear reactions that occurred in that brief window set the chemical composition of the universe for the next 13.8 billion years. Everything that followed – the first stars, galaxies, planets, chemistry, life – was built from the materials those three minutes produced.
