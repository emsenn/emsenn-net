From Recombination to the CMB

2026-03-31

  • Why this matters

For the first 380,000 years, the universe was opaque. Photons could not travel more than a short distance before scattering off free electrons. Then, in a geologically brief period, the universe became transparent. The light released in that transition is still visible today.

The opaque universe

After nucleosynthesis, the universe was a hot plasma: atomic nuclei (mostly hydrogen and helium), free electrons, and photons, all tightly coupled. Photons scattered incessantly off free electrons – a process called Thomson scattering. The mean free path of a photon was short. The universe was like the interior of a star: luminous but opaque, because light could not travel in a straight line.

During this epoch, radiation pressure from the photons opposed the gravitational collapse of matter. The result was acoustic oscillations – sound waves propagating through the plasma. Regions of high density compressed, heated, and rebounded, creating a pattern of oscillation that would leave a permanent imprint.

Recombination

By about 380,000 years after the Big Bang, the universe had cooled to approximately 3,000 kelvin. At this temperature, free electrons had slowed enough to be captured by nuclei, forming neutral atoms (mostly hydrogen). This is recombination.

With no free electrons to scatter off, photons were suddenly free. They streamed outward in every direction, carrying with them a snapshot of the universe at that moment – its temperature, its density fluctuations, its acoustic oscillation pattern. This moment is called “decoupling”: the moment radiation decoupled from matter.

The cosmic microwave background

Those photons have been traveling through space for 13.8 billion years. The expansion of the universe has stretched their wavelengths by a factor of about 1,100, cooling them from ~3,000 K to 2.725 K – placing them in the microwave band of the electromagnetic spectrum. This is the cosmic microwave background (CMB).

The CMB comes from every direction. It is not the light of any particular object. It is the glow of the entire universe at age 380,000 years, redshifted to microwaves.

What the CMB tells us

The CMB is almost perfectly uniform – the same temperature in every direction to one part in 100,000. This confirms the cosmological principle: the early universe was homogeneous and isotropic.

The tiny deviations from uniformity – the anisotropies – are the most valuable data in cosmology. Their angular power spectrum (how much temperature variation exists at each angular scale) encodes:

  • The total density of the universe (which determines its geometry)
  • The ratio of baryonic matter to dark matter
  • The Hubble parameter
  • The amplitude and tilt of the primordial density fluctuations
  • Whether inflation occurred

The satellite missions COBE (1992), WMAP (2001-2010), and Planck (2009-2013) measured these anisotropies with increasing precision. The Planck results constrain the six parameters of the Lambda-CDM model to better than 1% in most cases.

Why this matters

Recombination is the boundary between the observable and the unobservable. Everything before it is hidden behind the “wall” of the opaque plasma. The CMB is a photograph of the universe at its youngest visible age. The information encoded in its fluctuations is the empirical foundation on which modern cosmology is built.
