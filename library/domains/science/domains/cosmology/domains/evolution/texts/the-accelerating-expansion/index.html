The Accelerating Expansion

2026-03-31

In 1998, two teams of astronomers measuring distant supernovae found something nobody expected: the expansion of the universe is speeding up.

The expectation

For most of the 20th century, cosmologists assumed the expansion of the universe was decelerating. Gravity attracts. Every bit of matter in the universe pulls on every other bit. This mutual attraction should slow the expansion over time, like a ball thrown upward. The open question was not whether expansion was decelerating, but by how much – and whether it would eventually halt and reverse.

The measurement

To measure the deceleration, you need a way to compare the expansion rate at different epochs. Type Ia supernovae provided the tool. These explosions have nearly uniform peak luminosity, making them standard candles: if you know how bright something intrinsically is, you can calculate its distance from how bright it appears.

Two teams – the Supernova Cosmology Project (led by Saul Perlmutter) and the High-z Supernova Search Team (led by Brian Schmidt, with Adam Riess as lead analyst) – measured dozens of distant Type Ia supernovae. The supernovae were fainter than expected. Not slightly. Significantly. They were farther away than a decelerating universe would place them.

The conclusion: the expansion of the universe is not decelerating. It is accelerating. Something is pushing the universe apart faster and faster.

The cause

Accelerating expansion requires a component with negative pressure – something that acts repulsively at cosmological scales. The simplest candidate is the cosmological constant Λ\Lambda: a constant energy density filling all of space. In the Friedmann equations, Λ\Lambda produces a term that drives a¨>0\ddot{a} > 0 (positive acceleration).

This component is called dark energy. It constitutes about 68% of the universe’s total energy content. Unlike dark matter (which clumps and decelerates expansion), dark energy has a uniform density that does not dilute as space expands.

The timeline

The expansion history has two phases. For the first ~9 billion years, matter dominated the energy budget, and gravitational attraction decelerated the expansion. About 5 billion years ago, the matter density had diluted enough (through expansion) that dark energy’s contribution became dominant. Since then, the expansion has been accelerating – and will continue to accelerate indefinitely, as far as current data can tell.

The recognition

Perlmutter, Schmidt, and Riess shared the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics. The discovery was described by the Nobel committee as “the most surprising discovery that the expansion rate of the universe is accelerating.”

Why this matters

The accelerating expansion transformed cosmology. It required introducing a new component (dark energy) that dominates the universe’s energy budget. It implies that the universe will expand forever, with distant galaxies eventually receding faster than the speed of light (permissible because it is space expanding, not objects moving through space). It raised one of the hardest problems in physics: what is dark energy, and why does the cosmological constant have the value it does?
