The Cosmic Web

2026-03-31 If you could see the universe from outside, it would look like a sponge -- a vast three-dimensional network of filaments, sheets, and dense knots surrounding enormous empty voids.

If you could see the universe from outside, it would look like a sponge – a vast three-dimensional network of filaments, sheets, and dense knots surrounding enormous empty voids.

¶How the web formed

The cosmic microwave background shows that the early universe was almost perfectly uniform, but not quite. Tiny density fluctuations – about one part in 100,000 – were present 380,000 years after the Big Bang. These fluctuations were the seeds.

Regions slightly denser than average exerted slightly stronger gravitational pull, attracting nearby matter and growing denser still. Dark matter, which does not interact with light and therefore is not slowed by radiation pressure, collapsed first. It formed an invisible scaffolding – a web of dark matter filaments and halos. Baryonic matter then fell into this scaffolding, collecting in the densest regions to form stars and galaxies.

¶The structure

The cosmic web has four components:

Clusters. The densest nodes, where filaments intersect. Galaxy clusters sit at these intersections, containing hundreds to thousands of galaxies bound together by gravity.

Filaments. Long, thin bridges of galaxies and dark matter stretching between clusters. Filaments contain most of the mass in the web and can extend for hundreds of megaparsecs.

Sheets (walls). Flattened structures forming the boundaries between voids. The “CfA Great Wall,” mapped in 1989, was one of the first large-scale structures identified.

Voids. Enormous regions, up to 100 megaparsecs across, containing very few galaxies. Voids occupy most of the volume of the universe.

¶Mapping the web

Galaxy surveys map this structure by measuring the positions and redshifts of millions of galaxies. The Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS), the 2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey, and the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) have progressively mapped larger volumes with higher precision. The pattern of clustering encodes information about the universe’s total matter content, the nature of dark energy, and the physics of the early universe.

¶Why this matters

At small scales the universe looks chaotic – stars, nebulae, galaxies scattered apparently at random. The cosmic web reveals the underlying order. It connects the tiny fluctuations in the CMB to the galaxies we see today, through the gravitational amplification of structure over 13.8 billion years. Understanding the web is understanding how the universe built everything in it.
