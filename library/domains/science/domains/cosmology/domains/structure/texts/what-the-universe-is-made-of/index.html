What the Universe Is Made Of

Everything you have ever seen, touched, or tasted is made of protons, neutrons, and electrons. This ordinary matter – baryonic matter – accounts for every star, every planet, every grain of dust, every living thing. It is also less than five percent of the universe.

¶The visible inventory

Start with stars. A star is a sphere of plasma held together by gravity, generating energy through nuclear fusion. Stars range from dim red dwarfs (the most common) to blue supergiants thousands of times more luminous than the Sun. Fusion in stellar cores builds the periodic table: hydrogen fuses into helium, helium into carbon, carbon into oxygen, and so on up to iron. Everything heavier than iron is forged in supernova explosions.

Stars collect into galaxies. The Milky Way contains roughly 200 billion stars. Galaxies come in spirals, ellipticals, and irregular shapes. They are not distributed uniformly – they cluster into groups, clusters, and superclusters, arranged along filaments of the cosmic web.

Between the galaxies is diffuse gas, mostly hydrogen and helium, some of it hot enough to glow in X-rays. This intergalactic gas contains a substantial fraction of the universe’s baryonic matter – more than what is in stars.

¶The cosmic pie chart

The total energy content of the universe, as measured by the cosmic microwave background and galaxy surveys, divides roughly as:

  • ~5% baryonic matter: everything visible – stars, gas, dust, planets
  • ~27% dark matter: gravitates but does not emit or absorb light
  • ~68% dark energy: drives the accelerating expansion of space

This means that everything accessible to telescopes – everything we can photograph, spectrum-analyze, or chemically assay – is a small minority of what exists. The dominant components of the universe are invisible and, so far, unexplained.

¶Why this matters

The composition of the universe determines its past and its future. The balance between matter (which gravitationally decelerates expansion) and dark energy (which accelerates it) determines whether the universe will expand forever, coast to a halt, or collapse. Understanding what the universe is made of is not an academic catalog. It is the starting point for understanding why the universe looks the way it does and what will happen to it.
