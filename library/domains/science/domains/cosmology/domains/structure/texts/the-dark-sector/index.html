The Dark Sector

2026-03-31

Ninety-five percent of the universe is made of stuff we cannot see, cannot touch, and do not understand. This is not a metaphor. It is the quantitative result of decades of independent observations.

Dark matter: the evidence

Dark matter makes itself known through gravity. The evidence comes from multiple independent sources, and no single one would be decisive, but together they are overwhelming.

Galaxy rotation curves. In the 1970s, Vera Rubin and Kent Ford measured the orbital speeds of stars in spiral galaxies. Stars far from the galactic center orbit just as fast as those near it. Newtonian gravity predicts speeds should fall off with distance from the center (as they do in the solar system). They do not. The flat rotation curves imply a large halo of invisible mass surrounding each galaxy.

Galaxy clusters. Fritz Zwicky noticed in 1933 that galaxies in the Coma Cluster move too fast to be held together by their visible mass. The cluster should have flown apart long ago. Something invisible provides the missing gravitational binding.

Gravitational lensing. Light from distant galaxies bends as it passes massive foreground structures. The amount of bending reveals the total mass of the lens, including the dark component. The Bullet Cluster (two galaxy clusters that collided) is particularly striking: the visible gas was stripped in the collision, but the gravitational lensing signal follows the dark matter, which passed through unimpeded.

The CMB. The pattern of fluctuations in the cosmic microwave background is sensitive to the ratio of dark matter to baryonic matter. The observed pattern requires roughly five times as much dark matter as ordinary matter.

Dark energy: the evidence

Dark energy was discovered in 1998 through observations of distant Type Ia supernovae. These “standard candles” appeared fainter than expected, meaning they were farther away than a decelerating universe would predict. The expansion of the universe is not slowing down. It is speeding up.

Subsequent observations confirmed this. The CMB power spectrum, combined with galaxy surveys, requires a component with negative pressure that constitutes about 68% of the total energy density. The baryon acoustic oscillation signal in galaxy distributions independently confirms the same picture.

Dark matter vs. dark energy

The two “dark” components are fundamentally different despite sharing the label. Dark matter clumps: it collapses gravitationally into halos around galaxies and clusters. Dark energy is uniform: its density does not change as space expands. Dark matter slows cosmic expansion by adding gravitational attraction. Dark energy accelerates expansion by exerting negative pressure. Dark matter’s existence is inferred at scales from individual galaxies to the CMB. Dark energy’s effects are only visible at cosmological scales, in the expansion rate of the universe itself.

Neither one has been identified. We do not know what particle dark matter is. We do not know what physical mechanism produces dark energy. These are two of the most important open problems in physics.
