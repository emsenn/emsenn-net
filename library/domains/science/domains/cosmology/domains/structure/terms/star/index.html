Star

2026-03-31 A star is a gravitationally bound sphere of plasma that generates energy through nuclear fusion in its core, converting lighter elements into heavier ones and radiating the resulting energy as [light](../../../terms/light.md).

A star is a gravitationally bound sphere of plasma that generates energy through nuclear fusion in its core, converting lighter elements into heavier ones and radiating the resulting energy as light.

Stars form when clouds of gas (primarily hydrogen) collapse under their own gravity. As the cloud contracts, its core heats up. When the core reaches approximately 15 million kelvin, hydrogen nuclei begin fusing into helium, releasing energy that creates an outward pressure balancing gravitational collapse. This equilibrium – hydrostatic equilibrium – defines a star’s main-sequence lifetime.

A star’s mass determines its fate. Low-mass stars (like the Sun) burn for billions of years, eventually shedding their outer layers as planetary nebulae and leaving behind white dwarfs. High-mass stars burn hotter and faster, dying in supernova explosions that scatter heavy elements into the interstellar medium and leaving behind neutron stars or black holes. The heavy elements forged in stellar cores and supernova explosions – carbon, oxygen, iron, and everything heavier – are the raw material of planets, chemistry, and life.

Stars are the building blocks of galaxies. The observable universe contains roughly 102410^{24} stars.
