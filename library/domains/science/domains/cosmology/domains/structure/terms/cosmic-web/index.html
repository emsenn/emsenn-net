Cosmic Web

The cosmic web is the large-scale pattern of matter distribution in the universe: a network of filaments connecting dense nodes (where galaxy clusters sit) and surrounding vast, nearly empty voids.

The web formed through gravitational amplification of tiny density fluctuations present in the early universe. Regions slightly denser than average attracted more matter, grew denser still, and eventually collapsed into structures. The dark matter collapsed first (because it does not interact electromagnetically and therefore does not resist compression through radiation pressure), forming a scaffolding along which baryonic matter subsequently collected. The result is a three-dimensional structure that looks, roughly, like a sponge.

The web has four morphological components: clusters at the densest intersections, filaments stretching between clusters (containing most of the mass), sheets (or walls) forming the boundaries of voids, and voids – enormous regions, up to 100 megaparsecs across, containing very few galaxies.

Galaxy surveys such as the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS) and the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) map this structure in detail. The pattern encodes information about the universe’s composition, expansion history, and the nature of gravity. On scales above roughly 300 megaparsecs, the web becomes statistically uniform – the basis for the cosmological principle.
