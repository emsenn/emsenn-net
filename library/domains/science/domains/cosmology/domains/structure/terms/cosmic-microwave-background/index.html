Cosmic Microwave Background

2026-03-31

The cosmic microwave background (CMB) is thermal radiation left over from recombination, approximately 380,000 years after the Big Bang – the oldest light in the universe, now cooled by the expansion of space from roughly 3,000 kelvin to 2.725 kelvin, placing it in the microwave band of the electromagnetic spectrum.

Before recombination, the universe was a hot plasma of free electrons and atomic nuclei. Photons scattered constantly off free electrons and could not travel far – the universe was opaque, like the interior of a star. When the universe cooled enough for electrons to combine with nuclei into neutral atoms, photons were freed. The CMB is the light from that moment, arriving from every direction in the sky.

The CMB spectrum is the most precisely measured thermal (blackbody) spectrum in nature. Its near-perfect uniformity across the sky confirms the cosmological principle: the early universe was homogeneous and isotropic. But the CMB is not perfectly uniform. Tiny temperature fluctuations – about one part in 100,000 – map the density variations that would later grow, through gravitational collapse, into galaxies, clusters, and the cosmic web.

The angular power spectrum of these fluctuations, measured by the COBE (1992), WMAP (2001-2010), and Planck (2009-2013) satellite missions, constrains the parameters of the Lambda-CDM model with extraordinary precision. It is the single most informative observation in cosmology.
