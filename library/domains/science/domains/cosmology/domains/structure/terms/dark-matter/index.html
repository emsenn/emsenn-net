Dark Matter

2026-03-31 Dark matter is matter that interacts gravitationally but not electromagnetically -- invisible to all telescopes across the entire [electromagnetic spectrum](../../../terms/electromagnetic-spectrum.md), detectable only through its gravitational effects on visible matter and [light](../../../terms/light.md).

Dark matter is matter that interacts gravitationally but not electromagnetically – invisible to all telescopes across the entire electromagnetic spectrum, detectable only through its gravitational effects on visible matter and light.

The evidence for dark matter comes from multiple independent observations. Galaxy rotation curves: stars in the outer regions of spiral galaxies orbit faster than the visible mass can explain (Rubin and Ford, 1970s). Galaxy clusters: the galaxies move too fast to be held together by visible mass alone (Zwicky, 1933). Gravitational lensing: light from distant galaxies bends more than the visible mass of foreground clusters can account for. The cosmic microwave background: the pattern of temperature fluctuations requires a component that gravitates but does not interact with photons. Large-scale structure formation: without dark matter, the observed galaxies and clusters could not have formed in the time since the Big Bang.

“Cold” dark matter (CDM) means the particles were moving slowly compared to the speed of light when they decoupled from the rest of the universe. This is the version that fits observations. “Hot” dark matter (light, fast-moving particles like neutrinos) would have erased small-scale structure, which we observe.

Despite overwhelming gravitational evidence, the particle identity of dark matter is unknown. Candidates include weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs), axions, and sterile neutrinos. No direct detection has yet been confirmed. This is one of the central open problems in physics.
