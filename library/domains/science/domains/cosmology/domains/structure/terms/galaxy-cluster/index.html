Galaxy Cluster

2026-03-31

A galaxy cluster is the largest gravitationally bound structure in the universe, containing hundreds to thousands of galaxies embedded in hot intracluster gas and dark matter halos.

The visible galaxies account for only a few percent of a cluster’s total mass. The hot gas between galaxies (the intracluster medium), detectable in X-rays, contributes roughly 15%. The remaining 80% or more is dark matter. Galaxy clusters are among the strongest pieces of evidence for dark matter: the gravitational mass needed to hold the cluster together far exceeds the mass visible in stars and gas. Fritz Zwicky first noticed this discrepancy in the Coma Cluster in 1933, calling the missing component “dunkle Materie.”

Clusters form at the densest nodes of the cosmic web, where filaments of matter intersect. Typical clusters span 10-30 megaparsecs and have total masses of 101410^{14} to 101510^{15} solar masses. The Virgo Cluster (our nearest large neighbor), the Coma Cluster, and the massive Abell clusters are well-studied examples. Gravitational lensing by clusters provides a direct measurement of their total mass, including the dark matter component.
