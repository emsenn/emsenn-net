Hawking Radiation

2026-03-31 Hawking radiation is thermal radiation predicted to be emitted by [black holes](./black-hole.md) due to quantum effects near the [event horizon](./event-horizon.md). Stephen Hawking derived this result in 1974-1975, showing that black holes are not perfectly black.

Hawking radiation is thermal radiation predicted to be emitted by black holes due to quantum effects near the event horizon. Stephen Hawking derived this result in 1974-1975, showing that black holes are not perfectly black.

The mechanism involves quantum field theory in curved spacetime. In the vacuum near the horizon, virtual particle-antiparticle pairs are constantly created and annihilated. Near the horizon, it is possible for one particle to fall in while the other escapes to infinity. The escaping particles constitute a thermal flux with a characteristic temperature:

TH=ℏc38πGMkBT_H = \frac{\hbar c^3}{8\pi G M k_B}

where MM is the black hole’s mass, ℏ\hbar is the reduced Planck constant, and kBk_B is Boltzmann’s constant. The temperature is inversely proportional to mass: small black holes are hot; large black holes are cold. A stellar-mass black hole has a temperature of about 10−810^{-8} kelvin – far below the cosmic microwave background temperature, making it undetectable with current technology.

Because Hawking radiation carries away energy, the black hole loses mass over time. This is black hole evaporation. A stellar-mass black hole would take far longer than the age of the universe to evaporate, but the theoretical implication is profound: black holes are not eternal.

Hawking radiation connects general relativity, quantum mechanics, and thermodynamics. It established that black holes have temperature and entropy, completing the analogy between black hole mechanics and thermodynamics. It also raised the black hole information paradox: if a black hole evaporates completely, what happens to the information about everything that fell in?

See also: entropy and surprise for the information-theoretic meaning of thermal radiation.
