Black Hole

2026-03-31
Defines Black Hole, black holes
Requires
  • spacetime
  • gravity
  • general-relativity

A black hole is a region of spacetime enclosed by an event horizon, from which no signal – not even light – can escape to an outside observer.

Black holes are predicted by general relativity. Karl Schwarzschild found the first exact solution to Einstein’s field equations in 1916, describing the spacetime geometry around a spherically symmetric, non-rotating mass. The solution has a critical radius – the Schwarzschild radius – below which nothing can escape. For many decades, black holes were treated as mathematical curiosities. The 1960s and 1970s brought theoretical work by Penrose, Hawking, and others establishing that black holes are physical predictions of gravitational collapse.

Black holes form when massive stars exhaust their nuclear fuel and collapse. Stars above roughly 20-25 solar masses leave behind stellar-mass black holes. Supermassive black holes (10610^6 to 101010^{10} solar masses) sit at the centers of most galaxies, including the Milky Way (Sagittarius A*, ~4 million solar masses). How supermassive black holes formed is not fully understood.

Observational evidence is now abundant: X-ray binaries, gravitational wave detections from merging black holes (LIGO/Virgo, first detection 2015), and direct imaging of the shadow of the supermassive black hole in M87 by the Event Horizon Telescope (2019).

Black holes sit at the intersection of general relativity, quantum mechanics, thermodynamics, and information theory – making them central objects in fundamental physics.
