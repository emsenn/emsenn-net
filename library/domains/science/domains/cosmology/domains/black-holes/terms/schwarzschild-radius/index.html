Schwarzschild Radius

2026-03-31

The Schwarzschild radius is the radius of the event horizon of a non-rotating, uncharged black hole:

rs=2GMc2r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}

where MM is the mass, GG is Newton’s gravitational constant, and cc is the speed of light.

Karl Schwarzschild derived this in 1916 as part of the first exact solution to Einstein’s field equations. The formula says the horizon radius is proportional to mass. For the Sun, rs≈3r_s \approx 3 kilometers. For the Earth, rs≈9r_s \approx 9 millimeters. For the supermassive black hole at the center of the Milky Way (~4 million solar masses), rs≈12r_s \approx 12 million kilometers.

The Schwarzschild radius sets the length scale for strong-field gravity. If an object is compressed below its Schwarzschild radius, it becomes a black hole. For ordinary objects this requires absurd compression – the Sun would need to be squeezed into a sphere 3 km across. But for sufficiently massive objects (the cores of massive stars, or the supermassive objects at galactic centers), no known force can prevent collapse past this radius once the object’s fuel is exhausted.

The Schwarzschild solution assumes no rotation and no electric charge. Real astrophysical black holes rotate (described by the Kerr metric), which modifies the horizon geometry, but the Schwarzschild radius remains a useful order-of-magnitude scale.
