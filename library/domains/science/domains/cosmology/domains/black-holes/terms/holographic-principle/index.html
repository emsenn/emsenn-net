Holographic Principle

The holographic principle is the conjecture that the information content of a region of space can be fully encoded on its boundary, at a density of at most one bit per Planck area (lP2≈2.6×10−70l_P^2 \approx 2.6 \times 10^{-70} m2^2).

The principle originates from black hole entropy. The Bekenstein-Hawking entropy – the maximum entropy a region can contain before collapsing into a black hole – is proportional to the boundary area, not the volume. Gerard ’t Hooft (1993) and Leonard Susskind (1995) elevated this observation into a general principle: the fundamental degrees of freedom of any region of space reside on its boundary.

This is counterintuitive. Ordinary physics is local and volumetric: the number of possible states in a box should scale with the volume. The holographic principle says the true count scales with the surface area. The interior is, in some sense, a projection of boundary data – hence “holographic,” by analogy with optical holograms that encode three-dimensional information on a two-dimensional surface.

The most concrete realization is the AdS/CFT correspondence (Maldacena, 1997), which establishes a precise mathematical equivalence between a gravitational theory in a negatively curved spacetime (anti-de Sitter space) and a non-gravitational quantum field theory on its boundary. This duality is not a vague analogy. It is an exact equivalence: every calculation in the bulk theory has a corresponding calculation on the boundary, and they give the same answers.

The holographic principle connects deeply to information theory: it places a fundamental physical limit on information density. See also the information-theoretic stability framework for an interpretation of the holographic mapping in terms of stability functionals.
