Bekenstein-Hawking Entropy

2026-03-31

The Bekenstein-Hawking entropy is the entropy of a black hole, proportional to the area of its event horizon:

SBH=kBc3A4Gℏ=kBA4lP2S_{BH} = \frac{k_B c^3 A}{4 G \hbar} = \frac{k_B A}{4 l_P^2}

where AA is the horizon area, kBk_B is Boltzmann’s constant, GG is Newton’s gravitational constant, ℏ\hbar is the reduced Planck constant, cc is the speed of light, and lP=Gℏ/c3l_P = \sqrt{G\hbar/c^3} is the Planck length.

Jacob Bekenstein proposed in 1972-1973 that black holes must carry entropy, reasoning as follows: if a black hole had zero entropy, you could reduce the entropy of the universe by throwing high-entropy matter into it, violating the second law of thermodynamics. Bekenstein conjectured that the entropy is proportional to the horizon area. Hawking’s calculation of Hawking radiation in 1974 confirmed the proportionality and fixed the constant.

Two features of this formula are remarkable:

Area, not volume. Ordinary systems have entropy proportional to their volume. Black hole entropy is proportional to the area of the boundary. This observation is the origin of the holographic principle.

Enormous magnitude. A solar-mass black hole has an entropy of roughly 1077kB10^{77} k_B – vastly larger than the entropy of the star that collapsed to form it. Black holes are the highest-entropy objects of a given size that physics allows.

The Bekenstein-Hawking formula sits at the intersection of gravity (GG), quantum mechanics (ℏ\hbar), relativity (cc), and thermodynamics (kBk_B). Any future theory of quantum gravity must reproduce it. See also: entropy and surprise for the information-theoretic interpretation of entropy.
