Event Horizon

The event horizon is the boundary of a black hole – the surface beyond which events cannot affect an outside observer. It is defined by the requirement that outgoing light rays at the horizon remain trapped: they can neither escape nor fall inward, but hover at the boundary forever.

The event horizon is not a physical surface. There is no wall, no barrier, no material boundary. An observer falling through it would notice nothing locally remarkable – no sign, no sensation, no discontinuity. This is a consequence of the equivalence principle: locally, spacetime looks flat everywhere, including at the horizon. The horizon’s significance is global, not local. Once you cross it, no path through spacetime leads back out, because all future-directed paths inside the horizon lead to the singularity.

For a non-rotating black hole, the event horizon is a sphere at the Schwarzschild radius. For a rotating black hole (the Kerr solution), the horizon has a more complex structure, with an ergosphere outside the horizon where spacetime is dragged by the rotation.

The event horizon is central to black hole thermodynamics. The Bekenstein-Hawking entropy is proportional to the horizon’s area, not the volume it encloses. The holographic principle generalizes this observation: the maximum information content of any region of space is determined by its boundary area, not its volume.
