The Information Paradox

2026-03-31
Table of contents

Hawking radiation creates a problem. If a black hole evaporates completely, what happens to the information about everything that fell in?

The setup

Quantum mechanics is unitary. This means the evolution of a quantum system is reversible: given the final state, you can in principle reconstruct the initial state. No information is ever truly lost.

But Hawking radiation appears to be exactly thermal – random noise determined only by the black hole’s mass, charge, and angular momentum. It carries no detailed information about the specific matter that fell in. A black hole formed from a collapsing star and a black hole formed from an equivalent mass of encyclopedias would emit the same radiation. If the black hole evaporates completely, the detailed information about its contents is gone.

Either information is destroyed (violating quantum mechanics) or it escapes somehow (but how, if nothing can get out of a black hole?). This is the black hole information paradox.

Why it matters

This is not a technical puzzle. It is a direct conflict between the two most fundamental theories in physics: general relativity (which says nothing escapes the event horizon) and quantum mechanics (which says information cannot be destroyed). At least one of them must be modified, or there is some subtlety we are missing.

The Page curve

In 1993, Don Page argued that if black hole evaporation is unitary, the entropy of the emitted radiation should follow a specific curve: rising initially (as thermal radiation is emitted) and then falling (as the remaining black hole becomes increasingly entangled with the radiation already emitted). The radiation entropy peaks at roughly the midpoint of evaporation – the “Page time” – and returns to zero when the black hole is gone. This is the Page curve.

If the actual entropy of Hawking radiation follows the Page curve, information is preserved. If it does not (if entropy just keeps rising), information is destroyed.

Recent progress

Starting around 2019, work by Penington, Almheiri, Maharlika, Maldacena, and others (the “island formula”) showed that careful calculations within the framework of the holographic principle and the AdS/CFT correspondence reproduce the Page curve. The key insight: at late times, regions inside the black hole (called “islands”) must be included in the entropy calculation for the radiation. When they are, the entropy turns over and follows the unitary prediction.

This does not fully resolve the paradox – it does not explain the physical mechanism by which information escapes, and it relies on the AdS/CFT framework, which applies to a spacetime geometry different from our universe. But it provides the strongest evidence yet that information is not destroyed.

The holographic principle

The holographic principle – the conjecture that the information content of a region of space is encoded on its boundary – emerged directly from thinking about black hole entropy. If a black hole’s entropy (its information content) scales with the area of the horizon rather than the volume it encloses, then the fundamental degrees of freedom of the region live on the boundary, not in the bulk.

The AdS/CFT correspondence (Maldacena, 1997) provides a concrete realization: a gravitational theory in the bulk is exactly equivalent to a non-gravitational quantum theory on the boundary. In this picture, the paradox dissolves: the boundary theory is manifestly unitary, so information is preserved by construction. The challenge is understanding how the boundary description maps to the bulk experience of an observer falling through the horizon.

Why this matters

The information paradox is one of the few places where general relativity and quantum mechanics are in direct, unavoidable conflict. Resolving it will require, or at least constrain, a theory of quantum gravity. It has already produced the holographic principle, the AdS/CFT correspondence, and new insights into the relationship between spacetime geometry and information. The paradox is not a curiosity. It is a signpost pointing toward the next revolution in fundamental physics.
