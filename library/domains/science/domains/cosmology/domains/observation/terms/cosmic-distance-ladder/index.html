Cosmic Distance Ladder

2026-03-31
Defines Cosmic Distance Ladder, cosmic distance ladder, distance ladder
Requires
  • light
  • redshift

The cosmic distance ladder is the sequence of techniques used to measure distances in the universe, each method calibrating the next: parallax to Cepheid variables to Type Ia supernovae to the Hubble flow.

No single technique works at all scales. Parallax is geometrically precise but only reaches nearby stars (up to a few kiloparsecs with current instruments). Cepheid variable stars – whose pulsation period correlates with luminosity – extend the range to nearby galaxies (~30 megaparsecs). Type Ia supernovae, brighter and visible at cosmological distances, extend the range to billions of light-years. At the greatest distances, redshift and the Hubble parameter provide distance estimates.

Each rung depends on the calibration of the rung below it. Parallax calibrates Cepheids. Cepheids calibrate supernovae. Errors at any rung propagate upward. This cascading dependence is both the ladder’s power and its vulnerability.

The “Hubble tension” – the persistent disagreement between the value of H0H_0 measured locally (via the distance ladder, ~73 km/s/Mpc) and the value inferred from the cosmic microwave background (~67 km/s/Mpc) – may reflect a systematic error in the ladder, new physics, or both. Resolving this tension is one of the most active problems in observational cosmology.
