Parallax

2026-03-31 Parallax is the apparent shift in position of a nearby object against a distant background, caused by a change in the observer's viewpoint. In astronomy, stellar parallax uses Earth's orbit around the Sun as a baseline to measure distances to nearby stars.

Parallax is the apparent shift in position of a nearby object against a distant background, caused by a change in the observer’s viewpoint. In astronomy, stellar parallax uses Earth’s orbit around the Sun as a baseline to measure distances to nearby stars.

As Earth orbits the Sun, a nearby star appears to shift slightly against the background of much more distant stars. The shift is tiny – measured in arcseconds (1/3600 of a degree). The parallax angle pp (half the total apparent shift over six months) is related to distance by d=1/pd = 1/p, where dd is in parsecs and pp is in arcseconds. One parsec (about 3.26 light-years) is the distance at which a star has a parallax of one arcsecond.

Parallax is the most direct distance measurement in astronomy – pure geometry, with no assumptions about the source’s physical properties. Its limitation is range: even the nearest star (Proxima Centauri) has a parallax of only 0.77 arcseconds. Ground-based telescopes can measure parallax out to a few hundred parsecs. The Hipparcos satellite (1989-1993) extended this to about 1,000 parsecs. The Gaia mission (launched 2013) measures parallaxes to milliarcsecond precision for over a billion stars, reaching distances of several kiloparsecs.

Parallax calibrates every other rung of the cosmic distance ladder. If parallax distances are wrong, every subsequent distance measurement inherits the error.
