Type Ia Supernova

2026-03-31

A Type Ia supernova is a thermonuclear explosion of a white dwarf star that reaches a nearly uniform peak luminosity, making it a reliable standard candle for measuring cosmological distances.

The standard model: a white dwarf in a binary system accretes matter from a companion star. When the white dwarf’s mass approaches the Chandrasekhar limit (~1.4 solar masses), carbon and oxygen in its interior ignite in a runaway thermonuclear detonation. The explosion completely destroys the white dwarf.

Because the detonation occurs near a fixed critical mass, the energy released – and therefore the peak luminosity – is approximately the same for every Type Ia supernova. After a correction for the rate at which the light curve declines (the Phillips relation: brighter supernovae fade more slowly), the scatter in peak luminosity is reduced to about 7%. This makes Type Ia supernovae precise distance indicators visible across billions of light-years.

Type Ia supernovae were the tool that revealed the accelerating expansion of the universe. In 1998, two teams measured distant supernovae that were fainter (farther) than a decelerating universe would predict, demonstrating that the expansion is speeding up.
