Gravitational Lensing

2026-03-31

Gravitational lensing is the bending of light by massive objects, predicted by general relativity. When light from a distant source passes near a massive foreground object (a galaxy, galaxy cluster, or other concentration of mass), the curvature of spacetime deflects the light’s path, distorting, magnifying, or multiplying the image of the background source.

Three regimes are distinguished:

Strong lensing. The foreground mass is dense enough to produce multiple images, arcs, or complete rings (Einstein rings) of the background source. Galaxy clusters frequently produce strong lensing.

Weak lensing. The distortion is too small to see in individual images but can be detected statistically by measuring the coherent shape distortions of many background galaxies. Weak lensing maps the total mass distribution – including dark matter – of foreground structures.

Microlensing. A compact foreground object (such as a star) briefly brightens a background source as it passes in front. Used to detect exoplanets and dark compact objects.

Gravitational lensing is one of the most direct probes of dark matter. The lensing signal depends on total mass, not just luminous mass. The Bullet Cluster – two galaxy clusters that collided – provided striking evidence: the visible gas was displaced in the collision, but the lensing signal (tracing the total mass) remained centered on the galaxies and their dark matter halos. The dark matter passed through the collision largely unaffected, while the gas interacted and lagged behind.
