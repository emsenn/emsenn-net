How We Measure the Universe

2026-03-31

How do you measure the distance to something you cannot visit? You use a ladder.

The problem

Distance is the most fundamental measurement in cosmology, and the hardest to make. You cannot stretch a tape measure to a galaxy. You cannot bounce a radar signal off something billions of light-years away. Every technique for measuring cosmic distances relies on indirect methods, each with its own assumptions and limitations.

Rung 1: Parallax

The first rung is geometry. As Earth orbits the Sun, nearby stars appear to shift slightly against the background of distant stars. This apparent shift is parallax. The parallax angle gives the distance directly, by trigonometry, with no assumptions about the star’s properties.

Parallax is the gold standard – pure geometry – but it only works for nearby stars. Even with the Gaia satellite’s milliarcsecond precision, parallax reaches only a few kiloparsecs. To go further, you need the next rung.

Rung 2: Cepheid variables

Cepheid variable stars pulsate, and their pulsation period is correlated with their intrinsic luminosity (the Leavitt law, discovered by Henrietta Leavitt in 1912). Measure the period, infer the luminosity, compare to observed brightness, calculate the distance. Cepheids are bright enough to see in nearby galaxies, extending the ladder to about 30 megaparsecs.

Rung 3: Type Ia supernovae

Type Ia supernovae are thermonuclear explosions of white dwarf stars with nearly uniform peak luminosity. After correction for the light curve shape, they are precise standard candles visible across billions of light-years. This is the rung that revealed the accelerating expansion.

Rung 4: The Hubble flow

At the greatest distances, redshift itself becomes a distance proxy. If you know the Hubble parameter H0H_0, then a galaxy’s redshift gives its distance via Hubble’s law (with corrections for the expansion history).

The Hubble tension

Each rung calibrates the next, so errors propagate upward. The local distance ladder (parallax → Cepheids → supernovae) gives H0≈73H_0 \approx 73 km/s/Mpc. The cosmic microwave background, analyzed independently using the Lambda-CDM model, gives H0≈67H_0 \approx 67 km/s/Mpc. The disagreement is statistically significant (~5 sigma) and persistent. It may reflect a systematic error in one or both methods, or it may be a sign of physics beyond the standard model. This is the Hubble tension, and resolving it is one of the most active problems in cosmology.

Why this matters

Every number in cosmology – the age of the universe, its size, the distances to galaxies, the expansion rate – ultimately rests on the distance ladder. If the ladder is wrong, the numbers are wrong. Understanding how distances are measured, and where the measurements might fail, is essential to understanding how much we actually know.
