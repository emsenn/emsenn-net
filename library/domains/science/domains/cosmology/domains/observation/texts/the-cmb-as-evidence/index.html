The CMB as Evidence

2026-03-31 In 1965, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson were testing a microwave antenna at Bell Labs and found a persistent noise they could not eliminate. It came from every direction. It did not vary with the time of day or the season. They had discovered the cosmic microwave background.

In 1965, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson were testing a microwave antenna at Bell Labs and found a persistent noise they could not eliminate. It came from every direction. It did not vary with the time of day or the season. They had discovered the cosmic microwave background.

¶The prediction

The CMB was predicted before it was found. In the 1940s, George Gamow, Ralph Alpher, and Robert Herman reasoned that if the universe began in a hot, dense state, the radiation from that early epoch should still be present, cooled by the expansion of space. They estimated a present-day temperature of a few kelvin. The prediction was largely forgotten until Penzias and Wilson’s accidental discovery, which earned them the 1978 Nobel Prize.

¶What we see

The cosmic microwave background is a nearly perfect blackbody spectrum at 2.725 kelvin. It comes from every direction in the sky. It is the afterglow of recombination – the moment 380,000 years after the Big Bang when the universe became transparent.

The COBE satellite (1992) confirmed two crucial facts. First, the CMB spectrum is a nearly perfect blackbody – the most precisely measured thermal spectrum in nature. This rules out alternative explanations (such as starlight scattered by dust). Second, the CMB has tiny temperature fluctuations: about one part in 100,000. These anisotropies are the imprint of density variations in the early universe.

¶The power spectrum

The angular power spectrum of the CMB anisotropies – how much temperature variation exists at each angular scale – is the most information-rich observable in cosmology.

The largest fluctuations (at angular scales around 1 degree) correspond to the first acoustic peak: sound waves in the pre-recombination plasma that had just completed one full compression cycle by the time of decoupling. Subsequent peaks at smaller scales correspond to higher harmonics. The positions, heights, and ratios of these peaks encode:

  • The total energy density of the universe (which determines spatial geometry – flat, open, or closed)
  • The ratio of baryonic matter to dark matter
  • The Hubble parameter
  • Whether the primordial fluctuations are scale-invariant (as inflation predicts)
  • The optical depth to reionization (when the first stars re-ionized the intergalactic medium)

¶From COBE to Planck

COBE made the first detection. WMAP (2001-2010) mapped the fluctuations with enough precision to determine the basic cosmological parameters. Planck (2009-2013) refined the measurements to sub-percent precision for most parameters, producing the tightest constraints on the Lambda-CDM model.

The six parameters of Lambda-CDM – the baryon density, the dark matter density, the age of the universe, the scalar spectral index, the amplitude of fluctuations, and the optical depth to reionization – are all constrained primarily by the CMB power spectrum.

¶Why this matters

The CMB is the single most important piece of evidence in cosmology. It confirms the Big Bang, confirms the cosmological principle, constrains the composition of the universe, and provides the empirical foundation for the standard model. If one observation had to carry the weight of the entire field, it would be this one.
