Redshift

2026-03-31
Defines Redshift, redshift, cosmological redshift
Requires
  • light
  • electromagnetic-spectrum
  • cosmic-expansion

Redshift is the stretching of light to longer wavelengths. In cosmology, the dominant cause is the expansion of space itself: as light travels through an expanding universe, the space it traverses stretches, and the light’s wavelength stretches with it. Longer wavelength means redder, hence “redshift.”

The redshift zz of a source is defined by:

1+z=λobservedλemitted=anowaemit=1aemit1 + z = \frac{\lambda_{\text{observed}}}{\lambda_{\text{emitted}}} = \frac{a_{\text{now}}}{a_{\text{emit}}} = \frac{1}{a_{\text{emit}}}

where λ\lambda is wavelength and aa is the scale factor. A galaxy at z=1z = 1 emitted its light when the universe was half its current size. The cosmic microwave background has z≈1100z \approx 1100.

Three types of redshift should be distinguished:

Cosmological redshift. Caused by the expansion of space. This is the dominant effect for distant objects and the one most relevant to cosmology.

Doppler redshift (or blueshift). Caused by relative motion between source and observer. A source moving away is redshifted; a source approaching is blueshifted. Familiar from the change in pitch of a passing siren.

Gravitational redshift. Caused by light climbing out of a gravitational potential well. A photon emitted near a massive object loses energy (increases wavelength) as it escapes. Predicted by general relativity and confirmed experimentally.

In practice, an observed redshift is a combination of all three effects, but at cosmological distances the expansion term dominates. Redshift is the primary observable in cosmological surveys: it tells you how much the universe has expanded since the light was emitted, which (given a cosmological model) tells you the distance and the look-back time.
