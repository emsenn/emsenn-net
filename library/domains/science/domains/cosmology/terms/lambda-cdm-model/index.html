Lambda-CDM Model

2026-03-31

The Lambda-CDM model is the standard model of cosmology. It describes a universe that is spatially flat, governed by general relativity, and composed of approximately 5% baryonic matter, 27% cold dark matter (CDM), and 68% dark energy in the form of a cosmological constant (Lambda, Λ\Lambda).

The model has six free parameters:

  1. Ωbh2\Omega_b h^2 – baryon density
  2. Ωch2\Omega_c h^2 – cold dark matter density
  3. θ∗\theta_* – angular size of the sound horizon at recombination
  4. τ\tau – optical depth to reionization
  5. nsn_s – scalar spectral index (tilt of the primordial fluctuation spectrum)
  6. AsA_s – amplitude of primordial fluctuations

These six numbers, combined with the Friedmann equations and the physics of the pre-recombination plasma, predict the angular power spectrum of the cosmic microwave background, the distribution of galaxies, the abundances of light elements, the expansion history, and the age of the universe. The predictions match observations across all these independent tests.

The model is called the “concordance model” because all major observations agree on the same parameter values. It is remarkably successful and remarkably unsatisfying: it works, but 95% of the universe’s content is “dark” – we know its gravitational effects but not its fundamental nature. Lambda-CDM is a parametric fit, not a derivation from first principles. Why Λ\Lambda has the value it does (the cosmological constant problem), what dark matter is, and whether general relativity is the correct theory at all scales remain open questions.
