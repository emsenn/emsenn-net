General Relativity

2026-03-31 General relativity is Einstein's theory of gravity (1915), in which the geometry of [spacetime](./spacetime.md) is determined by the distribution of mass and energy, and the geometry of spacetime in turn determines how matter moves.

General relativity is Einstein’s theory of gravity (1915), in which the geometry of spacetime is determined by the distribution of mass and energy, and the geometry of spacetime in turn determines how matter moves.

The Einstein field equations relate two mathematical objects: the Einstein tensor GμνG_{\mu\nu} (encoding spacetime curvature) and the stress-energy tensor TμνT_{\mu\nu} (encoding the density and flow of mass-energy):

Gμν+Λgμν=8πGc4TμνG_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}

The constant Λ\Lambda is the cosmological constant, which Einstein originally introduced and later abandoned, and which was revived in 1998 when the accelerating expansion was discovered.

General relativity is the foundation of modern cosmology. Applied to a universe that is homogeneous and isotropic on large scales (the cosmological principle), it yields the Friedmann equations, which govern the expansion of the universe. Applied to the collapse of massive objects, it predicts black holes. Applied to the bending of light around massive structures, it predicts gravitational lensing.

The theory’s predictions have been confirmed by the perihelion precession of Mercury, the deflection of starlight by the Sun, gravitational redshift, gravitational waves (detected by LIGO in 2015), and the imaging of black hole shadows (Event Horizon Telescope, 2019).
