The Lambda-CDM Model

2026-03-31
Table of contents

  • How well it works
  • What it cannot explain
  • The Hubble tension
  • Why this matters

Modern cosmology has a standard model, and it is embarrassingly successful.

What the model says

The Lambda-CDM model describes a universe that:

  • Began in a hot, dense state approximately 13.8 billion years ago (the Big Bang)
  • Has been expanding ever since, with the expansion now accelerating
  • Is spatially flat (the spatial geometry is Euclidean)
  • Contains roughly 5% baryonic matter, 27% cold dark matter, and 68% dark energy (the cosmological constant Λ\Lambda)
  • Satisfies the cosmological principle: homogeneous and isotropic on large scales

The dynamics are governed by the Friedmann equations, which follow from general relativity applied to a homogeneous, isotropic universe. The model has six free parameters. Given these six numbers, it predicts:

  • The angular power spectrum of the cosmic microwave background
  • The distribution and clustering of galaxies
  • The abundances of light elements from nucleosynthesis
  • The expansion history and age of the universe
  • The growth of large-scale structure over time

How well it works

The model fits all major observations. The CMB power spectrum predicted by Lambda-CDM matches the Planck satellite data to extraordinary precision. The predicted baryon-to-photon ratio from the CMB agrees with the independent measurement from primordial deuterium abundances. The predicted matter power spectrum matches galaxy survey data. The predicted expansion history matches Type Ia supernova distances.

This is not one test. It is a web of independent, mutually constraining observations that all converge on the same set of parameter values. The probability that this agreement is accidental is negligible.

What it cannot explain

Lambda-CDM is a parametric model, not a derivation from first principles. It fits the data, but it does not explain why the data look that way. The model has several deep problems:

What is dark matter? The model requires a non-baryonic matter component that makes up 27% of the universe. It specifies the gravitational behavior of this component but not its particle identity. Decades of direct-detection experiments have found nothing.

What is dark energy? The cosmological constant Λ\Lambda can be interpreted as vacuum energy, but quantum field theory predicts a value roughly 1012010^{120} times too large. This is the cosmological constant problem. Why Λ\Lambda has the observed value – small but nonzero – is perhaps the most extreme fine-tuning problem in physics.

What happened at the very beginning? The model describes the universe from the first fraction of a second onward, but it says nothing about the first instant. Cosmic inflation addresses some of the puzzles (horizon problem, flatness problem), but inflation itself is a hypothesis, not a confirmed mechanism.

Why is there matter at all? The laws of physics (as we understand them) treat matter and antimatter nearly symmetrically. The universe should contain equal amounts of both, which would have annihilated into radiation. The observed baryon asymmetry – the fact that we exist at all – is not explained by Lambda-CDM.

The Hubble tension

The model also faces an empirical puzzle: the Hubble parameter measured locally (~73 km/s/Mpc via the distance ladder) disagrees with the value inferred from the CMB (~67 km/s/Mpc). If this is not a measurement error, it may point to physics beyond Lambda-CDM.

Why this matters

Lambda-CDM is the most successful scientific model of the universe ever constructed. Its success is real and should not be diminished. But its success is also shallow: it tells us what the universe does without telling us why. Ninety-five percent of the content is dark. The initial conditions are imposed, not derived. The model works, but it leaves the deepest questions unanswered.
