Open Questions in Cosmology

2026-03-31

The Lambda-CDM model fits the data. It does not answer the questions. Here are the five deepest open problems in cosmology, each representing a gap in our understanding of fundamental physics.

1. What is dark matter?

Dark matter constitutes about 27% of the universe’s energy content. Its gravitational effects are observed at every scale from individual galaxies to the cosmic microwave background. But its particle identity is unknown.

Leading candidates include weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs), axions, and sterile neutrinos. Direct detection experiments (LUX, XENON, PandaX) have progressively excluded large regions of the WIMP parameter space without a detection. Indirect searches (gamma rays from dark matter annihilation) and collider searches (production at the LHC) have also yielded null results.

Alternative explanations – modified gravity theories (MOND, TeVeS) – can fit galaxy rotation curves but struggle with the CMB and large-scale structure data. The consensus remains that dark matter is a particle, but after decades of searching, no one knows which one.

2. What is dark energy?

Dark energy constitutes about 68% of the universe and drives the accelerating expansion. The simplest model is the cosmological constant Λ\Lambda – a fixed energy density of the vacuum.

The problem: quantum field theory predicts a vacuum energy roughly 1012010^{120} times larger than observed. This is not a small discrepancy. It is the worst prediction in the history of physics. Why the cosmological constant is small but nonzero is the cosmological constant problem.

Alternative models include quintessence (a dynamical scalar field whose energy density evolves over time) and modifications to general relativity at cosmological scales. Current and future surveys (DESI, Euclid, the Vera Rubin Observatory) aim to measure whether dark energy’s equation of state is truly constant (w=−1w = -1, as Λ\Lambda predicts) or varies with time.

3. What happened at the very beginning?

The Big Bang model describes the universe from roughly 10−610^{-6} seconds onward. Before that, the physics is increasingly speculative. Cosmic inflation is the leading hypothesis for what happened in the first 10−3210^{-32} seconds, but the inflaton field has not been detected, and the mechanism that started and ended inflation is unknown.

More fundamentally: what, if anything, happened at t=0t = 0? General relativity predicts a singularity, but this is universally understood as a breakdown of the theory. A theory of quantum gravity is needed to describe the Planck epoch (t≲10−43t \lesssim 10^{-43} seconds), and no complete theory exists.

4. Why is there more matter than antimatter?

The laws of physics as currently understood are nearly symmetric between matter and antimatter. The Big Bang should have produced equal amounts of both, which would have annihilated into radiation, leaving an empty universe. Instead, a small excess of matter survived – roughly one baryon per billion photons. This is the baryon asymmetry.

The conditions for generating a baryon asymmetry were identified by Andrei Sakharov in 1967: baryon number violation, C and CP violation, and departure from thermal equilibrium. The Standard Model of particle physics contains all three, but not in sufficient quantities to produce the observed asymmetry. New physics is required, but its nature is unknown.

5. How does gravity work at the quantum scale?

General relativity is a classical theory. It does not incorporate quantum mechanics. At ordinary scales this is fine – quantum effects are negligible for planets and stars. But at the Planck scale (∼10−35\sim 10^{-35} meters, ∼10−43\sim 10^{-43} seconds), quantum gravitational effects should dominate. This regime is relevant for the singularities inside black holes and the very earliest moments of the universe.

The black hole information paradox is the sharpest manifestation of this problem: general relativity and quantum mechanics make contradictory predictions about what happens to information that falls into a black hole. The holographic principle and the AdS/CFT correspondence provide partial answers, but a complete theory of quantum gravity – one that reduces to general relativity at large scales and to quantum mechanics at small scales – does not yet exist.

String theory, loop quantum gravity, and other approaches are under active development. None has made a testable prediction that distinguishes it from the others.

Why these questions matter

These five problems are not independent. A theory of quantum gravity might explain dark energy. The particle identity of dark matter might connect to baryogenesis. The physics of the Planck epoch might determine the initial conditions that Lambda-CDM takes as given. The deepest questions in cosmology converge on the same gap: the missing theory that unifies gravity with quantum mechanics. Filling that gap is the central problem of fundamental physics.
