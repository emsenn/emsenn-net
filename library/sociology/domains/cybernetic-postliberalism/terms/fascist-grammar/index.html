Fascist grammar

In July 1932, Joseph Goebbels told a crowd of Germans that their nation was collapsing, that the Weimar system was in ruins, that parasites and traitors had corrupted the community, that years of humiliation would be redeemed through struggle, that the German people’s destiny was assured, and that the storm would come and sweep away the old order.

In September 2025, Stephen Miller stood before tens of thousands in Arizona and declared that enemies had tried to kill Charlie Kirk and made him immortal, that “we are the ones who build” while the enemies “create nothing,” that the movement’s lineage stretched from Athens to Rome to Monticello, that “the light will defeat the dark,” and that “we are the storm.”

The vocabulary is different. The grammar is the same.

Fascist grammar is emsenn’s term, developed in “A storm is a storm is a storm” (2025), for the rhetorical sequence that fascist political speech follows regardless of its surface content. The concept distinguishes grammar from ideology: an ideology is a set of beliefs, but a grammar is a structure that generates statements regardless of their specific content. Fascist grammar can generate fascist politics — but it can also generate postliberal Catholic integralism, left-wing populism, or liberal crisis rhetoric. The grammar is the deep structure. The politics is the surface.

The six steps

  1. Declare crisis — assert that the present moment is one of existential threat. Not merely imperfect — fundamentally broken.
  2. Name a pure community — identify the group whose survival is at stake: the Volk, “the West,” the working class, the faithful.
  3. Denounce enemies as sterile or corrupt — characterize opponents as agents of decay. The enemy creates nothing, produces nothing, builds nothing. They are parasites on the community’s generativity.
  4. Reframe suffering as sacrifice — convert the community’s pain into evidence of its moral standing. Humiliation becomes proof of chosen status. Martyrdom becomes political sacrament.
  5. Promise destiny — assert that the community’s future greatness is assured if it acts now. History, God, or nature guarantees restoration.
  6. Assert inevitability — collapse the distinction between what should happen and what will happen. The storm is coming. Or: we are the storm.

The steps need not appear in this order, and not all six need be present in every instance. The grammar is recognized by its pattern, not by a checklist.

Strategic forgetting

The grammar survived 1945 through what emsenn calls strategic forgetting: the laundering of fascist categories into post-war conservative discourse, stripped of their explicit associations while retaining their structural function.

The process was systematic. Oswald Spengler’s civilizational cycles (The Decline of the West, 1918–22) reemerged as Cold War declinism — Allan Bloom’s Closing of the American Mind (1987) reprised Spengler’s fears in academic idiom. Carl Schmitt’s friend/enemy distinction — his argument that the fundamental category of politics is the identification of the existential enemy — returned as Reagan’s “Evil Empire” moral clarity. Arthur Moeller van den Bruck’s yearning for unity beyond parliamentary division reappeared as “Western heritage” education and Samuel Huntington’s Clash of Civilizations (1996). Ernst Junger’s exaltation of the productive Worker resurfaced as Reaganomics’ “makers versus takers.”

The Volk became “Western civilization.” Humiliation became martyrdom. Schmitt’s friend/enemy was baptized into evangelical moral dualism. The vocabulary changed. The grammar held.

This laundering prepared the ground for postliberal thinkers like Patrick Deneen (Why Liberalism Failed, 2018), Adrian Vermeule (“common good constitutionalism”), and Sohrab Ahmari (The Unbroken Thread, 2021), who argue that liberal neutrality must be replaced by civilizational inheritance. They rarely cite Schmitt or Goebbels. But their categories — civilization over nation, moral truth over procedure, sacrifice over compromise — belong to the same family tree.

Liberalism as dialectical twin

emsenn’s sharpest move is showing that liberalism does not escape fascist grammar by opposing it. Liberalism inhabits fascist grammar in reverse:

  • Fascism declares crisis; liberalism promises progressive stability (John Dewey’s managerial optimism)
  • Fascism exalts organic community; liberalism offers universal citizenship (Hans Kelsen’s procedural Rechtsstaat)
  • Fascism names enemies; liberalism answers with toleration (Karl Popper’s Open Society and Its Enemies)
  • Fascism glorifies sacrifice; liberalism valorizes compromise (John Rawls’s veil of ignorance)
  • Fascism promises destiny; liberalism replies with progress (Rawls’s incremental justice, Dewey’s social learning)
  • Fascism asserts inevitability; liberalism defends procedure (Isaiah Berlin’s value pluralism)

Walter Benjamin targeted exactly this: the faith that history already leans toward justice is a mirror image of fascism’s fatalism. Hannah Arendt showed how liberal proceduralism generates the isolation that fascism exploits. Theodor Adorno and Max Horkheimer saw how liberal optimism, by aestheticizing progress, prepared subjects for authoritarian aesthetics.

Liberalism is not fascism’s opposite. It is its dialectical twin — preserving the grammar by countering it step-for-step, remaining bound to the structure it seeks to negate.

Anarchist grammar

emsenn contrasts fascist grammar with what they call anarchist grammar, which follows a different sequence entirely: condition, commons, relation, solidarity, desire, immanence. Where fascist grammar moves from crisis to inevitability, anarchist grammar moves from present conditions to present possibility:

  • Condition replaces crisis: catastrophe is not an event demanding salvation but the ongoing condition of living under capitalism, empire, and the state (Achille Mbembe’s necropolitics)
  • Commons replaces pure community: shared, negotiated relations rather than purified identity (Hannah Arendt’s plurality, Kropotkin’s mutual aid)
  • Relation replaces enemy: domination is relational, not metaphysical — relations can shift, solidarities can emerge (Judith Butler’s grievability)
  • Solidarity replaces sacrifice: the refusal to abandon one another, not the glorification of suffering
  • Desire replaces destiny: aspiration in the present, not fantasy projected onto history (Lauren Berlant’s critique of cruel optimism)
  • Immanence replaces inevitability: nothing is guaranteed, everything depends on what we do here and now (Arendt’s natality, Benjamin’s “now-time”)

Fascism glorifies builders but builds nothing. It feeds parasitically on the commons, on solidarity and care, on the practices it denounces as weakness. Anarchist grammar actually produces futures. The storm survives only by consuming the worlds that anarchism generates.
