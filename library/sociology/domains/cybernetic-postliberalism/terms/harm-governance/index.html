Harm governance

Someone shares a small act of direct action online — stealing banner materials, organizing unpermitted aid, refusing to comply with public health abandonment. The immediate response is not about the target of the action or its consequences. It is about the terms under which it was shared. Was it safe to post? Will someone misinterpret it? Did the actor properly disclose the risk? The pushback is not strategic critique from within an organizing context. It is supervision — the internalized logic of an administrative state, reproduced between people who no longer trust the state itself.

At the same time, calls emerge for a general strike — an action historically known to trigger state repression, with no infrastructure in place to support it. The same people who warned against the small direct action now defend the strike on the grounds that its scale makes it safe. Enough people doing it together will make it legitimate.

This is harm governance. emsenn develops the term in “Between care and control” (2025) to name the logic that requires political action to be managed — administered through risk frameworks — in order to count as legitimate.

Care collapsed into risk management

The central distortion harm governance introduces is the equation of care with risk management. To care for someone becomes synonymous with managing the risks they face or pose. And management is only seen as legitimate when it resembles institutional or state practice — when it has protocols, oversight, measurable outcomes. Individual care that falls outside these frameworks is treated as naive at best, dangerous at worst.

emsenn identifies the specific inversion this produces: individual action is seen as inherently risky because it lacks validation, while mass action is seen as safe because institutional form makes it feel legitimate. The framework cannot recognize that institutional form itself introduces risks — of co-optation, of slowness, of dependence on the structures being challenged.

What makes this analysis sharp is emsenn’s observation about what happens when people lose faith in the state. The administrative logic does not disappear. It migrates. People who no longer trust the state begin to reproduce its role in how they interact with each other. They become the administrators of the discourse. They warn, contextualize, preempt, and correct — not always because they want to, but because they have been trained to understand responsibility as managing the behavior of others.

Community organizations adopt grant metrics. Mutual aid groups develop intake forms. Care networks build hierarchies. The administrative apparatus of the state migrates into the spaces that were supposed to be its alternatives — not because anyone chose this, but because harm governance has become the only available grammar for legitimating action.

The masking example

emsenn deploys this analysis through the case of masking during COVID. When someone says “you can evaluate a person’s political consistency by whether they’re still masking,” the pushback does not contest the claim that abandoning masking caused real harm. It does not deny that immunocompromised and disabled people have been excluded. What it does is reframe the conversation: away from harm and toward whether the judgment of the unmasked has been ethically justified.

This is what Elizabeth Povinelli names as a defining feature of late liberalism: harm is not denied but tolerated, as long as the actor can be shown to have been constrained. If a choice can be framed as non-choice — if behavior can be understood as economically or emotionally rational — then harm becomes acceptable. The question shifts from “what happened?” to “how do we feel about what happened?”

Care is not governance

emsenn draws a firm boundary. Care is not governance. Care does not wait for consensus. Care does not require everyone to understand. Care is not afraid of smallness, or inconsistency, or discomfort. Care is practice.

The people who continue to mask are not morally pure. They are acting in alignment with an analysis they already held. They did not stop when the mandates ended because they were never masking out of obedience. They were masking because they understood the system had already abandoned them — and others. And they understood that action did not require consensus to be valid.

The people still stealing supplies, organizing unapproved aid, refusing to perform normalcy — they are not reckless. They are no longer oriented around permission.

What harm governance reveals is how many others still are. People want mass action because it feels legitimate. They reject individual initiative because it feels ungoverned. They describe both impulses as “care,” but the framework is governance all the way down.

Harm governance as californication mechanism

Harm governance is one of californication’s interlocking mechanisms. It formats the felt urgency of care — the impulse to help, to act, to respond to suffering — into an administratively manageable form. The subject who wants to care is told they must first assess, plan, document, and seek validation. By the time the administrative requirements are met, the urgency has been absorbed into process. The radical content of mutual aid — people helping each other through shared need, without hierarchy or institutional oversight — is preserved in name while the form is captured by the state’s administrative logic.

This is what makes harm governance distinct from mere bureaucratization. It operates at the level of affect: it captures the feeling of care and reroutes it through the form of governance. The subject who follows the procedure feels like they are caring. The procedure ensures that the caring does not escape the system’s tolerance for disruption.
