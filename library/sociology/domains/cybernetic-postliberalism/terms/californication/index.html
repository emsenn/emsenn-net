Californication

Californication is the process by which liberal governance sustains itself under structural contradiction by formatting crisis as personally manageable affect. The term names what happens when a political system cannot resolve its own contradictions but can teach its subjects to experience those contradictions as personal conditions — as mood, trauma, growth opportunity, or lifestyle. The subject who meditates through a housing crisis, journals through a pandemic, or microdoses through ecological collapse is not failing to respond politically. They are doing exactly what the system requires: converting structural incoherence into self-regulatory behavior.

emsenn develops the concept within cybernetic postliberalism to name a process that several major theorists have described in parts but that no existing term captures as a unified circuit.

The problem: liberalism’s constitutive contradictions

Liberalism promises both universalism and individualism — that all people are equal and that each person is sovereign. These commitments are structurally incompatible. Universalism requires shared standards; individualism requires their refusal. Under stable conditions, this tension is manageable. Under stress — economic crisis, pandemic, ecological collapse, institutional delegitimization — the contradiction becomes visible. The system cannot deliver on both promises simultaneously, and the gap between what it promises and what it produces becomes experientially available to its subjects.

At this point, the system faces what emsenn calls the liberalism legitimacy crisis: liberalism can no longer produce the resolutions it promises. It shifts from promising resolution to promising regulation — from solving problems to managing them. This shift is where californication begins.

The theoretical circuit

Foucault: power that produces subjects

Michel Foucault, the French historian and philosopher of power, showed that modern governance does not operate primarily through prohibition or repression. It operates through the production of subjects — through what he called governmentality. The state does not merely tell you what you cannot do; it shapes who you are, what you desire, and how you understand yourself. Foucault’s concept of confession is particularly relevant: modern power incites people to speak about themselves, their desires, and their beliefs. This speech is not free expression — it is an act of subjection that binds the speaker to the ideological system they confess within. Social media, as emsenn argues, industrializes this confessional logic at scale.

Berlant: attachment to what harms you

Lauren Berlant, the American literary and affect theorist, coined the term cruel optimism to describe a condition in which the thing you desire is itself an obstacle to your flourishing. The fantasy of the good life — stable job, upward mobility, political representation — persists even after the structural conditions that once made it plausible have disintegrated. People maintain attachment to the fantasy not out of ignorance but because the attachment itself provides a livable structure of feeling.

Berlant’s other key contribution is genre. In her use, a genre is not a literary category but a set of expectations about how situations unfold — what counts as crisis, what counts as resolution, what feelings are appropriate at each stage. Genre calibrates affect: the self-help genre formats job loss as an opportunity for reinvention; the therapeutic genre formats it as a wound; the activist genre formats it as evidence of exploitation. The material event is the same. The genre determines what it means.

Berlant also names crisis ordinariness: the condition in which ongoing instability is absorbed into daily life rather than recognized as crisis. Crisis does not arrive as rupture. It persists as condition.

Hochschild: prescribed feeling

Arlie Russell Hochschild, the American sociologist, demonstrated that institutions prescribe not just behavior but feeling. Her concept of feeling rules names the social expectations governing which emotions are appropriate in which contexts. Her study of flight attendants showed that workers are required not merely to perform warmth but to cultivate it internally — this is emotional labor: the management of feeling to produce a publicly observable display.

Californication extends Hochschild’s insight from the workplace to the whole of liberal subjectivity. The system prescribes which affects count as legitimate responses to crisis. Serenity, resilience, growth-mindedness — these are not personal choices. They are the feeling rules of a system that requires its subjects to self-regulate rather than demand structural change.

Cybernetics: governance through feedback

Norbert Wiener, the American mathematician who coined the term cybernetics in 1948, defined it as the study of control and communication in animals and machines. His central insight was that systems maintain stability through feedback: they detect deviation from a desired state and correct for it. Stafford Beer, the British management cybernetician, extended this into organizational theory with the Viable System Model, which describes how organizations maintain viability by distributing correction across multiple feedback layers operating on different timescales. W. Ross Ashby formulated the Law of Requisite Variety: a system remains stable only if its internal complexity matches the complexity of its environment.

Californication is, in cybernetic terms, a feedback mechanism. The subject detects crisis (deviation), processes it affectively (correction), and returns to functional participation (stability). The correction does not require understanding. It requires self-regulation. The subject who routes crisis into mood — through therapy, wellness practice, content consumption, or confessional posting — is performing a cybernetic function: absorbing system-level deviation as personal adjustment.

Povinelli: endurance without resolution

Elizabeth Povinelli, the American anthropologist of late liberalism, describes a governance regime that no longer promises resolution. What it demands instead is endurance — the ongoing performance of survival under conditions that do not change. Her concept of the quasi-event names a disturbance that appears to open possibility but is already embedded in the logic of its own containment. The event never actually happens because the system has already processed its potential disruption before it arrives.

Californication is the affective mechanism of Povinelli’s endurance. The subject endures not through stoic resignation but through active self-management — routing crisis into affect, affect into content, content into participation. The quasi-event is what crisis looks like under californication: volatile on the surface, already contained at the structural level.

California as projection zone

The term references California not as geography but as historical laboratory. The Franciscan missions (Misiones Californianas) established the template: convert populations through the management of labor, belief, and daily practice. The Gold Rush formatted extraction as opportunity. Hollywood industrialized the production of inhabitable fantasy. The counterculture — Esalen Institute, psychedelic experimentation, the Summer of Love — transmuted political contradiction into spiritual journey. Silicon Valley built the platform infrastructure that made affect governance operative at planetary scale. The wellness industry — the Calm app, Burning Man, lifehacking, microdosing — commodified self-regulation as lifestyle.

Each of these formations performs the same operation: structural contradiction is reprocessed as personal experience, and that reprocessing is sold back to the subject as freedom.

How californication operates

The process works through several interlocking mechanisms, each named by other terms in the cybernetic postliberalism framework:

  • Genre calibration tunes expectations, formatting crisis as personal narrative rather than structural condition
  • Zen fascism enforces which affects are legitimate, pathologizing distress and eroticizing detachment — making crisis desirable as a source of healing and growth
  • Feeling rules prescribe the appropriate emotional responses — resilience, not rage; growth, not grief
  • Socially ontogenic media distributes inhabitable subject positions, modeling how to be inside contradiction without breaking
  • Harm governance collapses care into risk management, so that even resistance is administered

The subject produced by this process is what emsenn calls the savior-slave subject: simultaneously responsible for managing global crises (savior) and governed by systems they cannot transform (slave). Collapse is not hidden. It is narrativized, monetized, and aestheticized. It becomes sustained because it is wanted — the subject has been incited to desire crisis as source of healing, spiritual growth, and personal betterment. Wanting to resolve collapse becomes structurally incommensurable with the systems and affects that sustain it.

On platforms specifically, californication produces the neurotic platformal intellectual: a subject incited into performing expertise under conditions of anxiety, whose learning under crisis is formatted by platform feeling rules into authentic-seeming content. What circulates is not understanding but the affect of understanding.

What californication adds

Each of the theorists above describes part of the circuit. Foucault describes how power produces subjects. Berlant describes how subjects maintain attachment to harmful conditions. Hochschild describes how institutions prescribe feeling. Cybernetics describes how systems self-correct through feedback. Povinelli describes how late liberalism governs through endurance.

Californication names the specific process that connects them: structural contradiction is formatted as affect, affect is managed through self-regulation, self-regulation is the feedback mechanism that preserves system stability, and platforms make this operative at scale. The subject does not resist or comply. They self-regulate — and that self-regulation is governance.
