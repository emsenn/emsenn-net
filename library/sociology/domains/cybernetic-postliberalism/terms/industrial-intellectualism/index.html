Industrial intellectualism

A scholar writes about an abolitionist movement. The movement failed — the facility it opposed was built and is operating. But the paper succeeds. It moves through the correct arc: death-world (thesis), life-worlding (antithesis), building-and-fighting (synthesis). The citations confirm genre membership. The analysis produces the satisfaction the genre demands. The paper circulates, gets cited, gets taught. The facility continues operating. The paper does not need the facility to have been stopped. It needs the narrative to resolve correctly.

Industrial intellectualism is emsenn’s term for the structural condition in which intellectual labor is organized as a production process. Thought is converted into narrative products — threads, essays, papers, podcasts, newsletters — packaged for recognizability within a genre and evaluated by whether the analysis feels right, not by whether it corresponds to material outcomes. The term names not a type of person or a moral failing but a condition: the condition in which intellectual work, regardless of the worker’s intentions, is formatted by its market of circulation.

Hochschild’s insight, extended

The concept begins with Arlie Russell Hochschild’s framework of feeling rules and emotional labor. Hochschild showed that flight attendants are required not merely to perform cheerfulness but to cultivate genuine warmth as a condition of employment. The institution prescribes not just behavior but affect — and the worker who cannot produce the prescribed feeling is failing at their job.

emsenn extends this to intellectual labor. Scholars, journalists, and public intellectuals are required to produce analysis that satisfies the affective expectations of their genre. A scholar writing within the abolitionist genre must produce grief-turning-into-solidarity. A journalist writing within the liberal genre must produce concern-turning-into-procedural-hope. A public intellectual writing within the critical genre must produce complexity-turning-into-insight. The analysis succeeds when it feels right — when it satisfies the genre’s feeling rules — not when it corresponds to outcomes.

This is “industrial” in a specific sense. Thought is organized as a production process with inputs (events, data, citations) and outputs (narratives, takes, analyses) optimized for market circulation. The dialectical form — thesis, antithesis, synthesis — is, as emsenn puts it, “one of the most efficient production templates because it generates resolution at the level of narrative without requiring resolution at the level of fact.”

Citation as containment

In “Citing for containment,” emsenn shows this production process operating through citation itself. The essay examines how an academic account of the Stop Cop City movement uses the theorists — Berlant, Glissant, Gilmore, Moten and Harney, Mbembe — who were already part of how people on the ground were thinking and acting. But in the academic text, these theorists do not appear as companions in contradiction. They appear as citations that justify choices already made.

Berlant’s Cruel Optimism was used on the ground to navigate hard decisions — when does hope become a trap? when does staying become stuck? — in ways that slowed things down and sometimes prevented harm. In the academic text, Berlant’s work does not introduce tension. It resolves it. The arrest becomes transformation. The forest becomes a “life-world.” The carceral becomes fertile ground. The citations function, as emsenn writes, “less as tools than as signals, marking each step in a narrative arc that was already set.”

This is industrial intellectualism operating through what emsenn calls citing for containment: citation as genre performance rather than analytical claim. The citation does not make a claim about the world. It signals that the writer belongs to a genre. It confirms that the narrative arc has the right shape. It produces the affective satisfaction the market of circulation demands.

The production process

Industrial intellectualism converts embedded knowledge into circulable product. The conversion follows a pattern:

Practices that were fragile by design — intentionally quiet, dependent on trust, not meant to be made legible — are extracted from the contexts that made them possible and turned into narrative material. Leanne Betasamosake Simpson and Eve Tuck have warned that not all knowledge is meant to be extracted, cited, or scaled. Some knowledge is held in place — geographically, relationally, politically — because that is where it works. When it is lifted out and circulated as insight, it does not just lose context. It causes harm. It changes what others expect to find in those places. It turns embedded practice into transferable content.

Under industrial intellectualism, what survives is what can be turned into narrative. What cannot be separated from its conditions — what Sylvia Wynter would identify as knowledge that remains legible only because it can be reformatted, stripped of land, specificity, and obligation — falls away. The most vital knowledge is not the most portable. But portability is what the industry selects for.

The formatting loop

Industrial intellectualism connects to californication as one of its key mechanisms. The genre calibrates what counts as a satisfying analysis. The analysis is produced to satisfy. The satisfaction generates circulation. The circulation generates the metrics (engagement, citation, attention) that determine the intellectual’s standing. The standing determines what they can say next. The loop is cybernetic: each output feeds back as input for the next cycle.

This is why emsenn argues, in “The theory that survived the war,” that the survival of a theory is not evidence of its strategic value. It is evidence that the theory has been formatted for survivability — made usable by the systems that destroyed its referents. Gramsci’s organic intellectual survives in the archive not because the concept protects anyone but because the concept is “valuable, fundable, format-compatible.” Its ability to circulate without resistance suggests its formatting. Industrial intellectualism is the name for the condition that produces this formatting.
