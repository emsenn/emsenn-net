2026-03-20 A JurisdictionalDeclination is a triple (A, φ, G) — a forum A, a matter φ for which A holds valid jurisdiction J with JV(A, φ, t) ∈ H*_t, and a declination ground G — such that A's act introduces ¬Exercise(A, φ, t) ∈ H*_t: A will not apply σ^J_t to φ despite having the jurisdictional authority to do so. The defining structure: declination is the retraction of a nucleus's operation on a specific matter, distinct from dismissal (which finds JV ∉ H*_t) and from Δ_t-failure (where jurisdiction expires). A declines what it validly could do.

JurisdictionalDeclination

Formal definition

A JurisdictionalDeclination is a triple (A,φ,G)(A, \varphi, G):

(A:Authority,  φ∈HtJ,  G:DeclGround)(A : \mathrm{Authority},\; \varphi \in H^J_t,\; G : \mathrm{DeclGround})

where:

  • AA is the declining authority — the forum that holds valid jurisdiction over φ\varphi; the critical presupposition is that JV(A,φ,t)∈Ht∗J\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \in H^{*\mathcal{J}}_t — jurisdiction is established before declination can occur
  • φ∈HtJ\varphi \in H^J_t is the matter — the active matter before AA (introduced by an JurisdictionalInvocation); it is within AA’s sub-fiber
  • GG is the declination ground — the normative basis on which AA declines; ground is required because declination is not a right but a power with conditions; the normative system that constitutes AA specifies which grounds are valid

JurisdictionalDeclination vs. dismissal. These are structurally opposite outcomes following invocation:

JV(A, φ, t) Nucleus action Result
Proceed ∈Ht∗J\in H^{*\mathcal{J}}_t σtJ\sigma^{\mathcal{J}}_t applied to φ\varphi Forum acts on the matter
Dismissal ∉Ht∗J\notin H^{*\mathcal{J}}_t σtJ\sigma^{\mathcal{J}}_t returns ⊥\bot for φ\varphi No jurisdiction; dead letter
JurisdictionalDeclination ∈Ht∗J\in H^{*\mathcal{J}}_t σtJ\sigma^{\mathcal{J}}_t retracted for φ\varphi Forum has jurisdiction but does not exercise it

Dismissal is a factual finding (jurisdiction does not exist). JurisdictionalDeclination is a normative choice (jurisdiction exists but is not exercised).

Canonical grounds for declination. Different normative systems recognize different grounds:

  • Forum non conveniens (inconvenient forum): another forum is substantially more appropriate — it has more direct territorial scope, better access to evidence, or stronger connection to the parties; AA declines to allow the more appropriate forum to act
  • Ripeness failure: φ\varphi is within AA’s jurisdiction in principle but the matter is not ready for determination — the relevant fiber elements have not yet settled sufficiently for σtJ\sigma^{\mathcal{J}}_t to produce a meaningful output
  • Deference to concurrent jurisdiction: when A1A_1 and A2A_2 have concurrent jurisdiction and A1A_1 is already exercising its nucleus on φ\varphi, A2A_2 may decline to avoid conflicting resolutions
  • Mandatory declination: a meta-norm within AA’s constituting normative system requires AA to decline for specific classes of matter (certain matters are constitutionally committed to other forums)

Nuclear reading

Declination presupposes that JV(A,φ,t)=pos(A,t)∧φ\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) = \mathrm{pos}(A, t) \wedge \varphi is already in Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) — jurisdiction is established. The nuclear question is what the fixed-point structure implies about the relationship between this settled jurisdictional validity and the separate exercise proposition.

Definition (exercise proposition). Let Exercise(A,φ,t)∈Ht\mathrm{Exercise}(A, \varphi, t) \in H_t be the proposition “authority AA exercises its jurisdiction over matter φ\varphi at tt.” This is a distinct fiber element from JV(A,φ,t)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t): jurisdiction is the structural position; exercise is the act. Declination is the condition:

JV(A,φ,t)∈Fix(σt)andExercise(A,φ,t)∉Fix(σt).\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) \quad \text{and} \quad \mathrm{Exercise}(A, \varphi, t) \notin \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t).

This is a definition. The two conditions are about different fiber elements and are not in logical tension.

Proposition 1 (Fix(σ_t) does not force exercise from jurisdiction). The nuclear axioms alone do not entail that JV(A,φ,t)∈Fix(σt)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) implies Exercise(A,φ,t)∈Fix(σt)\mathrm{Exercise}(A, \varphi, t) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t).

Proof. The nuclear axioms — extensiveness (a≤σt(a)a \leq \sigma_t(a)), idempotence (σt∘σt=σt\sigma_t \circ \sigma_t = \sigma_t), meet-preservation (σt(a∧b)=σt(a)∧σt(b)\sigma_t(a \wedge b) = \sigma_t(a) \wedge \sigma_t(b)) — are stated for arbitrary elements of HtH_t and make no claims about implication between distinct elements. The settled status of one element JV(A,φ,t)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) does not, by these axioms alone, determine the settled status of a different element Exercise(A,φ,t)\mathrm{Exercise}(A, \varphi, t). The two elements are related by the normative structure of the system, not by the nuclear axioms. □\square

Remark. The claim that Exercise(A,φ,t)∉Fix(σt)\mathrm{Exercise}(A, \varphi, t) \notin \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) can coexist with JV(A,φ,t)∈Fix(σt)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) is consistent with the nuclear axioms, but it requires that Exercise(A,φ,t)≱JV(A,φ,t)\mathrm{Exercise}(A, \varphi, t) \not\geq \mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) in the Heyting order, or that the fiber does not have the implication JV(A,φ,t)≤Exercise(A,φ,t)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \leq \mathrm{Exercise}(A, \varphi, t) as a valid proposition. Whether the Heyting algebra HtH_t contains such an implication depends on the normative content, not on the nuclear axioms.

Proposition 2 (the declination act, if settled, is stable under idempotence). Let δdecl∈Ht\delta_{\mathrm{decl}} \in H_t be the declination act proposition. If δdecl∈Fix(σt)\delta_{\mathrm{decl}} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t), then σt(δdecl)=δdecl\sigma_t(\delta_{\mathrm{decl}}) = \delta_{\mathrm{decl}} and σt(σt(δdecl))=δdecl\sigma_t(\sigma_t(\delta_{\mathrm{decl}})) = \delta_{\mathrm{decl}}.

Proof. Direct application of idempotence (see Idempotence): for any a∈Fix(σt)a \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t), σt(a)=a\sigma_t(a) = a, so σt(σt(a))=σt(a)=a\sigma_t(\sigma_t(a)) = \sigma_t(a) = a. □\square

This means: a validly issued declination does not become “more declined” or “less declined” under iterated application of the nucleus. Settlement of the declination act is a one-step condition.

Proposition 3 (meet of declination with jurisdiction). If JV(A,φ,t)∈Fix(σt)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) and δdecl∈Fix(σt)\delta_{\mathrm{decl}} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t), then JV(A,φ,t)∧δdecl∈Fix(σt)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \wedge \delta_{\mathrm{decl}} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t).

Proof. Meet-preservation: σt(JV(A,φ,t)∧δdecl)=σt(JV(A,φ,t))∧σt(δdecl)=JV(A,φ,t)∧δdecl\sigma_t(\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \wedge \delta_{\mathrm{decl}}) = \sigma_t(\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t)) \wedge \sigma_t(\delta_{\mathrm{decl}}) = \mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \wedge \delta_{\mathrm{decl}} (see Meet Preservation). □\square

The joint proposition “jurisdiction holds AND declination is issued” is a settled fiber element when both components are settled. This is the nuclear expression of the well-definedness of the declination: the fact that jurisdiction obtains and the fact that it is not exercised can be simultaneously settled without contradiction.

What is not derivable from the nuclear axioms alone. The normative consequences of declination — that AA acquires a duty to explain, that the invoker acquires a review power, that accountability attaches — are not consequences of the nuclear axioms. They require additional axioms connecting settled propositions in the exercise sub-fiber to Hohfeldian normative positions. The nuclear axioms establish that δdecl\delta_{\mathrm{decl}} can be settled and that Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) is closed under meets; they do not interpret the content of settled elements as generating specific normative consequences.

Transfer-stability. The question whether δdecl∈Fix(Δt)\delta_{\mathrm{decl}} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) — that is, whether for every s⊥ts \perp t there exists b∈Hs⋆tb \in H_{s \star t} with H(is,t)(b)=δdeclH(i_{s,t})(b) = \delta_{\mathrm{decl}} (see Transfer Nucleus) — is not decidable from the nuclear axioms alone. It depends on the extension structure of the fiber bundle. Commutation of σt\sigma_t and Δt\Delta_t (see Commutation) ensures the two conditions are compatible, but does not establish which elements satisfy both.

JurisdictionalDeclination and accountability

AA is Accountable for the exercise of its jurisdiction: A:Outcomes(J)→{A}A : \mathrm{Outcomes}(\mathcal{J}) \to \{A\} means AA is answerable for all outcomes within J\mathcal{J}, including the outcome of not acting. A declination is itself an outcome within J\mathcal{J}: it appears in Outcomes(J)\mathrm{Outcomes}(\mathcal{J}) and AA is accountable for it. This is the formal basis of “a court cannot escape accountability by simply refusing to hear cases within its jurisdiction without cause.”

Open questions

  • Whether declination corresponds formally to a retraction of the sub-topos inclusion — a natural transformation from the sub-topos back toward the terminal object — or whether it is better modeled as a partial application of the nucleus (the nucleus is defined on HtJH^J_t but chooses not to fire on φ\varphi specifically).
  • Whether mandatory declination (constitutionally required non-exercise) is structurally different from voluntary declination: whether the mandatory case corresponds to a nucleus that is literally undefined on the matter (structural absence) vs. a nucleus defined but retracted (functional choice).
