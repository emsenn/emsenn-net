2026-03-20 Jurisdiction is a four-tuple (A, T_J, H_J, V) — an authority A, a sub-site T_J ⊆ T of the history monoid, a sub-fiber H_J ⊆ H|_{T_J} of the fiber bundle restricted to T_J, and a validity condition V — such that A's instructions create valid duties within T_J × H_J and have no normative force outside it. The defining structure: jurisdiction is a sub-topos of the full relational universe within which an authority's second-quartet powers are operative. Acts within jurisdiction propagate toward H*_t; acts outside it do not.

Jurisdiction

Formal definition

Jurisdiction is a four-tuple J=(A,TJ,HJ,V)\mathcal{J} = (A, T_J, H_J, V):

J=(A:Authority,  TJ↪T,  HJ↪H∣TJ,  V:TJ×HJ→Ω)\mathcal{J} = (A : \mathrm{Authority},\; T_J \hookrightarrow T,\; H_J \hookrightarrow H|_{T_J},\; V : T_J \times H_J \to \Omega)

where:

  • AA is the authority — the party whose normative powers are operative within the jurisdiction; AA holds second-quartet Hohfeldian Powers within (TJ,HJ)(T_J, H_J) and lacks them outside it
  • TJ⊆TT_J \subseteq T is the history sub-site — the temporal/spatial/subject-matter scope of the jurisdiction; acts at histories t∈TJt \in T_J fall within jurisdiction; acts at t∉TJt \notin T_J do not; TJT_J is equipped with the Grothendieck topology induced from (T,J)(T, J)
  • HJ⊆H∣TJH_J \subseteq H|_{T_J} is the fiber sub-bundle — the sub-fiber of the history fiber restricted to TJT_J that the jurisdiction covers; not all of HtH_t at t∈TJt \in T_J need be within jurisdiction — jurisdiction is typically restricted to a subject-matter sub-fiber (criminal matters, admiralty, equity)
  • V:TJ×HJ→ΩV : T_J \times H_J \to \Omega is the validity condition — the predicate that an act at (t,a)∈TJ×HJ(t, a) \in T_J \times H_J satisfies in order to fall within AA’s jurisdiction; acts that satisfy VV are jurisdictionally valid; those that fail VV are ultra vires

Jurisdiction as sub-topos. J\mathcal{J} corresponds to a sub-topos:

Sh(TJ,J∣TJ)↪Sh(T,J)\mathbf{Sh}(T_J, J|_{T_J}) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{Sh}(T, J)

The inclusion is a geometric morphism — the fiber bundle over TJT_J is a sub-sheaf of the full bundle. Within this sub-topos, AA’s nucleus σtA\sigma^A_t is operative: AA’s acts propagate toward Ht∗AH^{*A}_t. Outside it, σtA\sigma^A_t is not defined on the relevant elements.

What this is

Jurisdiction is the formal structure of “where authority applies.” It is more precise than scope (Area) because it specifies not just a region but a sub-fiber within that region (subject-matter jurisdiction), a validity condition (the type of acts covered), and the embedding of that sub-structure into the full relational universe.

The intuition: an authority’s normative powers do not operate on all of HtH_t for all tt. A maritime court has jurisdiction over admiralty matters (HJH_J = admiralty sub-fiber) at histories where maritime jurisdiction is invoked (TJT_J = histories with maritime nexus). Its orders are valid within this sub-topos; outside it, the orders are ultra vires regardless of the court’s formal existence.

Three jurisdiction types

Personal jurisdiction (TJT_J determined by the subject’s presence or consent): whether the authority can bind the specific person GG. The history sub-site TJT_J is the set of histories where GG is present in or consented to the authority’s domain. Personal jurisdiction without subject-matter jurisdiction is insufficient for valid orders.

Subject-matter jurisdiction (HJH_J determined by the type of claim): whether the authority can adjudicate or govern this type of matter. HJH_J is the sub-fiber of propositions of the relevant type. A probate court has subject-matter jurisdiction over estate matters; it lacks it over criminal conduct — the criminal-matter sub-fiber is outside HJH_J even if the history t∈TJt \in T_J.

Territorial/temporal jurisdiction (determined by TJT_J directly): whether the authority’s acts cover this place and time. The history sub-site TJT_J is a spatio-temporal region. An authority’s instructions at t∈TJt \in T_J propagate toward HTJ∗H^*_{T_J}; at t∉TJt \notin T_J, the instructions do not create duties in the relevant fiber.

Concurrent jurisdiction: two authorities A1A_1 and A2A_2 both have jurisdiction at (t,a)∈TJ×HJ(t, a) \in T_J \times H_J — their jurisdiction sub-topoi overlap. This is the formal analog of overlapping legal systems (federal and state courts; maritime and domestic courts). Conflict rules (lex posterior, lex superior, lex specialis) determine which authority’s acts propagate toward Ht∗H^*_t when their orders conflict within the overlap.

Jurisdiction and judgment

Judgment requires zone ZZ — the scope within which the judging officer has discretionary authority. Zone ZZ is the jurisdiction restricted to the officer’s personal scope: Z=J∣B=(B,TJ∩TB,HJ∩HB,V∣TB×HB)Z = \mathcal{J}|_B = (B, T_J \cap T_B, H_J \cap H_B, V|_{T_B \times H_B}) where TBT_B and HBH_B are the officer’s temporal and fiber positions. An act α\alpha within ZZ is both within the officer’s jurisdiction and within their discretionary scope; it is intra vires for the officer.

Excess of jurisdiction in judgment is the condition α∉Z\alpha \notin Z: the officer acts within J\mathcal{J} (the authority’s full jurisdiction) but outside ZZ (the officer’s personal sub-jurisdiction). The act is valid within J\mathcal{J} but unauthorized within ZZ — an insubordinate act rather than an ultra vires one.

An ultra vires act is α∉J\alpha \notin \mathcal{J} entirely: the act is not within any jurisdiction that would make it normatively valid. Ultra vires acts produce elements in Ht∖Ht∗H_t \setminus H^{*}_t that the normative system’s nucleus will not propagate — they are dead letters.

Jurisdiction and delegation

Delegation from AA to BB within scope DsubD_{\mathrm{sub}} creates a derived jurisdiction:

JB=(B,  TJ∩TDsub,  HJ∩HDsub,  V∣Dsub)\mathcal{J}_B = (B,\; T_J \cap T_{D_{\mathrm{sub}}},\; H_J \cap H_{D_{\mathrm{sub}}},\; V|_{D_{\mathrm{sub}}})

BB’s derived jurisdiction is a restriction of AA’s jurisdiction to the sub-scope of the delegation. The key properties:

  1. JB⊆JA\mathcal{J}_B \subseteq \mathcal{J}_A: the delegate’s jurisdiction is always a subset of the principal’s
  2. JA≠JB+JA∖B\mathcal{J}_A \neq \mathcal{J}_B + \mathcal{J}_{A \setminus B}: the principal’s remaining jurisdiction after delegation is not defined by subtraction; AA retains the full jurisdiction JA\mathcal{J}_A, with BB holding a derived sub-jurisdiction within it
  3. The anti-override domain (Captain invariant 4): within Π⊆S\Pi \subseteq S, external authorities cannot override AA’s sub-jurisdiction — JA∣Π\mathcal{J}_A|_{\Pi} is immune from external power

Nuclear reading

The nuclear structure of jurisdiction concerns which elements of the fiber Heyting algebra HtH_t are in the reach of a given authority’s saturation nucleus, and what the nuclear axioms alone entail about that reach.

Definition (jurisdictional scope at t). Given jurisdiction J=(A,TJ,HJ,V)\mathcal{J} = (A, T_J, H_J, V), the jurisdictional scope at tt is the sub-object HtJ⊆HtH^J_t \subseteq H_t consisting of those fiber elements φ\varphi for which t∈TJt \in T_J and φ∈HJ\varphi \in H_J. This is a definition: HtJH^J_t is specified by the jurisdiction parameters, not derived from the nuclear axioms.

Definition (jurisdictional validity proposition). For φ∈HtJ\varphi \in H^J_t with V(t,φ)=⊤V(t, \varphi) = \top and pos(A,t)∈Ht\mathrm{pos}(A, t) \in H_t (the proposition that AA holds the relevant positional authority at tt), the jurisdictional validity proposition is the meet:

JV(A,φ,t)  :=  pos(A,t)∧φ  ∈  Ht.\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \;:=\; \mathrm{pos}(A, t) \wedge \varphi \;\in\; H_t.

This is a definition. The meet is the fiber-level expression of the joint condition “authority holds AND act is within scope AND validity is satisfied.” We use the Heyting algebra meet ∧\wedge on HtH_t; no additional structure is required.

Proposition 1 (within-scope settlement propagates from settled authority). Suppose φ∈HtJ\varphi \in H^J_t, V(t,φ)=⊤V(t, \varphi) = \top, and pos(A,t)∈Fix(σt)\mathrm{pos}(A, t) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t). Suppose additionally that φ∈Fix(σt)\varphi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) (the matter is itself past-saturated). Then JV(A,φ,t)=pos(A,t)∧φ∈Fix(σt)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) = \mathrm{pos}(A, t) \wedge \varphi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t).

Proof. By hypothesis σt(pos(A,t))=pos(A,t)\sigma_t(\mathrm{pos}(A, t)) = \mathrm{pos}(A, t) and σt(φ)=φ\sigma_t(\varphi) = \varphi. By meet-preservation of σt\sigma_t (see Meet Preservation):

σt(pos(A,t)∧φ)=σt(pos(A,t))∧σt(φ)=pos(A,t)∧φ.\sigma_t(\mathrm{pos}(A, t) \wedge \varphi) = \sigma_t(\mathrm{pos}(A, t)) \wedge \sigma_t(\varphi) = \mathrm{pos}(A, t) \wedge \varphi.

So JV(A,φ,t)∈Fix(σt)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t). □\square

Proposition 2 (unsettled authority blocks settlement of jurisdictional validity). Suppose pos(A,t)∉Fix(σt)\mathrm{pos}(A, t) \notin \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t), i.e., σt(pos(A,t))>pos(A,t)\sigma_t(\mathrm{pos}(A, t)) > \mathrm{pos}(A, t). Then for any φ∈Ht\varphi \in H_t:

σt(JV(A,φ,t))=σt(pos(A,t))∧σt(φ)≥pos(A,t)∧φ=JV(A,φ,t),\sigma_t(\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t)) = \sigma_t(\mathrm{pos}(A, t)) \wedge \sigma_t(\varphi) \geq \mathrm{pos}(A, t) \wedge \varphi = \mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t),

with equality iff σt(pos(A,t))∧σt(φ)=pos(A,t)∧φ\sigma_t(\mathrm{pos}(A, t)) \wedge \sigma_t(\varphi) = \mathrm{pos}(A, t) \wedge \varphi. This can fail: if σt(pos(A,t))>pos(A,t)\sigma_t(\mathrm{pos}(A, t)) > \mathrm{pos}(A, t) strictly, then JV(A,φ,t)∉Fix(σt)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \notin \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) whenever φ>⊥\varphi > \bot (since σt(JV(A,φ,t))≥σt(pos(A,t))∧φ>pos(A,t)∧φ\sigma_t(\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t)) \geq \sigma_t(\mathrm{pos}(A, t)) \wedge \varphi > \mathrm{pos}(A, t) \wedge \varphi). So AA’s instruction cannot be settled if AA’s positional authority is unsettled.

Proof. Apply meet-preservation: σt(pos(A,t)∧φ)=σt(pos(A,t))∧σt(φ)\sigma_t(\mathrm{pos}(A,t) \wedge \varphi) = \sigma_t(\mathrm{pos}(A,t)) \wedge \sigma_t(\varphi). If σt(pos(A,t))>pos(A,t)\sigma_t(\mathrm{pos}(A,t)) > \mathrm{pos}(A,t) and σt(φ)≥φ>⊥\sigma_t(\varphi) \geq \varphi > \bot (by extensiveness), then the product strictly exceeds pos(A,t)∧φ\mathrm{pos}(A,t) \wedge \varphi. □\square

What is not derivable from the nuclear axioms alone. The claim that an act φ\varphi outside HtJH^J_t cannot be advanced into Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) “by AA’s act” requires the additional axiom that AA’s act can only produce propositions in HtJH^J_t. The nuclear axioms σt∘σt=σt\sigma_t \circ \sigma_t = \sigma_t, a≤σt(a)a \leq \sigma_t(a), σt(a∧b)=σt(a)∧σt(b)\sigma_t(a \wedge b) = \sigma_t(a) \wedge \sigma_t(b) govern the behavior of σt\sigma_t on all of HtH_t uniformly; they do not restrict the domain of the nucleus to HtJH^J_t. The scope boundary HtJH^J_t is an additional structural specification of the jurisdiction, external to the nuclear axioms. Accordingly: the claim “ultra vires acts cannot reach Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t)” is not derivable from the nuclear axioms alone; it requires the scope-boundary axiom as an additional hypothesis.

Proposition 3 (idempotence: jurisdictional validity is not iterated). σt(σt(JV(A,φ,t)))=σt(JV(A,φ,t))\sigma_t(\sigma_t(\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t))) = \sigma_t(\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t)).

Proof. Idempotence of σt\sigma_t (see Idempotence) applied to JV(A,φ,t)∈Ht\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \in H_t. □\square

There is no “more jurisdictionally valid” beyond the first application of σt\sigma_t. An act that reaches Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) is fully settled; an act that does not is not advanced by further applications.

Proposition 4 (meet of jurisdictional validity propositions). If JV(A,φ1,t)∈Fix(σt)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi_1, t) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) and JV(A,φ2,t)∈Fix(σt)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi_2, t) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t), then JV(A,φ1,t)∧JV(A,φ2,t)∈Fix(σt)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi_1, t) \wedge \mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi_2, t) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t).

Proof. Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) is closed under meets by meet-preservation (this is Proposition 1 of constitution.md, applied here). □\square

This is the conjunction property for jurisdictional authority: if two instructions are both within jurisdiction, their conjunction is also within jurisdiction.

Transfer-fixedness and the transfer nucleus. The commutation axiom σt∘Δt=Δt∘σt\sigma_t \circ \Delta_t = \Delta_t \circ \sigma_t (see Commutation) ensures that Fix(σt)∩Fix(Δt)=Ht∗\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) = H^*_t is well-defined. An element JV(A,φ,t)∈Ht∗\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) \in H^*_t iff σt(JV(A,φ,t))=JV(A,φ,t)\sigma_t(\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t)) = \mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) (past-saturated) AND Δt(JV(A,φ,t))=JV(A,φ,t)\Delta_t(\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t)) = \mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) (transfer-stable, meaning for every s⊥ts \perp t there exists b∈Hs⋆tb \in H_{s \star t} with H(is,t)(b)=JV(A,φ,t)H(i_{s,t})(b) = \mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t); see Transfer Nucleus). Whether a specific JV(A,φ,t)\mathrm{JV}(A, \varphi, t) is transfer-stable depends on the structure of the fiber bundle, not on the nuclear axioms alone; it is not derivable in general and must be checked case by case.

Open questions

  • Whether every jurisdiction can be represented as a sub-topos inclusion, or whether there are jurisdictions that require a more general geometric morphism (e.g., a surjection or a localization rather than a full sub-topos embedding).
  • Whether conflicting jurisdictions — two authorities with overlapping jurisdiction sub-topoi — can be formally ranked by a priority order on authorities, and whether this priority order is a natural transformation between the inclusion functors.
  • Whether “declining jurisdiction” (a court that refuses to hear a case despite having jurisdiction) corresponds to a retraction of the inclusion morphism — a natural transformation from the sub-topos back to a point — and whether this retraction creates any normative obligations for the declining authority.
