Sociology

2026-03-20 A Revocation is a triple (Q, G, B) — a principal Q who granted authority G to beneficiary B — together with a retraction act ρ that removes G from B's operative domain and restores it to Q's exclusive domain. The defining structure: revocation is the left inverse of delegation; it retracts the sub-presheaf H_G ↪ H_Q that was shared with B, collapsing B's claim on G back to ⊥. Three cases: full revocation (entire grant withdrawn), partial revocation (sub-grant G' ⊆ G withdrawn while G G' remains), and conditional revocation (withdrawal triggered by B's breach or changed circumstances). Revocation is always unilateral by Q — it does not require B's consent. Formally: a sub-presheaf retraction, the left inverse of the inclusion morphism that constituted the delegation.

Revocation

Formal definition

A Revocation is a triple R=(Q,G,B)\mathcal{R} = (Q, G, B):

R=(Q:Principal,  G⊆Authority(Q),  B:Beneficiary)\mathcal{R} = (Q : \mathrm{Principal},\; G \subseteq \mathrm{Authority}(Q),\; B : \mathrm{Beneficiary})

together with a retraction act ρ:HG→⊥HQ\rho : H_G \to \bot_{H_Q}:

ρ:HG↪HQ  →  ⊥HQ\rho : H_G \hookrightarrow H_Q \;\to\; \bot_{H_Q}

where:

  • QQ is the revoking principal — the original grantor of GG; the party who holds the authority GG as part of their operative domain Authority(Q)\mathrm{Authority}(Q) and who retains the Hohfeldian power to withdraw it
  • GG is the grant being revoked — the sub-domain of QQ’s authority that was delegated to BB; GG is a subset G⊆Authority(Q)G \subseteq \mathrm{Authority}(Q) that was shared as a sub-presheaf HG↪HQH_G \hookrightarrow H_Q
  • BB is the beneficiary — the agent from whose operative domain GG is being withdrawn; after ρ\rho, BB no longer has authority within GG
  • ρ\rho is the retraction act — the formal event (written order, notification, explicit withdrawal) that collapses BB’s access to GG back to ⊥HQ\bot_{H_Q}: the sub-presheaf HGH_G is retracted from BB’s domain

Three invariants. R\mathcal{R} is a valid revocation iff:

  1. Grantor authority: QQ holds GG — either G⊆Authority(Q)G \subseteq \mathrm{Authority}(Q) originally, or QQ has been re-granted GG before revoking. A party cannot revoke a grant they did not make. The Hohfeldian power to revoke is held by the grantor: only QQ can undo what QQ granted.

  2. Grant existence: GG was actually delegated to BB — there is a delegation record establishing that BB currently holds GG. Revoking a non-existent grant is void: G⊈Authority(B)G \not\subseteq \mathrm{Authority}(B) means there is nothing to retract.

  3. Unilaterality: ρ\rho does not require BB’s consent. Revocation is the principal’s unilateral exercise of the Hohfeldian power to alter BB’s normative position. BB may challenge the revocation (e.g., claiming contractual protection), but the revocation act itself is not void merely because BB objects.

The three cases

Full revocation

The entire grant GG is withdrawn: ρ:HG→⊥HQ\rho : H_G \to \bot_{H_Q} collapses all of GG back to the bottom element. After ρ\rho, BB’s operative domain is Authority(B)∖G\mathrm{Authority}(B) \setminus G — everything BB held before the delegation, minus GG.

Full revocation is the simplest case and the default. The delegation is treated as having never existed from the moment ρ\rho is effective. Third parties who relied on BB’s authority within GG must now deal directly with QQ (unless the revocation was not properly notified — see invariant 3 of Delegation).

Partial revocation

Only a sub-grant G′⊊GG' \subsetneq G is withdrawn. BB retains authority within G∖G′G \setminus G'. The retraction act ρ\rho is:

ρG′:HG′↪HG→⊥HG\rho_{G'} : H_{G'} \hookrightarrow H_G \to \bot_{H_G}

and HG∖G′↪HQH_{G \setminus G'} \hookrightarrow H_Q remains active. Partial revocation is a sub-presheaf retraction: the sub-presheaf HGH_G is replaced by the smaller sub-presheaf HG∖G′H_{G \setminus G'}; the difference HG′H_{G'} is collapsed.

Partial revocation is common in commercial practice: a principal limits an agent’s authority after over-reaching but retains the agent for the remaining scope. In military practice: a commanding officer restricts a subordinate’s authority over a specific domain (e.g., revokes their authority to approve expenditures over a certain threshold) while leaving command authority intact.

Conditional revocation

The retraction act ρ\rho is triggered by a predicate — typically a Breach by BB or a change in circumstances that makes the grant inappropriate. The revocation is not effective until the trigger condition is satisfied. Before trigger: GG remains active. After trigger: ρ\rho fires automatically.

Conditional revocation is common in agency law and contract: “This authority is revocable on breach” is a conditional revocation clause. In the formal framework: the retraction act ρ\rho is a morphism from the breach-conditioned section of HGH_G to ⊥HQ\bot_{H_Q} — a morphism with a guard condition on BB’s breach status.

Math grounding

A delegation from QQ to BB for grant GG creates a sub-presheaf sharing: the sub-presheaf HG↪HQH_G \hookrightarrow H_Q of QQ’s authority presheaf is made accessible to BB. In terms of the relational universe morphism (Relational Universe Morphism): the delegation is an inclusion morphism (ϕG,αG):UG↪UQ(\phi_G, \alpha_G) : \mathbf{U}_G \hookrightarrow \mathbf{U}_Q where ϕG\phi_G is the inclusion of GG’s history category into QQ’s and αG\alpha_G is the identity on the shared portion of the presheaf.

Revocation is the left inverse of this inclusion: a retraction rG:UG→⊥r_G : \mathbf{U}_G \to \bot such that rG∘(ϕG,αG)=0r_G \circ (\phi_G, \alpha_G) = 0 (the zero morphism, collapsing all content to ⊥\bot). In Heyting algebra terms: the grant GG was a proposition g∈HQg \in H_Q that was shared downward; revocation applies the settling nucleus σt\sigma_t in the dissolving direction — σt(g)revoke=⊥HQ\sigma_t(g)_{\mathrm{revoke}} = \bot_{H_Q} after ρ\rho.

The three cases map to:

  • Full revocation: retraction of the entire inclusion (ϕG,αG)(\phi_G, \alpha_G)
  • Partial revocation: retraction of a sub-inclusion (ϕG′,αG′)⊊(ϕG,αG)(\phi_{G'}, \alpha_{G'}) \subsetneq (\phi_G, \alpha_G)
  • Conditional revocation: retraction guarded by a local section predicate (breach status of BB)

Renunciation is the beneficiary-initiated return of a grant: BB retracts their own access to GG and returns it to QQ’s exclusive domain. This is not revocation (which is QQ-initiated) but has the same formal effect: HGH_G is no longer accessible to BB.

Revocability and irrevocability

Not all grants are revocable at will. A grant may be:

  • Gratuitous and freely revocable: a simple delegation without consideration; QQ can revoke at any time without liability
  • Coupled with an interest: if BB paid consideration for the grant, revocation may constitute breach of contract — QQ retains the Hohfeldian power to revoke but incurs liability; the revocation is effective but wrongful
  • Irrevocable: a grant made by deed or supported by specific consideration under terms that preclude revocation; the Hohfeldian power to revoke has been alienated

In the formal framework: irrevocability is the alienation of the Hohfeldian power-to-revoke. After alienation, QQ no longer holds the power ρ\rho; the retraction act is no longer available to QQ. The sub-presheaf HGH_G remains accessible to BB for the agreed period regardless of QQ’s wishes.

Open questions

  • Whether the Hohfeldian power-to-revoke is itself a section of the authority fibration — a section sρ:{revokeG}→Ht∗∗s_{\rho} : \{\mathrm{revoke}_G\} \to H^*_{t^*} — and whether alienating this power is a retraction of sρs_{\rho} (the power-to-revoke is revoked by the grantor’s own act of alienation).
  • Whether partial revocation that progressively reduces a grant — a sequence G⊃G1⊃G2⊃…⊃Gn=∅G \supset G_1 \supset G_2 \supset \ldots \supset G_n = \emptyset — forms a filtration of the sub-presheaf HGH_G, and whether this filtration has a canonical limit (total revocation as the colimit of the partial retractions).
  • Whether conditional revocation (triggered by breach) has the structure of a Lawvere–Tierney topology: whether the breach condition defines a nucleus j:Ω→Ωj : \Omega \to \Omega on the subobject classifier such that the revocation is exactly the jj-closed subobject of HGH_G.
