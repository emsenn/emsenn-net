2026-03-21 A Polity is a quadruple (T_P, J_P, Aut, C_P) — a constitutional theory T_P (a geometric theory classifying valid institutional forms), a recognition topology J_P (the Grothendieck topology grounding collective recognition within the polity), an autarkeia certificate Aut (the polity is self-sufficient: its classifying topos [T_P] has a conservative set of points — every institutional question within the polity is decided by some model of T_P), and a constituent power C_P (the founding act that constitutes T_P without itself deriving authority from any prior T). The defining structure: the polity is the entity that grants flags to ships, issues the authorizing act A that makes charters binding, and provides the background topology within which all institutions within it acquire constitutional force. Mathematical correspondence: the polity is the classifying topos [T_P] = Sh(Site(T_P), J_T) for the geometric theory T_P — every institution within the polity is a model of T_P, hence a geometric morphism from the institution's topos into [T_P]. Aristotle's autarkes (self-sufficient community, Politics I.2, 1253a2): the polity is the level at which the community is complete — it contains family, civil society, and the legal order as articulated members, and does not require appeal to a higher community for its internal legitimacy.

Polity

Formal definition

A Polity is a quadruple P=(TP,JP,Aut,CP)\mathcal{P} = (T_\mathcal{P}, J_\mathcal{P}, \mathrm{Aut}, C_\mathcal{P}):

P=(TP:ConstitutionalTheory,  JP:RecognitionTopology,  Aut:Autarkeia,  CP:ConstituentPower)\mathcal{P} = (T_\mathcal{P} : \mathrm{ConstitutionalTheory},\; J_\mathcal{P} : \mathrm{RecognitionTopology},\; \mathrm{Aut} : \mathrm{Autarkeia},\; C_\mathcal{P} : \mathrm{ConstituentPower})

where:

  • TPT_\mathcal{P} is the constitutional theory — a geometric theory (a theory expressed in geometric logic: existential quantifiers, finite conjunctions, arbitrary disjunctions, no negation, no universal quantifiers in the primary clauses) whose models are the valid institutional forms within the polity; TPT_\mathcal{P} specifies what kinds of institutions, charters, ships, and enterprises are constitutionally recognized; a specific ship or institution within the polity is a model of TPT_\mathcal{P}
  • JPJ_\mathcal{P} is the recognition topology — the Grothendieck topology on the polity’s history site (TP-T,JP)(T_\mathcal{P}\text{-}T, J_\mathcal{P}) that determines what counts as a valid covering within the polity’s collective recognition structure; this is the background topology within which all the polity’s institutions acquire their sheaf-theoretic force
  • Aut\mathrm{Aut} is the autarkeia certificate — the property that the classifying topos [ ⁣[TP] ⁣][\![ T_\mathcal{P} ]\!] has a conservative set of points: for every geometric sentence φ\varphi in the language of TPT_\mathcal{P}, if TP⊬φT_\mathcal{P} \not\vdash \varphi, then there exists a model M\mathcal{M} of TPT_\mathcal{P} in which φ\varphi is false; the polity is self-sufficient iff its classifying topos decides all relevant institutional questions internally — without appeal to a higher polity
  • CPC_\mathcal{P} is the constituent power — the founding act that creates TPT_\mathcal{P} without itself deriving authority from any prior constitutional theory; CPC_\mathcal{P} is the initial object in the category of constitutional acts within P\mathcal{P}: every authorization chain within P\mathcal{P} traces back to CPC_\mathcal{P}; CPC_\mathcal{P} is the source of all the polity’s delegations

Four invariants. P\mathcal{P} is a polity iff it satisfies:

  1. Containment completeness: the constitutional theory TPT_\mathcal{P} is rich enough that every institutional form operative within the polity — every ship, institution, charter, enterprise, and company — is a model of TPT_\mathcal{P}. The polity contains all the institutional forms it governs; there are no institutions within the polity that operate outside the scope of TPT_\mathcal{P}.

  2. Autarkeia (self-sufficiency): the classifying topos [ ⁣[TP] ⁣][\![ T_\mathcal{P} ]\!] has a conservative set of points. No legitimacy-conferring act within the polity requires reference to an external polity’s constitutional order. This is Aristotle’s criterion (Politics I.2, 1253a2): the polis is the community that is complete (teleia) — it comes into being for the sake of living but exists for the sake of living well. The self-sufficient community is one that contains all the conditions for the good life of its members; a polity that lacks autarkeia is a partial order within a larger polity.

  3. Flag and charter authority: the polity exercises two derived authorities flowing from CPC_\mathcal{P}:

    • Flag authority: the polity can register ships — extend its legal order to vessels operating under its flag. A flag state (UNCLOS Art. 91) is precisely a polity that has exercised this authority: the ship’s registration morphism ϕ:Tship→TP\phi : T_{\mathrm{ship}} \to T_\mathcal{P} is the legal act by which the polity’s legal order extends to the ship’s history structure.
    • Charter authority: the polity is the source of the authorizing act AA in the Charter spec — the authority that makes a charter binding rather than merely advisory. A charter without a valid authorizing act is a specification (a blueprint) but not a constitution; the authorizing act traces to CPC_\mathcal{P} through a chain of delegations.

  4. Constitutional closure: the polity has a procedure for revising TPT_\mathcal{P} — a constitutional amendment process that changes the geometric theory while maintaining continuity with the existing institutional order. Constitutional amendment is a geometric morphism f:[ ⁣[TP] ⁣]→[ ⁣[TP′] ⁣]f : [\![ T_\mathcal{P} ]\!] \to [\![ T'_\mathcal{P} ]\!] between the old and new classifying toposes. The polity is constitutionally closed iff every valid revision of TPT_\mathcal{P} is generated by geometric morphisms within the polity’s own constitutional structure — no revision requires appeal to an external authority.

Aristotle’s three levels and the polity

Aristotle, Politics I.1–2 (1252a1–1253a37), establishes the polity as the culmination of three levels of community:

oikos (household)  ⊂  kome (village)  ⊂  polis (polity)\mathrm{oikos\ (household)} \;\subset\; \mathrm{kome\ (village)} \;\subset\; \mathrm{polis\ (polity)}

The household exists for daily necessity — the ongoing reproduction of life. The village is a collection of households cooperating for something beyond daily necessity. The polis is the community that comes into being for the sake of living but exists for the sake of living well — it is the first community that is complete (teleia), not merely necessary. The polis is the telos of the community-hierarchy: the level at which the community becomes self-sufficient.

Aristotle’s argument at 1252b29–1253a3: the polis is prior in nature to the individual and to the household, even though it is posterior in time. The priority in nature means: the polis is the completed form of which the household and village are parts — they are explicable only by reference to the whole they constitute. The polity is not an aggregate of households and villages but the organic whole within which households and villages have their function. Applied to the relational universe: individual institutions and ships are elements in the classifying topos [ ⁣[TP] ⁣][\![ T_\mathcal{P} ]\!]; they are constituted by the polity’s constitutional theory, not prior to it.

Constitutional registration as universe morphism: three proved results

Sources: RelationalUniverseGeometricTheory, HyperverseInitiality.

The internal object. A polity is a relational universe RP=Sh(TP,JP)R_\mathcal{P} = \mathbf{Sh}(T_\mathcal{P}, J_\mathcal{P}) — a history site (TP,JP)(T_\mathcal{P}, J_\mathcal{P}) equipped with a fiber sheaf HPH_\mathcal{P}, RelationalHistoryFiberSaturatingNucleus σP\sigma_\mathcal{P}, RelationalHistoryFiberTransferringNucleus ΔP\Delta_\mathcal{P}, and their joint fixed-point sub-sheaf HP∗H^*_\mathcal{P} (RelationalHistoryFixedFiber). The “constitutional theory TPT_\mathcal{P}” in the formal definition is the relational universe axioms instantiated at the polity level; the polity’s site (TP,JP)(T_\mathcal{P}, J_\mathcal{P}) is the concrete object, and RPR_\mathcal{P} is the relational universe it generates.

What “institution within polity” means. An institution RIR_I is within polity RPR_\mathcal{P} iff there exists a RelationalUniverseMorphism (ϕ,α):RI→RP(\phi, \alpha): R_I \to R_\mathcal{P} where:

  • ϕ:TI→TP\phi: T_I \to T_\mathcal{P} is a history functor embedding institution histories into polity histories
  • α:ϕ∗HP⇒HI\alpha: \phi^* H_\mathcal{P} \Rightarrow H_I is a natural transformation of fiber sheaves (constitutional data: how polity propositions restrict to institution propositions)
  • Nucleus compatibility: α∘σP=σI∘α\alpha \circ \sigma_\mathcal{P} = \sigma_I \circ \alpha and α∘ΔP=ΔI∘α\alpha \circ \Delta_\mathcal{P} = \Delta_I \circ \alpha

This is definitional: “within” IS defined as the existence of such a morphism. What can be proved is that these morphisms are closed under composition and preserve the constitutional hard core.

Proposition (Composition of registrations). If (ϕ,α):RI→RP(\phi, \alpha): R_I \to R_\mathcal{P} is a registration morphism and (ψ,β):RJ→RI(\psi, \beta): R_J \to R_I is a sub-institution morphism, then (ϕ∘ψ,  β∘ψ∗α):RJ→RP(\phi \circ \psi,\; \beta \circ \psi^* \alpha): R_J \to R_\mathcal{P} is a registration morphism.

Proof.

  1. ϕ∘ψ:TJ→TP\phi \circ \psi: T_J \to T_\mathcal{P} is a functor (composite of functors).
  2. ψ∗α:ψ∗ϕ∗HP⇒ψ∗HI\psi^* \alpha: \psi^* \phi^* H_\mathcal{P} \Rightarrow \psi^* H_I is the pullback of α\alpha along ψ\psi, using (ϕ∘ψ)∗=ψ∗∘ϕ∗(\phi \circ \psi)^* = \psi^* \circ \phi^*.
  3. β∘ψ∗α:(ϕ∘ψ)∗HP⇒HJ\beta \circ \psi^* \alpha: (\phi \circ \psi)^* H_\mathcal{P} \Rightarrow H_J is the vertical composite: natural in TJT_J because both ψ∗α\psi^* \alpha and β\beta are.
  4. Nucleus compatibility of the composite: (β∘ψ∗α)∘σP=β∘ψ∗(α∘σP)=β∘ψ∗(σI∘α)=β∘(σI∘ψ∗α)=σJ∘(β∘ψ∗α)(\beta \circ \psi^* \alpha) \circ \sigma_\mathcal{P} = \beta \circ \psi^*(\alpha \circ \sigma_\mathcal{P}) = \beta \circ \psi^*(\sigma_I \circ \alpha) = \beta \circ (\sigma_I \circ \psi^* \alpha) = \sigma_J \circ (\beta \circ \psi^* \alpha), using nucleus compatibility of α\alpha then β\beta in sequence. Similarly for ΔP\Delta_\mathcal{P}. □\square

Proposition (Constitutional hard core preserved by registration). For every registration morphism (ϕ,α):RI→RP(\phi, \alpha): R_I \to R_\mathcal{P} and every history t∈TIt \in T_I, the component αt:HP(ϕ(t))→HI(t)\alpha_t: H_\mathcal{P}(\phi(t)) \to H_I(t) sends RelationalHistoryFixedFiber elements to RelationalHistoryFixedFiber elements: if c∈HP∗(ϕ(t))c \in H^*_\mathcal{P}(\phi(t)), then αt(c)∈HI∗(t)\alpha_t(c) \in H^*_I(t).

Proof.

  1. Let c∈HP∗(ϕ(t))c \in H^*_\mathcal{P}(\phi(t)), meaning σP(ϕ(t))(c)=c\sigma_\mathcal{P}(\phi(t))(c) = c and ΔP(ϕ(t))(c)=c\Delta_\mathcal{P}(\phi(t))(c) = c.
  2. σ\sigma-compatibility gives: σI(t)(αt(c))=αt(σP(ϕ(t))(c))=αt(c)\sigma_I(t)(\alpha_t(c)) = \alpha_t(\sigma_\mathcal{P}(\phi(t))(c)) = \alpha_t(c).
  3. Δ\Delta-compatibility gives: ΔI(t)(αt(c))=αt(ΔP(ϕ(t))(c))=αt(c)\Delta_I(t)(\alpha_t(c)) = \alpha_t(\Delta_\mathcal{P}(\phi(t))(c)) = \alpha_t(c).
  4. Steps 2–3 give αt(c)∈HI∗(t)\alpha_t(c) \in H^*_I(t). □\square

Corollary (Constitutional commitment is transitive). Every doubly-stable proposition c∈HP∗c \in H^*_\mathcal{P} constitutes a constitutional commitment that every registered institution must satisfy via pullback through α\alpha. Removing cc from HP∗H^*_\mathcal{P} requires a constitutional amendment (a change to JPJ_\mathcal{P} that changes the nuclei σP,ΔP\sigma_\mathcal{P}, \Delta_\mathcal{P}) and automatically releases all registered institutions from that commitment.

Constitutional amendment = topology change. A constitutional amendment replaces JPJ_\mathcal{P} with JP′J'_\mathcal{P}, yielding RP′=Sh(TP,JP′)R'_\mathcal{P} = \mathbf{Sh}(T_\mathcal{P}, J'_\mathcal{P}) with new nuclei σP′,ΔP′\sigma'_\mathcal{P}, \Delta'_\mathcal{P} and new fixed fiber HP′∗H'^*_\mathcal{P}. By the charter amendment theorem (Charter §Charter amendment, governing principles, and the threshold), the amendment is constitutionally valid iff the induced morphism f:RP→RP′f: R_\mathcal{P} \to R'_\mathcal{P} preserves the polity’s founding hard core: the constitutional core CP⊆HP∗C_\mathcal{P} \subseteq H^*_\mathcal{P} embeds as a sub-fixed-point of HP′∗H'^*_\mathcal{P}. If CP⊈f∗(HP′∗)C_\mathcal{P} \not\subseteq f^*(H'^*_\mathcal{P}), the amendment dissolves the constitutional core and constitutes a new polity rather than amending the old one.

CLAIM (not proved in general): polity as classifying object. The analogy with the classifying topos — that RelationalUniverseMorphisms RI→RPR_I \to R_\mathcal{P} are in natural bijection with models of the polity’s constitutional axioms in RIR_I — holds if and only if RP≅Rsyn(TP,JP)R_\mathcal{P} \cong R_\mathrm{syn}(T_\mathcal{P}, J_\mathcal{P}), the syntactic relational universe freely generated by the polity’s history site. By RelationalUniverseGeometricTheory, the global classifying object for all relational universes is RsynR_\mathrm{syn} — the initial model of the RelationalUniverseGeometricTheory TRU\mathbb{T}_\mathbf{RU}, whose geometric morphisms classify all relational universes universally. A polity RPR_\mathcal{P} is a local classifying object for its institutional forms iff RPR_\mathcal{P} is the freely-generated relational universe over (TP,JP)(T_\mathcal{P}, J_\mathcal{P}) — verified by SyntacticRelationalUniverseInitiality for the specific history site. This verification is the polity-level completeness condition; its general status is open.

The universal polity. By HyperverseInitiality, the hyperverse H=lim→⁡nR(n)\mathcal{H} = \varinjlim_n R^{(n)} is the initial object in the category of hyperverse models: every hyperverse model V\mathbf{V} receives a unique morphism H→V\mathcal{H} \to \mathbf{V}. In the direction of registration morphisms (from institution to polity), the hyperverse is the smallest structure that maps into every model — and every polity RPR_\mathcal{P} is a specific relational universe within the hyperverse. The hyperverse is the polity that contains all relational universes; every other polity is a quotient of it via the unique morphism H→RP\mathcal{H} \to R_\mathcal{P}.

Flag and charter authority in the relational universe

Flag registration. The Ship spec defines the ship’s flag as the history functor component ϕ:Tship→TF\phi: T_\mathrm{ship} \to T_F where TFT_F is the flag state’s history category. In polity terms: TF=TPT_F = T_\mathcal{P}, and the flag registration is the history-functor component ϕ\phi of the full constitutional registration RelationalUniverseMorphism (ϕ,α):Rship→RP(\phi, \alpha): R_\mathrm{ship} \to R_\mathcal{P}. The fiber component α:ϕ∗HP⇒Hship\alpha: \phi^* H_\mathcal{P} \Rightarrow H_\mathrm{ship} carries the constitutional data: how the polity’s doubly-stable propositions restrict to the ship’s fiber.

UNCLOS Art. 91 requires that ships have a genuine link to the flag state — a real connection, not merely nominal. In relational universe terms: the RelationalUniverseMorphism (ϕ,α)(\phi, \alpha) must be non-trivial — the fiber component α\alpha must carry genuine constitutional content from HP∗H^*_\mathcal{P} into Hship∗H^*_\mathrm{ship} (the Hard Core Preservation proposition above requires this content to be non-empty for the registration to be constitutionally meaningful). A flag of convenience corresponds to a morphism (ϕ,α)(\phi, \alpha) where α\alpha is trivially the zero map or factors through the two-element fiber of the minimal relational universe: the ship’s hard core Hship∗H^*_\mathrm{ship} receives no constitutional content from the polity.

Charter authorization. The Charter spec defines the authorizing act AA as the authority that makes the charter binding rather than merely advisory. In polity terms: AA is the data of the constitutional registration RelationalUniverseMorphism (ϕcharter,αcharter):Rcharter→RP(\phi_\mathrm{charter}, \alpha_\mathrm{charter}): R_\mathrm{charter} \to R_\mathcal{P} — the fiber component αcharter\alpha_\mathrm{charter} witnesses the charter’s constitutional validity by showing how the polity’s fixed fiber maps into the charter’s fixed fiber.

Delegation chains: if an institution II within the polity issues a sub-charter to a company K\mathcal{K}, the sub-charter’s authorizing act is the composite RelationalUniverseMorphism (ϕP∘ϕI,  αI∘ϕI∗αP):RK→RP(\phi_\mathcal{P} \circ \phi_I,\; \alpha_I \circ \phi_I^* \alpha_\mathcal{P}): R_\mathcal{K} \to R_\mathcal{P} — established by the Composition of Registrations proposition above. Every delegation chain is a composable sequence of RelationalUniverseMorphisms with RPR_\mathcal{P} as terminal target.

Autarkeia: internal candidate and open equivalence

Source: RelationalHistoryFixedPresheafAutomorphismRigidityAxiom.

The autarkeia condition — self-sufficiency — has the following internal candidate traced through named constructs.

Internal candidate (RelationalHistoryFixedPresheafAutomorphismRigidityAxiom applied at polity level). The polity RPR_\mathcal{P} is autarkic iff its fixed fiber sheaf HP∗H^*_\mathcal{P} satisfies the following: for any two registration morphisms (ϕ,α):RI→RP(\phi, \alpha): R_I \to R_\mathcal{P} and (ϕ′,α′):RI′→RP(\phi', \alpha'): R_{I'} \to R_\mathcal{P} with RI≇RI′R_I \not\cong R_{I'}, there exists a history t∈TPt \in T_\mathcal{P} and a doubly-stable proposition c∈HP∗(t)c \in H^*_\mathcal{P}(t) such that αt(c)≠αt′(c)\alpha_t(c) \neq \alpha'_t(c) — the pullback values of cc to the two institutions are distinct.

This is the polity-level extension of RelationalHistoryFixedPresheafAutomorphismRigidityAxiom. That axiom states: no nontrivial automorphism of TPT_\mathcal{P} preserving JPJ_\mathcal{P} is invisible to HP∗H^*_\mathcal{P} — the fixed fiber distinguishes all histories. The autarkeia extension says: HP∗H^*_\mathcal{P} not only distinguishes histories within the polity but distinguishes institutions registered within it. A polity where two distinct institutions RI≇RI′R_I \not\cong R_{I'} agree on all doubly-stable polity propositions has a failure of autarkeia: it cannot distinguish the two institutions from within.

What autarkeia failure looks like internally. A polity fails autarkeia iff there exist two institutions RI≇RI′R_I \not\cong R_{I'} with registration morphisms (ϕ,α)(\phi, \alpha) and (ϕ′,α′)(\phi', \alpha') such that αt(c)=αt′(c)\alpha_t(c) = \alpha'_t(c) for all t∈TPt \in T_\mathcal{P} and all c∈HP∗(t)c \in H^*_\mathcal{P}(t). In this case, the polity’s constitutional hard core HP∗H^*_\mathcal{P} carries no information that distinguishes RIR_I from RI′R_{I'}: the two institutions are constitutionally indistinguishable. A polity in this state must appeal to a higher-level polity (a polity RP′R_{\mathcal{P}'} with RP→RP′R_\mathcal{P} \to R_{\mathcal{P}'} as a sub-polity morphism) whose larger HP′∗H^*_{\mathcal{P}'} can distinguish them.

SEMANTIC INTERPRETATION: The external “conservative set of points” condition and this internal “fixed-fiber separation” condition are semantically equivalent — both say the polity distinguishes its institutional content from within. Whether they are formally identical requires deriving that the evaluations {HP∗(t)}t∈TP\{H^*_\mathcal{P}(t)\}_{t \in T_\mathcal{P}} constitute a conservative set of points of RPR_\mathcal{P} in the topos-theoretic sense. This requires showing that the fixed fiber evaluations separate morphisms in RPR_\mathcal{P} globally — a consequence of the RelationalHistoryFiberHeytingAlgebraIsomorphism (collapse isomorphism: when the RelationalHistoryFiberSaturatingNucleus transfer stable fiber injectivity condition holds, Fix(Δt)≅Ht∗\mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) \cong H^*_t) combined with the sheaf separation condition. Whether the collapse isomorphism applies at every history of every polity — or only at histories where the saturation-transfer injectivity condition holds — is open. The formal equivalence is a consequence of this open condition, not independently established.

Nuclear derivation

The polity’s institutional propositions inhabit a fiber at the polity level of the hyperverse tower — the level above individual institutions:

HtP  =  { φ∈L(TP) : φ is a geometric sentence in the language of TP}H^\mathcal{P}_t \;=\; \bigl\{\, \varphi \in L(T_\mathcal{P}) \,:\, \varphi \text{ is a geometric sentence in the language of } T_\mathcal{P} \bigr\}

Constitutional validity. A geometric sentence φ\varphi is constitutionally valid at history tt iff it is a theorem of TPT_\mathcal{P} — iff TP⊢φT_\mathcal{P} \vdash \varphi. This is the polity-level analog of RelationalHistoryFixedFiber: the doubly-stable propositions at the polity level are the theorems of TPT_\mathcal{P}. Every institution within the polity must satisfy these theorems; the theorems are the polity’s hard core in the enterprise sense — they require a change to TPT_\mathcal{P} (a constitutional amendment) to remove, not merely a change to individual institutions’ nuclear pairs.

Polity-level nuclei. The polity has its own nuclear pair:

  • RelationalHistoryFiberSaturatingNucleus at the polity level: the operator taking a geometric sentence φ\varphi to the weakest theorem above φ\varphi in TPT_\mathcal{P} — the backward-stable closure within the polity’s constitutional theory
  • RelationalHistoryFiberTransferringNucleus at the polity level: the operator taking φ\varphi to its forward-implication under the constitutional theory — what φ\varphi commits the polity to in future constitutional moments

The polity’s RelationalHistoryFixedFiber is the set of theorems of TPT_\mathcal{P}: propositions whose nuclear closure in both directions is themselves.

Constitutional amendment as topology change. At the individual institution level, operating within the institution means operating within a fixed topology JJ; operating on the institution means changing JJ (see Institution §Beck-Chevalley). At the polity level, the same distinction operates: operating within the polity’s constitutional order means operating within TPT_\mathcal{P}; operating on the polity’s constitutional order means changing TPT_\mathcal{P} itself — the polity-level analog of a change to the Grothendieck topology. The polity’s constitutional amendment process is the formal procedure for changing TPT_\mathcal{P} in a constitutionally authorized way (i.e., in a way sanctioned by CPC_\mathcal{P}, the constituent power).

The three traditions

Aristotle (Politics, 350 BCE): the polis is the complete community — the telos of the association hierarchy, prior in nature to its parts, constituted by its constitution (politeia) as its soul. The three constitutional forms (monarchy, aristocracy, polity) and their degenerations (tyranny, oligarchy, democracy) are the possible specifications of the polity’s constitutional theory TPT_\mathcal{P}.

Pettit (Republicanism, 1997): the polity is the community constituted by non-domination — members stand in relations of freedom from arbitrary interference. In classifying topos terms: the theory TPT_\mathcal{P} has the non-domination axiom as a theorem — no model of TPT_\mathcal{P} includes a relation in which one party can arbitrarily interfere with another without institutional accountability. Republican constitutionalism is the program of making non-domination a theorem of TPT_\mathcal{P} by the right choice of constitutional theory.

MacIntyre (After Virtue, 1981; Whose Justice? Which Rationality? 1988): the polity is the community within which practices (enterprises in the enterprise spec sense) can pursue their internal goods. The polity’s constitutional theory TPT_\mathcal{P} includes the recognition of internal goods as goods — not merely as instrumental to external goods (market value, political power). A polity whose TPT_\mathcal{P} reduces all goods to external goods has degenerated in the MacIntyrean sense: its enterprises can no longer pursue genuine internal goods because the constitutional order does not recognize them.

Open questions

  • Whether every polity’s constitutional order is Boolean (every proposition in HP∗H^*_\mathcal{P} satisfies excluded middle: c∨¬c=⊤c \vee \neg c = \top) or genuinely intuitionistic (some propositions admit intermediate degrees of settledness). The existing relational universe uses intuitionistic Heyting algebras at every fiber; a polity with a Boolean fixed fiber HP∗H^*_\mathcal{P} would have a fully decidable constitutional order where every institutional question is definitively settled or definitively unsettled. Whether there are polities with genuinely non-Boolean HP∗H^*_\mathcal{P} — where constitutional questions admit degrees corresponding to the Heyting implication structure — is open. The connection to Rawls’s publicity condition (analyzed in Office) may constrain this.
  • Whether the dissolution of a polity corresponds to a formal condition in the polity containment order: whether there exists a smallest polity containing any given institutional complex, or whether the containment relation on polities (ordered by registration morphisms RI→RPR_I \to R_\mathcal{P}) is not well-founded below the hyperverse H\mathcal{H}. The HyperverseInitiality theorem establishes H\mathcal{H} as the maximum (every polity receives a unique morphism from H\mathcal{H}); it does not establish a minimum. Whether every institutional complex has a unique smallest enclosing polity is unresolved.
  • Whether the classifying-object claim for RPR_\mathcal{P} can be established: whether every polity’s relational universe is freely generated by its history site (TP,JP)(T_\mathcal{P}, J_\mathcal{P}) in the sense of SyntacticRelationalUniverseInitiality. If yes, the “institution within polity = RelationalUniverseMorphism into RPR_\mathcal{P}” bijection becomes the full classifying-object universal property. If no, polities carry extra unchosen structure beyond their history sites, and the constitutional uniqueness theorem of HyperverseInitiality (no accidental models) would fail at the polity level.
  • Whether the formal equivalence between autarkeia and fixed-fiber separation is derivable: whether the RelationalHistoryFiberSaturatingNucleus transfer stable fiber injectivity condition (which gives Fix(Δt)≅Ht∗\mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) \cong H^*_t and enables the separation argument in the autarkeia section) holds at every history of a well-formed polity, or only at saturation-complete histories. If the condition fails at some histories, the polity has histories where its fixed fiber does not separate institutions — localized failures of autarkeia at specific histories, rather than global failure.
  • Whether the polity’s constituent power CPC_\mathcal{P} can be specified internally: whether CPC_\mathcal{P} is the initial object in the category of RelationalUniverseMorphisms from the polity’s own RPR_\mathcal{P} to itself (the identity morphism in the self-registration sense), or whether the constituent power is necessarily at a higher tower level (the polity-founding act lives in R(n+1)R^{(n+1)} if the polity itself lives at level nn). The HyperverseInitiality corollary — the four initiality results form a single stratified freeness pattern — suggests that the constituent power is the level-(n+1)(n+1) initiality morphism from the hyperverse into the polity: CP=ϕn:R(n)→VC_\mathcal{P} = \phi_n: R^{(n)} \to \mathbf{V} at the appropriate tower level. Sieyès’s paradox (the constituent power creates the order but cannot be authorized by it) may correspond to the fact that this morphism lives one tower level above the polity, exactly as a RelationalUniverseMorphism R(n)→RPR^{(n)} \to R_\mathcal{P} is a morphism between tower levels, not within a single level.
  • Asabiyyah and polity stability: the connection between shadow density in HPH_\mathcal{P} and the polity’s group solidarity is addressed in Asabiyyah, which establishes the shadow partition as the formal substrate and provides the proved level-0 and level-1 propositions. Whether the polity-level shadow density directly corresponds to Ibn Khaldun’s cycle at the polity level — rather than at the level of individual institutions — remains open.
