Sociology

2026-03-20 A NormConflict is a triple (N₁, N₂, R) — two norms N₁ and N₂ whose simultaneous satisfaction is impossible at history t (their required outputs are incompatible in H_t), and a resolution rule R that determines which norm yields. The defining structure: N₁ requires a ∈ H*_t and N₂ requires b ∈ H*_t but a ∧ b = ⊥ (incompatible elements). Without R, the normative system is inconsistent at t — its nucleus demands the impossible. With R, the conflict is resolved by priority: one norm's output enters H*_t; the other is defeated.

Norm Conflict

Formal definition

A NormConflict is a triple (N1,N2,R)(N_1, N_2, R):

NC=(N1:Norm,  N2:Norm,  R:NormPriority)\mathcal{NC} = (N_1 : \mathrm{Norm},\; N_2 : \mathrm{Norm},\; R : \mathrm{NormPriority})

where:

  • N1=(φ1,O1,N)N_1 = (\varphi_1, O_1, \mathcal{N}) and N2=(φ2,O2,N)N_2 = (\varphi_2, O_2, \mathcal{N}) are Norms within the same normative system N\mathcal{N}
  • The conflict condition: at history tt, satisfying N1N_1 requires some element a∈Ht∗a \in H^*_t and satisfying N2N_2 requires some b∈Ht∗b \in H^*_t, but a∧b=⊥a \wedge b = \bot in HtH_t — the two requirements are incompatible; no element in HtH_t satisfies both simultaneously
  • RR is the resolution rule — the priority ordering or conflict-resolution operator that selects which norm’s requirement enters Ht∗H^*_t; without RR, the conflict renders the normative system inconsistent at tt

Conflict condition formally. Let σtN1\sigma^{N_1}_t and σtN2\sigma^{N_2}_t be the local saturation nuclei induced by N1N_1 and N2N_2 respectively on HtH_t. The norms conflict at tt iff:

Fix(σtN1)∩Fix(σtN2)={⊥}\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_1}_t) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_2}_t) = \{\bot\}

Their fixed-fiber intersection is trivial — no non-trivial element of HtH_t satisfies both simultaneously.

Conflict taxonomy

Four structurally distinct conflict types arise in normative systems:

Type I — Direct conflict: O1(φ)O_1(\varphi) and O2(¬φ)O_2(\neg \varphi) — obligation to do φ\varphi and obligation not to do φ\varphi. The most obvious case. Nuclear form: σtN1\sigma^{N_1}_t requires a∈Ht∗a \in H^*_t and σtN2\sigma^{N_2}_t requires ¬a∈Ht∗\neg a \in H^*_t, but a∧¬a=⊥a \wedge \neg a = \bot in the Heyting algebra.

Type II — Contrary-to-duty (Chisholm’s Paradox): the conflict arises from nesting. A primary norm O(p)O(p) and a secondary norm O(¬p⇒q)O(\neg p \Rightarrow q) where qq is incompatible with pp’s consequence. The paradox: if pp is obligatory but violated (¬p\neg p occurs), then qq becomes obligatory — but satisfying qq may be incompatible with satisfying pp, creating a new conflict. Nuclear form: the conditional norm O(¬p⇒q)O(\neg p \Rightarrow q) is only active when ¬p∈Ht∖Ht∗\neg p \in H_t \setminus H^*_t; once ¬p\neg p occurs, a new nucleus σtN2\sigma^{N_2}_t activates and conflicts with σtN1\sigma^{N_1}_t. Contrary-to-duty conflicts are the formal structure of remedial obligations after breach: “Breach of D creates a new duty D’ to remedy, which may conflict with other standing duties.”

Type III — Dilemma (moral dilemma, Antigone): both norms have equal weight by any priority ordering; the conflict is genuine and cannot be resolved without residue. Neither norm can be defeated without normative cost. Nuclear form: R(N1,N2)=12R(N_1, N_2) = \frac{1}{2} — the resolution rule is undefined or yields a tie. The formal system cannot resolve this; it must be escalated to an authority with power to establish a new priority or grant an exception.

Type IV — Temporal conflict: N1N_1 applies at t1t_1 and N2N_2 applies at t2t_2 where t1t_1 and t2t_2 are the same history but the norms were issued at different times. Lex posterior resolves: the later norm supersedes the earlier. Nuclear form: the later nucleus σt2N2\sigma^{N_2}_{t_2} has precedence over σt1N1\sigma^{N_1}_{t_1} for the overlapping sub-fiber.

Resolution rules

Five canonical resolution operators:

Lex superior (hierarchy of authority): N1≻N2N_1 \succ N_2 iff N1N_1 is issued by an authority higher in the hierarchy than N2N_2’s issuer. The higher norm’s fixed-fiber requirement wins. Nuclear form: σtN1\sigma^{N_1}_t has priority; the conflict is resolved by Fix(σtN1)∩Ht\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_1}_t) \cap H_t over Fix(σtN2)∩Ht\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_2}_t) \cap H_t.

Lex posterior (temporal priority): N1≻N2N_1 \succ N_2 iff N1N_1 was issued later than N2N_2. Later norms override earlier on the same subject matter. Nuclear form: the nucleus activated by the later issuance supersedes the earlier.

Lex specialis (specificity): N1≻N2N_1 \succ N_2 iff N1N_1 applies to a narrower class of situations than N2N_2. More specific norms override more general ones for the specific situation. Nuclear form: the sub-fiber over which N1N_1 operates is smaller than N2N_2’s; within that sub-fiber, N1N_1’s nucleus applies.

Lex mitior (lenity): in penal contexts, the less burdensome norm prevails. The norm creating fewer obligations or lighter sanctions is preferred where the conflict is unresolved by other rules. Nuclear form: the nucleus with the smaller gap-demand wins.

Explicit priority declaration: a meta-norm N3N_3 in the normative system explicitly ranks N1N_1 over N2N_2 for conflict situations of type T\mathcal{T}. Nuclear form: N3N_3 introduces an ordering on the nuclei σtN1≻σtN2\sigma^{N_1}_t \succ \sigma^{N_2}_t for elements in T\mathcal{T}’s sub-fiber.

Residue: the defeated norm is not erased

When RR resolves a conflict in favor of N1N_1 over N2N_2, N2N_2 is defeated — its requirement does not enter Ht∗H^*_t at the current conflict point. But N2N_2 is not erased: it remains in the normative system and continues to apply in situations where it does not conflict with N1N_1. The defeat is local; the norm persists globally.

The formal content: the nuclei σtN1\sigma^{N_1}_t and σtN2\sigma^{N_2}_t both exist; the resolution rule introduces a priority ordering such that σtN1\sigma^{N_1}_t is applied first (or exclusively) in the conflict zone, but σtN2\sigma^{N_2}_t continues to operate in its non-conflict sub-fiber.

This is the formal basis of the “moral residue” in dilemmatic situations: even when N2N_2 is defeated, the agent bears a residue obligation — a duty to acknowledge the loss, compensate, or take corrective action — because the defeated norm’s demands do not vanish, they are merely overridden.

Norm conflict in judgment

Judgment is where norm conflicts are resolved in practice. The officer at (Z,I,f,α)(Z, I, f, \alpha) encounters a conflict when two or more norms within ZZ generate incompatible requirements on α\alpha. The officer’s phronesis includes the ability to:

  1. Identify the conflict type
  2. Apply the appropriate resolution rule RR
  3. Select the action α\alpha that satisfies the prioritized norm
  4. Acknowledge the residue of the defeated norm (if any)

An officer who resolves a norm conflict incorrectly — selecting the action required by the lower-priority norm — commits an error of judgment without necessarily violating any norm in isolation. The conflict resolution is the distinctively judgment act; each individual norm is perfectly consistent.

Nuclear reading

We work in the fiber Heyting algebra HtH_t at a fixed history t∈Tt \in T, with saturation nucleus σt\sigma_t and transfer nucleus Δt\Delta_t. Both are extensive, idempotent (Idempotence), meet-preserving (Meet Preservation), and commuting (Commutation).

Definition (Norm-induced nucleus). A norm NN with required output aN∈Hta_N \in H_t induces a nucleus σtN\sigma^N_t on HtH_t whose fixed fiber is the principal up-set:

Fix(σtN)=↑aN={b∈Ht:aN≤b}\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^N_t) = {\uparrow}a_N = \{b \in H_t : a_N \leq b\}

The operator σtN\sigma^N_t is extensive (b≤σtN(b)b \leq \sigma^N_t(b) for all b∈Htb \in H_t, since σtN(b)=b∨aN≥b\sigma^N_t(b) = b \vee a_N \geq b), idempotent (σtN(σtN(b))=σtN(b∨aN)=b∨aN∨aN=b∨aN=σtN(b)\sigma^N_t(\sigma^N_t(b)) = \sigma^N_t(b \vee a_N) = b \vee a_N \vee a_N = b \vee a_N = \sigma^N_t(b)), and meet-preserving (σtN(b1∧b2)=(b1∧b2)∨aN=(b1∨aN)∧(b2∨aN)=σtN(b1)∧σtN(b2)\sigma^N_t(b_1 \wedge b_2) = (b_1 \wedge b_2) \vee a_N = (b_1 \vee a_N) \wedge (b_2 \vee a_N) = \sigma^N_t(b_1) \wedge \sigma^N_t(b_2), by distributivity of the Heyting algebra). Thus σtN\sigma^N_t is a nucleus in the sense of Meet Preservation.

Proposition (Norm conflict = mutual incompatibility of requirements). Let N1N_1 require a1a_1 and N2N_2 require a2a_2. The intersection of their fixed fibers is:

Fix(σtN1)∩Fix(σtN2)=↑a1∩↑a2=↑(a1∨a2)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_1}_t) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_2}_t) = {\uparrow}a_1 \cap {\uparrow}a_2 = {\uparrow}(a_1 \vee a_2)

This intersection contains only ⊤t\top_t iff a1∨a2=⊤ta_1 \vee a_2 = \top_t.

Proof. An element bb lies in both ↑a1{\uparrow}a_1 and ↑a2{\uparrow}a_2 iff a1≤ba_1 \leq b and a2≤ba_2 \leq b, iff a1∨a2≤ba_1 \vee a_2 \leq b. So ↑a1∩↑a2=↑(a1∨a2){\uparrow}a_1 \cap {\uparrow}a_2 = {\uparrow}(a_1 \vee a_2). This equals {⊤t}\{\top_t\} iff a1∨a2=⊤ta_1 \vee a_2 = \top_t. □\square

Definition (Norm conflict at tt). Norms N1N_1 and N2N_2 conflict at tt iff a1∨a2=⊤ta_1 \vee a_2 = \top_t in HtH_t — equivalently, iff Fix(σtN1)∩Fix(σtN2)={⊤t}\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_1}_t) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_2}_t) = \{\top_t\}, so that the only element satisfying both norms simultaneously is ⊤t\top_t: the normative system demands the impossible from any duty-bearer.

Proposition (Direct conflict implies degenerate combined system). Suppose N1=O(φ)N_1 = O(\varphi) and N2=O(¬φ)N_2 = O(\neg\varphi) — obligation to do φ\varphi and obligation not to do φ\varphi — with corresponding requirements a1=φa_1 = \varphi and a2=¬φa_2 = \neg\varphi where ¬\neg is the Heyting negation in HtH_t. Then the saturation nucleus σtN1\sigma^{N_1}_t requires φ∈Fix(σtN1)\varphi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_1}_t) and σtN2\sigma^{N_2}_t requires ¬φ∈Fix(σtN2)\neg\varphi \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_2}_t). The meet-preservation of the system’s combined closure gives:

σtN1(φ∧¬φ)=σtN1(⊥t)=⊥t∨φ=φ≠⊥t\sigma^{N_1}_t(\varphi \wedge \neg\varphi) = \sigma^{N_1}_t(\bot_t) = \bot_t \vee \varphi = \varphi \neq \bot_t

so applying σtN1\sigma^{N_1}_t to ⊥t\bot_t yields φ\varphi, and by the same reasoning σtN2(⊥t)=¬φ\sigma^{N_2}_t(\bot_t) = \neg\varphi. The combined system σtN1∘σtN2\sigma^{N_1}_t \circ \sigma^{N_2}_t applied to ⊥t\bot_t gives σtN1(¬φ)\sigma^{N_1}_t(\neg\varphi). In a Heyting algebra where φ∧¬φ=⊥t\varphi \wedge \neg\varphi = \bot_t, the element ¬φ\neg\varphi satisfies φ≤σtN1(¬φ)=¬φ∨φ\varphi \leq \sigma^{N_1}_t(\neg\varphi) = \neg\varphi \vee \varphi, which is not ⊥t\bot_t unless φ=⊥t\varphi = \bot_t. The normative system has no non-trivial joint fixed point: it cannot settle any element below ⊤t\top_t as simultaneously satisfying both norms.

Proof. Immediate from the Proposition above: a1∨a2=φ∨¬φa_1 \vee a_2 = \varphi \vee \neg\varphi. In a Boolean algebra this equals ⊤t\top_t. In a general Heyting algebra, φ∨¬φ≤⊤t\varphi \vee \neg\varphi \leq \top_t with equality not guaranteed; if the system is classical (Boolean) at tt, then φ∨¬φ=⊤t\varphi \vee \neg\varphi = \top_t and the conflict is total. □\square

Remark (Consistency condition for normative systems). A consistent normative system at tt must have aN<⊤ta_N < \top_t for every norm NN in force — each norm requires something strictly below ⊤t\top_t. Moreover the system is jointly consistent iff ⋁NaN<⊤t\bigvee_N a_N < \top_t — the join of all requirements is not the top element, so there exist non-trivial elements satisfying all norms simultaneously.

Proposition (Resolution rule restricts the nucleus to the conflict zone). Given a conflict between N1N_1 and N2N_2 at tt, with resolution rule RR prioritizing N1N_1: define the conflict sub-fiber Htconf={b∈Ht:b≤a1∨a2}H^{\mathrm{conf}}_t = \{b \in H_t : b \leq a_1 \vee a_2\}. The resolved effective nucleus on HtconfH^{\mathrm{conf}}_t is σtN1∣Htconf\sigma^{N_1}_t|_{H^{\mathrm{conf}}_t}, with:

Fix(σtN1∣Htconf)=↑a1∩Htconf\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_1}_t|_{H^{\mathrm{conf}}_t}) = {\uparrow}a_1 \cap H^{\mathrm{conf}}_t

a1a_1 enters the fixed fiber on the conflict zone; a2a_2 does not. Outside HtconfH^{\mathrm{conf}}_t, σtN2\sigma^{N_2}_t continues to operate unchanged — the defeated norm is not erased from the wider fiber.

Proof. On HtconfH^{\mathrm{conf}}_t, RR instructs that σtN1\sigma^{N_1}_t applies in place of σtN2\sigma^{N_2}_t. Then Fix(σtN1∣Htconf)={b∈Htconf:a1≤b}=↑a1∩Htconf\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_1}_t|_{H^{\mathrm{conf}}_t}) = \{b \in H^{\mathrm{conf}}_t : a_1 \leq b\} = {\uparrow}a_1 \cap H^{\mathrm{conf}}_t. For bb outside HtconfH^{\mathrm{conf}}_t (i.e., b>a1∨a2b > a_1 \vee a_2), neither norm is in conflict and σtN2\sigma^{N_2}_t applies without restriction from RR. □\square

Contrary-to-duty — conditional nucleus. Primary norm N1=O(p)N_1 = O(p) has Fix(σtN1)=↑p\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_1}_t) = {\uparrow}p. Secondary norm N2=O(¬p⇒q)N_2 = O(\neg p \Rightarrow q) has a conditional nucleus on HtH_t:

σtN2(b)={b∨qif ¬p≤bbotherwise\sigma^{N_2}_t(b) = \begin{cases} b \vee q & \text{if } \neg p \leq b \\ b & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}

Before breach (¬p∉Ht\neg p \notin H_t below bb): σtN2\sigma^{N_2}_t is the identity on elements not above ¬p\neg p — it contributes nothing. After breach (¬p\neg p enters HtH_t at some successor history t′t', so ¬p≤b\neg p \leq b for relevant bb): σtN2\sigma^{N_2}_t activates and requires qq. The conflict between N1N_1 and N2N_2 is not present at tt before breach; it arises at t′t' only if p∧q=⊥tp \wedge q = \bot_t. This resolves the Chisholm paradox: the conflict is conditional on breach entering the history.

Open question (transferred to open questions section). Whether the degenerate case ⊥t∈Fix(σtN)\bot_t \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^N_t) for some norm NN (i.e., aN=⊥ta_N = \bot_t) implies that all elements of HtH_t are in Fix(σtN)=↑⊥t=Ht\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^N_t) = {\uparrow}\bot_t = H_t is immediate: ↑⊥t=Ht{\uparrow}\bot_t = H_t since ⊥t≤b\bot_t \leq b for all bb. This is the degenerate case where a norm requires nothing (its required output is ⊥t\bot_t), making every element of HtH_t a fixed point of σtN\sigma^N_t — a vacuously satisfied norm. A degenerate normative system in the stronger sense — one where the consistent normative reading of ⊥t\bot_t as “the bottom obligation” means something pathological — requires further analysis of what it means for ⊥t\bot_t to be normatively required rather than vacuously satisfied. This is an open question.

Open questions

  • Whether the fixed-fiber intersection Fix(σtN1)∩Fix(σtN2)={⊥}\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_1}_t) \cap \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma^{N_2}_t) = \{\bot\} fully captures all types of norm conflict, or whether there are conflicts where both fixed-fibers are non-trivially inhabited but their joint satisfaction is behaviorally impossible for a finite agent (the resources-and-time conflict, not captured by the Heyting algebra alone).
  • Whether there is a complete set of resolution rules — whether every norm conflict within a well-designed normative system can be resolved by some combination of lex superior, posterior, specialis, and explicit priority — or whether genuine dilemmas (Type III) are unavoidable in any sufficiently expressive normative system.
  • Whether the contrary-to-duty conflict (Type II) can be dissolved by a more careful formulation of the Zeno-settling structure: whether the conditional obligation O(¬p⇒q)O(\neg p \Rightarrow q) should be modeled as a nucleus that only activates at histories where ¬p∈Ht\neg p \in H_t, making the conflict truly conditional rather than a standing tension.
