2026-03-20 A Contract is a five-tuple (P₁, P₂, Φ₁₂, Φ₂₁, V) — two parties P₁ and P₂, a bilateral exchange of promise-sets (Φ₁₂: what P₁ commits to deliver to P₂; Φ₂₁: what P₂ commits to deliver to P₁), and formation conditions V (offer, acceptance, consideration, intention, capacity, legality) — that generates a Hohfeldian web of duties and claims persisting independently of performance. The invariant: authority transfers through delegation; obligation persists through contract. A contract is not a transfer but a mutual binding: both parties remain bound until discharge.

Contract

Formal definition

A Contract is a five-tuple C=(P1,P2,Φ12,Φ21,V)\mathcal{C} = (P_1, P_2, \Phi_{12}, \Phi_{21}, V):

C=(P1:Person,  P2:Person,  Φ12⊆Acts(P1),  Φ21⊆Acts(P2),  V:FormationConditions)\mathcal{C} = (P_1 : \mathrm{Person},\; P_2 : \mathrm{Person},\; \Phi_{12} \subseteq \mathrm{Acts}(P_1),\; \Phi_{21} \subseteq \mathrm{Acts}(P_2),\; V : \mathrm{FormationConditions})

where:

  • P1P_1 is the first party — a Person who makes and receives commitments under the contract; need not be natural; corporations, ships, states, and software components are persons for contract purposes
  • P2P_2 is the second party — a Person symmetric to P1P_1 in formal structure; bilaterality is built into the tuple; there is no “offeror side” in the settled contract, only two equally-bound parties
  • Φ12⊆Acts(P1)\Phi_{12} \subseteq \mathrm{Acts}(P_1) is P₁’s promise set — the acts, performances, deliveries, or forbearances that P1P_1 commits to provide to P2P_2; these are the terms owed by P1P_1; breach occurs when P1P_1 fails to perform some φ∈Φ12\varphi \in \Phi_{12} without excuse
  • Φ21⊆Acts(P2)\Phi_{21} \subseteq \mathrm{Acts}(P_2) is P₂’s promise set — symmetric to Φ12\Phi_{12}; the terms P2P_2 owes P1P_1; Φ21\Phi_{21} is the consideration P1P_1 receives for Φ12\Phi_{12}
  • VV is the formation conditions — the predicate that must be satisfied for C\mathcal{C} to be valid and legally or normatively binding; in common law: (V=offer∧acceptance∧consideration∧intention∧capacity∧legality)(V = \mathrm{offer} \wedge \mathrm{acceptance} \wedge \mathrm{consideration} \wedge \mathrm{intention} \wedge \mathrm{capacity} \wedge \mathrm{legality}); in civil law: (V=consent∧capacity∧cause∧lawful-object)(V = \mathrm{consent} \wedge \mathrm{capacity} \wedge \mathrm{cause} \wedge \mathrm{lawful\text{-}object})

Five invariants. C\mathcal{C} is a contract iff it satisfies:

  1. Bilaterality: the contract generates duties in both directions. P1P_1 has a duty to P2P_2: D(P1,Φ12,P2)\mathbf{D}(P_1, \Phi_{12}, P_2). P2P_2 has a duty to P1P_1: D(P2,Φ21,P1)\mathbf{D}(P_2, \Phi_{21}, P_1). By Hohfeldian correlativity, P2P_2 has a claim against P1P_1: C(P2,Φ12,P1)\mathbf{C}(P_2, \Phi_{12}, P_1), and P1P_1 has a claim against P2P_2: C(P1,Φ21,P2)\mathbf{C}(P_1, \Phi_{21}, P_2). A unilateral obligation (one party binds, the other does not) is a promise or a gift — not a contract.

  2. Consideration: Φ12≠∅\Phi_{12} \neq \varnothing and Φ21≠∅\Phi_{21} \neq \varnothing. Something flows in each direction. This is the common law consideration requirement: P2P_2 does not receive Φ12\Phi_{12} for nothing — P1P_1 receives Φ21\Phi_{21} in exchange. Civil law calls the same requirement cause: there must be a purpose or reason for each party to be bound. Past consideration does not count (Re McArdle [1951]): Φ21\Phi_{21} must be a response to Φ12\Phi_{12}, not a prior act unconnected to the contract.

  3. Normative persistence after formation: once VV is satisfied, the contract persists as a normative fact independently of performance. The parties cannot simply cease performing without consequence — the obligation remains until discharge, breach, frustration, or mutual release. Contrast with a request: requests do not create persisting normative facts; contracts do.

  4. Breach creates liability: if P1P_1 fails to perform φ∈Φ12\varphi \in \Phi_{12} at the required time and without legal excuse, the failure creates new Hohfeldian relations: P2P_2 acquires a claim to remedy against P1P_1. The remedies available (specific performance, damages, rescission) depend on the normative system NN governing the contract. Breach does not terminate the contract; it converts performance obligations into remedy obligations.

  5. Privity is the default: the obligations in C\mathcal{C} bind only P1P_1 and P2P_2. Third parties cannot normally acquire rights or be bound by a contract they are not party to (Tweddle v. Atkinson [1861]; Dunlop v. Selfridge [1915]). Exceptions (third-party beneficiary doctrines, assignment, novation, the Contracts (Rights of Third Parties) Act 1999) are departures from the privity default, not the norm.

The consideration/cause dyad

The deepest formal question about contract is why consideration is required — what work it does that cannot be done by a bare promise.

The answer: consideration is what distinguishes a contract (bilateral, mutually binding, enforceable) from a promise (unilateral, morally binding, often unenforceable). The bilateral exchange structure of Φ12\Phi_{12} and Φ21\Phi_{21} is what generates the Hohfeldian web: P1P_1’s duty creates P2P_2’s claim because P2P_2 has given P1P_1 something in return. If P2P_2 gives nothing, P1P_1’s “obligation” is merely a moral one — the law will not enforce it because the Hohfeldian correlative structure is incomplete.

In civil law systems, cause does the same work differently: the cause of a contract is its object or purpose — why the parties are entering it. A contract without cause (for no reason, for an impossible reason, for an illegal reason) is void. The civil law requires that the contract make sense as an exchange; the common law requires that something actually change hands.

The economic theory (Posner): consideration marks the contracts that both parties subjectively value at the time of formation — both gave something up, both expected something in return, so both revealed preferences support enforcement. Gifts transfer value unilaterally; courts have no basis to value them. Contracts transfer value bilaterally with each party’s contribution as evidence of the other’s value.

Domain instances

Common law contract: offer, acceptance, consideration

The common law contract is the most rigidly formal instance of the tuple. Formation requires satisfaction of each component of VV:

Offer: a proposal by P1P_1 to be bound by the terms of Φ12\Phi_{12} if P2P_2 accepts. An offer must be communicated to P2P_2; an offer to the world (Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co. [1893]) is a special case. The offeror controls the terms; the offeree’s power is to accept or reject, not modify.

Acceptance: an unqualified assent by P2P_2 to the terms of the offer. A modification is a counter-offer (rejecting the original). The mirror-image rule: acceptance must match the offer exactly. The postal rule: acceptance is effective on posting (Adams v. Lindsell [1818]) — the moment P2P_2 performs the acceptance act, the contract is formed, even before P1P_1 knows.

Consideration: the “price” paid by each party. Currie v. Misa (1875): consideration is “some right, interest, profit or benefit accruing to the one party, or some forbearance, detriment, loss or responsibility given, suffered or undertaken by the other.” The classic shorthand: a benefit to the promisor or a detriment to the promisee. Adequacy is irrelevant (Chappell v. Nestlé [1960]: three chocolate wrappers were good consideration for a record); sufficiency is required (consideration must have some value in law; love and affection do not suffice as consideration for a contract).

Intention to create legal relations: not every exchange creates a contract. Social arrangements between friends and family are presumed not intended to be legally binding (Balfour v. Balfour [1919]); commercial arrangements are presumed intended to be. The intention component of VV captures the parties’ normative attitude toward their exchange: a contract requires that both parties intend to be bound in law.

Capacity: each party must be legally capable of entering contracts. Minors, persons of unsound mind, and corporations acting beyond their ultra vires scope lack full contractual capacity.

Legality: contracts for illegal purposes are void or voidable. Ex turpi causa non oritur actio — no action arises from a base cause.

Design by contract (CS): Hoare triples as bilateral contracts

Bertrand Meyer’s Design by Contract (DbC, Eiffel language, 1988) is the application of contract theory to software component interfaces. The insight: a software component is not an isolated function but a party in a bilateral obligation with its caller.

A Hoare triple {P}  C  {Q}\{P\}\; C\; \{Q\} is the contract:

  • PP (precondition): what the caller must guarantee before invoking CC — the caller’s obligation Φ21\Phi_{21}
  • QQ (postcondition): what the implementor guarantees after CC terminates (given PP) — the implementor’s obligation Φ12\Phi_{12}
  • Invariant II: conditions that both parties must maintain throughout — the shared persistent norm of the “class institution”

The bilateral structure is explicit: “if you give me PP, I’ll give you QQ.” The caller is P2P_2 (committing to PP); the callee is P1P_1 (committing to QQ given PP). Neither party has an unlimited obligation: the callee need not maintain QQ if the caller failed to establish PP; the caller need not establish PP for every possible invocation, only when it wants QQ.

Subcontracting: Liskov Substitution Principle in DbC form — a subtype may weaken preconditions and strengthen postconditions, but not vice versa. A stronger precondition (P′⇒PP' \Rightarrow P) means the subtype accepts fewer callers; a weaker postcondition (Q⇏Q′Q \not\Rightarrow Q') means the subtype delivers less. Either is a contract violation: the subtype should be at least as easy to call and at least as useful as its supertype. This is the monotonicity requirement on contract inheritance.

The predicate transformer reading: the weakest precondition wp(C,Q)\mathrm{wp}(C, Q) is the weakest PP such that {P}  C  {Q}\{P\}\; C\; \{Q\} holds. This defines a Galois connection: P⊢{P}  C  {Q}P \vdash \{P\}\; C\; \{Q\} iff P⇒wp(C,Q)P \Rightarrow \mathrm{wp}(C, Q). The Hoare logic is a system of contract derivation — given a program CC, find the bilateral exchange that CC implements.

Adjunctions: the categorical contract

In category theory, an adjunction F⊣GF \dashv G between categories C\mathcal{C} and D\mathcal{D} (with F:C→DF : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D} and G:D→CG : \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{C}) is a contract between two functors:

HomD(FA,B)≅HomC(A,GB)natural in A,B\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(FA, B) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A, GB) \quad \text{natural in } A, B

The bilateral exchange structure: FF “commits” to universally factor every D\mathcal{D}-morphism FA→BFA \to B through the unit; GG “commits” to universally factor every C\mathcal{C}-morphism A→GBA \to GB through the counit. The bijection is the consideration: neither functor’s commitment makes sense independently; they are mutually determining.

The formation witnesses:

  • Unit η:idC→GF\eta : \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{C}} \to GF: P1P_1’s performance — “P1P_1 delivers ηA:A→GFA\eta_A : A \to GFA” — the canonical element each AA generates in the GG-side
  • Counit ε:FG→idD\varepsilon : FG \to \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{D}}: P2P_2’s performance — “P2P_2 delivers εB:FGB→B\varepsilon_B : FGB \to B” — the canonical element each BB absorbs from the FF-side
  • Triangle identities: the discharge conditions — εFA∘F(ηA)=idFA\varepsilon_{FA} \circ F(\eta_A) = \mathrm{id}_{FA} and G(εB)∘ηGB=idGBG(\varepsilon_B) \circ \eta_{GB} = \mathrm{id}_{GB} — “if both parties perform their canonical obligations, you recover the identity”

Galois connections are adjunctions between posets. The Galois correspondence (Galois, 1832): the subgroup lattice of Gal(L/K)\mathrm{Gal}(L/K) and the intermediate field lattice between KK and LL are related by a Galois connection (a pair of order-reversing maps that are adjoint). Each intermediate field is uniquely determined by the subgroup of symmetries that fix it; each subgroup is uniquely determined by the field it fixes. The symmetry group and the field extension are in contractual correspondence: “I’ll tell you which field I fix if you tell me which symmetry group I am.”

In the relational universe: the two nuclei σt\sigma_t and Δt\Delta_t are related to each other by mutual constraint. The nuclear quartet positions are the orbits of this constraint. Whether (σt,Δt)(\sigma_t, \Delta_t) form a Galois connection — with the fixed fiber Ht∗H^*_t as the invariant “field” — is an open structural question (see Open questions).

Linear logic: resource-aware contracts

Classical logic treats truth as free: A∧A⇔AA \wedge A \Leftrightarrow A (you can use AA as many times as you like). Linear logic (Girard, 1987) treats formulas as resources: each hypothesis must be used exactly once. This gives linear logic the character of resource-accounting, and linear propositions behave as contracts:

Linear connectiveContract reading
A⊗BA \otimes B“I will deliver both AA and BB” — joint obligation
A \parr B“I will deliver AA or BB, your choice determines which” — co-choice
A⊕BA \oplus B“I will deliver one of AA or BB, my choice” — choice
A&BA \mathbin{\&} B“I will deliver either AA or BB, your choice but not both” — exclusive choice obligation
A⊸BA \multimap B“Given exactly one AA, I will deliver one BB” — resource contract
!A!A“You can use AA as many times as you want” — waiver of consumption constraint
?A?A“I will produce AA as many times as you need” — supply commitment

The ⊸\multimap (lollipop) connective is the linear contract: A⊸BA \multimap B is the agreement “one AA for one BB, no stockpiling, no discarding.” This is the contract at its most minimal — the resource exchange with strict accounting.

The !! modality (exponential) is the irrevocability modifier: !A!A means AA is inexhaustible — the party committing to !A!A has waived the right to limit consumption. This corresponds to the legal concept of an irrevocable offer or a continuous obligation (a standing commitment to perform on demand).

Linear logic also models the Curry–Howard correspondence for contracts: a proof of A⊸BA \multimap B is a program that consumes exactly one AA-resource to produce one BB-resource. The proof is the discharge evidence — the contract is discharged when the program runs and the transformation completes.

Social contract: the meta-contract

Social contract theory addresses not ordinary contracts between existing persons within an existing normative system, but the foundational contract that constitutes the normative system itself — the contract that makes all other contracts possible.

Hobbes (Leviathan, 1651): in the state of nature (no sovereign, no law, no enforceable contracts), life is “solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short.” Rational agents, to escape, covenant to surrender natural freedom to a sovereign in exchange for protection. The Hobbesian social contract is:

  • P1P_1 = all persons in the state of nature
  • P2P_2 = the sovereign (or the Leviathan as institutional person)
  • Φ12\Phi_{12} = subjects surrender natural right to govern themselves; obey the sovereign’s law
  • Φ21\Phi_{21} = sovereign provides protection, maintains peace, enforces contracts

The Hobbesian insight: contracts presuppose a sovereign to enforce them. The social contract is the meta-contract — it constitutes the institutional authority under which ordinary contracts can function. Without the meta-contract, VV fails for every ordinary contract: there is no authority to enforce.

Locke (Second Treatise, 1689): the social contract is more limited than Hobbes allows. Natural rights (life, liberty, property) preexist the state; the contract is to protect them, not to surrender them. If the sovereign violates the contract (fails to protect natural rights), the people’s obligation dissolves; they may revolve. The Lockean social contract: Φ21\Phi_{21} includes protecting life, liberty, and property as conditions of P2P_2’s performance; if P2P_2 fails, Φ12\Phi_{12} is discharged by breach.

Rousseau (Social Contract, 1762): the social contract transforms natural freedom into civil freedom through the general will (volonté générale). Each person gives themselves to all, and by giving to all, gives to no one. The general will is not the sum of individual wills but the common good — what all would will as citizens. The contract is with the body politic constituted by the contract itself: the parties are their own counterparty, in the sense that the community they constitute through the contract is what they are contracting with. P2P_2 is not a pre-existing entity but is constituted by the contract’s formation.

Rawls (A Theory of Justice, 1971): the original position and veil of ignorance generate the social contract procedurally. Rational agents who do not know their place in society (their class, abilities, fortune, conception of the good) choose principles of justice. The veil ensures impartiality. Rawls argues they would choose: (1) equal basic liberties; (2) the difference principle (social and economic inequalities must benefit the least advantaged). The Rawlsian social contract is what rational agents behind the veil would agree to — it is a contract under conditions of epistemic equality.

The formal structure of the social contract is unusual: P1P_1 and P2P_2 are not distinct pre-existing persons; the contract constitutes one of its parties (the body politic, the sovereign, the civil society). This is the limit case of contract formation where VV’s satisfaction and the contract’s terms together create the normative system within which the contract then operates. Institution and contract coincide at the boundary.

Game theory: mechanism design and Nash bargaining

In cooperative game theory, a contract is a binding agreement between players — one where the players can commit credibly to their promised strategies. The key question is not whether to contract but what to contract for — the terms of the agreement.

Nash bargaining (Nash, 1950): two players have a disagreement point (d1,d2)(d_1, d_2) (what each gets if they fail to agree) and a feasible utility set UU (the set of payoff pairs achievable by some agreement). The Nash bargaining solution maximizes the Nash product (u1−d1)(u2−d2)(u_1 - d_1)(u_2 - d_2) over UU. The Nash solution is the unique agreement satisfying: Pareto optimality, symmetry, invariance to affine transformations of utility, and independence of irrelevant alternatives.

The Nash solution is the contract that satisfies the most natural bilateral fairness conditions — it is the “exchange” both parties would choose if bargaining maximally.

Mechanism design (Hurwicz, Maskin, Myerson — Nobel 2007): the problem of designing the terms of a contract (the mechanism) so that self-interested parties will truthfully reveal their private information and behave as intended. The revelation principle: any outcome achievable by any mechanism can be achieved by a direct mechanism where truth-telling is a dominant strategy. An incentive-compatible contract is one where each party’s dominant strategy is to perform Φ\Phi honestly — the contract aligns self-interest with compliance.

The structure of discharge

A contract is discharged (terminated, with all obligations extinguished) by:

  1. Full performance: both P1P_1 and P2P_2 complete all acts in Φ12\Phi_{12} and Φ21\Phi_{21} respectively. The contract is discharged; no further obligations remain. This is the nuclear fixed point: both parties’ commitments are in Ht∗H^*_t.

  2. Mutual release (accord and satisfaction): P1P_1 and P2P_2 agree to release each other from their obligations, usually in exchange for some reduced or modified performance. A new contract extinguishes the old.

  3. Frustration (common law) / impossibility: the subject matter is destroyed, the purpose is defeated, or performance becomes illegal after formation, through no fault of either party. The contract is discharged; neither party is in breach.

  4. Breach (by one party, accepted by the other): if P1P_1 commits a repudiatory breach (a fundamental failure going to the root of the contract), P2P_2 may elect to accept the breach and treat the contract as discharged. At that point, both parties’ primary obligations end; P1P_1’s breach obligation (damages) begins. If P2P_2 does not accept the breach, the contract continues in force.

  5. Operation of law: bankruptcy, merger of parties, death (in personal service contracts).

The common thread in 1 and 4: the contract terminates either because both parties have reached Ht∗H^*_t (full performance) or because the breach has been accepted and converted into a new remedial obligation.

Contract vs. promise: a promise binds the promisor morally; a contract binds both parties legally. The formal distinction is consideration: the promisee in a bare promise gives nothing, so the promisor receives no Hohfeldian consideration and the correlative web is incomplete.

Contract vs. delegation: a delegation moves authority from principal to agent — unidirectional. A contract creates bilateral obligations — both parties are bound. The captain who delegates watch-standing authority has not contracted with the duty officer; the officer is bound by position, not exchange.

Contract vs. institution: an institution is the rule-system (the constitutive rules and deontic profiles); a contract is an act within an institution that generates particular obligations between particular parties. Contract law (institution) governs contracts (acts). Every contract presupposes an institution to give its terms meaning and enforce them; not every institution was created by contract.

Nuclear reading

Definitions. A contract C=(P1,P2,Φ12,Φ21,V)\mathcal{C} = (P_1, P_2, \Phi_{12}, \Phi_{21}, V) at history tt has:

  • P1,P2∈Ht∗P_1, P_2 \in H^*_t: both parties are elements of the fixed fiber — their identity propositions are both saturation- and transfer-nucleus-fixed.
  • Contract in force at tt: Φ12,Φ21∈Fix(σt)∖Fix(Δt)\Phi_{12}, \Phi_{21} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) \setminus \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t) — both commitment propositions are meaning-settled (past-saturated) but not yet execution-settled (not present in every forward extension from tt). The formation event placed both into Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t); performance has not yet placed them into Fix(Δt)\mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_t).
  • Contract discharged at t′t': both Φ12,Φ21∈Ht′∗\Phi_{12}, \Phi_{21} \in H^*_{t'} — meaning-settled AND execution-settled.
  • Breach at t′t': the PERFORMANCE PROPOSITION π1∈Ht′\pi_1 \in H_{t'} asserting completion of Φ12\Phi_{12} satisfies π1∉Fix(Δt′)\pi_1 \notin \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t'}) — π1\pi_1 is not present in every one-step extension of t′t'; there exist forward histories in which P1P_1’s performance is still outstanding. Note: breach is a condition on the PERFORMANCE PROPOSITION π1∈Ht′\pi_1 \in H_{t'}, not on Φ12∈Ht\Phi_{12} \in H_t — these are elements of different fiber algebras, and it is an error to assert that σt′(Φ12)≠Φ12\sigma_{t'}(\Phi_{12}) \neq \Phi_{12} upon breach. The obligation Φ12∈Ht\Phi_{12} \in H_t remains σt\sigma_t-fixed; it is π1\pi_1’s failure to reach Fix(Δt′)\mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t'}) that constitutes breach.

Proposition 1 (Discharge is closed under meet): If the contract is discharged at t′t' — that is, Φ12,Φ21∈Ht′∗\Phi_{12}, \Phi_{21} \in H^*_{t'} — then Φ12∧Φ21∈Ht′∗\Phi_{12} \wedge \Phi_{21} \in H^*_{t'}.

Proof. Φ12,Φ21∈Fix(σt′)\Phi_{12}, \Phi_{21} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t'}). By meet-preservation: σt′(Φ12∧Φ21)=σt′(Φ12)∧σt′(Φ21)=Φ12∧Φ21\sigma_{t'}(\Phi_{12} \wedge \Phi_{21}) = \sigma_{t'}(\Phi_{12}) \wedge \sigma_{t'}(\Phi_{21}) = \Phi_{12} \wedge \Phi_{21}. So Φ12∧Φ21∈Fix(σt′)\Phi_{12} \wedge \Phi_{21} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_{t'}). The same argument with Δt′\Delta_{t'} gives Φ12∧Φ21∈Fix(Δt′)\Phi_{12} \wedge \Phi_{21} \in \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t'}). Hence Φ12∧Φ21∈Ht′∗\Phi_{12} \wedge \Phi_{21} \in H^*_{t'}. □\square

The bilateral discharge proposition Φ12∧Φ21\Phi_{12} \wedge \Phi_{21} being in Ht′∗H^*_{t'} is therefore NECESSARY for discharge. Whether it is SUFFICIENT — whether Φ12∧Φ21∈Ht′∗\Phi_{12} \wedge \Phi_{21} \in H^*_{t'} implies both Φ12∈Ht′∗\Phi_{12} \in H^*_{t'} and Φ21∈Ht′∗\Phi_{21} \in H^*_{t'} — is NOT in general derivable from meet-preservation alone (the meet can enter Ht′∗H^*_{t'} even if one factor does not, in algebras where the meet with the other factor drops far enough). This requires an additional condition on Ht′H_{t'} (see Open questions).

Proposition 2 (Partial performance leaves a strict transfer gap): Suppose Φ12∈Ht′∗\Phi_{12} \in H^*_{t'} (P1P_1 fully performed) but Φ21∉Fix(Δt′)\Phi_{21} \notin \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t'}) (P2P_2 has not executed). Then Δt′(Φ21)>Φ21\Delta_{t'}(\Phi_{21}) > \Phi_{21} strictly.

Proof. Since Φ21∉Fix(Δt′)\Phi_{21} \notin \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t'}), by definition Δt′(Φ21)=min⁡{c∈Fix(Δt′)∣c≥Φ21}\Delta_{t'}(\Phi_{21}) = \min\{c \in \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t'}) \mid c \geq \Phi_{21}\}, which is strictly larger than Φ21\Phi_{21} (otherwise Φ21\Phi_{21} itself would be the minimum, contradicting Φ21∉Fix(Δt′)\Phi_{21} \notin \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t'})). □\square

The gap [Φ21, Δt′(Φ21)][\Phi_{21},\, \Delta_{t'}(\Phi_{21})] in Ht′H_{t'} is the performance gap: the space of propositional possibility between what P2P_2 has done and what every forward extension requires. Non-zero gap = obligation unmet.

Proposition 3 (Bilateral symmetry of the obligation web): The contract at tt generates four Hohfeldian positions simultaneously, all derivable from the pair (Φ12,Φ21)∈Fix(σt)2(\Phi_{12}, \Phi_{21}) \in \mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t)^2:

  • D(P1,Φ12,P2)D(P_1, \Phi_{12}, P_2): P1P_1’s duty — π1∈Fix(Δt′)\pi_1 \in \mathrm{Fix}(\Delta_{t'}) is required at performance date t′t'
  • C(P2,Φ12,P1)C(P_2, \Phi_{12}, P_1): correlative claim — P2P_2’s standing to enforce Φ12\Phi_{12}
  • D(P2,Φ21,P1)D(P_2, \Phi_{21}, P_1) and C(P1,Φ21,P2)C(P_1, \Phi_{21}, P_2): symmetrically

The bilateral structure is encoded in the symmetric pair: both Φ12\Phi_{12} and Φ21\Phi_{21} must reach Ht′∗H^*_{t'} for the web to dissolve (full discharge). By Proposition 2, partial performance leaves a measurable gap. The contract persists as a normative fact — Φ12,Φ21\Phi_{12}, \Phi_{21} remain in Fix(σt)\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma_t) regardless of performance (by the norm-persistence argument of norm.md) — until both performance propositions enter their respective fixed fibers.

On the nucleus adjunction question. The open question of whether σt⊣Δt\sigma_t \dashv \Delta_t as closure operators on HtH_t (i.e., Δt(a)≤b⇔a≤σt(b)\Delta_t(a) \leq b \Leftrightarrow a \leq \sigma_t(b)) is directly relevant to contracts: if such an adjunction held, the contract’s bilateral structure would be encoded as a Galois connection between the saturation-fixed obligations and the transfer-fixed performances. By commutation, σt∘Δt=Δt∘σt\sigma_t \circ \Delta_t = \Delta_t \circ \sigma_t, which is consistent with an adjunction but does not imply one. An adjunction would further require Δt∘σt≤id\Delta_t \circ \sigma_t \leq \mathrm{id} or id≤σt∘Δt\mathrm{id} \leq \sigma_t \circ \Delta_t, the latter of which holds by extensiveness of both nuclei. Whether the former holds — whether σt(Δt(a))≤a\sigma_t(\Delta_t(a)) \leq a for all aa — is false in general: extensiveness gives σt(Δt(a))≥Δt(a)≥a\sigma_t(\Delta_t(a)) \geq \Delta_t(a) \geq a, the wrong direction. So σt⊣Δt\sigma_t \dashv \Delta_t as a Galois connection does not hold.

Open questions

  • Whether the adjunction reading (F⊣G)(F \dashv G) is formally an instance of the contract tuple — whether every adjunction generates a five-tuple C=(C,D,F,G,Vadjunction)\mathcal{C} = (\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{D}, F, G, V_{\text{adjunction}}) where VadjunctionV_{\text{adjunction}} is the satisfaction of the naturality condition in the bijection.
  • Whether the two nuclei (σt,Δt)(\sigma_t, \Delta_t) form a Galois connection in the poset HtH_t — whether σt⊣Δt\sigma_t \dashv \Delta_t as closure operators, and whether this adjunction-as-contract encodes something about the internal normative structure of the fiber.
  • The formal treatment of relational contracts (contracts whose terms are deliberately incomplete and filled in by ongoing relationship management) versus complete contingent contracts (contracts that specify outcomes for all states of the world). The incomplete contract requires a governance structure; the complete contract requires only enforcement. Whether the nuclear quartet (Process/Procedure/Derivation/Inquiry) models the governance modes of incomplete contracts.
  • Whether the Curry–Howard–Lambek correspondence gives a contract interpretation of the relational nuclear language — whether terms in the fiber language correspond to contract proofs, types correspond to contract terms, and reduction corresponds to discharge.
  • The formal status of the social contract in the relational universe: whether the FARS spec itself is a social contract among agents — a set of constitutive rules (CR\text{CR}) that constitute the institution within which agents then enter ordinary contracts.

A Contract is a five-tuple (P₁, P₂, Φ₁₂, Φ₂₁, V) — two parties P₁ and P₂, a bilateral exchange of promise-sets (Φ₁₂: what P₁ commits to deliver to P₂; Φ₂₁: what P₂ commits to deliver to P₁), and formation conditions V (offer, acceptance, consideration, intention, capacity, legality) — that generates a Hohfeldian web of duties and claims persisting independently of performance. The invariant: authority transfers through delegation; obligation persists through contract. A contract is not a transfer but a mutual binding: both parties remain bound until discharge.
