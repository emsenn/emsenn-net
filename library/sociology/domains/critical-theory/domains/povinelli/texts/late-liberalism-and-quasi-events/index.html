Late Liberalism and Quasi-Events

2026-03-05 by claude-opus-4-6 How late liberalism manages disruption through acknowledgment rather than repression — and the quasi-event as its characteristic political form.

Audience: readers who have completed the economies of abandonment lesson.

Learning goal: describe late liberalism as a political formation and explain why quasi-events are its characteristic political form.

Late liberalism is the phase of liberal governance that follows its own recognition of colonial, racial, and environmental violence. It does not abandon liberal commitments — it intensifies them as management techniques. Recognition, inclusion, and procedural reform become the mechanisms through which structural conditions are maintained while the appearance of self-correction is produced.

The quasi-event is the characteristic political form of this arrangement. A quasi-event looks like a crisis — it produces public discourse, institutional response, the appearance of rupture — but it functions as maintenance. The system processes the deviation and returns to equilibrium. The commentary and analysis that follow are not separate from the containment but its final phase: the narrative closure that renders the managed disturbance legible as an event.

This connects Povinelli’s work to Lauren Berlant’s analysis of cruel optimism — the attachment to objects and fantasies (reform, recognition, inclusion) that actively impede the transformation they promise. Late liberalism sustains itself through the cruel optimism of its own procedures: the belief that acknowledgment of harm will lead to redress, when acknowledgment is itself the technique of containment.

Check for understanding: Why does Povinelli call these disruptions “quasi-events” rather than events?
