Geontopower

2026-03-05 The form of power that operates through the governance of the Life/Nonlife boundary — determining whether a forest is an ecosystem with interests or a timber resource available for extraction.
Defines geontopower
<p>Geontopower is the form of power that operates through the governance of the distinction between Life and Nonlife. Where biopower names governance through the management of living populations, geontopower names governance through the maintenance of the boundary that determines what counts as living in the first place. Biopower presupposes the Life/Nonlife distinction — it governs populations already classified as living. Geontopower governs the classification itself.</p> <p>Povinelli introduces the term in <em>Geontologies: A Requiem to Late Liberalism</em> (2016) to identify a mode of power that Foucault&rsquo;s framework cannot fully capture. Geontopower determines whether a forest is an ecosystem (Life, with interests to be managed) or a timber resource (Nonlife, available for extraction); whether a river is a living entity (as in Maori and other Indigenous legal traditions) or an inert waterway (as in settler-colonial resource law); whether a geological formation has standing or is merely substrate.</p> <p>Povinelli identifies three figures through which geontopower operates. The Desert is the figure of Nonlife threatening to overwhelm Life — ecological collapse, resource exhaustion, the dead zone expanding. The Animist is the figure that refuses the Life/Nonlife distinction altogether — insisting that rocks, rivers, and ancestors are alive and agentive, challenging the ontological infrastructure that makes extraction possible. The Virus is the figure that crosses the boundary from within — neither fully alive nor fully inert, disrupting the classification by occupying the zone between Life and Nonlife.</p> <p>Geontopower is not exercised through dramatic acts of sovereignty or visible techniques of discipline. It operates through the quiet maintenance of ontological categories — through the classifications embedded in property law, environmental regulation, resource management, and scientific taxonomy that sort the world into what can suffer and what cannot, what has standing and what does not. When a court denies legal personhood to a river, it exercises geontopower. When a mining permit classifies land as inert substrate, it exercises geontopower. When a scientific taxonomy defines certain entities as &ldquo;non-living,&rdquo; it produces the ontological conditions under which extraction is not violence but use.</p>
