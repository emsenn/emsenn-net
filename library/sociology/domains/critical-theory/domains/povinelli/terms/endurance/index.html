Endurance

2026-03-05 Povinelli's term for the political practice of persisting under conditions of abandonment — the ongoing work of continuing to exist in a world organized to make your persistence irrelevant.
<p>Endurance, as theorized by Elizabeth Povinelli in <em>Economies of Abandonment</em> (2011), is the political practice of persisting under conditions of abandonment — not resistance in the heroic sense of confrontation but the ongoing work of continuing to exist in a world that has been organized to make your persistence irrelevant.</p> <p>In economies of abandonment, survival is not passive. It requires the active maintenance of social worlds, kinship networks, and material practices in the face of withdrawal — the continued habitation of places that have been declared unviable, the continued exercise of practices that have been classified as residual, the continued assertion of relations that the geontological order classifies as Nonlife. When a remote Indigenous community continues to maintain ceremonial obligations on land that the state has designated for mining, that is endurance. When a neighborhood keeps functioning after infrastructure has been withdrawn — after the hospital closes, the school consolidates, the water system degrades — that is endurance. The work is not dramatic. It is the daily, unglamorous maintenance of a world that power has decided should not exist.</p> <p>Endurance is not resilience. Resilience, in contemporary governance discourse, has become a term of management — the capacity of a system to absorb shocks and return to equilibrium. Resilience is what late liberal governance wants from the populations it abandons: absorb the withdrawal, adapt to diminished conditions, and do not make demands. Endurance is not the system&rsquo;s capacity to bounce back. It is the refusal to accept the timeline the system has set for your disappearance.</p> <p>Endurance is not hope. It does not promise that persistence will produce recognition, redress, or transformation. The community that endures may never be recognized. The practices it maintains may never be valued by the institutional framework that abandoned them. Endurance names the political significance of not disappearing — of refusing to confirm that the withdrawal was justified by demonstrating that without support, the community could not survive. The community survives. That survival is not a vindication of the system that abandoned it. It is an indictment.</p>
