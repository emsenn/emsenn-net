Kyriarchism

Kyriarchism

Kyriarchy (from Greek kyrios, lord/master + archein, to rule) is Elisabeth Schüssler Fiorenza’s term for the interlocking systems of domination that organize social life. Where “patriarchy” names the rule of men over women, kyriarchy names the rule of lords — the structure in which multiple axes of domination (gender, race, class, sexuality, ability, citizenship, age) interlock and reinforce each other. Patriarchy, white supremacy, capitalism, heteronormativity, and ableism are not separate systems that happen to coexist. They are a single structure of rule that operates differently on different bodies depending on where those bodies sit in the intersection.

The concept matters because single-axis analysis (class only, race only, gender only) consistently fails to describe how domination actually works. The experience of a Black disabled woman under capitalism cannot be decomposed into the sum of “race oppression” + “disability oppression” + “gender oppression” — the intersection produces specific conditions that no single axis can capture. Kyriarchy names the structure that produces those intersections.
