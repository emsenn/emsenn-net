Economies of Abandonment

2026-03-05 Povinelli's name for the mode of governance in which late liberalism manages populations through withdrawal of resources, infrastructure, and recognition — producing the evidence of unviability that retroactively justifies the withdrawal.
Defines economies of abandonment

Economies of abandonment, as theorized by Elizabeth Povinelli in Economies of Abandonment: Social Belonging and Endurance in Late Liberalism (2011), names the mode of governance in which late liberalism manages populations and places not primarily through direct oppression but through withdrawal — the removal of resources, infrastructure, and recognition from those it deems unviable.

Abandonment is not neglect. It is a governed process. The state does not simply forget communities — it assesses them, classifies them, and determines their viability through administrative, economic, and demographic metrics. When a community is found unviable — too small, too remote, too costly to service — the withdrawal of resources follows as a rational, procedural outcome. The community is not attacked; it is abandoned. The distinction matters because abandonment operates through the same procedural frameworks that late liberalism treats as neutral: cost-benefit analysis, demographic modeling, infrastructure planning.

This means that abandonment produces its own evidence. The community that has been deprived of infrastructure appears to lack the capacity for self-governance. The population that has been denied resources appears to have failed to thrive. The withdrawal produces the conditions that retroactively justify the withdrawal — a feedback loop in which the managed exhaustion of a community becomes the proof that the community was unviable in the first place.

Endurance — the ongoing work of persisting under these conditions — becomes the central political act in economies of abandonment. Not resistance in the heroic sense, but the refusal to disappear on the schedule that abandonment has set.
