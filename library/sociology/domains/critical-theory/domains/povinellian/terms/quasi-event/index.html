Quasi-Event

2026-03-05 Povinelli's term for a disturbance that appears to open possibility but is already embedded in the logic of its own containment — a managed deviation that restores prior order under the appearance of having confronted something.
Defines quasi-event

A quasi-event, as theorized by Elizabeth Povinelli in Economies of Abandonment (2011), is a disturbance that appears to open possibility — to disrupt the ordinary, to create a moment of contingency — but is already embedded in the logic of its own containment. The quasi-event is not a failed event but a managed one: a deviation that the system has already accounted for, that activates existing protocols of response, and that concludes with the restoration of the prior order under the appearance of having confronted something.

The quasi-event is the characteristic political form of late liberalism. Where earlier modes of governance confronted disruption through repression (crush the threat) or incorporation (absorb the demand), late liberalism processes disruption through acknowledgment. A crisis erupts — an environmental disaster, a police killing, a financial collapse. The system responds: investigations are launched, reports are commissioned, reforms are proposed. The response produces the appearance of rupture — political volatility, institutional introspection, public discourse about change — while the underlying structures remain stable. The system demonstrates its capacity for self-correction without altering the geontological and economic conditions that produced the crisis.

What makes the quasi-event insidious is not that it does nothing. It does something: it absorbs the energy that might otherwise produce actual contingency. The investigation channels outrage into procedure. The report translates lived harm into administrative categories. The reform adjusts the mechanism without changing the structure. The narrative closure that follows — the commentary, the analysis, the lessons-learned — is not separate from the containment but its final phase. The managed disruption is rendered legible as an event, and the legibility itself becomes the evidence that the system works.

The quasi-event is dangerous not because it is invisible but because it is entirely visible. It looks exactly like a crisis being confronted. The difference between a quasi-event and an actual event — a disturbance that genuinely opens new possibilities — is not available at the surface. It is visible only in the aftermath, when the structural conditions that produced the crisis remain unchanged and the next quasi-event is already being prepared.
