Governmentality

2026-01-01 Foucault's concept for the rationality through which modern states govern populations — not by commanding obedience but by shaping the conditions within which people conduct themselves.
Defines governmentality, art of government, conduct of conduct
Requires power-knowledge
<p>Governmentality is the rationality through which modern states govern populations — not primarily by commanding obedience but by shaping the conditions within which people conduct themselves. Foucault fused &ldquo;governing&rdquo; and &ldquo;mentality&rdquo; to capture a specific point: governance is not just what governments do but a way of thinking about how conduct should be conducted. The &ldquo;conduct of conduct&rdquo; — directing the ways people direct themselves — is the formula that defines governmental reason.</p> <p>Foucault introduced the term in his 1977–78 Collège de France lectures (<em>Security, Territory, Population</em>). He traced its emergence to the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, when the problem of government separated from two older models. Sovereignty asked how a king could hold territory and demand obedience — the relationship between sovereign and subject, mediated by law. Pastoral care asked how a shepherd could guide each soul toward salvation — the relationship between pastor and flock, mediated by conscience. The new problem was different from both: how to manage a population — its health, its wealth, its productivity, its security — which required new forms of knowledge: political economy, statistics (<em>Statistik</em>, the &ldquo;science of the state&rdquo;), and demography. The population became an object with its own regularities, measurable and optimizable.</p> <p>Governmentality extends biopower by specifying its institutional logic. Where biopower names the general fact that modern power takes biological life as its object, governmentality names the specific rationality through which that management is organized. Different governmentalities produce different subjects. A liberal governmentality governs through the market and the framework of rights — it produces the autonomous, self-interested subject who must be left free to pursue their own ends. A neoliberal governmentality governs through competition and entrepreneurial norms — it produces the self-investing, self-optimizing subject who treats their own life as human capital to be managed. A welfarist governmentality governs through social provision and risk management — it produces the citizen-client of the welfare state. Each rationality depends on specific power/knowledge formations that make its subjects visible, calculable, and administrable.</p> <p>The 1978–79 lectures (<em>The Birth of Biopolitics</em>) extended the analysis to neoliberal governmentality specifically, examining how the Chicago School and German ordoliberalism reimagined the relationship between state and market — not as state withdrawal but as the active construction of market conditions through government intervention.</p>
