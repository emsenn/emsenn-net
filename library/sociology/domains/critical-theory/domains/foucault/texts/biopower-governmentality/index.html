Biopower and Governmentality (Lesson)

Audience: readers who have completed the power/knowledge lesson and want to understand how Foucault’s analysis applies to the governance of populations.

Learning goal: explain the shift from sovereign power to biopower, describe how governmentality organizes the management of populations, and analyze a contemporary institution in biopolitical terms.

From the scaffold to the census

In 1757, the French state executed Robert-François Damiens by public torture — his body was the site where sovereign power made itself visible. By the mid-19th Century, the same state was conducting national censuses, tracking birth and death rates, regulating sanitation, and managing public health campaigns. The state’s relationship to its subjects had changed: it was no longer primarily threatening them with death. It was managing their lives.

Michel Foucault called this shift the emergence of biopower — a form of political power that takes the biological life of populations as its object [@foucault1978]. The old sovereign formula was “the right to take life or let live.” The biopolitical formula inverts it: “the right to make live and let die.”

Two levels of biopower

Biopower operates at two levels, each with its own techniques:

The individual body: discipline. Disciplinary techniques — examined in Discipline and Punish [@foucault1977] — target individual bodies. Drill, timetable, spatial organization, examination, and surveillance produce what Foucault called “docile bodies”: subjects trained to behave in predictable, productive ways. The soldier, the student, the factory worker, the prisoner — each is shaped by disciplinary techniques specific to the institution that manages them.

The population: regulation. Regulatory techniques — introduced in the History of Sexuality and developed in the Collège de France lectures [@foucault2003] — target populations as biological entities. Demographics, epidemiology, public health, insurance, and urban planning treat the population as an object with its own regularities: birth rates, mortality curves, endemic disease patterns, rates of reproduction. These regularities aren’t managed by disciplining individual bodies but by adjusting the conditions under which the population lives — sanitation, housing, nutrition, vaccination.

The two levels work together. Discipline produces individual compliance; regulation manages population-level outcomes. The school disciplines individual students and produces aggregate data (test scores, graduation rates, attendance statistics) that feed into population-level management.

Governmentality: the rationality behind management

Biopower names the general fact that modern power takes life as its object. Governmentality names the specific rationality through which that management is organized — the “art of government” that determines what should be managed, how, and toward what ends [@foucault2007].

Foucault traced the emergence of governmentality to the 16th and 17th centuries, when the problem of government separated from two older models:

  • Sovereignty: the king holds territory and demands obedience. The relationship is between sovereign and subject. The relevant knowledge is law.
  • Pastoral care: the shepherd guides each soul toward salvation. The relationship is between pastor and flock. The relevant knowledge is conscience.

The new problem was different from both: how to manage a population — its health, its wealth, its productivity, its security. This required a new kind of knowledge: political economy, statistics (Statistik, the “science of the state”), and demography. The population became an object with its own regularities, measurable and optimizable.

Different governmentalities produce different subjects. A liberal governmentality governs through the market and the framework of rights — it produces the autonomous, self-interested subject. A neoliberal governmentality governs through competition and entrepreneurial norms — it produces the self-investing, self-optimizing subject. A welfarist governmentality governs through social provision and risk management — it produces the citizen-client of the welfare state. Each relies on specific forms of power/knowledge to make its subjects visible and administrable.

A contemporary example: public health

Public health is biopower operating in plain sight. Consider the management of an epidemic:

  1. Surveillance: the state collects data on infection rates, hospitalizations, and deaths — tracking the population as a biological entity with measurable regularities.
  2. Regulation: the state issues mandates (masking, quarantine, vaccination schedules) that adjust the conditions under which the population lives — not by disciplining each individual but by shaping the environment.
  3. Knowledge production: epidemiologists, statisticians, and public health officials produce the knowledge through which the epidemic becomes visible and manageable. “Flattening the curve,” “herd immunity,” “R-naught” — these categories don’t describe the virus alone. They describe the population-as-managed-by-the-state.
  4. Normalization: public health establishes what “normal” health looks like and identifies populations that deviate from that norm — the unvaccinated, the immunocompromised, the non-compliant.

None of this is inherently oppressive. The point of a biopolitical analysis isn’t that public health is bad. The point is that public health governance operates through the production of knowledge about populations, and that this knowledge isn’t neutral — it carries assumptions about what lives matter, what risks are acceptable, and whose compliance can be compelled.

Extensions

Foucault’s analysis of biopower and governmentality has been extended by thinkers who argue that it doesn’t go far enough:

  • Achille Mbembe argues that biopower doesn’t account for the production of death as a technique of governance. His concept of necropolitics names the power to determine who may live and who must die — power exercised not through managing life but through the management of death.
  • Elizabeth Povinelli argues that biopower presupposes a prior governance of the Life/Nonlife distinction itself. Her concept of geontologies names the governance of the boundary between living and nonliving — a distinction that biopower assumes as given but that is itself historically produced and politically maintained. See the Povinellian school for the full framework.
  • Giorgio Agamben argues that biopower reveals the hidden structure of Western sovereignty: the production of bare life — life stripped of political status, exposed to sovereign violence without legal protection.

Check your understanding

1. What is the difference between sovereign power and biopower?

Sovereign power operates through the right to take life or let live — the king can execute or pardon. Its paradigmatic act is the public execution. Biopower operates through the right to make live and let die — the state manages the biological life of populations through regulation, statistics, and public health. Its paradigmatic act is the census, the vaccination campaign, the public health mandate. The shift isn’t from bad power to good power but from one logic of governance to another.

2. Why does Foucault distinguish between discipline and regulation?

Because they operate at different scales and use different techniques. Discipline targets individual bodies — it produces compliant subjects through surveillance, examination, timetable, and spatial organization. Regulation targets populations — it manages aggregate outcomes through statistics, demographics, public health, and environmental adjustment. Both are forms of biopower, but they work differently: discipline individualizes, regulation totalizes.

3. A city installs public health dashboards showing real-time air quality data and links poor air quality to asthma rates in specific neighborhoods. Analyze this in biopolitical terms.

The dashboard makes the population visible as a biological entity with measurable health outcomes — this is regulatory biopower, tracking population-level data. The knowledge it produces (air quality indices, asthma rates by neighborhood) is simultaneously a form of power: it determines which neighborhoods are classified as “at risk,” which populations become objects of intervention, and what counts as an acceptable level of exposure. The dashboard doesn’t just describe reality — it produces the categories through which governance operates. A biopolitical analysis would ask: who decided what to measure, what thresholds define “poor” air quality, and whose health outcomes count as the baseline against which deviation is measured?
