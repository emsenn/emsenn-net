Libidinal Investment

2026-03-02 The attachment of psychic energy or desire to an object, idea, person, or social structure — explaining why people are bound to things that may not serve their interests.
Defines Libidinal Investment, libidinal economy, cathexis

Libidinal investment (Besetzung in Freud, often translated as cathexis) is the attachment of psychic energy or desire to an object, idea, person, or social structure. In psychoanalysis, the concept explains why people are bound to things that may not serve their interests — the attachment is not rational but driven by desire. Freud used Besetzung (literally “occupation,” as in military occupation of a territory) to describe how the psyche distributes its finite energy across the objects of its world: some objects become charged with desire, others are abandoned, and the economy of this distribution shapes what a person can do, feel, and want.

Gilles Deleuze and Félix Guattari reworked the concept in Anti-Oedipus (1972), arguing that libidinal investment operates at the social level, not just the individual. People do not merely submit to oppressive social arrangements — they desire them, invest in them libidinally. Fascism, for Deleuze and Guattari, is not a matter of false consciousness (being tricked into accepting arrangements that harm you) but of genuine desire directed toward repressive structures. The question is not why people are deceived but why they actively desire their own repression. Wilhelm Reich had asked this question earlier, but Deleuze and Guattari radicalized it by rejecting the psychoanalytic framework of lack: desire does not arise from a missing object but is itself a productive force that assembles the social field.

This reframing shifts the political question from ideology (what do people believe?) to desire (what are people invested in?). A libidinal economy describes the distribution and circulation of these investments across a social field — which structures attract desire, which repel it, and how these attachments are produced and maintained. A workplace, a political party, a family, a nation — each is sustained not only by material necessity and ideological legitimation but by the desire its participants invest in it. When that desire withdraws, the structure becomes fragile regardless of how materially stable or ideologically justified it is.

The concept connects to Lauren Berlant’s cruel optimism: the condition in which people maintain libidinal attachment to objects (a job, a relationship, a political project) that are actively harmful to them, because the attachment itself has become the condition of their sense of possibility. The investment persists not because the object delivers what it promises but because withdrawing the investment would mean losing the structure of desire itself — a loss more threatening than the ongoing harm the attachment produces.
