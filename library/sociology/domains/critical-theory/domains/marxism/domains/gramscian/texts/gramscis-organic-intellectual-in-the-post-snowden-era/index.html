Gramsci's Organic Intellectual in the Post-Snowden Era

2025-04-13 by emsenn
Abstract

An analysis of how the organic intellectual's strategic legibility has become a site of vulnerability under post-Snowden surveillance conditions, and an argument for strategic illegibility in radical pedagogy.

How Legibility Under Surveillance Facilitates Neutralization

I. Introduction: The Organic Intellectual and the Problem of Legibility

I am writing to understand how the figure of the organic intellectual—originally posed by Antonio Gramsci as essential to counter-hegemonic struggle—has come to occupy a more precarious, if not paradoxical, position in the contemporary landscape of power. Where Gramsci cast the organic intellectual as a strategic asset in the long war of position, I argue that in today’s infrastructural and institutional conditions, the very legibility that once enabled political effectiveness has become a site of vulnerability.

This is not a rhetorical shift. It is a structural one. The transformation I am tracing begins with the problem of articulation. For Gramsci, organic intellectuals are not merely thinkers from below, but builders of collective sense. They do not represent a class or people from outside; they reorganize perception from within. Their task is to give ideological coherence to the scattered experiences of exploitation, racialization, dislocation. And in doing so, they must become visible. They must speak, write, organize, clarify. This is not incidental—it is their function.

But that visibility carries cost. To become legible—to one’s own people, to the state, to the institution—is to enter the field of recognition. And recognition is rarely neutral. As James C. Scott notes in Seeing Like a State, legibility is the precondition for control. It is how complex realities are simplified into governable categories. In Gramsci’s time, this meant that organic intellectuals could be surveilled, arrested, or killed. Today, the mechanisms are softer, more diffuse, and in some ways more insidious.

In the post-Snowden era, where influence is mapped through social graphs and intellectual authority is parsed through citation networks, the figure of the organic intellectual has been drawn into new regimes of visibility. Under the layered logics of surveillance capitalism, nonprofit professionalization, and platform-based discourse, the very work of theorizing from struggle is often pre-formatted for legibility—optimized for institutional uptake, algorithmic circulation, and fundable outputs. The result is what I will call soft capture: the containment of radical epistemic labor through the very systems that appear to celebrate it.

This paper has three aims. First, I revisit Gramsci’s concept of the organic intellectual to illuminate the strategic importance—and political risk—of legibility. Second, I trace a genealogy of surveillance that moves from COINTELPRO’s targeted repression of Black radical intellectuals to the infrastructural conditions of visibility that shape intellectual life today. Third, I propose that radical pedagogy must develop new ethics of strategic illegibility—modes of knowledge production that remain coherent to movements but resistant to institutional absorption.

This is not a call for obscurantism, nor a rejection of clarity. It is a refusal of compulsory visibility. The stakes are not aesthetic; they are tactical. Because in a system trained to metabolize resistance faster than it can be generated, being seen too clearly may be the end of the work.

II. From Necessity to Liability: Gramsci, Articulation, and the Surveillance of Coherence

The organic intellectual appears in Gramsci’s Prison Notebooks not as an abstraction, but as a tactical response to a structural problem: how to develop political consciousness capable of contesting hegemonic rule. Against the liberal fantasy of the autonomous intellectual—the “traditional” figure who claims universality through institutional detachment—Gramsci insists that all intellectuals are produced by, and in service to, a particular class position. What distinguishes the organic intellectual is not their education, but their embeddedness. They emerge from within a class formation, articulate its lived contradictions, and recompose its “common sense” into a coherent political force. “Every social group,” Gramsci writes, “creates alongside itself, organically, one or more strata of intellectuals which give it homogeneity and an awareness of its own function” (Gramsci, 1971, p. 5).

Articulation, then, is central. The organic intellectual does not simply reflect a group’s position—they organize it ideologically. They make the incoherent legible. They convert experience into analysis, pain into strategy. And this work, while necessary, is inherently risky. In clarifying the internal logic of struggle, the organic intellectual also exposes that logic to those outside the struggle. In translating subaltern knowledge into contestatory force, they also render themselves—and the movements they help shape—visible to power.

Gramsci, writing under fascist incarceration, understood this danger. But his theory still operates under the assumption that visibility is a terrain to be seized, not one that is already saturated by extractive attention. The demand for clarity—to one’s own class, to one’s comrades, to the public sphere—was a strategy in the war of position, a slow and cumulative counter-hegemonic struggle fought across civil society. Gramsci’s political terrain was one in which intellectual labor was largely illegible to the dominant order unless it crossed into formal institutions. Today, that assumption no longer holds.

The contemporary state does not rely on distance or misrecognition. It anticipates resistance. It monitors discourse. It maps coherence. In this sense, Gramsci’s call for clarity now courts danger—not just because the state may respond, but because institutional systems have been trained to recognize and incorporate critique itself.

This is where the work of James C. Scott becomes analytically useful. In Seeing Like a State (1998), Scott argues that modern statecraft depends on rendering populations legible: forests are turned into “timber resources,” people into “taxable citizens,” customs into “tradition” fit for regulation. Legibility enables administration—but also suppression. While Scott is concerned with the simplification of social life for governance, his insight extends to knowledge production: visibility enables classification, and classification enables control.

If Gramsci teaches us that the production of counter-hegemonic coherence is a necessary political act, Scott reminds us that that coherence—once produced—can be tracked, indexed, and used. In today’s political economy of knowledge, the conditions of visibility have changed. Intellectual labor is no longer hidden in the obscurity of civil society; it is tagged, quantified, and circulated through institutions hungry for critical legitimacy. In such a context, the organic intellectual becomes not just a target, but a signal—a recognizable pattern in the data. And pattern recognition, in a world saturated by surveillance, is not neutral. It is infrastructure.

It is in this transformation—from articulation to detectability—that we locate the shift: the organic intellectual, once a figure of necessity, becomes a figure of liability.

III. COINTELPRO and the Surveillance of Popular Epistemics

If Gramsci helps us understand the strategic role of the organic intellectual in articulating counter-hegemonic worldviews, the history of U.S. state repression—especially through the FBI’s Counter Intelligence Program (COINTELPRO)—shows how that same articulation has long been treated as a tactical threat. Between 1956 and 1971, COINTELPRO operated as a covert domestic surveillance initiative targeting groups deemed “subversive,” with an intensified focus on Black radical organizations in the late 1960s. While widely remembered for its brutality and overreach, COINTELPRO was also a program of epistemic disruption. Its targets were not chosen only for their visibility or militancy, but for their capacity to organize knowledge from within struggle—to articulate insurgent theory in popular terms.

This becomes clear when we examine the internal logics of COINTELPRO’s targeting decisions. A March 4, 1968 FBI memo articulated one of the program’s most infamous directives: “Prevent the rise of a ‘messiah’ who could unify and electrify the militant black nationalist movement.” While this phrase is often read through the lens of charisma or mass appeal, it also betrays a deeper anxiety: the fear of ideological synthesis. The state was not simply worried about charismatic oratorical power—it was concerned about figures who could make coherence. Those who could gather the fragments of racial capitalism, community defense, and Third World internationalism into a shared political grammar.

Fred Hampton embodied this risk. As deputy chairman of the Illinois chapter of the Black Panther Party, Hampton helped build programs that blurred the line between theory and practice: free breakfasts, health clinics, political education sessions. His ability to narrate these programs as manifestations of socialist principle—not charity, but power-building—made him legible not only to movement participants, but to state surveillance. His assassination by Chicago police, coordinated with the FBI, was not merely an act of local repression; it was a national message about the consequences of insurgent sense-making.

Stokely Carmichael (Kwame Ture) was similarly positioned. His shift from SNCC to the Black Panther Party, and later his turn toward Pan-Africanism, marked a transition from movement spokesperson to organic theorist. Carmichael developed a vocabulary that translated the conditions of Black life in the U.S. into an anti-imperialist, global framework. His intellectual work did not occur in seminar rooms, but in speeches, pamphlets, and dialogues with exiled revolutionaries. For the FBI, this was more dangerous than armed struggle—it was ideological internationalism that could not be quarantined.

George Jackson, whose prison writings fused Marxist analysis with visceral accounts of carceral racial violence, posed yet another version of the threat. His letters, compiled in Soledad Brother (1970), circulated in both activist and literary circuits, unsettling any clear boundary between personal testimony and revolutionary instruction. For Jackson, theory was survival. It emerged not after struggle, but as its immediate consequence. His murder by San Quentin guards during a staged “escape attempt” in 1971 followed years of state efforts to contain his reach—not because of what he did, but because of what he meant.

These cases point to a pattern: what the state feared most were not simply leaders, but what we might call epistemic centers—individuals whose work allowed movements to understand themselves, to name their enemies, to envision new totalities. That is: organic intellectuals in Gramsci’s full sense.

COINTELPRO’s tools—surveillance, infiltration, disinformation, character assassination—were not random. They were applied precisely where articulation was strongest. The goal was not only to destroy movements, but to disrupt the production of meaning. The state was not just watching. It was reading. It was analyzing political discourse, diagramming networks of thought, identifying ideological coherence as a threat vector.

This was not an anomaly. It was an early iteration of what would become the normative function of state and institutional power: to pre-empt the development of radical knowledge by recognizing and intervening upon its sources. The organic intellectual, once imagined as the vanguard of working-class self-awareness, was now someone to be found, watched, and broken.

As I will argue in the next section, this logic did not vanish with the exposure and formal dismantling of COINTELPRO. It evolved. And in the context of digital surveillance, institutional professionalization, and para-academic knowledge economies, it has re-emerged in quieter, more durable forms.

IV. Contemporary Continuity: From COINTELPRO to Cultural Capture

The logic that animated COINTELPRO—the identification and neutralization of epistemic threats—did not disappear with its exposure. It adapted. If COINTELPRO operated through wiretaps, infiltration, and physical elimination, the post-9/11 era of mass surveillance introduced a different tactic: anticipation through visibility. Where previous regimes of control sought to discover emerging threats, contemporary systems are designed to predict them, often before they materialize as organized force. This predictive turn does not depend on suppressing political speech. Instead, it thrives on its circulation.

The mechanism of capture has changed from disruption to recognition.

This transition is enabled by a convergence of forces: the rise of surveillance capitalism, the professionalization of radical discourse, and the datafication of intellectual life. Together, they constitute a regime in which the organic intellectual is no longer simply watched, but platformed, funded, followed, and—crucially—formatted.

1. Surveillance Infrastructures and Influence Mapping

Following Edward Snowden’s 2013 disclosures, it became evident that the U.S. surveillance apparatus—particularly the NSA—was less concerned with content than with pattern. As Snowden and later reporting by The Intercept revealed, intelligence agencies focused on identifying networks of influence, not just tracking direct threats. Social graph analysis, metadata collection, and algorithmic clustering now allow institutions to monitor the circulation of ideas, flag anomalous associations, and construct influence hierarchies without needing to understand the content of a single message.

In this paradigm, epistemic threat is not located in ideology, but in contagion. The question is no longer “What are they saying?” but “How far will it spread?” This shift aligns closely with what Shoshana Zuboff (2019) terms “surveillance capitalism”: an economic logic that harvests behavioral surplus and reorganizes human experience into data flows for prediction and control. In such a system, intellectual work is not immune; it is a dataset.

What began with national security now defines the infrastructure of thought.

2. Institutional Formatting and the Political Economy of Recognition

But surveillance alone doesn’t explain the contemporary containment of radical thought. If digital infrastructures render influence legible to states and platforms, institutions—particularly in academia, nonprofits, and media—reward that legibility. Here, the mechanism is not discipline, but incentive.

Following the post-2008 expansion of social justice discourse into philanthropic and academic circuits, radical vocabulary became increasingly institutionally viable. Grants were issued for “abolitionist” projects. Fellowships began to favor applicants who could articulate critiques of neoliberalism. University departments opened spaces for research framed around decolonial, feminist, and anti-racist commitments—so long as that research arrived in the expected containers: peer-reviewed articles, keynote addresses, op-eds, white papers.

This development is not trivial. It represents what Dylan Rodríguez (2020) calls the “carceral logic of the nonprofit industrial complex”: the tendency for institutions to absorb and manage the energies of resistance under the banner of progress. INCITE!’s The Revolution Will Not Be Funded (2007) maps this pattern precisely, arguing that by offering resources in exchange for format, institutions turn radical critique into manageable content. Not destroyed—just structured.

Under this model, the organic intellectual is not targeted for elimination. They are curated.

And curation, like legibility, is a risk vector.

3. Contentification: The Circulation Trap

This dynamic is nowhere clearer than in the rise of what might be called the contentification of theory. In the social media era, intellectual labor—particularly in movement spaces—often appears in bite-sized forms optimized for circulation: threads, memes, explainers, linked Google Docs, podcast clips. These formats are not inherently apolitical, but they are governed by metrics of visibility that reward recognizability over depth, scale over specificity.

This is not a rejection of accessibility. It is a recognition that in an economy of attention, what travels fastest is what fits cleanly into known categories. As Wendy Chun (2016) argues, networks do not merely transmit information—they shape the form of knowledge itself. The interface becomes epistemology. The scroll becomes pedagogy. In this environment, the clearest signal wins, and clarity is defined by infrastructural compatibility.

Radical ideas, once difficult to translate, are now increasingly pre-formatted. Theories of abolition, decolonization, or mutual aid circulate as hashtags, often stripped of their material commitments. The result is a loop: intellectual work must be visible to gain traction, but the terms of visibility ensure that traction occurs on the system’s terms.

What COINTELPRO once pursued through infiltration, the algorithm now achieves through uptake.

And unlike COINTELPRO, which required surveillance agents to read and understand their targets, the new system works best when it doesn’t understand—when it simply tracks what performs.

In this way, the organic intellectual has not been eliminated. They have been formatted. And the cost of visibility—of legibility—is no longer arrest or death, but professionalization, saturation, and containment.

In the next section, I turn to the thinkers, organizers, and pedagogues who have begun to resist this capture—not by speaking louder, but by refusing to be read in the ways that the system expects.

V. Strategic Illegibility: Refusal as Pedagogy

If legibility has become a vector for containment—if visibility now functions as a mechanism by which insurgent thought is tracked, formatted, and absorbed—then the organic intellectual must develop new strategies. Not simply to evade detection, but to refuse its terms. Not by retreating into mystification, but by cultivating modes of clarity that do not translate upward into institutional intelligibility.

This is the turn toward strategic illegibility: a form of knowledge production that resists uptake without abandoning coherence. Illegibility here does not mean obscurantism, nor a refusal to teach, explain, or theorize. It means refusing to be understood in the ways that dominant systems require. It is a form of anti-translation. A refusal to format.

This is not a speculative proposition. It is already in practice.

1. Audra Simpson and the Politics of Refusal

In her work on Indigenous sovereignty and settler colonialism, anthropologist Audra Simpson articulates a politics of refusal grounded not in recognition, but in opacity. In Mohawk Interruptus (2014), Simpson critiques the assumption that visibility is politically valuable, especially when imposed by structures that demand subaltern subjects make themselves legible to the settler state. Against this, she presents refusal as a form of political agency—not a silence or a lack, but a deliberate decision not to translate one’s political identity into forms the dominant system can assimilate.

For Simpson, refusal protects what opacity holds: the integrity of meaning in context. It challenges the notion that power must be confronted on its own terrain, and instead constructs a relation to knowledge that is territorial, embodied, and relational. In her words: “Recognition…is not always desirable.”

This reframes the organic intellectual’s task. Rather than making struggle legible to power, the work is to make struggle coherent to those within it, even if that coherence resists external interpretation.

2. Moten and Harney: The Undercommons as Study in Hiding

Fred Moten and Stefano Harney take a parallel route in The Undercommons: Fugitive Planning and Black Study (2013), where they argue that the university—like other institutions of knowledge—disciplines insurgent thought through its demand for productivity, rigor, and clarity. Their proposition is not to abandon thought, but to reposition it: to practice “study” in the undercommons, in spaces not designed for legibility, in relationships not monitored by institutional logics.

The undercommons is not a metaphor. It is a mode of life in which theory is lived, not submitted. It is “the social life of sociality,” where learning occurs in conversation, in shared rhythms, in what cannot be published or cited. Moten and Harney are clear: the point is not to hide for safety, but to refuse the terms of capture. “The only possible relationship to the university today is a criminal one,” they write—not to destroy it, but to study within, against, and beyond it.

In this framework, strategic illegibility is not a retreat from theory—it is theory, in fugitive form.

3. Leanne Betasamosake Simpson: Knowledge That Doesn’t Scale

Anishinaabe scholar and artist Leanne Betasamosake Simpson deepens this commitment to relational knowledge in As We Have Always Done (2017), where she critiques extractive intellectual practices as extensions of settler colonial logic. Simpson argues that Indigenous knowledge cannot be separated from the land, bodies, and kinship networks that produce and sustain it. As such, it resists disembedding. It does not scale. It does not circulate well.

This is not a failure. It is a refusal. Simpson teaches that some knowledges are meant to remain localized—not because they are parochial, but because they are sacred. Their power lies in their embeddedness. To lift them into visibility for institutional recognition is to do violence to their form.

What Simpson offers, then, is not only a model of radical pedagogy, but a warning: not all knowledge is meant to move through all systems. Strategic illegibility is not merely a tactic for evading surveillance. It is a practice of fidelity to place, to people, to specificity.

4. Mariame Kaba: Against the Logics of the Public Intellectual

Perhaps the most striking contemporary example of this practice is the abolitionist organizer and educator Mariame Kaba, who has long resisted the pressures of visibility even as her work has become central to the mainstreaming of abolitionist frameworks. Kaba declines TED Talks. She avoids self-branding. She uses social media to educate, but refuses to center herself as a figure of expertise. Her pedagogical method privileges community, replication, and movement infrastructure over intellectual celebrity.

Kaba’s refusal is quiet but deliberate. She shares, she teaches, she uplifts—but she does not give the system what it wants: a legible icon to endorse, critique, or replace. Her thought circulates without being captured. It is alive because it is held—not by platforms, but by people.

What all these figures share is a refusal to perform insurgent knowledge in ways that render it predictable, fundable, or citable. Their work is not less rigorous. It is rigorously disloyal to the infrastructures that demand recognition as the price of circulation.

Strategic illegibility, then, is not a rejection of intellectual work—it is its preservation. It is a practice of maintaining the integrity of theory under conditions that demand its extraction. It insists that knowledge is not a resource to be mined, but a relationship to be kept.

In the final section, I return to the pedagogical implications of this shift. What does it mean to teach theory in a way that does not prepare it for capture? What forms of circulation are compatible with liberation? What must we give up to keep our knowledge alive?

VI. Conclusion: Clarity, Containment, and the Stakes of Recognition

The figure of the organic intellectual, as conceived by Gramsci, emerged as a strategic necessity in the long war of position. It offered a model of embedded knowledge production—intellectual labor rooted in lived struggle, tasked with converting disorientation into analysis, ideology into collective practice. But that figure, once central to radical political formation, now finds itself increasingly compromised by the very infrastructures it must navigate.

In today’s landscape of datafied social life, institutional formatting, and visibility-as-value, the organic intellectual is caught in a new bind. Their work must still organize coherence. It must still build shared language, common analysis, collective direction. But the moment that coherence is recognized—by the algorithm, the platform, the funder, the university—it becomes susceptible to capture. The critique is metabolized. The theorist is absorbed. The work continues to circulate, but often in forms no longer accountable to the struggle that produced it.

This is not an inevitable outcome, but it is a predictable one.

What I have argued in this paper is that the condition of legibility—once a requirement for insurgent strategy—now functions as a liability. Surveillance no longer depends on violent repression. It depends on recognition. It needs theory to be formatted. It needs critique to be tagged. It needs pedagogy to scale.

What we are seeing is not the elimination of radical thought, but its institutionalization.

And what that demands, in response, is a deliberate shift in how we understand—and practice—the production of political knowledge.

Strategic illegibility is one name for this shift. It is not a call for silence, but for selectivity. It is not a retreat into obscurity, but a reorientation toward specificity, fidelity, and relationality. It draws on practices already in motion: Simpson’s refusal of state legibility, Moten and Harney’s fugitive study, Kaba’s disciplined refusal of the spotlight, Simpson’s insistence on the non-scalability of Indigenous thought.

Taken together, these are not fragments. They form a pedagogy of anti-extraction.

They ask:

  • Who are we making ourselves clear to?

  • Whose systems does our clarity empower?

  • What does our formatting allow to pass through—and what does it flatten?

Radical pedagogy must begin to teach these questions. Not as afterthoughts, but as core to the practice of theory itself. Because in a system that knows how to cite resistance, fund resistance, and tweet resistance, the survival of liberatory thought depends on knowing when not to explain, when not to scale, when not to be seen.

The task is no longer only to develop theory. The task is to protect it.

And to protect it, we must know who we’re speaking to—and who we’re training our thought to serve.

Because in this moment, clarity is not always the first step toward liberation.

Sometimes, it is the final step before containment.
