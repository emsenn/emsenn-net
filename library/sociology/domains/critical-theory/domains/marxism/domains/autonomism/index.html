Autonomism

Autonomism

The school of Marxist analysis originating in the Italian workerist (operaismo) movement of the 1960s and 70s. Where orthodox Marxism centered the party and the dialectic of productive forces, autonomism centers workers’ self-activity — the capacity of the working class to act autonomously from both capital and the party, refusing the terms of the labor relation rather than merely negotiating within them.

Mario Tronti’s inversion — that capital reacts to workers’ struggles, not the other way around — is the foundational move. Workers’ refusal of work (absenteeism, sabotage, wildcat strikes) forces capital to restructure, and that restructuring (automation, globalization, the shift to immaterial labor) is capital’s response to the threat from below. Antonio Negri extended this into the analysis of the “social factory” — the claim that production is no longer confined to the factory floor but has colonized all of social life, making every sphere of existence a site of exploitation and therefore a site of struggle.

Silvia Federici’s analysis of reproductive labor — the unwaged work of care, childrearing, housework, and social maintenance that capitalism depends on but refuses to recognize as labor — is the autonomist tradition’s most consequential extension. The subdomain on operaismo covers the Italian origins; the broader autonomism domain covers the extensions through Negri, Federici, Virno, and the analysis of immaterial and affective labor.
