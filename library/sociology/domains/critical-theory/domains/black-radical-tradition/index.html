Black Radical Tradition

Black Radical Tradition

The Black radical tradition is the lineage of Black political thought that analyzes how capitalism and racism are not separate systems but a single structure — what Cedric Robinson named racial capitalism. The tradition traces from the resistance practices of enslaved people, through Du Bois’s analysis of the color line and the general strike of the enslaved during the Civil War, through C.L.R. James’s analysis of the Haitian Revolution as the first successful anti-colonial revolt, through Robinson’s Black Marxism (1983) which argued that the Black radical tradition precedes and exceeds Marxism, through Angela Davis’s prison abolition and Ruth Wilson Gilmore’s geographic analysis of mass incarceration.

What unites the tradition is the insistence that Black liberation is not a special case of some more general emancipatory project — it is the vantage point from which the structure of modern domination becomes visible. Du Bois saw the color line as the problem of the twentieth century. Robinson saw racial capitalism as the foundational logic of modernity. Gilmore sees the prison as the spatial fix for the crises that racial capitalism produces. Each extends the analysis, and each insists that the analysis begins from Black experience because that is where the structure is most fully exposed.

The tradition is a lineage, not a school: it does not share a single method or theoretical framework but a continuity of concern, analysis, and commitment transmitted across generations.
