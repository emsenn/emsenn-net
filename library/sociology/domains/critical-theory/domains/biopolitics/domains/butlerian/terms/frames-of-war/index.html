Frames of War

2026-03-29 Butler's analysis of how the visual and discursive frames through which war is presented determine in advance which lives are grievable and which are available for destruction.
Defines Frames of War, framing (Butler)
Requires grievability

Frames of war, as Butler develops the concept in Frames of War: When Is Life Grievable? (2009), names the visual, discursive, and institutional frames through which war is presented to publics — and through which the lives destroyed in war are rendered either grievable or invisible. The frame is not a neutral window onto events. It is a structure that determines what appears within it and what is excluded, what registers as a life and what does not, what counts as violence and what counts as security.

Butler draws on Erving Goffman’s frame analysis and extends it into the domain of war and political violence. A photograph of a dead civilian can produce outrage or indifference depending on the frame within which it circulates. The embedded journalist’s footage of “precision strikes” frames destruction as surgical and necessary. The cell-phone footage of the same strike circulated outside institutional media frames it as massacre. The content is the same. The frame determines the response.

The frame operates at multiple levels simultaneously. At the level of visual representation, it determines what is shown and what is cropped out, what is captioned and what is left unnarrated. At the level of state rhetoric, it determines which populations are framed as threats (whose destruction is necessary for security) and which are framed as protected (whose lives are sacred). At the level of legal categories, it determines who counts as a combatant (killable) and who counts as a civilian (protected), and who falls into the gap between these categories where killing is neither murder nor war but something the legal framework does not name.

The frame is not all-powerful. Butler argues that frames can break — that the reality they exclude can force its way in through images, testimony, and solidarity that refuse the official framing. But the breaking of a frame is itself a political act, not a neutral disclosure of truth, because what replaces the broken frame is another frame with its own inclusions and exclusions. The political task is not to achieve framelessness but to contest the frames that render specific lives ungrievable.
