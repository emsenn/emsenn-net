Precarious Life

2026-03-29 Butler's framework for understanding how vulnerability is differentially distributed — some lives are protected while others are exposed to violence, abandonment, and erasure as a condition of governance.
Defines Precarious Life, precariousness (Butler)
Requires grievability

Precarious life, as Butler develops it in Precarious Life: The Powers of Mourning and Violence (2004), names the condition of exposure to violence, deprivation, and abandonment that is not equally shared but differentially distributed. All human life is precarious in the ontological sense — every body is vulnerable to injury, illness, and death. But precariousness in the political sense is produced: specific populations are made precarious by the withdrawal of legal protection, economic security, and social recognition, while other populations are shielded by the same structures that expose the first.

Butler wrote Precarious Life in the aftermath of September 11, 2001, asking why certain deaths (American, Western, civilian) produced massive collective grief while other deaths (Afghan, Iraqi, Palestinian, detained) were absorbed into silence or actively justified. The answer is not that some people care and others do not. The answer is that the frames through which lives appear — the visual media, the state rhetoric, the legal categories of citizenship and combatant status — determine in advance which lives register as lost. A life that was never apprehended as living cannot be mourned when it is destroyed.

This makes precariousness a biopolitical concept: the governance of who is exposed to harm is the governance of who counts. The differential distribution of precariousness tracks familiar axes of power — race, citizenship, gender, class, disability — but Butler insists the mechanism is not just material (who has resources) but perceptual (whose suffering is visible). A population can be materially deprived and perceptually invisible simultaneously, and the invisibility is what makes the deprivation politically sustainable.

The political demand that follows is not for the elimination of precariousness (which is impossible — vulnerability is constitutive of embodied existence) but for its equal distribution and for the recognition that precariousness obligates. If all life is precarious, then the structures that expose some lives while protecting others are not neutral but are themselves forms of violence.
