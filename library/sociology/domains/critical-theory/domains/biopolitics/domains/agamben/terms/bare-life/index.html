Bare Life

2026-03-06 Agamben's term for human existence stripped to its biological minimum — life included in the political order only by being excluded from it.
<p>Bare life (<em>nuda vita</em>) is Giorgio Agamben&rsquo;s term for human existence stripped to its biological minimum — life that is included in the political order only by being excluded from it. Agamben develops the concept in <em>Homo Sacer: Sovereign Power and Bare Life</em> (1995; English translation 1998), drawing on Foucault&rsquo;s biopolitics and Carl Schmitt&rsquo;s theory of sovereignty.</p> <p>The <em>homo sacer</em> is a figure from archaic Roman law: a person who could be killed by anyone without the killing counting as murder, yet could not be sacrificed in religious ritual. This double exclusion — from both human law and divine law — produces a life that has no standing in either order. The homo sacer exists in a zone of indistinction: neither fully inside political life (where killing is regulated by law) nor fully outside it (where killing has no political meaning). Agamben argues that this figure is not an archaic curiosity but the hidden foundation of modern sovereignty. The sovereign is the one who decides which lives count as political (citizens with rights) and which are reduced to bare life (the refugee, the detainee, the person on life support).</p> <p>Bare life is not natural life. It is life that has been produced as bare by a political operation — the sovereign exception that simultaneously includes and excludes. Every sovereignty claims the power to declare an exception — to suspend the law while remaining within its authority — and it is this exception that produces bare life. The state of exception is not an aberration but the structure through which sovereignty constitutes itself: by deciding who falls outside the law&rsquo;s protection while remaining subject to its force.</p> <p>The concentration camp, in Agamben&rsquo;s analysis, is the paradigmatic space where bare life is produced. In the camp, law is suspended and the inhabitants exist as pure biological life under total administrative control. They are not outside the political order — they are inside it in the specific mode of having been stripped of everything except their biological existence. The camp is not an exception to modern politics but its hidden truth, the space where the sovereign exception becomes permanent and the production of bare life becomes the normal operation of power.</p> <p>Agamben&rsquo;s later work in the <em>Homo Sacer</em> series extends this analysis: <em>State of Exception</em> (2003) traces how the exception has become the rule in modern governance, <em>Remnants of Auschwitz</em> (1998) examines what testimony is possible from the position of bare life, and <em>The Highest Poverty</em> (2011) explores forms of life that resist the sovereignty/bare-life structure.</p>
