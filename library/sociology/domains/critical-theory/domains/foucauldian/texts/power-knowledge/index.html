Power/Knowledge (Lesson)

Audience: readers who have completed the genealogy lesson and want to understand the mechanism that genealogical analysis reveals.

Learning goal: explain how power and knowledge constitute each other, identify the examination as the paradigmatic mechanism of power/knowledge, and analyze a concrete institution in these terms.

The common assumption genealogy overturns

Most people hold two assumptions about the relationship between power and knowledge:

  1. Knowledge is neutral. Scientists discover facts; politicians may misuse those facts, but the facts themselves are innocent.
  2. Knowledge liberates. The more we know, the freer we are. Education, research, and transparency are weapons against oppression.

Michel Foucault’s concept of power/knowledge challenges both assumptions — not by saying that knowledge is bad, but by showing that the production of knowledge is never separable from the exercise of power.

The examination

The clearest demonstration is the examination. Consider a school exam. It appears to be a neutral tool for measuring what students know. Foucault argued that it does at least four things simultaneously [@foucault1977]:

  1. It exercises power. The teacher controls the setting, the questions, the time limit, and the consequences. The student submits to the process.
  2. It produces knowledge. The exam generates scores, rankings, and classifications. The institution now “knows” something about the student — their ability level, their position relative to peers, their compliance with the curriculum.
  3. It creates subjects. The student becomes a case — an individual documented by grades, records, and files. The categories the institution uses to describe the student (gifted, average, struggling, disruptive) become part of the student’s identity — a process Foucault calls subjectification.
  4. It normalizes. The exam establishes what “normal” performance looks like. Students who fall outside that range become visible as problems to be managed.

The same structure operates in the clinical examination (the physician produces diagnostic knowledge while exercising authority over the patient), the criminal examination (the judge produces knowledge of the accused while exercising judicial power), and the job performance review (the manager produces knowledge of the employee while exercising organizational power).

In each case, the knowledge produced isn’t neutral — it is produced by the exercise of power, and it extends that power by making the subject more visible, more classifiable, and more manageable.

Why this matters for genealogy

Genealogy traces how practices and concepts emerge through contingency and conflict. Power/knowledge explains why those histories look the way they do. Institutions don’t just impose rules — they produce knowledge that justifies and extends their authority. The prison doesn’t just punish criminals. It produces the category of “the delinquent” — through case files, psychiatric evaluations, behavioral observation — and that category justifies the prison’s continued existence and expansion.

This is why genealogical histories aren’t just stories about power being exercised. They are stories about power producing the knowledge that sustains it — and about knowledge producing the categories through which power operates.

The categories that power produces

Foucault showed in The History of Sexuality [@foucault1978] that “the homosexual” as a type of person — a species, an identity — didn’t exist before the 19th Century. Same-sex acts existed, and they were judged morally or legally. But the idea that a person who engages in same-sex acts is a particular kind of person — with a distinct psychology, a characteristic pathology, a discoverable etiology — was produced by 19th-Century psychiatry, sexology, and criminal anthropology.

The production of this category was simultaneously an exercise of power (surveillance, pathologization, legal regulation) and a production of knowledge (case studies, taxonomies, etiological theories). The knowledge made the subjects visible; the power made the knowledge producible. Neither came first.

This is what Foucault meant by saying that power is “productive, not just repressive.” It doesn’t only say “no.” It says “you are this kind of person,” and it produces the knowledge that makes that categorization appear scientific, objective, and natural.

Contesting power/knowledge

If power and knowledge are co-constitutive, then contesting a regime of power requires contesting the knowledge that supports it — and vice versa. You can’t dismantle a disciplinary institution simply by removing the people in charge if the knowledge categories it produced remain intact. The diagnostic categories of psychiatry continue to classify people long after any individual psychiatrist retires.

This is why Foucault was skeptical of the idea that “more knowledge” automatically produces liberation. The knowledge produced by institutions of power — however rigorous and evidence-based — carries the structure of the power that produced it. Statistics about “criminal recidivism” are real data, but the category of “the recidivist” is itself a product of the carceral system’s need to classify and manage its subjects.

Check your understanding

1. A university defends its grading system by saying it objectively measures student learning. How would a power/knowledge analysis respond?

A power/knowledge analysis wouldn’t deny that grades measure something. It would point out that the grading system simultaneously exercises power (the institution determines what counts as learning, how it is measured, and what consequences follow) and produces knowledge (grades generate classifications — honor roll, probation, failing — that become part of the student’s institutional identity). The “objectivity” of the measurement doesn’t make it neutral: the system produces the categories through which students are known, ranked, and managed, and those categories reflect the institution’s interests and methods, not just the student’s abilities.

2. Why can't you separate the knowledge produced by the examination from the power exercised through it?

Because the knowledge is produced by the exercise of power (the examiner’s authority over the setting, questions, and consequences creates the conditions under which knowledge about the subject is generated) and the power is exercised through the knowledge produced (the classifications, rankings, and case files generated by the examination extend institutional control over the subject). Remove the power, and you can’t produce the knowledge. Remove the knowledge, and the power loses its mechanism.

3. Give an example of a category that appears to describe a pre-existing type of person but was produced by institutional practices.

Any answer that identifies a category produced by specific institutional practices counts. Examples: “the juvenile delinquent” (produced by the intersection of juvenile courts, social work, and developmental psychology in the late 19th Century), “the learning-disabled student” (produced by the intersection of standardized testing, special education law, and educational psychology), or “the terrorist” (produced by the intersection of intelligence agencies, criminal law, and political categories of threat). The key insight is that these categories don’t describe types that existed before the institutions classified them — the institutions’ practices of observation, classification, and management produced the types.
