Audience: readers new to critical theory who want to understand what Foucault’s work does and why it matters.

Learning goal: explain what questions Foucault asks, how his two methods (archaeology and genealogy) relate to each other, and why his analysis of power differs from common understandings.

A different kind of question

Most political theory asks: who has power, and is their use of it legitimate? Michel Foucault asked a different question: how does power work?

Consider psychiatric diagnosis. A conventional analysis might ask whether psychiatrists abuse their authority or whether diagnostic categories are accurate. Foucault asked something prior: what institutional practices — the clinical examination, the case file, the asylum’s spatial organization — made it possible for some people to speak authoritatively about others’ mental states in the first place? The question isn’t “is this psychiatrist right?” but “what had to be in place for this kind of claim to count as knowledge?”

This shift — from evaluating power to analyzing its conditions — runs through all of Foucault’s work. He studied madness (Madness and Civilization, 1961), clinical medicine (The Birth of the Clinic, 1963), the human sciences (The Order of Things, 1966), punishment (Discipline and Punish, 1975) [@foucault1977], and sexuality (The History of Sexuality, 1976) [@foucault1978]. In each case, the question was the same: how did the institutions, practices, and forms of knowledge that govern this domain come into being?

Two methods, one project

Foucault developed two methods across his career, each suited to a different aspect of that question.

Archaeology asks: what are the rules that determine what can be said in a given period? It maps the structure of a discourse — the conditions under which statements become possible and authoritative. When Foucault studied the emergence of modern biology in The Order of Things, he wasn’t tracing how one naturalist influenced another. He was asking what rules of classification and ordering had to be in place for biological statements to be possible at all. Archaeology is synchronic: it describes the configuration of a discourse at a moment in time. See the Archaeology lesson for the full treatment.

Genealogy asks: how did those rules come to be, and what forces sustain them? It traces the contingent, conflict-ridden history through which practices and concepts emerged — not as rational developments but as products of institutional struggle and accident. When Foucault studied the prison in Discipline and Punish, he traced how military discipline, monastic schedules, school examinations, and hospital case files converged on the management of criminal populations. Genealogy is diachronic: it traces the forces that produce and destabilize discursive structures over time. See the Genealogy lesson for the full treatment.

The two methods are complementary. Archaeology describes what a discourse looks like; genealogy explains how it got that way.

Power as productive, not just repressive

The most common misunderstanding of Foucault is that he was against power — that his work was a critique of how institutions oppress people. His argument was more unsettling than that.

Foucault argued that power isn’t just something that says “no.” Power produces. It produces knowledge (through the examination, the case study, the survey). It produces subjects (the “delinquent,” the “homosexual,” the “mentally ill” are categories that institutions create, not pre-existing types that institutions discover). It produces the very norms against which people measure themselves.

This is the concept of power/knowledge: power and knowledge aren’t separable. The institution that studies you exercises power over you through that study, and the knowledge it produces sustains its authority. The school examination simultaneously teaches, evaluates, ranks, and normalizes — and the student who has been examined carries the categories of that examination forward as self-understanding.

The trajectory of the curriculum

This curriculum moves through Foucault’s methods and concepts in roughly the order he developed them:

  1. This overview — the questions Foucault asks and why they matter
  2. Archaeology — analyzing the rules of discourse
  3. Genealogy — tracing how concepts emerge through contingency and power
  4. Power/Knowledge — the co-constitution of power and knowledge
  5. Biopower and Governmentality — how modern states govern populations through life itself

Each lesson builds on the previous one. Archaeology provides the framework for analyzing discourse; genealogy explains how discursive formations arise; power/knowledge names the mechanism that connects them; biopower and governmentality apply these tools to the modern state.

Check your understanding

1. What is the difference between asking "who has power?" and asking "how does power work?"

“Who has power?” assumes power is a possession — something held by individuals or groups who may use it well or badly. “How does power work?” treats power as a set of relationships and practices that produce effects — including the production of knowledge, subjects, and norms. Foucault’s question doesn’t ask whether power is legitimate but how it operates at the level of institutions, practices, and discourse.

2. How do archaeology and genealogy differ, and why does Foucault need both?

Archaeology maps the rules that govern a discourse at a given moment — what can be said, by whom, with what authority. Genealogy traces how those rules came to be through contingent historical forces. Archaeology is synchronic (a snapshot); genealogy is diachronic (a history). Foucault needs both because understanding a discourse requires knowing its structure and the forces that produced and sustain it.
