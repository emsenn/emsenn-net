Archaeology (Lesson)

Audience: readers who have completed the overview and want to understand Foucault’s method for analyzing discourse.

Learning goal: explain how archaeology analyzes the conditions that make knowledge possible in a period, and apply the method to a concrete example.

The question archaeology asks

In the 18th Century, European naturalists classified living things by arranging them in tables according to visible features — the shape of leaves, the structure of flowers, the arrangement of teeth. In the 19th Century, biologists classified living things by tracing their descent from common ancestors through hidden developmental processes. Both were studying “nature.” But the rules governing what counted as a valid claim about nature had changed completely.

A historian of ideas might explain this shift by tracing how Darwin influenced later thinkers, or how one theory improved upon another. Michel Foucault’s archaeology asks a different question: what rules of formation had to be in place for these statements to appear at all? Not “who influenced whom?” but “what made certain kinds of statements possible and others unthinkable?”

Foucault developed archaeology in The Order of Things (1966) and formalized it in The Archaeology of Knowledge (1969) [@foucault1972].

Core concepts

The statement

The basic unit of archaeological analysis is not the sentence, the proposition, or the speech act. It is the statement (énoncé) — the function by which something enters discourse as sayable. A statement isn’t defined by its meaning or truth value but by the conditions that make it possible: who can say it, in what institutional setting, using what concepts, and according to what rules.

“The patient exhibits signs of hysteria” is a statement that requires a specific configuration: a qualified speaker (the physician), an institutional site (the clinic), a conceptual grid (diagnostic categories), and a set of practices (the clinical examination). Change any of these conditions and the statement either changes its meaning or becomes unsayable.

Discursive formations

A discursive formation is the set of rules that governs the production of statements in a domain. It determines:

  • Who can speak — what qualifications, institutional positions, and credentials authorize statement-making
  • From what sites — what institutions produce authoritative statements (the laboratory, the courtroom, the clinic)
  • Using what concepts — what categories and classificatory grids organize the domain
  • According to what strategies — what kinds of arguments, methods, and evidence are recognized as valid

Archaeology maps these rules. It doesn’t ask whether the statements produced are true or false. It asks what conditions make them possible.

The episteme

An episteme is the most general level of archaeological analysis — the configuration of knowledge that defines what can be ordered, classified, and known in an entire period. Foucault argued in The Order of Things that Western knowledge passed through three distinct epistemes:

  • The Renaissance episteme organized knowledge through resemblance. Things were known by their similarities to other things — signatures, sympathies, analogies.
  • The Classical episteme organized knowledge through representation and taxonomy. Things were known by their place in ordered tables.
  • The Modern episteme organized knowledge through history and hidden depths. Things were known by their development over time and their underlying structures.

The transitions between epistemes are not gradual refinements. They are breaks — moments when the rules governing knowledge change at a deep structural level. The Classical naturalist and the modern biologist are not doing the same thing more or less well. They operate within different epistemes.

Archaeology in practice

Consider medical diagnosis. Archaeology wouldn’t ask whether a particular diagnosis is correct. It would ask:

  1. Who speaks? The licensed physician, the board-certified specialist. Not the patient, not the nurse (in most institutional contexts), not the traditional healer.
  2. From what site? The clinic, the hospital, the laboratory. Not the street, not the home, not the church.
  3. Using what concepts? Disease categories defined by ICD codes, anatomical norms, statistical distributions of biological markers.
  4. According to what strategies? Evidence-based medicine, clinical trials, peer review, diagnostic algorithms.

These conditions determine what can be said about a body. A physician who said “this patient’s humors are imbalanced” wouldn’t be wrong in the way that a physician who misreads an X-ray is wrong. The physician would be making a statement that the current discursive formation doesn’t recognize — speaking from within a different set of rules.

Archaeology versus genealogy

Archaeology and genealogy ask different questions about the same material. Archaeology asks: what are the rules that govern this discourse right now? Genealogy asks: how did those rules come to be?

Applied to medical diagnosis: archaeology maps the current structure — the institutions, qualifications, concepts, and methods that determine what counts as medical knowledge. Genealogy traces how that structure was produced — the historical struggles between physicians and surgeons, the rise of the hospital as a site of knowledge production, the alliance between medical authority and state power, the specific institutional shifts that made evidence-based medicine dominant.

The two methods are complementary. Archaeology describes the synchronic structure of a discourse. Genealogy traces the diachronic forces that produced it. In Foucault’s own work, archaeology came first chronologically — he developed it in the 1960s and turned to genealogy in the 1970s — but neither method supersedes the other.

Check your understanding

1. A history of medicine explains that doctors stopped using leeches because they discovered that bloodletting doesn't work. What would an archaeologist say is incomplete about this explanation?

It treats the change as a correction within a stable framework — as though medicine has always been asking the same questions and gradually finding better answers. An archaeological analysis would argue that the shift involved a change in the rules governing what counts as medical evidence. The physician who prescribed leeches operated within a discursive formation organized around humoral theory, direct observation of symptoms, and bedside expertise. The physician who rejected bloodletting operates within a different formation organized around clinical trials, statistical evidence, and laboratory analysis. The question is not just “which treatment works?” but “what counts as evidence that a treatment works?” — and that question is answered differently in different discursive formations.

2. What is the difference between a statement and a sentence?

A sentence is a grammatical unit. A statement is a function — the conditions under which something enters discourse as sayable. The same sentence can be a different statement depending on who says it, where, and within what institutional framework. “The patient is mentally ill” said by a psychiatrist in a clinical setting is a statement authorized by the discursive formation of psychiatry. The same sentence said by a stranger on the street is not the same statement — it doesn’t carry diagnostic authority, doesn’t generate a case file, doesn’t trigger institutional consequences.

3. Pick a domain you know (education, law, journalism, technology). What rules determine who can make authoritative statements in that domain?

There is no single correct answer, but a good response would identify: (a) the qualifications or credentials that authorize speakers, (b) the institutional sites from which authoritative statements are produced, (c) the conceptual categories that organize the domain, and (d) the methods or evidence standards that determine what counts as a valid claim. For journalism, you might identify: credentialed reporters and editors as authorized speakers; newsrooms and editorial boards as institutional sites; categories like “news,” “opinion,” “analysis” as organizing concepts; and verification, sourcing, and editorial review as evidence standards.
