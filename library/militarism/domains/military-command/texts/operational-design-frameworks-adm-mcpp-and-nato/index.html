Operational Design Frameworks: ADM, MCPP, and NATO

<p>Learning goal: understand operational design as a family of doctrinal paradigms (Army ADM, Marine Corps design within MCPP, NATO AJP-5 planning doctrine) and learn how to translate between them without losing the underlying problem frame [@usarmy2015atp5_0_1; @usmc2020mcwp5_10; @nato2019ajp5].</p> <h2 id="the-shared-problem">The shared problem</h2> <p>All operational design frameworks exist to solve the same failure mode:</p> <ul> <li>headquarters do detailed planning too early,</li> <li>on an implicit theory of the environment,</li> <li>then defend the plan even as assumptions fail.</li> </ul> <p>Operational design replaces implicit theory with explicit artifacts that can be challenged and revised.</p> <h2 id="a-crosswalk-the-same-moves-in-different-vocabularies">A crosswalk: the same moves in different vocabularies</h2> <p>You can translate most design frameworks into four recurring moves:</p> <ol> <li><strong>Frame the operational environment.</strong> What system are we in? Who matters? What constraints are real?</li> <li><strong>Frame the problem.</strong> What must change, and why is it hard? What assumptions are doing the work?</li> <li><strong>Develop an operational approach.</strong> What is our theory of change? What sequence and priorities do we commit to?</li> <li><strong>Reframe.</strong> Which assumptions failed, and what does that do to the approach?</li> </ol> <p>The doctrinal value is not the labels. The value is that these moves are <em>required</em> before the staff optimizes tasks and timelines.</p> <h2 id="design-elements-as-decision-hygiene">Design elements as decision hygiene</h2> <p>Design elements (end state, objectives, decisive points, lines, phases, termination logic, etc.) are useful only insofar as they change decisions.</p> <p>Use the rule:</p> <ul> <li>if an element does not constrain a decision, it is decorative.</li> </ul> <p>See: <a href="../terms/operational-design-elements.md" class="link-internal">Operational design elements</a>.</p> <h2 id="design-to-planning-translation-what-to-carry-forward">Design-to-planning translation (what to carry forward)</h2> <p>A design effort is successful when it produces portable outputs for planning paradigms like <a href="../terms/mdmp.md" class="link-internal">MDMP</a> and <a href="../terms/jopp.md" class="link-internal">JOPP</a>:</p> <ul> <li>a problem statement (and what must change),</li> <li>5–10 explicit assumptions,</li> <li>an operational approach (narrative + graphic),</li> <li>candidate decision points and CCIR,</li> <li>and the top constraints (time, readiness, sustainment, institutions).</li> </ul> <h2 id="exercise-build-a-minimal-design-packet">Exercise: build a minimal design packet</h2> <p>Pick a historical operation, crisis, or long-duration security campaign and produce:</p> <ol> <li>An environmental frame (actors, constraints, dynamics).</li> <li>A one-paragraph problem statement.</li> <li>5 assumptions (each with a way you would notice it failing).</li> <li>A one-page operational approach (narrative + sketch).</li> <li>3 decision points with the information each needs.</li> </ol> <p>Then ask: which parts of this packet become planning inputs, and which parts must remain living design artifacts during execution?</p>

References
