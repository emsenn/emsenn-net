Cognitive Capitalism

2026-03-03
Defines Cognitive Capitalism

Cognitive capitalism is a concept from post-operaismo thinkers — Yann Moulier-Boutang, Carlo Vercellone, and others — describing the phase of capitalism in which knowledge, attention, and affective labor become the primary sources of value. Under cognitive capitalism, the production of ideas, communication, and interpretation is the central economic activity, not a supplement to material production. Value extraction shifts from the factory floor to the nervous system.

The concept builds on the operaismo tradition’s analysis of how capital reorganizes the labor process across historical phases. Where industrial capitalism extracted value from manual labor disciplined by the assembly line, cognitive capitalism extracts value from mental and relational activity that resists the same forms of measurement and control. The workplace dissolves into life itself: every conversation, every act of attention, every moment of creative problem-solving becomes potentially productive for capital.

Franco “Bifo” Berardi extended this analysis in The Soul at Work (2009) [cite:@berardi_SoulWork_2009], arguing that under cognitive capitalism, attention becomes both commodity and exhaust. The mind is put to work not occasionally but continuously, and burnout is not a failure of the system but a proof of yield. The worker’s exhaustion is the measurable trace of value successfully extracted.

emsenn draws on this framework in “Governing by confusion” to describe how curiosity and interpretive labor are harvested as system resources. The organic intellectual becomes a class of material, and their burnout registers not as dysfunction but as credentialed intensity — evidence that the system has successfully converted cognitive capacity into stabilizing output.
