Stability Dynamics in Cognitive Systems

<h1 id="abstract">Abstract</h1> <p>A cognitive system adapts to its sensory environment. This is the asymmetric stability setting: the environment (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span>) is fixed (or slowly changing), and the cognitive system (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>) adapts to it. The system&rsquo;s stability reward — the rate of divergence minimization between successive belief states — is a function of its coupling to the environment, not an intrinsic property.</p> <p>We show that this stability reward corresponds exactly to the negative time derivative of variational free energy in predictive-processing models (Friston 2010). The two-channel decomposition of <em>Information-Theoretic Stability as Reward Function</em> maps onto predictive processing: behavioral convergence is the reduction of prediction error, and informational coupling is the increase of mutual information between internal model and environment. The cognitive system&rsquo;s stability is its grip on the world.</p> <h1 id="1-introduction">1. Introduction</h1> <p>Predictive-processing theory describes perception and action as dual aspects of Bayesian inference (Rao &amp; Ballard 1999; Friston 2010). A cognitive system maintains an internal generative model of observations and latent causes; through recursive updating, it seeks to minimize surprise or free energy. The system adapts; the sensory environment (at the timescale of inference) does not.</p> <p>This is the asymmetric stability setting of <em>Information-Theoretic Stability as Reward Function</em>. The cognitive system is <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The sensory environment is <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The system&rsquo;s stability reward is a function of how well it couples to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span> — how well its internal states track the environment&rsquo;s structure.</p> <h1 id="2-background">2. Background</h1> <h2 id="21-cognitive-state-space">2.1 Cognitive State Space</h2> <p>Let the cognitive state at time <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t</annotation></semantics></math></span> be a distribution over latent causes, </p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>q</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>s</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>=</mo><mi>p</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>s</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><msub><mi>o</mi><mrow><mn>1</mn><mo>:</mo><mi>t</mi></mrow></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex"> q_t(s) = p(s_t | o_{1:t}), </annotation></semantics></math></span><p> where <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>o</mi><mrow><mn>1</mn><mo>:</mo><mi>t</mi></mrow></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">o_{1:t}</annotation></semantics></math></span> denotes all observations up to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>t</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">t</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The generative model specifies likelihood <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>o</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><msub><mi>s</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p(o_t|s_t)</annotation></semantics></math></span> and prior dynamics <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>p</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>s</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><msub><mi>s</mi><mrow><mi>t</mi><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">p(s_t|s_{t-1})</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The manifold <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="script">Q</mi><mo>=</mo><mo stretchy="false">{</mo><msub><mi>q</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>s</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo stretchy="false">}</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathcal{Q} = \{q_t(s)\}</annotation></semantics></math></span> carries the Fisher–Rao metric (Amari 2016).</p> <h2 id="22-variational-free-energy">2.2 Variational Free Energy</h2> <p>Following Friston (2010): </p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><mi>F</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>q</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>=</mo><msub><mi>D</mi><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">K</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">L</mi></mrow></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>q</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>s</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mtext> </mtext><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><mtext> </mtext><mi>p</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>s</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><msub><mi>o</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>−</mo><mi>ln</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi>p</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>o</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">.</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex"> F(q_t) = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_t(s)\,||\,p(s_t|o_t)) - \ln p(o_t). </annotation></semantics></math></span><p> Minimizing <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>F</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">F</annotation></semantics></math></span> reduces the divergence between the system&rsquo;s internal state and the true posterior — it is the mechanism by which <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> adapts to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span>.</p> <h2 id="23-stability-reward">2.3 Stability Reward</h2> <p>The stability reward of the cognitive system is </p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>R</mi><mi>s</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>=</mo><mo>−</mo><mfrac><mn>1</mn><mrow><mi>δ</mi><mi>t</mi></mrow></mfrac><mtext> </mtext><msub><mi>D</mi><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">K</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">L</mi></mrow></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>q</mi><mrow><mi>t</mi><mo>+</mo><mi>δ</mi></mrow></msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><msub><mi>q</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">.</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex"> R_s(t) = -\frac{1}{\delta t}\, D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{t+\delta}||q_t). </annotation></semantics></math></span><p> This measures how fast successive belief states are converging. It is not a property of the cognitive system alone — it depends on the structure of the sensory input <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span> that the system is adapting to.</p> <h1 id="3-stability-reward-equals-free-energy-descent">3. Stability Reward Equals Free-Energy Descent</h1> <h2 id="31-equivalence">3.1 Equivalence</h2> <p>Under natural gradient descent on free energy, </p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><msub><mover accent="true"><mi>q</mi><mo>˙</mo></mover><mi>t</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mo>−</mo><msub><mrow><mi stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>q</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex"> \dot q_t = -\mathrm{grad}_g\, F(q_t), </annotation></semantics></math></span><p> the free energy decreases monotonically: </p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∂</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mi>F</mi><mo>=</mo><mo>−</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∥</mi><msub><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">g</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">r</mi><mi </annotation></semantics></math></span><p> Comparing with the stability reward, </p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>R</mi><mi>s</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>≈</mo><mo stretchy="false">⟨</mo><msub><mover accent="true"><mi>q</mi><mo>˙</mo></mover><mi>t</mi></msub><mo separator="true">,</mo><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∇</mi><mi>q</mi></msub><mi>ln</mi><mo>⁡</mo><msub><mi>q</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><msub><mo stretchy="false">⟩</mo><mi>g</mi></msub><mo separator="true">,</mo></mrow><annotation free energy. The cognitive system&rsquo;s stability IS its free-energy descent.</p> <h2 id="32-two-channel-reading">3.2 Two-Channel Reading</h2> <p>In the asymmetric framing, free-energy minimization decomposes into two channels:</p> <ul> <li><strong>Behavioral convergence</strong>: reduction of prediction error (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>&rsquo;s marginal approaching target).</li> <li><strong>Informational coupling</strong>: increase of mutual information between <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>&rsquo;s internal model and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span>&rsquo;s structure.</li> </ul> <p>Prediction error reduction is the cognitive system&rsquo;s behavior becoming more appropriate. MI increase is the system&rsquo;s internal model becoming a better map of the environment. Free-energy descent drives both simultaneously, and the accounting identity holds: whatever free-energy reduction isn&rsquo;t going to prediction error reduction must be going to model improvement.</p> <h1 id="4-affect-as-stability-curvature">4. Affect as Stability Curvature</h1> <p>Define <strong>affect</strong> as the time derivative of stability reward: </p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>=</mo><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∂</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><msub><mi>R</mi><mi>s</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">.</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex"> A(t) = \partial_t R_s(t). </annotation></semantics></math></span><p> Positive <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A(t)</annotation></semantics></math></span> indicates acceleration toward stability — the system is getting better at getting better. Negative <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A(t)</annotation></semantics></math></span> indicates deceleration — uncertainty is amplifying.</p> <p>In predictive coding, precision <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>π</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">V</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">a</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">r</mi></mrow><mo stretchy="false">[</mo><msub><mi>ϵ</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">]</mo><msup><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow></msup></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\pi_t = (\mathrm{Var}[\epsilon_t])^{-1}</annotation></semantics></math></span> modulates update rate (Feldman &amp; Friston 2010): </p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><msub><mover accent="true"><mi>q</mi><mo>˙</mo></mover><mi>t</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mo>−</mo><msub><mi>π</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mtext> </mtext><msub><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">g</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">r</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">a</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">d</mi></mrow><mi>g</mi></msub><mi>F</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>q</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo separator="true">,</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex"> \dot q_t = -\pi_t\, \mathrm{grad}_g F(q_t), </annotation></semantics></math></span><p> so <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>∝</mo><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∂</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><msub><mi>π</mi><mi>t</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A(t) \propto \partial_t \pi_t</annotation></semantics></math></span>. Affective change tracks the system&rsquo;s changing confidence in its coupling to the environment.</p> <h1 id="5-hierarchical-stability">5. Hierarchical Stability</h1> <p>Cognitive systems are hierarchical: higher levels encode slower, more abstract causes; lower levels encode faster, sensory features (Friston 2008; Clark 2013). Each level <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>l</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">l</annotation></semantics></math></span> has its own stability reward: </p> <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi>R</mi><mi>s</mi><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>l</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msubsup><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>t</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>=</mo><mo>−</mo><mfrac><mn>1</mn><mrow><mi>δ</mi><mi>t</mi></mrow></mfrac><mtext> </mtext><msub><mi>D</mi><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">K</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">L</mi></mrow></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msubsup><mi>q</mi><mrow><mi>t</mi><mo>+</mo><mi>δ</mi></mrow><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>l</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow></msubsup><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">∣</mi><msubsup><mi>q</mi><mi>t</mi><mrow><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>l</mi><mo D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{t+\delta}^{(l)}||q_t^{(l)}). </annotation></semantics></math></span><p> Total stability is the weighted sum <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msubsup><mi>R</mi><mi>s</mi><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">t</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">o</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">t</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">a</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">l</mi></mrow></msubsup><mo>=</mo><msub><mo>∑</mo><mi>l</mi></msub><msub><mi>w</mi><mi>l</mi></msub><mtext> </mtext><msubsup><mi>R</mi><mi>s</mi><mrow><mo R_s^{(l)}</annotation></semantics></math></span>, with weights <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>w</mi><mi>l</mi></msub></mrow><annotation xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>w</mi><mi>l</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">w_l</annotation></semantics></math></span> — allocating resources to levels where the coupling to the environment is changing fastest (Dayan &amp; Abbott 2001).</p> <h1 id="6-discussion">6. Discussion</h1> <ol> <li> <p><strong>Cognitive stability is environmental coupling.</strong> The cognitive system has no intrinsic stability. Its stability reward is a function of its relationship to the sensory environment. A brain in a jar with no input has <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>R</mi><mi>s</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">R_s = 0</annotation></semantics></math></span> — perfect stationarity, zero coupling.</p> </li> <li> <p><strong>Disorders as coupling failures.</strong> Excessive rigidity (over-stabilization) means the system stops adapting to new environmental structure. Volatility (under-stabilization) means the system can&rsquo;t maintain coupling. Both are failures of the stability-environment relationship, consistent with neurocomputational accounts of schizophrenia and anxiety (Hohwy 2013; Friston 2017).</p> </li> <li> <p><strong>Affect tracks coupling dynamics.</strong> Pleasure and displeasure mark acceleration or deceleration of environmental coupling — the felt quality of getting better or worse at tracking the world.</p> </li> </ol> <h1 id="7-conclusion">7. Conclusion</h1> <p>A cognitive system&rsquo;s stability reward is its rate of coupling to the sensory environment. This is mathematically identical to free-energy descent. The two-channel decomposition separates prediction error reduction from model improvement. Affect tracks the second derivative of coupling. The cognitive system is not a thing that has stability — it is a process of stabilizing to its environment.</p> <h1 id="references">References</h1> <ul> <li>Amari, S.
