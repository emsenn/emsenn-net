<h2 id="abstract">Abstract</h2> <p>Consider a system <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> adapting to a fixed reference system <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span>. We define the <strong>stability reward</strong> of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> as the rate of divergence minimization between successive states of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>, and show that this quantity decomposes into two terms: the rate at which <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>&rsquo;s marginal behavior converges to target, and the rate at which <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>&rsquo;s mutual information with <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span> increases. Both terms are functions of the coupling between <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The stability of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> is not an intrinsic property of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> — it is a reward function of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>&rsquo;s relationship to <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span>.</p> <p>When <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> evolves by gradient descent on the joint divergence from equilibrium, the composite divergence decreases monotonically, and the two channels of convergence — behavioral and informational — must sum to that monotonic decrease at every instant. Neither can stall without the other compensating. Introduction</h2> <p>Information theory measures uncertainty and its reduction in probabilistic terms. This paper asks a specific question: when a system <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> adapts toward a fixed reference <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span>, what governs the rate at which <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> stabilizes?</p> <p>The answer has a clean form. <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>&rsquo;s stability — the rate at which its successive states become less divergent — decomposes into exactly two components: convergence of <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span>&rsquo;s marginal distribution toward target, and increase of mutual information between <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> and <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span>. The worst case for MI relative to the marginal is <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∂</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><mi>I</mi><mo>=</mo><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∂</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><msub><mi>D</mi><mi>x</mi></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\partial_t I = \partial_t D_x</annotation></semantics></math></span>, which occurs only at equilibrium (<span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">∂</mi><mi>t</mi></msub><msub><mi>D</mi><mtext>joint</mtext></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\partial_t D_{\text{joint}} = 0</annotation></semantics></math></span>).</p> <h2 id="5-related-work-and-positioning">5. Related Work and Positioning</h2> <p>The tools — KL divergence, Fisher–Rao metric, natural gradient descent — are standard information geometry (Amari 2016). The chain rule is textbook (Cover &amp; Thomas 2006; Csiszár &amp; Shields 2004). The connection between divergence minimization and entropy production is established in nonequilibrium thermodynamics (Crooks 1999; Jaynes 1957).</p> <p>The contribution is the specific framing:</p> <ol> <li><strong>Stability as coupling.</strong> By fixing one system and watching the other adapt, the stability reward becomes a function of the relationship, not of either system in isolation. The definition of stability reward is the negative entropy production rate by another name, but framing it as a reward of coupling orients the analysis differently.</li> <li><strong>Two-channel decomposition.</strong> The asymmetric chain rule gives exactly two channels (behavioral + informational). This is tighter than the symmetric case, which has three components and allows more complex fluctuation.</li> <li><strong>The MI-dominates-marginal inequality.</strong> Part (d) of the theorem — that MI improvement is always at least as large as marginal improvement — is a structural constraint on the path of adaptation, not just on its endpoint.</li> </ol> <h2 id="6-limitations">6. Limitations</h2> <p>The framework assumes <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>B</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">B</annotation></semantics></math></span> is fixed. When both systems adapt simultaneously, the decomposition has three moving parts and the MI-dominates-marginal inequality does not hold. The framework does not explain why <span class="katex"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">A</annotation></semantics></math></span> would minimize divergence — that is an external constraint (physical, biological, or engineered). Bounding the magnitude of temporary MI dips in terms of system parameters remains open.</p> <h2 id="7-conclusion">7. Conclusion</h2> <p>When a system adapts to a fixed reference, its stability reward is a function of the coupling between them. The joint divergence decomposes into marginal convergence and mutual information change — two channels that must sum to the monotonically nonneg composite convergence at every instant. MI improvement always matches or exceeds marginal improvement. The stability of one system is the reward function of its relationship to the other.</p> <h2 id="references">References</h2> <ul> <li>Amari, S.
