Decision: four-group directory structure with twelve cross-cutting types

2026-03-09
Table of contents
  • Context
  • Decision
  • Why gerund form
  • Why these four groups
  • Alternatives considered

Context

A knowing semioverse needs a vocabulary of cross-cutting directory types that can appear within any Thing. The repository had accumulated ad-hoc types (terms/, concepts/, texts/, curricula/, disciplines/, schools/, topics/) without a principled account of why those types exist, how they relate, or what types are missing.

Research into knowledge organization systems, intellectual differentiation frameworks, epistemological structure of knowledge work, and process ontology was commissioned and produced six research texts, synthesized at information/disciplines/knowledge-systems/texts/research/ directory-organization-for-knowledge-systems.md.

Decision

Twelve cross-cutting directory types, grouped into four functional groups:

governing (methods/, practices/, standards/) — how work is conducted within a Thing. Grounded in the methods/practices/standards triad from philosophy of science: methods are generalized inquiry forms, practices are situated enactments, standards are normative constraints (Bogen on data production; Douglas on scientific objectivity; Wenger on communities of practice).

engaging (learning/, researching/, applying/) — how knowledge flows into, out of, and through a Thing. The three modes of knowledge engagement: intake from external sources, output in accessible form, and use in concrete situations. The cycle is iterative: applying reveals gaps → researching fills them → learning structures what research produces.

situating (disciplines/, schools/, lineages/) — where knowledge is placed in intellectual context, along three independent axes identified by differentiation research:

  • disciplines: institutional/jurisdictional (Abbott)
  • schools: epistemic/stylistic (Kuhn, Fleck, Lakatos)
  • lineages: derivational/transmissive (Fleck’s apprenticeship and circulation; Wenger’s communities of practice as transmission structures)

defining (topics/, concepts/, terms/) — what knowledge consists of, at three levels of specificity grounded in terminology science (Dahlberg) and library science (Hjorland):

  • terms: linguistic handles for specific referents
  • concepts: units of knowledge emerging from and generalizing across terms; patterns in wider inferential systems
  • topics: aboutness-classes gathering many concepts under one region of concern

Why gerund form

All four group names are gerunds (governing, engaging, situating, defining). This reflects process-ontological commitment: categories track patterns of becoming rather than sorting finished things (Whitehead/Rescher/Seibt). Directory names name activities, not substances. The twelve member types are process-nouns for the same reason.

Why these four groups

The four groups represent two orthogonal distinctions:

Activity vs structure: governing and engaging are activity-oriented (what is DONE); situating and defining are structure-oriented (what is PRODUCED by activity). But all names are gerunds because even structural categories are activities of situating and defining.

Internal vs external: governing and defining are inward-facing (how THIS Thing works, what THIS Thing consists of); engaging and situating are outward-facing (how knowledge flows relative to the world, where this Thing sits in broader context).

Alternatives considered

  • Keep existing ad-hoc types without grouping: rejected — no principled account of what types exist or why, making it impossible to know whether a type is missing.
  • Single flat list without groups: rejected — the grouping carries semantic information about what each type does.
  • Subject-based grouping (following library science shelf classification): rejected — we classify by function within a Thing, not by the subject the content is about.
  • differentiating as the name for the third group: considered but rejected as not fitting the gerund pattern cleanly. situating better captures what disciplines, schools, and lineages do: they place knowledge in intellectual context.
  • communities/ as the third member of the situating group: considered; rejected in favor of lineages because lineages capture derivational transmission specifically, which is more precise than the broader “communities” and better fits the emsemioverse’s emphasis on intellectual descent (neuroreflexivity from relationality; semiotic-markdown from agential semioverse).
